[pageend4]5 VISIT TO CZECHOSLOVAKIA AFTER 43 YEARS I am getting off the plane at Ruzyň. A group of my relatives is expecting me — relatives I haven't met face to face for many many years. We are all visibly moved. But the custom formalities must be disposed of first. The officials seem to have no interest in my suitcases; they just want to know how many dollars I have brought with me, how much in cash,how much in travelers cheques. They write down carefully every figure. Soon we are in a bus, driving through Prague, the "Golden City" of all Czechs. It is a curiously depressing experience — much of the gold seems to have worn off. Most buildings seem to be neglected, many have scaffolding around them, to protect passers-by from the falling bricks and plaster. I hear that recently two women were killed in that fashion in Vodičkova Ulice. The trams are the same I remember from 40 years ago — and they were old even then.I hear some new prototypes have been introduced recently,but are not working out too well — the electrical wiring keeps burning out. We talk about the food supply. It too,seems to be beset with problems. Meat is available at the moment, but there is no sugar and no potatoes. My informants seem to be quite reconciled to this. "There is always a shortage" they shrug "if it's not the sugar, it is the onions or other vegetables, or something else. We are used to standing up in queues, it seems to be a permanent part of our 1 ives. " [pageend5]6 I am anxious to see my native district, around Tábor. It makes my heart sad. The beautiful forests I remember, are stripped off; logs are laying everywhere,ready for transportation to the mills. Sometimes the forest looks intact from the outside, but when you walk into it, you'll see that most of the trees have been cut out; only a few remain, standing far apart. The forest is cold at night, so that mushrooms have stopped growing there. "It used to be better even in war-time under Hitler," the oldtimers say. "Sure, he cut down the trees too, but he would make certain that new ones were planted in their place. Nobody seems to do that nowadays." I decide to make a sidetrip to Jablonec, a place famous for its glassware, where an international exhibition is held at this time. The government must have spent a lot of money on representation. The exhibits are interesting. I seem to create a mild sensation in the little mountain village where I stay. They notice my Sokol lapel button. Word spread around about a Sokol from San Francisco visiting them. We meet in the village inn that evening, and talk about Sokol, as it used to be, twenty odd years ago. They are all a little nostalgic, remember the good old times they used to have in Sokol before it was disbanr.ed. I tell them about our unit in San Francisco, and how we still keep the old way of life alive ... about our dances, gym classes, the old country saussages we still make for our members a couple of times a year. It is impossible to talk about all I saw and experienced on my trip — the whole Bulletin would not be big enough for that. I am flying back to Paris. The plane was 45 minutes late from Prague., nobody explains why. And here is France, we are getting out of the plane, the sun is bright and warm,the customs officers and the personnel seem friendlier and happier. Soon I catch the mood myself. Why not? This was to be my first Slet in America. I have read about the previous ones while still back in the old country, but I did not really know what to expect. How would this "Slet" compare with the magnitude, organization and enthusiasm of the "Slets" at home? The Xth Congress convened on Monday,June 21. I took part as a guest only, while our delegates were active into the night working on various committees, where most of the work was done. The Congress sessions were directed by br. A. Suster from Cleveland,Ohio. I was much impressed by the way he codtrolled the large gathering and the complex agenda.He kept the proceedings to the point,and quickly and tactfully intervened before the debate could bog down into a fruitless and long-winded oratory. A major accomplishment of the Congrass was the transfer of leadership into the hands of the newer generation. This was done smoothly,withour bitterness on the part of the retiring functionaries, and in a dignified manner. A large banquet attended by the delegate of President Johnson and by the delegates from other Slavonic Sokol organizations concluded this phase of the activities. * In the meantime, in the various gymnasiums in Berwyn, Sokols from all over the country competed iú gymnastics.All classes took part: men, women, junior boys and junior girls. The Omaha unit sent a contingent of 5 boys who were definitely outstanding. I am sure we'll hear a lot about them in the future. Our own district was successfully represented by sister Vlasta Vanda from L.A. , who placed 4th in the championship. [pageend10]11 Sunday, June 28*., was the main "Slet" day. It seems that the names California and Pacific have some special charm or significance for the people back east. The parade wound up in the Norton High School stadium. Flags were hoisted, the choir of CSA sang the anthems, and senator Hruška gave a welcoming speech. The stadium was filled with both spectators and gymnasts. The program itself went off very well, which was perhaps a little surprising after the morning rehearsal, when I was somewhat apprehensive as to the outcome. The number of gymnasts who took part,will probably be announced in time. In my own estimate, some 450 Junior Girls and 270 Junior Boys took part in a joint drill. So was the so-called Victory Dance, where the winners of various gymnastic competitions were to be introduced and rewarded publicly for the years of hard work, training and preparations. The manner in which thiář was done lacked order, dignity and good taste. The whole evening reminded me of a Beetle concert, complete with screaming teen-agers. Those two events were definitely poorly organized, which was inexcusable, considering the occasion and the lengths of preparation. What are the conclusions I can draw from all I have seen? I'd say, that the positive impressions of the whole far outweighed the few negative ones. I found that we are all working here in America under much less favourable conditions than we used to enjoy at home. The facilities of the AOS are very meager indeed. On the other hand, the enthusiasm of the leaders is as strong as we remember from the past. The involvement of the young people was the most heartening experience of all; you could see in their faces the eagerness, the tension and the disappointments, if a particularly difficult feat diun't <piite turn out.. These youngsters and their enthusiasm were well comparable with everything I have ever seen. They are guarantee that our movement will prosper. Here in the West,we may think that Sokol does not have the brightest future. This is a sad result of our virtual isolation. We are not in contact with the lively activities going on in the Mid-west [pageend11]12 and. the East. We are too far from that, and live too far from each other. Out here, lack of funds makes it almost impossible to send our instructors for training back East,or to invite their instructors over here. The result is general apathy, and gradual strophy. I wish, more of our youngsters could have seen the "Slet". Perhaps they would have caught some of the enthusiasm and dedication. We need young people in our gym, many more than the handful which now attends. We need a*team of gymnasts for the next "Slet". There is no reason why we could not have one the material is on hand, particularly in the Junior Girls and the Women sections. Our aim should be to get a team of competitors ready for the coming "Mid- Slet" in Dallas. It can be done. Let us all do our best to make it a reality! Our Cultural Corner: Spotlight On The Band When the history of American music in the Sixties is written, the name of our very own Sokol Orchestra will undoubtedly be mentioned prominently, together with the "Sauerkraut Band" and the "McDuffy's Washboard Trio". In order to give this little paper some class, we have decided today to acquaint you with few of the musicians who made the orchestra what it is.You may have often wondered who they were, those glamorous men up on the stage, what manner of hearts beat under their white tuxedoes, what kind of souls hide under their glassy stares. [pageend12]I 3 No need to wonder any longer ! .' In an unprecedented scoop, our fearless reporter managed to put together their life histories and lay bare the secret motives which drove them relentlessly past all obstacles and shaped their artistic growth. All this is a pretty potent stuff, by the way, and you may be well advised to keep this article from your grandmother and children. Meet the head of the ensemble:this fiery-looking man was born to poor but honest parents at a tender age. It soon became apparent that this was an extraordinary child—, who might leave a deep mark on the musical world. This expectation came true; in fact, the mark is so deep it may never mend. Young Charles, ...for that was his name., learned violin under Prof.Pik ( who tought zoology and lived in the upstairs flat ), 'cello under the spreading chestnut tree,and sundry other musical instruments under the influence of alcohol. Plays almost anything (best of all blackjack), but violin remained his favourite instrument. As a brave member of the gendarmerie, he often took it with him on his long and lonely patrols and filled the moonlit nights with his forceful music, thereby striking terror in the souls of chicken thieves and becoming something of a local legend. Nowadays.young Charles matured and reached his deserved niche. He leads the band with a firm and yet kind hand. Sometimes, if the mood is right and the moon full, he will descend from the podium, stroll amongst the guests and play his soul-rendering music into their ears,thus often reducing them to tears or violent behaviour. Charles' favourite composer is Dvorak,his favourite song the "beer Barrel March". "Meet a Bohemian and you will have met a musician"... goes an old saying. "Vladimir", the next member of the band on whom we shall concentrate, is a living proof of this adage, for he has indeed once met a Bohemian who was a musician. (Unfortunately, the poor man drank like a fish, and thus Vladimir, being a man of sober habits and given to drinking pure spring water, could not cultivate his acquaintance and thus give us further particulars. ) [pageend13]14 By the way, his real name is not Vladimir; that is but a pseudonym chosen in honor of Vladimir Ilyich Tschaikovsky. Like that famous Russian violinist, Vladimir also studied violin, but his deep love of nature and hiking soon led him outdoors, where his violin proved impractical..: the bow was always catching in the bushes and proved too dangerous while seducing the girls by the campfire,poking them in the eye and various other spots during the more tender passages. To alleviate the danger, Vladimir soon exchanged his beloved violin for a guitar,and as a result of that ( and his deep- set eyes,dashingly handsome features ahd seductive baritone) he has achieved much success in his chosen field. Vladimir does not play the guitar in the band now ----- it would be much too dangerous for the public morals — but even on the new instrument his performances exude deep yearning,passionate lyricism and sensuous emotionalism which makes girls swoon and mature women clutch their hearts. His favourite composer: Vladimir Tschaikovsky, his favourite song: " Come Maiden into my cabin, if only for a little while We have so far described two members of the band who actually stood at the cradle of it. They have given the ensemble a certain solid foundation,a sound artistic basis on which it could develop and mature. From time to time, however, a newcomer appears who brings with him a breath of fresh air:- new concepts and new artistic aims.— One of such newcomers is this dashing man who joined the band only recently. It hasn't been the same since. Many things have changed since he came. The booze ration which is given to the orchestra to supply the necessary inspiration, had to be trebled ---- to give just one example. To give another, for years the band used to play on the podium.If you'll come to our next dance, however, you will notice that it has moved down on the floor, for a simple reason: our newcomer kept falling off,and there were growing fears of his safety. While a certain amount of anguish and agony seems to be an inherent Lot of every great artist, it was felt that steps should be taken to prevent a permanent injury.(Apart from that it was hard on the chairs, and the repair bills for panel beating jobs on his trombone were something fierce). [pageend14]15 Our Zdenek, named after a famous Czech musician who hed to be locked up in a tower, is a product of Bohemian church music.. He sang in choirs, studied polyphony and learned to play a sweet horn, and sweet- and-sour trombone. As we mentioned, he gives a spirited performance and adds the needed "zing" to the evening.You may enjoy his artistry,but do come early ! If you miss him, look in the barjyou will find him there, lamenting over his lost youth, or singing bawdy songs in a pleasant sweet soprano. His favourite compositions are "Baches Fugue in D Minor"and "Our rooster is chasing the speckled hen again", (lyrics may be ontained upon request in plain unmarked en- velope ). Space does not allow us to cover any more members of the orchestra. The others will be dealt with in the future,providing there is any for us.Should the band fail to make appearance at the next dance, the readers will know the reason:the musicians will be sitting at home, writing their real life histories. A BIT OF SAN FRANCISCO HISTORY Not many people know the origin of the beautiful Rose Garden that is now enhancing the Golden Gate Park. About three years ago, this piece of ground was used by our athletic members for a volleyball field. Each Monday, the park employees had a hard time repairing the holes, left there by the terrific spiking of our players. It is not known which bright member of the Park Commission has come up with the idea of planting a few roses in some of the holes, instead of filling the mess up each week. This experiment proved to be astonishingly successful! The ground was soaked by sweat, lard drippings, bitter tears, and even blood from a few broken noses, which in retrospect seem to be the best ingredients for an outstanding fertilizer. And so it happened, that one Saturday, when the team turned out for its practice, it found a very beautiful flourishing Rose Garden where -the practice field used to be.
