How did the Slovaks get along with their neighbors: the Austrians and Czechs in the west and northwest, the Poles in the north, the Ukrainians in the east, and the Magyars in the south? Vienna, the capital of Austria, and Bratislava, the capital of Slovakia, are' just an hour's ride by auto from each other. After 1918, when the T. G. Masaryk and Dr. Edward Benes Czechs took over the reigns of the newly formed Czccho-SIovak State, the borders between Austria and Slovakia were closed: one needed a visa to cross the-border. This was a good neighborly relationship that was to the advantage of the Slovaks living in Vienna, or in Austria proper. There were some 80.000 Slovaks settled in Austria. Politically, the Slovaks lived their own lives in Vienna. Recognized political and church leaders visited them regularly. Monsignor Andrew Hlinka, founder of the Slovaks People's Party and champion of Slovak freedom and independence, had cordial contacts not only with Slovak leaders in Austria, but with influential Austrians as well.
