This proverb should be applied also to American Presidency — particularly vow, during the investigation of the Watergate affair, when anybody who just feels like it takes the liberty of slinging mud and tossing stone at the President and other h!gh officials whose involvement in that sad scandal has not been even proven. This is the heyday for the press and for the publicity seekers, from the opinionated, much too outspoken Mrs. Mitchell to the mealy- mouthed. self-made prophet Daniel Ellsberg. The recurrent theme of their song is either resignation of the President, or his impeachment. Few, if any, public figures, however, came forward to warn the nation that such unwise, impu’sive actions could precipitate a national catastrophe. We have already w tnessed the perilous effect of the Watergate affair on the international money market, and this is hurting us all. One way to prevent a disaster is to air the whole mess. The Senate hearings will eventually clarify many why’s and wherefore’s, but they will drag on forever and the interest in them will gradually diminish. They would be more, useful, if instead of the small fry. the Senate com mittce would call to the witness-stand first of all the centred figures-Messrs. Ehrlchman — Haldcrman and Dean, and if necessary, to request a detailed explanation or account from the President. This would settle. the issue one way or another, without imperiling our national interests in both the internal and foreign affairs. President Nixon has already issued a flat denial of any complicity and foreknowledge of the affa'r, but he should have done it sooner, if for no other reason. than to spare himself so many suspicions and insults. He probably underestimated the ferocity of his enemies. Their number may not be so great, but they are organized and ready to snipe at him and his Administration. It was not realistic to try to ignore them. The only way is to face them — eyeball to eyeball. After the dust settles — there may be even some positive gains from this unsavory experience. First of all, the world sees a proof that no citizen, not even the one in the highest office, is exempt from the law in the U. S. Also, freedom of the press has been confirmed again and again in this case, even though often enough the press has not proved itself to be able or willing to discern the golden middle road of objectivity and sensibility and has succumbed to scandalmongering. Another proof of free expression in the U. S. is the fact that a government agency, the Voice of America, - broadcasts abroad factual accounts of the duly Senate hearings and of the results of investigation. This will certainly provide some food for thought to other nations, particularly those under totalitarian rule. Finally, a financial reform of the election campaigns woidd be very sanitary for the whole system. However, the power of the Presidency must not be reduced. We need a strong President, now and in the future, not a mere figurehead. The ‘‘chief commander” must focus his attention — day and night — without recesses and vacations — on the most important tasks and he must be given sufficient room for such an operation. It is human to err. Every sing'e president In Ame- rcan history lias made some error of judgment or action. In some instances such errors cost hundreds of human lives. Fortunately, the Watergate affair eľd not result in such a tragedy.
