The Hodza Program Hodza puts himself forward as a spokesman for a federation of the states of Centrál and Eastern Europe based on “agrarian democracy.” The region between Germany and Russia, he argues, with its great indus- trial as well as agricultural resourcesand its population of one hundred million, can be made into a “fifth Great Power,” able to withstand pressure alike from East or West. The eight nations proposed for the federation - as Hodza makes clear in his Federation in Centrai Europe, published in London early in 1942 and to be published here in a revised edition - are Poland, Czechoslovakia, Austria, Hungary, Rumania, Yugo-- slavia, Bulgaria, and Greece. Under the pian any of these countries may retain monarchial institutions. As an agrarian democrat, Hodza claims affiliations with Maniu of Rumania, Machek of Croatiaand Stanislaw Kot of Poland. Eastern Europe, he asserts, is saturated with the peasant outlook; that alone presents the common economie philosophy and common social milieu which can serve as the basis for federation. By agrarian democracy, Hodza means specifically a poi- itical and economie systém primarily expressing the will of the smáli peasant landholders, equally opposed to private monopoly and to socialist collectivism. But, though thè social structure of the federation would be broadly based on the independent land- owner, Hodza accepts thè need in a strong modern state for industriai resources; these could be centered in such regions as Bohemia and Moravia. The federation would be headed by a federai pres- ident, elected by a conference of national prime minis- ters to serve for a year and empowered to appoint the other members of the government. Thè executive branch would control foreign affairs, agricultural mar- keting and other economie operations, especially for- eign trade, currency and tariffs and constitutional questions. The prospects of this federation, according to Hodza, are fairly encouraging. Bogolyub Yeftich, Yugoslav Ambassador to Great Britain, has told him that the Serbs are attracted to the idea of a large Centrai European federation because of the alleged fact that future cooperation with the Croations will be extremely difficult if not impossible. Hodza and the Slovak-Americans Hodza looks for the support of this program in large part to the Slovak-Americans. The example of the last war showed thè power of Američan foreign communi- ties in shaping the destiny of the homeland, and the Slovaks in America bear a higher numerical proportion to the Slovaks in Europe than Czech-Americans do to their cousins back home. About one quarter of all the Slovaks in the world, it is estimated, are in the United states, and their extraordinarily direct response to the politicai problems of the old countries is equalled only by the Polish-Americans and the Yugoslav-Americans. The Slovák League is the oldest and best known Slovak-American organization. Under the presidency of Joseph Husek, and with a predominantly Catholic membership, it went overboard after 1938 for Hitler’s quisling government in Slovakiaand urged recognition upon the State Department.
