On his recent visit to China the Secretary of State Henry Kissinger conformed with the wishes of the Chinese Communist government and formally recognized one China, of which Tailcan is a part. Undoubtedly, in view of the present situation, this was expedient diplomatic move to balance the relations with the two antagonistic Communist superpower. The idea of one China, however, resembles very much the old cry: “Ein Reich, ein Volk, en Fuhrer!” — One country, one nation, one leader — heard from the Nazis in their heyday. So far, the Chinese seem to be satisfied with the formal recognition, but they, too, have already proclaimed one leader, the infallible Chai r m a n Mao whose Marxist commandments apply to any kind of situation, from planting cucumbers to international politics. With the imagined or real danger of Soviet aggression, the Chinese are not likely to launch an attack against Taiwan at the present time, but who can tell what will happen in f vc. ten or twenty years? Just like the Nazis were hatching their ideology of one country, one nation and one leader for several years before their armed exploits, the Chinese Communists will use the same method for their propaganda until the day when an invasion is feasible. After the. war it was fashionable to create two versions of the same country: one Communist, one democratic or at least nonCommunist. There are two Germanys, two Koreas, two Vietnams, but there is supposed to be one Ch na only and Taiwan must become its integral part. Why? Taiwan has developed a- part from the Mainland, first (luring the long Japanese occupation, then under Chiang Kai-shek. The Taiwanese adopted the Japanese drive for progress and Chiang Kai-shek's regime also fostered a rapid industrialization of the country. Taiwan became the second most prosperous and progressive country in Asia. This was accompl sh- cd with ample assistance from the U.S., but no one can deny the Taiwanese people their perseverance and industry. Chiang Kai-shek’s government is not a demokratie one, nor do the Taiwanese have adequate representation in executive offices. The Mainlanders are still holding the power firmly in their hands, but in these past years a quiet reform is under way and improvements are observed almost in every direction. The Taiwanese want a reform, but not Communism in any form. The expulsion from the United Nations was a blow for them, however, so far it has not caused any economic reverses for the country, technological progress has not suffered and the economy is advancing steadily. The Mainlanders who came as Chiang Kai-shek’s fellow fighters are getting old and their children are now totally integrated in the Taiwanese life. It is, therefore, quite unrealistic for Chiang Kai-shek to st ek to his c’aims to the Mainland and visions of an overthrow of the Communist dictatorship. It would be considerably more pragmatic to let the people of Taiwan decide in a democratic way what future they want. Our bet is that they 'would prefer Taiwan as an indepen- ent country. With 13 milion people, it is within the range of medium-sized states and should be considered as a separate entity and not a part of China. Nobody really believes that the Taiwanese w o u I d launch an armed attack against China in order to reinstate Chiang Kai-shek. It could, however, happen the other way around and in view of our long support of Taiwan, we must prevent the Communists from seizing that island by force. We do not suspect the Nixon administration of any shady cleats with the Communists. We do not think that the American government would throw Taiwan to the wolves and let it be absorbed by the Communist power. That would be the same crime as the dismembering of Czechoslovakia after Munich 1938.
