[pageend3]Page 4 Věstník Sokols San Francisco July I970 Sue Samuelson: The Czech Immigration to the United States In 1633, Augustine Herman of Prague became the first Czech to immigrate to America. Soon he was followed by Frederick Philipse, one of whose descendants was John Jay, the first Chief Justice of the United States and hundreds more followed. So many, that by the second half of the seventeenth century, Czech was one of the eighteen different languages spoken in New York. The Czechs' reasons for coming to the U.S. were mainly political. The Battle of White Mountain in 1620 had signaled the beginning of three hundred years of subservience to the Austro-Hungarian empire. Religious toleration was no longer guaranteed to many of the Moravians who were followers of Jan Hus' teachings. These people immigrated in the 1730's and 1740's, first going to Georgia, then to North Carolina and Pennsylvania. During the years of subjugation, the Czechs became tenant farmers or serfs on their own land. The Austrian nobility commandeered the finest fruits of the harvest but they were absentee landlords, leaving the day- to-day affairs of managing the estates to Czech overseers. When serfdom was abolished in 1848 and the right to immigrate at will was instituted in 1848, many Czechs decided to go the United States as well as to other parts of Europe. For many years their number was very small that it did not even warrant a designation in the census and immigration records. Instead, they . were included under the Austro-Hungarian heading. Even today, there is confusion as to what a Czech or Czechoslovakian is. For the purposes of this paper, Czech will refer to those people from Bohemia and Moravia who speak the Czech language.While recent immigrants refer to themselves as Czechoslovakians, they claim this identification would never be made within the country: one is a Czech or a Slovak. The Slovaks certainly have many similarities to the Czechs but evidently the two groups feel there are also enough differences to warrant a separation. Read by Bro.
