Hwy 70 through central Kansas, we could soon see the huge sign on the sidejof the Hwy informing us that "Wilson is the Czech capitol of Kansas" and an arrow pointing toward a little town about two miles off the road. In a short time, we were on the main street of Wilson and having plenty of time before the gathering started, we went for little sight-seeing.Right there on the main street we could see a rather large two-story building made of limestone with the Czech sign"NORODNI SIN*(National Hall) above the main door.Further, our attention was drawn to the building next door, especially to the windows facing the street. On the glass of one window was a painting of Sokol gymnasts performing some pyramids, while on the other window was a painting of young girls in the national costumes. Above the main door again was the Czech sign "VITAME VAS" (Welkome), We decided to stop the first person walking near us and get some more information about the history of these buildings and maybe of Czech(Bohemian) settlers.We met a middle aged man and after introducing ourselves and revealing our interest, he looked rather surprised. "I am Frank Sevcik" he said "and my grandfather and grandmother always spoke Bohemian at home, but I remember only a few words',' We learned that this building was indeed the Sokol Hall many years ago and that they call it now "Opera" in Wilson.In the basement is the Bohemian Museum, but it is locked and the key is with Jim Spurny, Right away he offered to get the key as Spurný*s house is only a block away.I went with him to see Jim Spurny, but unfortunately nobody was at home and with that went also the visit of museum.Our time was running out and so we thanked Mr.Sevcik for information and finally asked him for the direction to the church hall,where Jilka’s reunion would be held. When the festivities, which we enjoyed very much, were over we still had enough time to visit the Wilson cemetery and find the graves of grandparents of my wife and her other relatives buried there. While searching through cemetery and reading all the names on the tombstones we were surprised at how many Czech settlers are resting there in peace. Probably about 75% of all names were of Czech origin and amongst them were Frank Jilka, born 1823 -died 1913 and his wife Antonie born 1857 in KLasterec and died 1905.We wondered why so many Czech(Bohemian) settlers came to central. Kansas and started the new life just here around that little town called Wilson? Who was inviting, directing and helping all Bohemian emigrants, as they were called, to this part of a wide open country? We did not have the answer to this question right there, but we learned that severalfweeks later. Following the reunion we were invited to stay a couple more days with the family . of my wifeys cousin Larry Jilka in Salina,Kansas. During the long discussions I expressed my interest about the history of Czech settlers in this part of Kansas.Our host told us that in the local library is the book "Kansas State Historical Society"and there is also a whole chapter about "Bohemians in Central Kansas". He promised to borrow this book and send me the copy of this chapter. Indeed in two or three weeks I received several copies of pages explaining the history of Bohemian settlersjin Kansas, The complete chapter for the Kansas Historical Society was written by Francis J. Swehla who settled here first,after a long journey and several misfortunes in other parts of his new country. He was born in 1845 in Albrechtice - southern Bohemia -and in 1854 his family with a number of others moved to USA.He made the first homestead entry of government land in the Salina land office by a Bohemian-American on May 16, 1874. When he looked around he found a lot of free land available from Uncle Sam and he decided to locate a Rohemlan(Czech) settlement in and around Wilson. He kept his [pageend4]June-July 1985 Věstník Sokola San Francisco Page 5 pen going, publishing reports about the available land in Bohemian-American papers until he drew the attention of all the reading public. Soon the letters and visitors were pouring in wanting the answers about the land. Some of them stayed here already. Sood all the land around Wilson was taken by the new settlers and Mr.Swehla had to look in the neighboring counties for the available land as more and more countrymen were coming,Also, the first families from Moravia came to the settlement in I878 from the village of Pisarova near Olomouc. They were Josef Macek,Fr.Kroboth,Jan Steiner and Frank Jilka - the grandfather of my wife Vlasta.But that was all Mr.Swehla wanted and now must spent a.lot of time even neglecting his own farm in order to help his countrymen. Societies in the colony were organized from the first.Most of the new owners of the land didn't know much about farming as they were all kinds of different tradesmen and had to be educated about the basics of farming.So in the fall of 1875 the first local association was formed and they called it "BLAHOBYT"(The wealth).In the following years many new societies were formed by the incoming numbers of new emigrants. Nearly all the settlers joined the Farmers union and had in town, besides the grain elevator and coal shed,their own creamery.In I896 they built a "Narodni Sin"(National Hall)which they made their social center. In 1892 came Vincent Hubalek, a young Czech fresh from the native land, full of enthusiasm and energy, and organized a club of SOKOL upon his arrival here,In a short time they were able to give public exhibitions of their skill and strength at picnics held in the groves on the banks of the Smoky Hill river.This was the second Sokol unit organized in Kansas, the first Sokol club started at the Bohemian Hall located in Noble township, Ellsworth county, and Vincent Hubalek was the first training master for both units.The Wilson Sokol "Karel Jonas" undertook the building of the hall,They secured donations from both countrymen and merchants of Wilson - countrymen generally donating work, hauling the building rock from the quarries etc.The merchants, besides subscribing money, donated articles of merchandise for the "Bazaar" conducted by the Sokols.Nevertheless a great indebtedness remained on the hall after all efforts to raise money were exhausted.The approximate cost of the building was $ 15.000.00. This being exclusively a farming community, everything depends on the farmer and his products and they don’t get a bumper crop every year.When the crops are short then all other enterprises, except banks and loan agents, have a shrinking profit.So it came to pass that Sokol "Karel Jonas" in Wilson was in imminent danger of a mortgage foreclosure and the building passing into private hands. To prevent such a disgraceful event lodge Vesmir No.115 of" CSA came to the rescue by becoming its joint owner.Later on the joint societies admitted a third society to become a joint owner with them, lodge "Antonin Dvorak" of Z.C.B,J.(Zapadni Cesko-Bratrska Jednota).The three societies joining saved the building and the mortgages have been wiped out. In 1897 Wilson Sokol was admitted as the forty-ninth organized unit to the Sokol Union of the United States but it had been chartered by the state of Kansas Sept,7,1895* Well, in the conclusion this is the story of events just about one century ago. Today in Wilson only the buildings are still there and lot of Czech names remind us of the origin of ancestors,but the language disappeared.Sokol become inactive many years ago and we never learned who is now the owner of Wilson Sokol Hall.Maybe next time we will have wnough time to find out and even we may be lucky to find the painter of the nice colored pictures of Sokol gymnsts and the girls in national costumes on the windows of "NARODNI SÍN" in Wilson, Kansas.
