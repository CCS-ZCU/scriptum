Roku 1968 budeme všichni oslavovat — obzvláště my ve svobodném světě — padesáté výročí založení republiky Československé. Československá národní rada americká chce oslavit padesáté výročí významným způsobem a na sjezdu v květnu se rozhodla vydat všekrajanskou kroniku. Chceme totiž dokázat Američanům, čím přispěli čeští a slovenští přistěhovalci: svým vzácným smyslem pro povinnost, svou pracovitostí, poctivostí, houževnatostí. Přivezli ssebou touhu po vzdělání, krásné dary staré kultury, hudbu, sokolskou myšlenku, pokrokovost, sociální smýšlení. — To všechno vychází na jevo při čtení životopisů ať již pionýrů na ztracených domovinách nebo v záznamech národních organisací. Kniha bude psána anglicky a bude se nazývat “Panorama of Three Hundred Years of the Czechs and Slovaks in America”. Chceme, aby i třetí a čtvrtá generace se dočetly o začátcích svých předků, o jejich cílevědomé práci a ideálech, s kterými sem přijeli ze staré vlasti a které zde uskutečňovali. Výlohy historického díla o krajanech budou kryty poplatky jednotlivců, dle rozměru jejich životopisu (tak jako se předplácí oznámka v časopise). Budou to malé poplatky za stručný životopis až po celostránková pojednání. Zamyslíte-li se nad tímto úkolem, budete s námi souhlasit, že máme před sebou ohromnou práci. Bude to ale dílo radostné. Vložme do toho nadšení a úctu k vykonané práci svých předchůdců. Oslavíme tak nejen krajanskou Ameriku, ale vneseme i jasné světlo do významné kapitoly československých dějin. Z hlediska čistě amerického naši krajané svojí přičinlivostí, pracovitostí a morálními hodnotami přispěli k vybudování nové vlasti společně s ostatními národy. Z hlediska československého naši krajané v Americe se stali mluvčími lidu doma při mnoha historických a důležitých událostech. Za dob starého Rakousko- Uherska naši krajané vysvětlovali Američanům, že proces národního uvědomění je nezadržitelný a že Češi a Slováci mají právo sebeurčení. Když přišla I Světová válka, naši krajané pomáhali Masarykovi vyvinout velikou propagandu ve prospěch založení Československa. Tak jako dnes, ani tenkráte americké veřejné mínění nebylo jednotné, co se týče politického uspořádání Střední Evropy. Když přišla II Světová Válka a Československo padlo do jha nazis- mu, krajané v Americe se znovu postavili za osvobození lidu ve staré vlasti.. Snahy našich krajanských organi- sací nebyly marné, a přispěly k vítězství. Dnes, kdy se náš lid doma znovu nalézá v otroctví, tentokráte komunismu, jsme jako krajané znovu konfrontováni novou realitou. Procházíme dobou nových zkoušek, nových pokušení, snad víc než v minulosti. Po komunistickém převratu v Československu mnozí naši krajané nevěděli, zdali si snad lid doma tento režim nepřál... Dnes jsme však došli k nezvratnému přesvědčení, že komunistický režim není z vůle lidu, a že proto musíme našemu národu znovu pomoci. Tím, že bojujeme proti komunismu, se nijak jako američtí občané nestavíme proti zájmům A- meriky... naopak, bojujeme proti společnému nepříteli I Musíme si však dnes položit otázku, do jaké míry se podílí každý z nás v této národní povinnosti, do jaké míry pomáhá v tomto boji za demokracii? Mnohý z nás je v pokušení zapomenout co se ve stare' vlasti děje.... Svým mlčením se stáváme spoluvinnými na vývoji, který ohrožuje americkou svobodu a prodlužuje utrpení lidu v Československu. When the lovely melodies of the opera died down under the rafters of the old Sokol Hall, we all went home with a warm feeling in our hearts. As the libretto said, a good cause has carried the day.- All cannot be wrong with today's youth, if they are willing to spend so much time and put in so much hard work for the sake of theatre and art.... Their only reward was the enthusiasm of their audience, and we can only hope, that the thrilL they obviously felt during the applause was some small compensation. Those beautiful arias of the opera took me right back into my youth. No doubt, the "Bride" was the best loved opera of all. Every student had to see it at least once;whole schools would arrange excursions to Prague just to see the performance at the " National ". I remember making the trip, when I was the freshman at the " gymnasium " in Kralupy.- We had been saving our pennies diligently all year, to be able to make the trip. We saw the afternoon performance, and I had a little adventure coming back.............As I remember, our train was late coming from Prague, and I missed my local train connection.— It was late at night by then.... Feeling rather too cocky for my own good,Irefused several offers of my fellow students, who wanted to put me up for the night.---- I decided to walk home instead.... a distance of some 7 miles. Unfortunately I did not take into consideration the eery stillness of the country lanes, the dark bushes which stood so menacingly by the roadside, and the two graveyards I had to pass on the way. I must have broken the Olympic distance running record that night... What was worse, I arrived home so hot and out of breath I did not dare to walk inside the house in that condition. So I sat under the big linden tree in the yard to rest.That's where my father found me asleep in the morning, cold and covered with dew. I could not talk myself out of that situation, and so my adventure from the previous night came out,much to the amusement of my sisters and friends. Bedrich Smetana and his music are closer to me than to perhaps most of you. The reason for it is simple: he lived for a number of years, and composed at least parts of his best known works, in our village Obristvi- Semilkovice, which used to be called Lamberk in the old days. Why this particular village ? [pageend20]19 Simple. The land around used to belong to Count Trautmansdorf __ their manor was right in the village. Naturally, the holdings had to be managed by someone............... the nobility used to come but briefly during summer, to hunt and race horses.- The manager's name was Fernandi, and- if you remember your history well, you will recollect that Smetana second wife's name was Fernandi----- Bettina Fernandi, to be exact. They were married right in the village, in the old church of St. John The Baptist, where I myself was christened many years later. Many a time the kind old priest showed me proudly the old register with the signatures of Bedrich Smetana and Bettina. Our village was full of old people who remembered the couple well. I listened to their stories almost constantly. How this came about is another story altogether.... You see, my father widowed rather early— I was only eleven when mother died. In a place such as ours people still cared about each other.-So, to help to ease our loneliness, few of the neighbors started dropping over in the evenings. This soon developed into regular visits practically all year around.- I can still see inny mind all the old-timers, each of them a character in his own right, who comprised the little group... There was old Kratochvil, retired master of the upholstering and saddle-making trade. In his youth he was employed at the houses of the nobility, upholstering their carriages or making saddles and harnesses for their horses. There was Grandpa Landa., a former overseer for the Trautmansdorfs. The next member of the group was " Mister Kocian ", who used to be an itinerant millhand in his youth; that was before the rivers of my country were regulated------------ a step which on one hand prevented the yearly floods, but on the other did away with all the old picturesque water-wheel mills - and all the charm and lore that went with them. "Mister Kocian's" last job happened to be with the old master-miller Polivka in our village, on one of the last remaining mills in the country.-- When the mill closed down, Kocian decided to call it a day, and stay right where he was. He bought himself a piece of land and a cow, and retired to a life of memories and story telling-------- and some stories they were, you should have heard them. The last member of the group was old Salansky, a coachman for Mr. Fernandi in his young years.. He in particular remembered Smetana and Bettina vividly, and often spoke about them. To go back to the gatherings I mentioned a while ago, — they were an almost daily occurrance.In summertime the group would sit under the big linden tree in the yard. If you read Czech, perhaps you know the book " In the Shade of a Linden Tree " ? That's exactly how it used to be; even the characters of the book resemble the people I just described. Perhaps the resemblance isn't so coincidental after all.......... The author of the book also lived in our village for eight years. In wintertime they would all gather in the kitchen,which was the only room in the house we could heat up.......... Those winter sessions were particularly hard on me. I was,as you remember, a student in those days, and as such supposed to study hard [pageend21]20 the ways of the old Romans and Greeks, the conquests of Alexander the Great and the adventures of Ceasar in Easter Galli- cia.- It was hard, damned hard.- There I would be sitting in one corner with my lamp and books, both fingers in my ears, trying to concentrate on my lessons, while in the other corner the old codgers would be sprawling at ease,swapping yarns and trying to outdo each other with a more interesting story. I don't mind admitting,now that my own sons are out of school, that I would often pull out one finger, just to find out what the talk was about.- And when the subject was interesting, as it quite often was,soon I'd be sitting there listening intently, Ceasar and Alexander the Great completely forgotten. One of the stories I remember quite well, was the one Salan- sky used to tell. He was the coachman, you remember. It seems that after his marriage to Bettina, Smetana would be a frequent guest at the Fernandi's home.- He could be seen often, sitting in the summer house in the garden, staring out at the lovely countryside in front of him-------- the fertile fields of Central Bohemia, with the mountain "Rip" sitting like a huge bell in the midst of them, or at the Melnik castle and vineyards which dominated the view to the right... Often he would snap out suddenly from his reverie, reach for a pen and paper and start composing.... And I tell you, the countrywide- with her flavor, earthiness and beauty, can be almost felt in his music... At least I can visualize it quite clearly listening to " My Country "- perhaps because I knew the same country so well... But back to the story... It happened one day that Smetana was due to arrive from Prague on the last afternoon train... The honor of bringing him home from the station fell on Salansky, the coachman. — Since this was a big occasion, he went to a good deal of trouble: brushed the horses,polished up the harness and all the brass on the landau ( which, as you know, is a kind of carriage,completely enclosed and heavily upholstered inside ). Everything was spick and span, as he waited for the train. Finally the train pulled in, Salansky welcomed the composer, put him in the landau, wrapped a blanket around him and off they went at an easy pace... It was getting dark, but the horses knew their way well along the country lanes.- This was late in fall, turning nippy, and a brisk wind was blowing from the fields. Salansky was sitting up in front, huddled in his coat, almost dozing off.... Suddenly, something seemed to dart out from the bushes by the roadside and run with a strange noise directly in path of the horses! They reared back,and before Salansky knew what was happening ---- took off at full gallop across the fields, landau and all. Salansky was holding on for dear life.- Luckily, they did not go too farjthe landau jumped a shallow brook and came to rest with the rear wheel against a plum tree which grew there. The wheel snapped off like a match, the axle burried itself into the’soft earth and brought the horses to a halt in a hurry—. [pageend22]21 There they stood, shaking all over, breathing hard. Salansky was shaken up and bruised, but he did not care about that.... his only thought was for the well-being of his passenger .SUU holding the reins in his hand he hurried to the door____ but & r;tana was already stepping out, quite unhurt and seemingly a'(Used by the incident. " Are you hurt, Maestro ?", enquired Salansky anxiously. " Of course not---- ", replied Smetana calmly. "How could I be, with all that upholstery around me? I hardly felt the bumps!" They stood and inspected the damage.... The horses were still jumpy, and the landau was- of course- useless. " What shall we do?", asked the helpless Salansky. " I’ll tell you what", said Smetana. " It's not too far to go and the walk will do me good. I'll go ahead,you stay with the horses, and I'll send some help along." As you know, we have lashed out rather bitterly in the past issue at what we thought was one of our serious shortcomings....the all pervading traditionalism. To our limited knowledge, our Bulletin thus became the first and only Sokol publication in the U.S.A, to openly criticize any aspect of the Sokol movement. We said "limited knowledge", because we are comparative newcomers in this field. However, we are now getting Sokol papers from all over the country. We have of late become avid readers of the A— merican Sokol (which is in our opinion a much underrated periodical, deserving to rank right behind the Sunday Punch and the Hospodář). We have also read all recent publications of the A.S.O., so that you may safely conclude that we are at least trying — to catch up fast. In all this deluge of printed words, 99.9% of the contents, were laudatory, contended, cheerful and inspirational, sometimes pathetically so. Critique was non-existent. Few mild, inconsequential and almost apologetic exhortations were all we could find ... Hardly a spark of insight anywhere .. just a selfsatisfied bosh. To judge solely from our press, everything about our organization is then peachy, noble and rosy. The movement prospers, the old members rule it wisely, the younger men bend their arms gladly to tasks on hand, while the laughter of their morally upright and Sokol-educated children echoes in our halls. True, there seem to be a few minor things that need correction ......... for example, for an organization which is supposed to be gymnastic, we certainly could use few more gymnasts.,, few more members would also come in handy, it seems. But never mind that, we are told, all that will come if we just try a little harder, for just a while longer,sacrifice ourselves just a bit more. Another notable fact emerges from that reading; every achievement, be it ever so minor and inconsequential, is hailed as a major triumph. Unrestrained praises are being scattered around with reckless abandon, until an outside observer, should he be for some inexplicable reason tempted to take interest in our doings,would have to feel like drowning in this sea of self-adulation. A classical example was the publicity attending the appearance of the Sokol Stamp some two years ago. Never mind that the stamp was tasteless, in fact incomprehensible, if not actually mis-leading, to most Americans (this did not matter, because the stamp was bought out by Sokols anyway, and the general public hardly saw it at all) Never mind the fact that the issuance itself had about as much significance as an election promise of some junior senator to his constituents in Upper Hicktown, Nebraska.Never mind the fact that just about every other section of our community, from "Barbershop Quartets", through "The Bund of the Funeral Directors",down to the "Warbling Birds Watchers Society" have been similarly " honored " in the past. Judging from our triumphant cries, which haven't as yet died down completely, you would think it was a historical victory of the first magnitude; comparable to the defeat of Napoleon [pageend43]41 at Waterloo, Our magnificent educational aims have evidently been -cro\med by this shattering achievement! To s: 3 that the above self-satisfaction is a sign of approaching decline, would be a gross understatement. Any organization which is still alive, which hasn't given up on itself,will still retain its competitivenes. It will strive to assess itself objectively, compare itself against others in its own sphere of activities; it will still be aware of its shortcomings,strugle against incompetency in its own ranks, leave no stone unturned to bring about an improvement where weakness exists, revitalize the departments which are falling behind in their aims. On the contrary, we are witnessing, at least here in the West, a general apathy amongst our members and a total disinterest,of the general Czech public. We are also told by private sources, that the same thing is happening, with few notable exceptions,all over the country. We said private sources, because, obviously,offici- ally we do not take cognizance of such things (probably because it would be impolite). But the private sources seem to be amply born out by the available statistics. It is without question that our goals are magnificent on the face of it, the very best, as noble as anyone can make them. Name any virtue under the sun, and we are striving for it. Some of us may even reasonably conclude, that our ideals are so high they actually have become sky-borne and vanished long ago beyond the dim mysterious horizons, to borrow the words from the immortal prose of one of our writers. We shall examine the question of our “Aims, Tasks and Goals" in a separate article soon. Let us say today, that it is cheap to put words on paper. Another thing is to look around and determine for yourself what makes the people around you tick, what motivates them, what they still hope to achieve and how. And here you will make a startling discovery: We have quietly supplanted those magnificent goals by another one, much more simple, down to earth and understandable: by the simple goal of self-preservation. Quietly, slowly, without anyone seem- ingly noticing, we have lost our ability for self-improvement. We have long ago lost any pretense of caring about our sky-high ideals, long ago realized that they never led anywhere. Many a member spent his whole life, toiling formany years for Sokol, only to see less at the end of his life than there was when he started. Give such a gathering an idea to play with, and you']! finish, millions of words later, with a lot of hot air.
