HAVE MORE NATIONAL PRIDE GRUMPY AND THE NEW REPUBLIC A nation comprises people with their individual and different qualities, interests. In- • clinations. predilections, aversions, prejudices and hatreds. Nevertheless, each nation does possess certain common traits, which constitute the common denominator we call national charefftor«4mpng these are: a common hisinr- icaLap^cuiraral heritage, similar political aspirations. In most cases a common language, and tn small nations a blood relationship that ' can be seen to some extent in common physiological characteristics. Furthermore, there usually are additional characteristics occurring with such frequency that they may be considered aa belonging to Che national character. The Slovak people are characterized as deeply religious, artistically endowed, hospitable. modest and diligent in work. They frequently are referred toastheettizens ofa dove- nation. which implies their peaceful and serene disposition. However, they manifest themselves as people who are traditionalist, passive, submissive, jealous and prone to petty contentiousness. It is these latter qualities that have contributed to the loss of Slovak national independence and have forestalled Its restoration, and consequently brought about an inferiority complex In Slovak national character. To those acquainted with Slovak history, this Inferiority complex and lack of national pride arc understandable. Throughout the greater part of their existence, the Slovaks lived within a framework wherein the political entities were highly unfavorable to them. After the fall of the Great Moravian Empire in 906. an Empire In which their nation played a vita) role, the Slovaks were subjugated by the conquering Magyars (Hungarians) and enslaved for over a thousand years. Constant efforts were made by the Magyars to assimilate them, particularly after the Austro-Hungarian agreement of 1867. Nor did the symbiotic association with Ihc Czechs from 1918 to 1939 in the Czechoslovak Republic alleviate the situation. The Slovaks still found their national aspirations not only threatened but also hampered. The short-lived period of national independence of the Slovak Republic from 1939 to 1945. did not endure long enough to effect a change In the character of the nation. In short, throughout their entire history the Slovaks suffered constant political abuse and economic exploitation. Consequently, these misfortunes combined to leave a negative imprint on the Slovak people tn the form of an inferiority complex While on the one hand the Slovaks underwent many adversities, on the other hand they accomplished many achievements throughout their history. Above ail. they preserved their national distinctness through the preservation of their language and culture. Out of their sensitively endowed soul, they produced treasures of popular art After the codification of the Slovak literary language in the eighteenth century. they created a perennial and rich literature primarily in the field of poetry. Without any doubt the Slovak nation had attained a place of recognition among the other civilized nations of Central Europe. Slovakia, the territory between the Tatra Mountains and the Danube River, the land always inhabited . by the Slovaks, is and should be, despite a long subjugation and Hungarian domination, a nation that inspires pride In all its children, whether living there or scattered throughout the world. There Is really no reason why Slovaks should feel inferior because of their Slovak descent. Especially In the United Slates where they have achieved such great accomplishments. The early Immigrants came from an enslaved and exploited country where they could scarcely eke out an existence. Here they sought to advance their standard of living. Lacking the proper education, they accept«! the most menial assignments whether In mines, factories or industry. Often their salaries were meager and the environment in hospitable.But because of their frugality, diligence, modesty and piety, they overcome the obstacles to better living, and soon fared much better than their relatives and fellow-countrymen In Slovakia. Under the leadership of their priests, they founded and organized many parishes particularly in the East and Midwest. These parishes became the centers of their religious, social and cultural life. While other Immigrant groups to the United States have generally sustained their national spirit With the aid of the mother country. the situation among the Slovaks has been the reverse, it was the Slovak immigrants here who helped to re-kindle the national life of their fellow-countrymen and actively assisted In liberating Slovakia. When the Austrians and Hungarians signed their agreement in 1867. the Magyars inaugurated a systematic and brutal campaign to magyarlze all ethnic groups within the territory of "Old Hungary." Among these groups, the Slovaks were a prime target. Between 1873 and 1875 all three Slovak high schools and the Slovak Literary Institute (Matica Slovenska) were suppressed. The Hungarian language was Instituted as the official language- Children in elementary schools had to pursue their courses of study in Hungarian, except for the catechism. A sustained effort by the Magyars to eradicate Slovak national con- scicusuess In the minds and hearts of children was proving successful- Towards the end of the ’ nineteenth century, national life in Slovakia was ebbing and its spirit dying. The proverbial "shot-in-the-arm” came from the Slovak immigrants in the United States. Here, under the sun of liberty, their national consciousness grew and matured. Both nationally and locally, they organized into Influential religious and fraternal associations and soon poured new lifeblood into national aspirations of their fellow-countrymen in Slovakia. During this time, on the political field, the American Slovaks sought to reassure the granting of Slovak national rights In the future Czecho-Siovak Republic. On May 30.1918, representatives of the Czechs and Slovaks Ln the U.S.A., together With Prof. Thomas G. Masaryk, head of the National Council and future president of Czechoslovakia, signed an agreement by which a full autonomy was guaranteed to Slovakia In the Czechoslovak Republic. Unfortunately, thestipulaiionsof the socalled Pittsburgh Pact were not inserted into the constitution of the newly established Czechoslovakia, and Prof. Masaryk as president of Czechoslovakia completely repudiated the Pittsburgh Pact as not binding him. Nevertheless. Che Pittsburgh Pact served as a basis for continued struggle of the Slovak nation for its autonomy In the framework of the Czechoslovak Republic and reached Its climax In the eventual separation of the Slovaks from the Czechs in 1939. The establishment of the Slovak Republic on March 14. 1939. is therefore an accomplishment for whJfh the people of Slovakia are deeply indebted to their American countrymen.
