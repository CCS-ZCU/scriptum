[pageend12]BULLETIN of the Czechoslovak National Council of America No. 38 1956 Joseph Triner: WHAT AMERICA MEANS TO ME From my parents and grandparents s who were immigrants from Bohemia, Austria-Hungary, I learned what American citizenship meant, their reasons for escaping from this tyranny and why they came to America to seek freedom, their unlimited opportunities in the country I was born in, as well as the many privileges they enjoyed. I took all these things for granted as one born and raised in the land of the free. Not until I went to school did I learn what price was paid in blood by American pioneers for thi§ priceless heritage. I was born in the district known as Plzen, 18th St. near Center, now Racine Avenue, in Chicago, and when I went to grammar school, I did not know a word of English because at home we spoke only Bohemian. This was true of children of Bohemian, Italian, Polish and German and Slovak heritage, with a result that districts in Chicago were known as Little Bohemia, Little Poland, Germany, Slovakia, etc. I attended Bohemian theaters, concertsand other forms of entertainment. When we moved away from the neighborhood my opportunity to speak Bohemian and participate in the Bohemian fraternal life was less. I carried with me through my adult life recollections of this early feeling of my descendency. The amalgamation of these different groups as well as the privilege of said groups to speak the language of their birth and to participate in such nationalistic fraternal life without condemnation of being other than good Americans, that is that what made America great. The free integration of education was also an impressive factor. When the first world war was declared, I volunteered as a private and found that a civilian had as great an op• ortunity to be promoted in the American Army as a career soldier. I remember my father and grandfather telling me of the hardships endured in the Austro-Hungarian army by the draftees and the arrogance of the career soldier and officer. After the War and upon my entry into business, I realized and appreciated the value of free enterprise under our constitution and the equal privileges to succeed for one who is willing to work. I realized the fairness of such laws as Social Security and Old Age Benefits and was pleased to participate in such worthy projects. I fully appreciated the democratic spirit prevailing in the Armed Forces and the indescribable patriotism exhibited by soldiers and officers alike in combat. During our darkest hour, shortly after Pearl Harbor, I realized what America meant to me, and I was shocked by the thought that I. might lose all of the privileges and freedom which I had enjoyed during my youth and adulf life. I know I bespeak the sentiments of all others who served in the second war when I say that that is what we fought for. When the ugly head of international communism showed itself by enslaving Czechoslovakia, Poland and other countries, and began to infiltrate among our citizens here at heme, I recognized the extreme danger and was more than willing to become a part of National Council (Národní Rada Americká) in its fight to combat communism. When one has lived more than three score years, one’s personal feelings are secondary; one’s chief concern is for one’s children and grandchildren. I want the America we all love to continue indefinitely for my children, grandchildren and their children and for America to mean to them what it has meant to me. In order that this may be done it is necessary for us to forewarn our children so that they may be forearmed against the greatest threat to their freedom, namely that of communism. [pageend14]
