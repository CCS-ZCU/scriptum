Ferdinand Peroutka The whole world is astonished at the sight of Rumania’s forceful, independent stance at the preparatory talks for the conference on European securi. ty in Helsinki. Such a display of self-assertion is very tare indeed among the Communist nations — and even though Rumania is following her own foreign policy for some time already, her attitude and even bravery in Helsinki are most admirable. Of course, she could not maintain her line, if it were not for the United States. It is a well known fact that President Ceausescu was deeply distressed by the Soviet invasion of Czechoslovakia and by the Brezhnev doctrine proclaimed shortly thereafter, and for that reason he sought a contact with the Chinese and American governments in hope of counterbalancing Rumania’s position between all these world powers. It is said that during his meeting with President Nixon he was given assurances of American support — and ever since he successfully walks the tightrope between the three blocs. Ceausescu is a pragmatic politician, and if the results of the November elections in the US were any different, Rumania would not be able to maintain her independent line in Helsinki, but would have to conform to the Soviet policy. Such is the reality of the East European life. However, for the time being she has gained ground also because of the harsh outlook of the Soviet econowc situation. Rumania has the advantage of a record crop, a good part of which she is selling to the Soviet Union for hard currency at high prices and the Soviets are forced to accept such conditions, which hardly have a precedent in the Communist bloc. The most interesting ques- tion is how far will Rumania’s example affect other nations living in the proximity of the Soviet Union. Even though the people in satellite countries admire Rumania’s foreign policy (much less so her internal policy), none of those countries has such good relations with the West — or with China — as Rumania, With the possible exception of Poland which is beginning to build closer ties with the U. S. This may be a very significant factor in the future developments in that area, particularly since there appear again signs of an unrest among Polish workers. There is a growing awareness in Poland of the Soviet exploitation — trains and boats are being loaded, with Polish potatos, cabbages and other produce and grain to supply the Soviets, and the Polish workers are understandably worried about the effect thereof on their own economic situation. Closer ties with the U.S. would give the Polish people an impetus to reassert themselves at home and possibly even on the international scene and this would mean, lots of trouble for the Soviets. So it almost seems that the Soviets demand for a conference on European security opened Pandora's box for the world Communism. The last thing they wanted to safeguard the rights and sovereignty of smaller nations. They just wished, to maintain the status quo as long as they are in an disadvantageous situation — and then to extend their domination in every possible direction. If the West seizes the opportunity, then true European security could be obtained by putting the Soviets where they belong — from Central Europe back behind, their own borders. So far, Rumania has the right spirit, but she needs help and it is our moral duty and obligation to the world peace to render her such a support.
