Věstník Sokola San Francisco. Leden-January 1982. / . / . v . Some thoughts on emigre artist. Jinna FUCHS. Czechoslovakia doesn't exist anymorg does it?, a lady asked me the other day. I assured her that it most certainly does. The land, after all, is still there; so are the people; so is the culture. And a culture does not come to being in a day, or a year, or even in twenty years, thus by the same token it cannot be destroyed by a day, a year, or even several decades of oppression. Being what it is, a culture cannot simply be wished away by those in power. Unlike a territory, it cannot be run over by foreign armies. And unlike a single man, it cannot be exiled from its own country. It is possible to distinguish two elements in any given culture. One of them is the obvious upper stratum of the day-to-day, year-to-year crust of ideas, attitudes, institutions and other entities, which are being constantly influenced, changed and re-shaped by the on-going political and social upheavals of the times. The other stratum is a long- range, deep-lying core, which, in a sense, serves as a kind of tribal memory that links the past with the present, evaluates experiences good and bad, preserves whatever is worth keeping, and - imperceptibly, but indelibly - marks the groundwork of a given nation's future. At times, the processes in the upper stratum may be so violent, that they may for a time eclipse the importance of the core. Nevertheless, the core is always there, and regardless of whatever the mode of the day may be, this core continues appraising, evaluating and making sense out of everything that is going on. Thus, what sometimes may appear to be a solid part of a culture within the context of the upper stratum, may - so far as the core stratum is concerned - not be a part of culture at all, and, conversely, what sometimes appears to remain totally outside and perhaps ignored or suppressed by the day-to-day stratum, may in the long run become an integral part of the deep lying core. In practice this means that no memory of a historical event can be ever erased from the collective consciousness of a people by deleting a page here and there in history textbooks. It means that neither a name, nor a book, nor a piece of art can be cast away from a given national culture by ignoring it, prohibiting it, removing it from the libraries’ shelves, or for that matter publicly burning it in a town square. z All that has been said so far, bears directly upon the so-called emigr^ culture that is upon a body of works of art that are being created outside a given national milieu by so-called emigre' artists, who are nothing more than living anachronisms, walking personifications of the utmost revolt known to humankind: the revolt against absurdity of prolonging a form of existence when the very conditions that make this form of existence possible themselves no longer exist. For once an artist leaves his country and his people, once the umbilical cord is cut, he is condemned to follow a road different from the highway along which his people travel; and this new path, regardless whether or not he wishes it, regardless whether he likes it or not, leads only farther and farther away from the point of departure. The current of time never reverses itself, and it inexorably carries an e'migr^ artist towards his death as an artist he once was. In order to defeat the absurdity of his life situation, an emigr^ artist may resort to one thing only: he has to become ultimately absurd - that is - he has to create, create in spite of everybody, himself included, in spite of anything and everything, and thus, and only thus, paradoxically, he - as an artist - may become reborn. Strana-Page 3. fulfilling his artistic potentialities depends not only upon his personal qualities as a man and artist, his temperament, talent, courage and perseverance, but partly also upon the form of his artistic expression. The technical obstacles in the way of an emigr4 painter, sculptor, composer, dancer or mime may be relatively small ar even even none. The obstacles that loom in the way of any artist whose work has something to do with language, are much more formidable. For an actor or prose writer they are grave, for a poet perhaps unsurmountable. For such an artist, the choices are obvious. He may either fiercely attack the bastion of the language spoken in his adopted country and struggle, as far as his strength and talent may carry him along this new road, or he may continue - as he had been doing up to the point of his departure from his native land - creating works of art by the means of his own language in spite of the fact that this language is, so to speak, no longer "there" (meaning that it is not in, out and altogether around the artist as it was back home). This means among other things, to reconcile himself to the certainty that the language he uses, the tool he has brought along with him, will, at some point in the future, become obsolete, because language - like an ever-growing tree - changes day to day, and he, simply by not being there, cannot keep his finger upon its living pulse. This means - to the burden of inner-aloneness, which every creator, in order to create, must experience - is added the burden of another energy-sapping loneliness, that is to create virtually without communication with the public, without the possibility of being able to confront the results of one's labors with the people whose language happens to be his instrument. It means finding courage to write books and poems and not being able to see them published. It means producing a recital of the finest poetry in the language and deriving one's satisfaction from the breathless attention of seventeen people who do attend. But it also means, and this applies not only to writers and actors, but also to other ^migré artists who keep on struggling in a new environment, confronting the surrounding foreign culture head-on and, as a result of this confrontation, to shed an illusion or two about the culture of one's own. It means, in many instances, to be able to go on expressing the truths which may constitute an indivisible part of a tribal memory, but which - at given moment - may not be freely expressed back at home. Finally, it means possessing a unique opportunity to enrich the horizons of one's own culture with the aspects, views and outlooks that sprout to existence along the edges where one culture meets another, and on the basis of ideas born out of this cross-pollination arrive at truths which to other artists not living in a constant tension of cultural duality remain inaccessible. According to the dictionary, culture is a summarizing definition for the concepts, habits, skills, arts, instruments, institutions and so on of a given people in a given period. Nowhere, in relation to culture does the dictionary speak about any particular "given" place. For an emigre artist living with an everpresent consciousness of his own doom, this may provide an excellent palliative for his pain. For this means that the concept of culture cuts not only across the limits in time and memory, but across those in space as well. And it also means, that a true art, regardless of the place from which it came into being and quite apart from its function as art per se, universal art, shall always find its way back into the core of that national culture which has made its' creator what he is, - an artist. Programs supervised by local police are sensible responses to an alarming rise in home burglaries, and they are proving to be effective. In Grand Rapids, Mich, neighborhoods participating in "Operation Safeguard" have reduced crime in their areas by 15%, Such programs teach citizens to observe what's happening around them and to report unusual activities. By developing a rapport with law enforcement agencies, people lose their fear of calling the police. This interaction increases the police department's effectiveness, and it provides citizens with a healthy alternative to simply living in fear. Very often, knowledge that such a program is in effect will discourage would-be criminals. Start a Crime Watch in your area by gathering your neighbors together and inviting police representatives to the meeting. The officers teach you how to be a good neighborhood observer, how to secure your own home, and how to meet the specific needs of your area. Supervised citizen involvement is a key factor in reversing the chilling crime statistics. Lawbreakers outnumber policemen, but they don't outnumber a watchful citizenry.
