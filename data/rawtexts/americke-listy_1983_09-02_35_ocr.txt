JeCONGRESSMAN BERNARD DWYER REPORTS Legislation that could save the government billions of dollars each year by reforming the spare parts purchasing practices of the Defense Department has recently been introduced in the Congress. H. R. 3734, which I have co-sponsored, is designed to address the many problems uncovered by several Federal agencies concerning spare parts purchasing. These agencies include the Pentagon itself, where Defense Secretary Caspar Weinberger has admitted that something must be done to bring the cost of spare parts under control. The Department of Defense spends approximately .“Jl 3-13 billion on spare parts each year. A study by the Small Business Administration (SBA) found, however, that only 7.9 percent of spare parts are presently purchased on an open, competitive basis. The Inspector General’s office of the Defense Department, in its audit of the procurement of aircraft engine spare parts, found that Pentagon buying centers, by not challenging sole source procurements, were paying premium prices to prime contractors. In most cases, these contractors are not the actual manufacturers, and therefore, prices included overhead and other mark-up factors. In response to this problem of inflated costs, H. R. 3784 would expand the “break-out” system of spare parts purchasing. Break-out efforts, which help the Department of Defense to locate the manufacturer who will make the part for the least amount of money, have already begun on a limited basis within the Air Force. With such information and incentives, significant cost savings—estimated at between $4 and $7 billion—could be realized. Moreover there would be less lag time between request and receipt of parts under more streamlined, direct procurement procedures. More orders for small businesses and more jobs—many in the blue.collar sector hardest hit by unemployment—would also be encouraged under this legislation.
