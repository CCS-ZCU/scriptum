JANUARY 27. 1988 ~ TRUTH AT MY TABLE BY JOSEPH C. KRAJSA, KSGG THE REAL ATMOSPHERE OF SLOVAKIA THE SLOVAK HERITAGE (The following is the speech delivered by Dr. Fred E. Dohra at the Slovak Heritage Weekend. Wayne State University. Michigan at a comr.irmarative event sponsored by the Slovak Research Institute of America. Detroit. Michigan. — EDITOR) 1 am honored on this occasion, and to Identify with a cause as honorable and Just as fliartrt4»fjependence for the Slovak people and -XheďFcourítry. For some of you. my presence at this great national celebration may be something of a question, so with your indulgence, may I offer a brief personal note. Like most Americans. my education followed the traditional pattern of learning a little of this, and a little of that, though with relatively little real perception and understanding of the world outside the United States. All too often, this meant the growth in my mind of national and ethnic stereotypes about other countries and peoples, a condition which unfortunately continues to characterize much American knowledge of other parts of the world. At more advanced levels, as a university student. I was fortunate not to be brainwashed with the standard views of American. German, and Russian historiography about Europe, and later the Soviet Empire. My training as a geographer demanded that I look at the world as it was. and as it Is. rather than merely how one or another school of history sees past events. A realistic view of that part of Europe which has become more and more my area of competence, shows clearly that the labels generally given to regions and countries can be terribly misleading — and in some cases tragically so. And. as you can readily understand. this realistic view, unquestionably leads to a recognition that the term and title, "CZECHOSLOVAKIA" is not only misleading, it is erroneous. Butalso. as most of you know, this is neither a popular nor well understood view in the United States. In scholarly and academic circles, the situation is much the same. Those lew of us who do recognize the fundamental differences between Czechs and Slovak are often confronted with some of the same attitudes of disbelief which you know so well. The idea still is not widely accepted that tlie Czech and Slovak peoples are different, and the idea that the Czech Lands and Slovakia are different and separate does not seem as yet to be fully appreciated, 1 fear, therefore, that all too many of our fellow Americans — even today — remain as ignorant of Czecho-Slovakia as was British prime Minister Chamberlain in those fateful days of Munich. 1938. when he called Czecho-' Slovakia. "A far away country (and) people of whom we know nothing." And even the relatively few who know what Slovakia is. even fewer know where Slovakia is. Yet in the where lies the root of the trouble, and the basic problem of freedom and independence. Think about it. Where is Slovakia? In the very heart of Europe — nearly five million Slovak people, surrounded by thirty-three million Poles on the north, forty million Ukrainians and 125 million Russians on the east, ten million Hungarians on the south, and to the west, nine million Czechs. The really remarkable fact is that against such tremendous odds and oppressors, the Slovak nation and culture have survived at all. This itself is a great tribute to the strength of the ideas and the people who have nurtured aiqj maintained Slovak nationalism. ’ * Then to. there is the idea of the Slovak land — the territory — which is the fundamental. even ultimate reality, on which the Slovak nation has rested, and been built for well over a thousand years. This idea of territory — the ■territorial imperative' — has gained widea^j ceptance as the real basis of nation and sovereignty. Without the solid base of ter ritory. where the roots of Slovak national thought and culture have existed nearly from the dawn of European history, there would be no Slovakia today. But it is the event that we commemorate here this weekend — March 14. 1939 — the day of the founding of the independent Republic of Slovakia — this is far too little known, and certainly the least understood. Primarily because that event, as well as others of that time, have usually been interpreted only from Czech. anti Nazi, or even Nazi viewpoints. All too rarely here in America is March 14th ever seen from the point of view of the people most concerned — the Slovaks Time and again I've said to my students, and often to some doubting colleagues — and I say it again today to any and all who would continue to question that road to freedom and independence for Slovakia — look at it from the Slovak viewpoint. See that opportunity for independence as the Slovaks did. Think about it their way — then you may understand- After a thousand years of Magyar domination. there came the bright rays of hope sparked by the agreements reached here in America, at Cleveland and Pittsburgh in 1915 and 1918 respectively. But those dreams proved to be only dreams as a new colonialism was established But rule this time came not from Budapest, but from Prague. And it seemed that nobody anywhere else in the ivorld appeared to give a damn'. And then came Munich. Here it must be said that it ill becomes those millions around the world, who had applauded that shabby sellout in Munich as "peace in our time.” to criticize the Slovak people for reaching an agreement for their country’s independence with the same man. What the Slovaks did was seize the opportunity of the moment — in fact, as events have shown — their only chance for freedom and independence — short lived though it turned out to be. Was it not Winston Churchill who. when questioned about British help to Communist Russia in 1941. said. "1 would gladly sign a pact with Lucifer himself, if it would help our righteous cause!" From Bratislava, from Kosice, from hundreds of towns and villages, the Slovak cause in 1939 was a righteous cause — and the Slovak cause remains a righteous cause here today. After the few years of a kind independence. there came at the end of the war. another imperial master, this time from the east. Czecho-Slovakia became a Soviet satellite — indeed a colony of the worst of imperialists. communist Russia. And Moscow's continuous propaganda machine hammers into Slovakia, and all the new colonial peoples of this communist European Empire, that they are constantly threatened by "bourgois nationalists" within their midst, and from the West as well. And that the conquest of their country by Russia, "liberation" as the Kremlin calls it. was the best thing that ever happened to Slovakia. For the rulers of the Kremlin, nationalism is a basic threat, because it demes the idea that communism Is a universal unifying force, easily capable of transcending what the communist views as the destructive element of nationalism. More simply, so long as there Is one Slovak nationalist making his claim for his nation and its rights, communism is threatened. Now. after years of communist rule, nationalist demonstrations must be viewed as hard evidence that this part of communist dogma simply is not true. But for the Politburo (who know this) to admit It, Is to say that the structure of communism Isn't sound — that it could even topple and fall, taking its ruler with it — if it were admitted that communism Is less important and attractive than nationalism. The tough rulers of the Kremlin know they arc threatened ideologically, and have their answer: never admit ideological failure and crush the nationalists. Because they have no illusions about the reality of the fires of nationalism which continue to burn brightly in the hearts and minds of not only the Slovak people, but all of the peoples of the Captive Nations as well. But where does this leave Slovakia on this day of commemoration? After six years, the new Federation of Slovakia and the Czech Lands has again turned out to be pretty much another colonialism, a sham inflicted on Slovakia In reality, the Federation is merely a facade for the continuation of Moscow's imperial rule, with help from the Prague communists. This situation becomes especially distressing when we look at what happened over the last thirty years elsewhere in the world — where we have seen the proliferation of an almost unbelievable number of independent states from former colonies. And nearly all of these new countries — large and small, rich or poor (though mostly very poor) — have become members of the United Nations. A look at the UN list shows that nearly half of the member countries today have either smaller populations, or smaller areas than does Slovakia. And their average level of living is far below that of Slovakia — despite the drag of the unwieldy communist system on the Slovak economy. But Slovakia asks only to be free of outside domination and oppression — to have the chance to live as a free people on their own territory. unhampered apd.npt threatened bý outside tyranny. Surely this is not an unusual desire, especially when America is approaching die two hundreth year of American independence. As something of a concerned specialist, and one who watches the changing pattern of world events fairly closely. 1 would be less than candid if I were to say there is a bright light at the end of the long, dark tunnel of Slovak independence. Yet the best hopes for the future rest on a continued expression of what we have here today — genuine Slovak unity. You all know without my telling you that one of the major problems sixty and thirty-six years ago. was a lack of unity and purpose among Slovaks. But there's plenty of evidence today that there's no lack of unity in Slovakia, and in resistance to oppression. But can this lead to freedom and independence? Very frankly, it doesn't seem likely without strong, determined and continued support from outside. In our time, such support can come only from the United States. But a United States which did not see support of similar movements In Hungary in 1956. or in 1968 in Czecho-Slovakia. as being in direct American interests, cannot be expected now to make any commitment to Slovakia. Any uprising within Slovakia would be ruthlessly crushed with only a mild American expression of concern, and no cen sure of the Soviet masters. Yet over the longer run. there must be something more encouraging than mere wishful thinking. Realistically, this Ues in evolu tion. essentially peaceful change — and not in revolution. Such erosion of Moscow's and Prague's dominance over Slovakia will, of course, require outside support and pressure. The real hope for Slovakia, therefore, rests In developing an informed and strong American public opinion based on the simple but accurate theme that Slovakia and Czechoslovakia arc not the same. This weekend celebration is one method. The road to gain such needed support may be long, possibly very long, and it probably means that yet another generation in Slovakia must continue under the outsider's yoke. But when — not if — this understanding Is brought about here in the United States, then we can truly and realistically begin to look forward to a genuinely free and independent Slovakia. Finally, let me add one more personal note. My very first entrance In 1958. into Moscow's communist empire was through Slovakia. I remember all too well, we crossed the Danube and entered what we could barely make out as the dimly lit and gloomy streets of Bratislava. The contrast with the lights and gaiety of Vienna where we had been the night before was astounding and somewhat frightening, even for one who had studied the communist world for many years. While things looked somewhat better in daylight, nevertheless, the all-pervasive communist apparatus dampened the view of what Bratislava had been and could be again. It was only when wc drove into the glorious countryside, the plains, the hills and mountains, the villages and towns — the people — that the real atmosphere of Slovakia began to emerge — a compelling realization of what free Slovakia could be — and indeed, someday will be! For me that day of freedom cannot come too soon! ABOUT THE AUTHOR. JANUARY 27. 1988 FROM OUR JEDNOTA HISTORY By Anthony X. Sutherland. Ph.D. THE JOB OF EDITOR The job of JEDNOTA editor is not an easy one. not now. not sixty-seven years ago. Besides fulfilling the stipulations found in the bylaws, the editor had to satisfy the officers and mem- ý bers. Often he had to walk a tightrope between his own convictions and the voices of thousands of members, each with a different point of view. Jozef Husek was the longest and most controversial editor of JEDNOTA. His days at JEDNOTA began before World War I and continued until 1937. Throughout those years Husek had to answer all sorts of criticism from all quarters. Perhaps most annoying of all . to Husek were those who did * not understand the work of the editor. In 1920. for example, some people began to say that Husek had a soft job at Jednota, that he had zlaty easy or good times there. They said the work of an editor, to type out articles, was not real Work: that if Husek wanted real work he should come to the mines or the factories. Husek admitted that work in a mine or factory was more difficult physically, but that did not mean the Job of editor was easy. Husek answered that at Jednota we work as hard as those in the mines. The work of editor, he said, is tiring, exhaustive, both physically and mentally. Furthermore. besides following the bylaws, the editor had to do a dozen more things. Husek emphasized that JEDNOTA was an important voice not only in the Society — the First Catholic Slovak Union — but in national and Church public life. Therefore, the responsibility of the editor was not small. The years after World War I. he added, were chaotic ones and it was often difficult to discern between a He and the truth. Štvrtok dňa 8. októbra 1987 Hneď po raňajkách sadáme do nášho autobusa a konečne s veľkým očakávaním odchádzame do známeho mesta Lurd. ktorý sme často spomínali ako aj naši starí rodičia. Ja osobne od môjho starého otca po matke. Keď sa jeden krát vracal zo Spoj- štátov amerických, mal možnosť sa tam zastaviť. Nuž všetci sme očakávali, kedy zazřeme toto tak často spomínané pútnické miesto. Dnes mesto je krásne vystavené, ale nás všetkých zaujímalo sa čím skorej dostať tam, kde stoji katedrála, kde sa odohralo to, Čo nám kresťanom pripomína, že áno, Panna Mária nás miluje a Panna Mária oroduje za nás všetkých u jej Syna Ježi- ôa Krista. Dňa 11. februára toho roku 1988 bude 130 rokov, čo sa po prvýkrát zjavila Panna Mária našej mladej Bernadette a zjavenia pokračovali až do 16. júla. vtedy 18 ráz sa jej Panna Mária zjavila. Od tohto času prichádzajú pútnici z celého sveta, aby sa poklonili Panne Márii. Ročne odhadujú, že prichodí do mesta Lurd vyše tri a pol milióna pútnikov. Práve, keď sme prichádzali na toto posvätné miesto, začínala svätá omša a boli sme dojatí tým, že koľko sa zúčastnilo nemocných a mrzákov, či už na vozidlách, alebo prichádzali samotní, ktorí veria, že cez orodovanie Matky Božej, Pán Boh sa zmiluje nad nimi a obsiahnu uzdravenie. Bol to skutočne ťažký pohľad na toľké nemocničné vozidlá, ktoré privážali tuná tisíce ošetrovateliek a na každom je chorá osoba. Náš dôstojný Otec však chcel mať tuná sv. omšu. S Kornelom Piačkom odišli hľadať. Konečne dostali sme miesto priamo v bazilike v kaplnke sv. Anny kde sme sa zúčastnili slovenskej sv. omše. Po sv. omši sme mali voľný čas a každý išiel svojou stranou. Osobne s manželkou, Štefan s Mária Gmák & Helena Gregová sme išli pozrieť krížovú cestu a mal som veľmi krásny dojem, keď som videl kráčať a modliť sa pútnikov z Východného Nemecka. Krásne spievali po nemecky Aké by bolo krásne, keby aj vláda dnes na Slovensku alebo v Cechách dovolila aj našim rodným bratom a sestrám takto slobodne putovať, či už do Ríma, Lurdu alebo Fatimy V tomto pútnickom a historickom meste je veľa čo vidieť a za tých niekoľko hodín nemôžeme všetko si pozrieť. Pravda to najhlavnejšie každý z nás si pozrel a bude mať dlho v pamiatke štvrtok dňa 8. októbra 1937. Počasie bolo daždivé, nuž ešte aj to nám akosi trocha pokazilo návštevu tohto krásneho pútnického miesta. Bolo už dosť neskoro, keď sa schádzame pri našom autobuse na parkovisku a zisťujeme, že aj tuná nájdete našich krajanov, na ktorých si spomíname všetci. Tak sa vraciame posilnení Duchom Svätým do hotela v meste Tarbes a očakávame ďalší deň našej pútnickej cesty. Piatok dna 9. októbra 1987. Najprv sme zašli do kostola na sv. omšu Opúšťame mesto Tar- Písal sa rok 1858, kedy dňu 11. februára prvýkrát sa zjavila Panna Mária mladej Bernadette a posledné zjavenie bolo lfi. júla 1858. Podľa údajov ročne prichodí na toto pútnické miesto vyše 3,500.000 pútnikov u v roku 1987 prišlo sem 54 pútnikov Slovákov z Kanady a jeden z USA. Veríme, že i v budúcnosti naši otcovia jezuiti alebo iní dôstojní otcovia znova zrnbia podobnú púť. akú sme mali Slováci z Kanady počas Mariánskeho ruku. Pútnicl si budú spomínať na kostol sv. Jedna naša pútnička Hanusko- vá bola v meste prepadnutá a všetko, čo mala pri sebe, jej ukradli. Vrátila sa do hotela a polícia prišla vyšetrovať. Krajan Vilko Mihalovič nám hovorí, že tuná je taký zvyk, že keď okradnú niekoho, tak peniaze a cenné veci zoberú a ostatne papiere snažia sa nejakým spôsobom postihnutej osobe vrátiť.
