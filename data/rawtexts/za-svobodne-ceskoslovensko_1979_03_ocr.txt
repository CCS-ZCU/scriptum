JAROSLAV HUTKA Jeden z nejlepších a najpopulárnejších československých zpěváků dnešní doby, signatar Charty 77, textař a spisovatel [pageend6]7 PŘÍSPĚVEK SPOLEČNOST PRO VELY A UMĚNI CZECHOSLOVAK SOCIETY OF ARTS AND SCIENCES The Future of the Czech Language outside of Czechoslovakia The preservation of a language in the world must be the concern of all those who are native speakers. However, practical conditions have to be considered. Few Czechoslovakians are now leaving their country as compared with the situation after the last three emigrations: before World War II; in 1948; and in 1968-69. Dr. Jiri Marvan, who received his PhD in Linguistics from Charles University in 1966, has concerned himself with all aspects of the Czech language. Fventhough there are three separate languages spoken in Czechoslovakia, Dr. Marvan concerned himself basically with the language of Prague which is also the language spoken by his ancestors. In his view of the Czech language, Dr. Marvan divided the spoken word from the written word with time being the dividing factor. For those who speak Czech at meetings and with friends, he feels that it is a hardship to force oneself to speak only linguistically correct Czech. Those who are trying to speak Czech are also having to conduct daily life in at least one other language. The time process as well as the auditory process have to be considered. The auditory process which aides us to understand what is spoken must be able to receive the message at a quick rate. As one is listening the mind must be preparing to correct vocal response. In writing the language this time process is not speeded and there is time for the writer to rewrite that which is not correctly written. The reader also has the opportunity to review what has been read. Dr. Marvan gave an example where the incorrect pronunciation has become the acceptable one. In his home, milk has always been called mliko, when mleko is the correct pronunciation. However when he tried to correct his family there was a great dispute as the one heard is considered the correct one. He feels that trying to change is not vital. It is difficult to update the Czech language as we know it with the new words for new inventions and ideas. Presently in Czechoslovakia projects have been started to update dictionaries and study the language, but most of these projects never get finished because of constant government policy changes. The other area which must be considered by native speakers is the teaching of Czech to others. He stated that by the age of 14 years, a student can not learn a language through the auditory method of teaching. The student after 14 should be taught by the grammar book and by reading. Understanding by listening should only be a small portion of the program. In summing up this discussion, Dr. Marvan restated that we can not expect help from inside Czechoslovakia to achieve any of our goals.
