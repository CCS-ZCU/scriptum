The real picture off Czechoslovakia SHUT UNDER the title “CZECHOSLOVAKIA — DIFFERENT PHILOSOPHY, BUT SIMILAR PRACTICES” the March issue of the Journalist carried an article which made me doubt that it was an English periodical and not an official Czech handout I was reading. An English colleague (and NUJ member) told me that it made him “choke over my toast and coffee” as he opened his copy at the breakfast table. At first I thought, naively, that its author, Denis Cox, must be a simple-minded English journalist who, on a short visit to Prague, swallowed the official hook, line and sinker, as fed him by Marcel Nolc, Secretary General of the Czechoslovak Union of Journalists (this trade union organisation being of the kind of those in Poland before August 1980). However, the New Statesman on April 10 revealed — and it is to be congratulated for so doing — that, whatever else he might be. Mr. Cox is certainly not simple-minded. He. and I quote, “runs his own public relations firm in London, and . . . looks after all industrial and commercial PR in Britain for the Czech Government.” UP...OR BE SHU That, as they say, puts a different complexion on things, more specifically on the article extolling the marvellous freedom of the Czechoslovak press under Dr. Husak. The Journalist, I am afraid, fell for a particularly nasty piece of skulduggery, though it also has to take some blame for not checking its sources. Mr. Cox, on the other hand, Czeched his very thoroughly indeed. His article is such an atrocious conglomeration of — to put it as politely as I can — untruths, half-truths and distortions (interestingly enough, he accuses the British press of “slanted and often distorted coverage” where Czechoslovakia is concerned) that it might seem not worthy of reply. But as one, who in his daily work, comes into constant contact with the victims of repressive regimes and of censorship, I feel that Mr. Cox’s apologia for one of the worst of these must not be allowed to go unchallenged. The first thing that has to be said is that we are not discussing, as Mr. Cox would like to insinuate, «“a dialogue between philosophies”. Czechoslovakia today is (and has been for the past twelve-and-a-half years) an occupied country whose government owes its existence solely to the presence of foreign troops and tanks. In such circumstances any discussion of “philosophies” is quite beside the point. Even if we ignore the Soviet occupation (or should I say the "temporary presence of fraternal forces” as Mr. Mole or Mr. Cox might put it), statements such as "the decision what to print or not to print rests entirely with the T UP editor” are again meaningless, since the editors themselves are Party appointees who know exactly wnat is expected of them, knowing too that it is more than their job (and sometimes their very liberty) is worth to step out of line — a line, by the way, set down on any and every conceivable subject by the Central Committee of the Party. Even a hired propagandist ought to know better than to claim that “there is certainly no censorship of these publications”. In a country like Czechoslovakia under its present regime — just as in the fifties and early sixties — nothing (and I mean nothing) can get into print that would not be acceptable to the authorities. Self-censorship, as indicated above, plays an important a role here as actual censorship. There can be no question of any criticism, no matter how mild, of the Party, its leaders, the government, or of course Big Brother in Moscow and his other “allies.” Unless, indeed, that ally steps momentarily out of line, as in Poland just now. The censorship, however, does not apply only to the press. It extends to the arts, to literature, films and the theatre, so that even the country’s greatest and bestloved living poet, the 80-year-old Jaroslav Seifert, is banned from publishing his work, together with most of the finest novelists and playwrights. Anyone who doubts my words has only to send for copies of Index on Censorship in which, over the past nine years, we have carried many an example of Czechoslovak postinvasion censorship, as well as censorship in numerous other [pageend4]countries such as the USSR, Poland, Chile, South Africa, South Korea, Indonesia, Iran and Iraq, Togo and Guinea, China and Taiwan, as well as articles on the fate of writers and journalists there and in countries like Argentina or Turkey, where they have in recent years faced other dangers, such as violent death or abduction and disappearance. Perhaps the most outrageous statement of all (a different choice, to be sure, in a 1,300-word article that is full of them): Rude Pravo, the Czech Communist Party organ, has, according to Mr. Cox, “a track record of courage”. Let us take a look at that track record. Before the war — when it did its best to undermine the democratic Czechoslovak Republic in which it was allowed to exist despite its consistent antigovernment agitation — Rude Pravo faithfully toed the Stalinist line through all the Soviet horrors of the thirties, the systematic starvation of the peasants, the. brutal purges and the horrendous! Gulags; it continued in the same sycophantic spirit in the post-waj Keriod, when the horrors were nc >nger perpetrated in the USSR alone but also at home in what used to be a democratic and humane Czechoslovakia, with the purges and show trials and executions and incarcerations and torture carried out in the fifties by its very own Communist Party at the behest and under the direction of Stalin and his KGB. (Perhaps Mr. Cox would care to look up what Rude Pravo, that fearless champion of truth and virtue, had to say on the trial and execution of Dr. Milada Horáková in 1949 or the trial and execution of the Communist Party’s Secretary General, Rudolf Slansky, and his fellowaccused in 1952.) After Stalin’s death in 1953, Rude Pravo fawned briefly upon his successor Malenkov and, when he was deposed shortly afterwards by Khrushchev, genial old Nikita became the recipient of the paper’s praises. Until such a time, of course, as he had to make way for Leonid Brezhnev . . . And I leave it to your readers’ imagination just how fervently Rude Pravo criticises anything he does! Yes, Rude Pravo did show courage — for a few weeks or months — in 1968 after the Soviet invasion. But I again offer no prizes for guessing what became of the journalists responsible for this “counter-revolutionary” activity. And ever.since that brief interlude Rude Pravo has been its nasty old self again, publishing fulsome editorials on each anniversary of the occupation to thank the invaders for their kindness in coming to Czechoslovakia’s assistance, marvelling at Brezhnev’s wisdom, statesmanship, and brotherly love for the Czechoslovak people. And, let us not forget, brazenly lying about this and other topics, such as the recent events in neighbouring Poland where, if Rude Pravo and similar organs in the USSR and the other East European countries were to be believed, those millions of workers and ordinary Polish people we have seen on our television screens (now there's a genuine track record of courage for Mr. Cox to crow about) are nothing but subversive counterrevolutionaries, out to wreck their own country or, at lest, gullible simpletons misled by a few foreign-inspired saboteurs. Read what Rude Pravo writes about Poland these days and you will see that no other interpretation is possible. The Journalist accompanied Mr. Cox’s article with a picture showing the front page of Rude Pravo, dated September 15,1980. It is, of course, too small for even a Czech reader to make out what it says, but anyone can tell at a glance that it is an extremely dull and boring paper.
