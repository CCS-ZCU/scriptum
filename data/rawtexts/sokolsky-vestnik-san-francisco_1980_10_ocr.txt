Věstník Sokola San Francisco fííjen - October 1980 To all Czechoslovaks in the Bay Area: In October, all of you. have an opportunity to see Leoš Janáček*s "Její pastorkyňa" sung in our native language at the San Francisco opera, I have had. the honor to interview the lady who is engineering a small miracle. She translates the whole opera for the actors and also presents the phonetical pronunciation. This way the actors know the meaning of what they are singing and it enables them to get emotionally involved as well. Thus they try to interpret what the author meant. The name of this lady is Yvette Graff - Synek who resides in New York and who studied music also in Paris. Through her work she introduces Czech operas to the American audience, in its original. This is the magnificent task she undertakes through which she inconspicuously and humbly carves her name into the history of cultural relations between the United States and Czechoslovakia. Here she oversees the coordination of the Czech interpretation of "Jenufa", how "Její pastorkyňa" is called outside of Czechoslovakia. This lady exudes dedication to music and love for operas of the Czech giants. It is interesting that Leoš Janáček during the last two to three years became the "newest discovery" on the american scene. They found in him the values they were searching for: richness and possibilities of discovering time and again new nuances which present themselves in a myriad of modifications of lasting value. Mrs. Graff talked about Rudolf Firkušný, Rafael Kubelik, director Adler and many others} all these persons form part of her rewarding career, bhe is wholly absorbed now with the preparation of "Liška bystrouška" and "Věc Macropulos" to be presented next spring by the New York City opera. She already did the same work with "Dalibor" and "Kaía Kabánová" in Carnegie Hall. But the most refreshing impression I derived from watching this extraordinary lady was when she talked about Czechoslovakia and the Czech language in particular. She radiated her love and affection of her mothertongue, her eyes all lit up - I wished I was a painter and was able to put it on canvas. I urge all our friends, Czech and American, to go and see this opera. For the duration of the performance all Czechs will be transfered back home /the actors are so good/ and our american friends would enjoy Janáček’ s musical composition. I hope I will see you all there. See the opera [pageend6]Rí^en - October 1980 Věstník Sokola San Francisco Strana- Page 7 program for dates of the "Jenufa" performance. The foreman rebukes him and says Steva has not been taken by the army. Laca is even more jealous. Steva eventually appears, very drunk. Jenufa tries to bring him around, but, instead, he boasts of his conquests with other women. Kostelnická appears, warning her.rich nephew Steva that unless he stops drinking for one year he cannot marry Jenufa. Grandmother sends Steva to bed and consoles Jenufa about life’s heartbreak. Alone, Jenufa tells Steva of her love for him, but he only vaguely comprehends in his stupor. Eventually, Jenufa is left alone and is joined by Laca, who provokes her by pinning to her dress flowers given to Steva by another admirer. Jenufa maintains her dignity; then Laca, very jealous, pretends to embrace her but slashes her across the cheek with a knife. Act II. Jenufa is sitting in the living room of Kostelnická’s house. He is now in love and plans to marry Karolka, the mayor’s daughter. When he leaves, Laca appears and says he wants to marry Jenufa, but is dismayed by the news of the birth.
