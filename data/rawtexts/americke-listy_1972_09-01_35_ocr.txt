That event, of course, was the invasion of Czechoslovakia by the combined force of between 500,000 and 750,000 troops, of which at least three- fourths were Soviet, the remainder being East German, Polish, Hungarian and Bulgarian. Mr. President, it was with anguish and despair that the people of Czechoslovakia first heard word of the invasion as the reform-minded Czech leadership made this announcement over the local airwaves at 1:50 a.m. on August 21, 1968. The events which took place in Czechoslovakia 4 years ago can not be simply dismissed as part of past history. The full force of those events are still with us. The most visible manifestation of that force is the series of trials that have been taking place since mid-July throughout Czechoslovakia. As of the end of last week, 46 Czechs—all of whom had been involved in Alexander Dubcek’s attempt to give a Communist system a “human face”—have been tried and found guilty of subversion. Ostensibly, they are being brought to trail for the “criminal acts” of printing and cir- culatin leaflets advising the Czech citizens of their constitutional rights in last November’s parlamentary elections. The leaflets advised that voters could cross out names from the printed ballots, write other in, or simply not vote. In reality, Mi'. President, these trials are directed at intimidating the intellectuals who as a body were in the forefront of Dubcek’s democratizing movement. And this effort has been successful. There is fear among the ranks of the intellectuals, the flow of underground publications and journals has dried up almost completely. ( Mr. President, over the last 4 years, nearly 40 percent of Czech journalists have been purged from the profession. In addition, tens of thousands of Dubček supporters, both Communist and ordinary citizens, have been deprived of work for their political convictions. Writers have been con- demmned to silence, theaters have been closed, famous directors are either out of work, or out of the country. The reality of the invasion lives today. It lives in the 80,000 Soviet troops who are still in Czechoslovakia, 4 years after the invasion. But the living reality of the invasion has implications that directly effect both us and other nations. Most striking—and one of immediate importance to us especially in light of the recent initiatives towards detente—is the timing of the invasion. ’ The Czech leadership was pressured into a meeting with the entire Soviet Politburo at Čierna, in Czechoslovakia, at the end of July 1968. The meeting lasted 3 days and the two countries agreed not interfere in each others affairs. This agreement took place just 3 weeks before that actual invasion, and it can be argued, therefore, that the most dangerous period in any confrontation with the Soviet Government is likely to be immediately after a political agreement has been reached. , Mr. President, there is one final implication of the invasion which we should consider. And that is the consideration as to why the Soviet Union found it necessary to extinguished the Czech experiment by force of arms. The Soviet Union itself has partly answered that question. According to the first Tass statement on the invasion made on August 21: Party and governmental leaders of the Czechoslovak Socialist Republic have asked the Soviet Union and other allied states to render the fraternal Czechoslovak people urgent assistance, including assistance with armed forces. A full 4 years after the invasion, those Czech party and government leaders who had supposedly requested the invasion have yet to be identified. Despite the fact that the invasion has consistently been portrayed in the Soviet and Eastern European press as a high example of Socialist unity and courage, the “heroes” who originally requested it have not been publicly honored, nor has anyone laid claim to such honor. With Alexander Dubcek’s program we find not merely another experiment, a tactical adjustment to insure continued control, but a fundamental commitment to a complete and definite change. Dubcek’s reforms did not challenge the socialist direction of the country, nor was Czechoslovakia’s position within the Warsaw Pact ever doubted.
