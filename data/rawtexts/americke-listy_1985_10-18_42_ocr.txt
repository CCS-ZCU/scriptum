The "Railroad Safety Improvement Act” is especially important in states like New Jersey where raill lines — crucial to the state’s industries — are in close proximity to large population centers. I was proud to support this vital safety legislation. The Federal Railroad Administration is charged with is especially important in states like New Jersey where rail lines, and promulgates and enforces rules to carry this out. As part of this charge, it inspects railroads for violations of safety laws and regulations. As one who has called upon the Federal Railroad Administration’s Office of Safety for periodic rail line inspections in our area, 1 know the importance of these programs — which had been targeted for elimination in the Administration’s budget proposal for fiscal year 1986. Throughout my tenure in the Congress. I have worked to maintain close relations with this vital agency. The report accompanying the bill, which reflects the Energy and Commerce Committee’s review of the legislation, notes that alcohol and drug abuse may be implicated in recent rail accidents. The Railroad Safety Improvement Act would require the Secretary of Transportation to issue a final rulemaking to ensure the prevention of alcohol and drug use in railroad operations. This lagislation also requires the Federal Railroad Administration to examine the maintenance and testing of grade crossing devices. Safety at grade crossings is a matter of great concern because these crossings account for the largest number of fatalities among rail accidents. The Railroad Safety Improvement Act is important to our district because we depend upon the transportation, by rail, of both people and freight. Consequently, our district is crisscrossed by many miles of tracks, often times in very populous areas. I strongly supported this measure, which will maintain and improve upon our existing rail safety program.
