CONFERENCE PARTICIPANTS: Leo Baca has published historical and genealogical works related to Czech-Americans, including Czech Immigration Passenger Lists, and has lectured widely on the subject of Czech immigration to Texas. Ronald Bartek, whose experience includes service on the staff of the House Armed Services Committee, the Department of State, and the Central Intelligence Agency, is a director of the American Friends of the Czech Republic and represents Mehl & Associates, an international business development company. Peter M. Bisek is the publisher and editor of the Czech-American independent bi-weekly Americké Listy (formerly Československý týdeník}. Dagmar Bradacova is director of the Czechoslovak Heritage Museum, Library, and Archives at the Headquarters of CSA Fraternal Life in Berwyn, Illinois. Retta Chandler, a public school teacher from DeSoto, Texas, is vice-president of the Czech Ex-Student Association of Texas and chairman of the board of directors of the Texas Czech Heritage and Cultural Center. David Chroust, who specializes in ethnic libraries and Germanic and Slavic collections, is a librarian at Texas A&M University, where he is currently building a Czech collection. Robert W. Doubek is president of the American Friends of the Czech Republic. 4 [pageend4]Ludmila Dutkova is working toward her doctorate in Second Language Acquisition and Teaching at the University of Arizona in Tucson. Michelle Fagan, Librarian at the University of Nebraska at Lincoln, is in charge of the major Czech collection built there by Joseph Svoboda. Bruce Garver is a professor of history at the University of Nebraska-Omaha whose works on Czech history include The Young Czech Party, 1884-1901: The Emergence of a Multi-Party System. Thomas G. Gibian, a retired chemical engineer and business executive whose positions included president and CEO of Henkel Corporation, the US subsidiary of the German-based multinational health-care and chemical firm, is a director of the Societe de Chimie Indus- trielle, American Section, and two firms specializing in biomedical technology. Milan Hauner, a historian who holds doctoral degrees from both Charles and Cambridge universities, has published books and articles on South and Central Asia, the Prague Spring, and other topics. George Hlavinka has been instrumental in the development of the Burleson County Heritage Museum in Caldwell, Texas, and is currently serving as president of the museum organization. Daniel Hrna, a Houston attorney, is the legal advisor and a member of the Board of Trustees of the Czech Heritage Society of Texas, and he is President of the Harris County Chapter. Zdenek Hruban is a retired professor of pathology from the University of Chicago, where he established the Archives of the Czechs and Slovaks Abroad. Robert Janak, a public school teacher from Beaumont, is a member of the Board of Trustees of the Czech Heritage Society of Texas and the author of several publications on the Czech heritage of Texas. Vladimir M. Kabes, an American specialist in international law who for many years held the position of Secretary-General of the International Touring Alliance in Geneva, Switzerland, and has served as a foreign advisor to President Vaclav Havel, now owns a business consulting firm. Edward Kasinec, head of the Slavic and Baltic Division at the New York Public Library, has published numerous reference works on the New York collections as well as studies in Russian bibliography. Roger Kollar is a professional architect whose firm of Cotera, Kolar and Negrete specializes in educational and institutional design and has projects throughout central and south Texas. Miroslav Koudelka is a Czech genealogist from Olomouc whose books include Czechoslovakia: A Short Chronicle of 27,094 Days and an English translation of Jan Habe- nicht’s History of Czechs in America. Sylvia Laznovsky is president of the American Sokol Organization-Southern District. Clinton Machann, professor of English at Texas A&M University, is chairman of the Board of Directors of The Czech Educational Foundation of Texas and the author of several books and articles about Texas Czechs. Daniela Sipkova-Mahoney of Portland, Oregon is an artist and publisher of children’s books as well as business consultant who specializes in foreign language services. Carolyn Sumbera Meiners is secretary and publicity chair of the Texas Czech Heritage and Cultural Center. Leonard Mikeska is vice-president of The Slavonic Benevolent Order of the State of Texas. Julie Novotna is a Czech librarian who worked with scientific collections in various institutions before immigrating to Toronto, where she has worked with the collections at the Masaryk Institute since 1991. 5 [pageend5]Milada Polisenska, a Czech historian and currently a visiting professor of history at Texas Tech University, recently has completed a book manuscript on Czechoslovak citizens deported to Soviet labor camps (1945-1950) and co-edited a collection of documents on early Czechoslovak foreign policy. Miloskjv Rechcigl, Jr., a retired scientist, former research director for the US Agency for International Development, and author of numerous publications on early Czech immigrations to America, was one of the founders and serves as the current president of the Czechoslovak Society of Arts and Sciences. Vera Marie Rollerova, a journalist and author of short stories, is manager of the Masaryk Institute in Toronto and chief editor of the journal Novy domov. Effie Rosene is Chairman of the Board of Directors of the Czech Cultural and Community Center of Houston. Miluse Saskova-Pierce is professor of Linguistics at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln. Bohuše Serych, whose positions included researcher for Czech Television and director of human services for one of the Prague districts, is now retired and works in a Prague district library. Robert L. Skrabanek, a retired professor of sociology from Texas A&M University, is the author of We’re Czechs and several journal articles on the role of ethnicity in social change. Woody Smith, who was recently elected president of Texans of Czech Ancestry, is the Russian language program coordinator in the Department of Modern Languages at Southwest Texas State University. Miroslav Synek is a physicist who works as an independent consultant and researcher in the San Antonio area. Jaromir J. Ulbrecht, a chemical engineer and technology consultant who held positions in the Department of Commerce and worked with the Industrial Research Institute and various Fortune 500 Companies before establishing his own firm, O.F.I. Technology Services, was elected Foreign Member of the Academy of Engineering of the Czech Republic in 1996. Alexandr Vondra, a Czech diplomat, journalist, and geographer who was one of the signatories of Charter 77, a co-founder of the Civic Forum, and an advisor to President Vaclav Havel, is the Ambassador of the Czech Republic to the United States. Martha Wilding is the first professional librarian at the National Czech and Slovak Museum at Cedar Rapids, Iowa. Ladislav Zezula is President of the Bexar County Chapter of the Czech Heritage Society of Texas and a member of the Czech Community Advisory Committee for the Institute of Texan Cultures in San Antonio. Žigmund Freud se narodil 6. května roku 1856 v moravském městě Příboru, v židovské rodině, která se později přestěhovala do Vídně. Zde Freud vystudoval a zahájil soukromou praxi. Psychoanalýza dr. Freuda vycházela z francouzské psychiatrické školy a zakládala se na technice volných asociací a výkladu snů. Ve svém stěžejním díle Výklad snů ukázal, že sny jsou, podobně jako neurózy, projevy potlačených tužeb sexuálního původu. Z psychoanalytického hlediska vytvořil i teorii o kultuře a náboženství. Dcera k tomu dodala: Představ si tu náhodu, bratr mé dobré přítelkyně je tč. starostou v Příboře. Svět je malý. Olomoučany bude zajímat, že dr. Freud vojančil v jejich městě, a to od 11. srpna do 9. září 1886, coby c.k. vrchní lékař u domobraneckého praporu 17. V análech praporu byl záložník Freud hodnocen velmi dobře, hůř už na tom byli jeho představení, jak o tom svědčí dopis, který Freud napsal svému příteli 1. září 1886 do Vídně. Vizme, co píše: „Hrajeme si na válku, jednou dokonce na stav obležení — při tom představuji zdravotníka (na vojně jsme jim říkali ,lapiduši‘ aneb ,nosiči vápna na mrtvoly4) a rozděluji štítky, na nichž jsou označena různá zranění. Když můj prapor nastupuje do útoku, jsem se svými saniťáky, střílí se slepými náboji a generál, který jede kolem, volá — pěšáci, pěšáci, kde by ste byli, kdyby měli ostré, nebyla by tady živá duše.“ Jen jedna věc je v Olomouci obstojná — kavárna se zmrzlinou, novinami a výbornými zákusky. Obsluha ale podléhá vojenské atmosféře, jako ostatně všechno zde. Jednou jsem se ale rozhodl k zoufalému pokusu a chytil jsem číšníka za frak s voláním — „člověče, já budu taky někdy generálem, přineste mi proboha už konečně alespoň sklenici vody!“ Před nastoupením vojenského cvičení, od října 1885 do února 1886, působil dr. Jan Zahradníček Prize for Czech Poetry was established in Los Angeles under the auspices of Czechoslovak Culture Club, Los Angeles in 1981. It was the first literary fund established solely for excellence in Czech poetry, in Czechoslovakia, as well as abroad, that based its decisions on non-political, artistic literary criteria and free, independent scholarship officially nonexistent in former Czechoslovakia, with the exception of a brief period in 1968, from 1948 until 1989. The award, given every two years, is announced on January 17, Jan Zahradníček’s birthday. The recipient receives a diploma by Czech artist George Karger as well as monetary honorarium of US $1000. The following Czech poets were honored in the past: Ivan Jelinek-1985, Ivan Diviš-1987, Karel Jan Kryl-1989, Zdeněk Rotrekl-1991, Rio Preisner-1993, and František Daniel Merth-1995.
