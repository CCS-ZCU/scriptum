Janis Andrups 131 Notes and Reviews : Meeting in Rome..................................Paul Tabori The Joseph Leftwich Appeal Book reviews by Alastair Guinan, Elizabeth Jennings and Ignazio Silone 140 Le Vieux Tzigane Our contributors 144 Arena News [pageend2]ARENA GAIN AND LOSS In the fourth issue of our review my friend and double colleague, Ivan Jelinek—my companion in poetry and in exile, not to mention, in addition to the three dimensions of space, the fourth dimension of time and the fifth of the subconscious—wrote a very fine article entitled “Loss and Gain’ about all the things a poet gains and loses when he leaves his country. Jelinek’s article was not only beautiful but true. I agree with almost all of his statements and it would need some close pedantic scrutiny to pick out a sentence or two with which I could argue. And yet: though I agree with every detail of the article, I cannot accept its entirety. I contest not so much the final conclusion of the piece as its last echoing tones. According to his conclusion the exile poet sings for the dead stones of the desert; but the note he ends on proclaims that he who lives without a country, without readers and followers, that is, without inspiration, themes and company, cannot really remain a poet—or can only remain one with great difficulty. After reading Jelinek’s article I decided to enumerate the great exiles of world literature from Aeschy- los loho fled to Sicily, to Leonidas of Tarent, from Ovid, Seneca and Ausonius to the heretic Gottschalk, from al-Mutamid, King of Seville, through Ibn Amar el Andalusi, Dante, Mickiewicz, Heine, Victor Hugo and Ibanez to the exiles of yesterday and today—• to prove that exile may be painful but it is by no 1 [pageend3]means barren. But the publication of the fifth issue of Arena fortunately freed me from this rather academic task. For in this issue my colleague Jelinek proved not with arguments (for it is well-known that formal logic cannot be applied to aesthetics) but with three masterly poems that it is possible to remain a true poet even in exile. After such a triple score against his own side, I would consider it unnecessary to add a single word to the problem raised by Ivan Jelinek. After all, it is the practical result that matters; as for a possible argument or dispute between us, I am resigned to sharing the opinion of the French writer who was a passionate partisan of Madame Bovary but unable to agree with the aesthetic opinions of its author; for this reason he compared Flaubert (and generally all good writers) to generals who knew little about strategy but always won their battles. I cannot argue therefore with Jelinek’s article as I share his opinion in everything he writes. Obviously we disagree in the things he does not write about. It is quite true that the exile poet has no readers, that he is lonely, that he is cut off from his only and true community and that he is unhappy, however free he may be. But the poets in our homelands have even fewer readers and if they serve the regime, they are even lonelier. For the exile, at least, lives in the community of the ostracized while those who remained at home are expelled by all communities whatever kind of prisoners they may be and therefore they are even more miserable. The exile poet sets goals for himself which are very very difficult to achieve; while the poet at home is set goals by others which are impossible to attain. According to my experience it 2 [pageend4]is a bitter fate to be an exile but to live as a poet in a dictatorship is far more bitter—quite independently of the fact what one thinks or does not think of the Communist regime. Let me sum up these experiences in a few brief comments. Court poetry under Stalin. Virgil, as he lived in the age of Augustus, was forced to trace the descent of the Gens Julia from Goddess Venus; Ariosto wove a bathetic passage about the Estes into his Orlando Furioso and Shakespeare wrote a very bad play about Henry VIII. But the divine descent of the Gens Julia does not harm the Aeneid, the eulogy of the Estes has not ruined Orlando Furioso and Shakespeare could have written a good play about Henry VIII. Yet in the Soviet Union and in the satellite countries not a single tolerable poem has ever been written about Stalin and his satraps. Why not! Because Augustus, Alphonso d’Este and Queen Elizabeth I desired that their poets should describe them as great and powerful. This was, sure enough, not a poetic subject, but it was true. Stalin primarily demanded of his poets to sing of his kindness, his nobility, his wisdom. Rakosi whose head was like an ostrich, egg, demanded of one of Hungary’s greatest poets a poem about his, Rakosi’s, manly beauty—and in addition to praise him as the greatest poet. It is easy to exaggerate, to lie, to flatter. If I am commanded to describe the sunset on the North Pole (though 1 have never seen it) I might be able to solve my task very well. But if the demand is made to describe in detail how the sun set on the North Pole and the lower it dipped under the horizon, the lighter the icefloes became—I break into a cold sweat. The same thing happens if I have to write a novel about the happy life of Communist Hungary, while outside the window of my Budapest flat, down 3 [pageend5]in the street, two hundred shabbily dressed and tired women, stand in line since eight o’clock in the morning to buy a little meat at the butcher’s. To put the obvious opposite of reality into words is impossible; it is not only morality that revolts against it but the material itself. The ideological dilema. The poet’s revolt against ideology is only the second step. It is almost negligible compared to the first—the ideology’s revolt against the poet. Socialist realism, mutati® mutandis, is by no means a new phenomenon; it is extremely old. For instance in the sixth century and after profane poetry, erotic poems, were hardly persecuted with less fervour than under Stalin. The theologians prescribed what poetry should be like. Its theme had to be taken from the Holy Writ and even the poet’s adjectives should be borrowed from it; its form should be iambic verse of four feet, grouped in four-line stanzas—that is, the Ambrosian. The obligatory optimistic ending was also prescribed: “Hail to the Father, Hail to the Son and also to Thee, balsamic Holy Ghost, century after century, millenium after millenium. . .” Jacopone da Todi, Thomasius a Celano, Pierre Abélard and others wrote magnificent hymns—these are integral parts of world literature. But they wrote at a time when the Church no longer insisted upon controlling the visions of the poets and therefore the poets were no longer obliged to put the decisions of the oecumenical councils into verse. Perhaps such an epoch is closer today behind the Iron Curtain than it was under Stalin-—but it has by no means arrived yet. Once, before Stalin’s seventieth birthday, I decided to attempt this vile self-humiliation and write a hymn to the Russian dictator. I sweated for days but it was no go; finally, to my great relief, I produ4 [pageend6]ced a ruthless satire against him, within twenty minutes. Then I hurried to the nearest library, ashed for the bound, copy of a hundred-year-old newspaper and, starting at the back of the volume, I underlined, single letters which, read consecutively, added up to the text of my poem. On my way home I tore the manuscript into shreds and dropped the pieces one by one into the open manholes—while reflecting upon my misery. But then I thought: what would have happened if I had written the hymn of praise1? I would be looking into the mirror, making grimaces, putting out my tongue at myself and would be a thousand times more miserable. Because the tongue in the mirror would not be my own; the image would, be that of the triumphant ideology settled in my triumphantly trepaned head. Moral and immoral solitude. In the next phase the poet in the Communist countries either writes for his desk drawer; later he does not write at all, only thinking that he might write a poem; and still later he does not even think of writing (this state is far more miserable and barren than any form of exile); or he goes on writing poems, following the set rules. At first he might even believe that he is writing for the people; later he is consoled by the thought that he is writing for an élite and the loathsome, rotten Fascist masses do not understand, him; then he realizes that there is no such élite. Finally he discovers with a shock that nobody reads him. I have a couple of dozen readers even in exile; a formerly outstanding poet, who became a militant Communist, complained to me bitterly in 1954 that of his volume of poetry, printed in 10,400 copies and rewarded by a huge payment, only a single copy was sold in the, bookshops. The others were distributed between libraries and various organisations—by official decree. “As if I had been, 5 [pageend7]talking into a dead microphone,” he wailed. About this time he changed his views and after that he wrote not only honest (this is not particularly interesting) but good poetry. But these good poems no longer found any readers. He only regained his popularity at the time of the Hungarian Revolution in which he played a leading part. But for this poetry was no longer sufficient; it needed actions. The solitude of the poets at home is much more cruel because they have become lonely in the midst of their own people and in this solitude they cannot even console themselves with the narcotics of morality. This does not mean that exile is not hell—but at least it is Dante’s passionate and clamorous inferno while that in our home countries is a hypocritical, pseudopuritan hell devouring its victims in haughty silence; they cannot even complain about their fate. George Faludy 6 [pageend8]PEN IN EXILE Lithuanians ALEKSIS RANNIT LES “DAÏNOS” ET L’AME DE LA LITHUANIE La Lithuanie possède un trésor extraordinairement grand de chants populaires aujourd’hui encore vivants (Lietvviu Tautosakos Archyvas a réuni, jusqu’à la seconde guerre mondiale, 225.000 Lieder accompagnés de 45.000 mélodies). Lessing, Goethe, Herder, Jacob Grimm, Chamisso et Dehmel ont été charmés par le sens profond de ces chants populaires qu’ils appèlent “Daïnos”. O. V. de L.-Milosz, l’éminent poète français d’origine lithuanienne, a transposé en français une série de ces Leder. Le poète russe Balmont, mort en exil, les a également traduits en russe, et nous voyons quelle profondeur d’expression philosophique, métaphysique et cosmique des choses ce simple peuple de paysans peut atteindre. Ces lieder ont inspiré à Lermontov, le Byron russe, une de ses plus belles œuvres. Dams son poème in- tulé “La Lithuanienne” il dit: “Ce n’est qu’un Lithuanie qu’on chante avec autant de douceur”. Celui qui n’a jamais entendu de lieder lithuaniens ne peut imaginer quels bienfaits émanent, comme des rayons, de ces chants populaires. Plus ces derniers sont anciens, plus ils se rapprochent du plain-chant grégorien qui confère à plusieurs d’entre eux une résonance orientale, ce qui confirmanait le fait que ce people aurait vécu jadis au bord du Gange. Sur d’iautre lieder, qui se servent de strophes la plus-part du temps simplement construites et ancestrales, règne 7 [pageend9]une mélodie rappelant un peu le chant grec. Si on compare par example ces lieder aux chants populaires de l’Europe occidentale, on remarque combien moins qu’eux ils s’apparentent à la musique classique. Dans leur “sauvage” et croissante vitalité ils n’ont presque rien de la musique classique a ses débuts ; aussi leur caractère mystique est-il à la fois si tendre et si fort, et la nostalgie dont ils sont lourds est-elle si profonde. “Singulier race, en vérité, que celle des Lithuaniens! — dit Constantin Balmont. — “Peu nombreuse, mais certainement appelée à jouer un rôle important en Europe Orientale. S’enfermant, après un glorieux passé, dans les limites modernes étroites, elle y cultivée aujourd’hui comme hier, un des beaux langages de ce monde!” Le folklore lithuanien porte l’empreinte d’une haute antiquité. Comme toute les manifestations d’art très primitives, il est tout ensemble simplicité et subtilité extrêmes. Voici un example de ses balbutiements les plus archaïques: Dans la prime tiédeur vernale, Le Croissant a pris la route solaire pour femme. L’épouse claire, fort matinale, Cherche en vain dans le ciel son nocturne conjoint. L’époux, errant a l’aventure, Rencontre l’Etoile du matin. Furieux, Perkunas, le maître du Tonnerre, Fend l’infidèle en deux. Comment as-tu osé abandonner ma fille, Quelle folie est la tienne d’errer comme tu fais Et de t’éprendre de la Matinale? Profonde est la douleur dans mon âme de dieux. (Traduit par O. V. de L.-Mïlosz) Jacob Grimm écrit en 1826 au sujet des “Daïnos”: “On remarque que le lied et le chant vivent encore, peuvent à chaque occasion grandir de nouveau et 8 [pageend10]ne sont jamais privés d’une certaine noblesse et de règles dont manque précisément ce qui chez nous en Allemagne porte encore le nom de chant populaire.” Mais il faut réussir à entendre ces lieder dans le texte original, sans chercher à les harmoniser. Plusieurs compositeurs et dirigeants de chœurs, en essayant de les adapter leur ont prêté un caractère trompeur. Extraits d’une série de chants populaires ils sont devenus romances et lieder slaves. Les prétendus “Raudos” sont pour la plupart à une seule voix, sans accompagnement; ils ne connaissent ni mesure ni mouvement et forment librement leur rythme en liant les tons accentués à ceux qui ne le sont pas. Cette musique aspire à traduire immédiatement et avec des accents expressifs des sentiments douloureux. Mais les “Daïnos” sont aussi pénétrés d’un désir et d’une souffrance obscurs du sentiment du beau, il y a très peu de chants populaires joyeux en Lithuanie; la joi débordante est bannie de ces lieder dont la polyphonie est cependant beaucoup plus riche, et dont l’harmonie chantante possède une intimité infiniment plus subtile et plus tendre que tous les autres. Les sentiments ne se poursuivent pas, les voix ne tombent pas dans les oppositions, comme c’est le cas pour les chants populaires russes. Le chant populaire lithuanien offre toujours de nouveau le sentiment d’une force peu commune, de la mélancolie douloureuse et de la résignation qui en deviennent le critère. Plongé dans les étendues, illimitées comme leur pays, de ce sentiment, l’homme a conscience d’être extrêmement petit et complètement perdu. L’éternité le contemple avec tranquilité et majesté et l’arrache à la terre. Ainsi naît du plus profond lointain la religion de cette poésie et de ce chant qui domine également toute la literature lithuanienne. Pour comprendre les chants populaires lithuaniens 9 [pageend11]on doit saisir le sens de la spiritualité des Lithuaniens. L’âme pour eux dépasse les réalités matérielles. Leur art révèle qu’ils sont plus aptes à se fondre dans un monde intérieur qu’à prendre seulement le monde extérieur pour modèle. Ce dernier n’est qu’un moyen. Pour ce people l’homme est ď abord un être qui sent et ensuite un être qui pense. Esprit analytique et esprit systématique n’importent pas au Lithuanien qui tient au contraire les mythes, les rêves et la poésie pure pour les seules révélations valables de la vérité. Ses créations, de nature lyrique, s’opposent formellement au rationalisme. Ce n’est pas la domination de soi mais le don de soi, ce n’est pas la tension mais l’abandon qui sont pour eux les qualités dominantes. L’absence de règles chez les Lithuaniens se marque déjà dans les changements de tons si vifs de la langue lithuanienne et qui en rendent la connaissance si difficile pour l’étranger. Le Lithuanien vit avec une confiance inconsciente en toutes choses. Il laisse irrésistiblement jaillir ses penchants naturels qui ressemblent souvent à des chevaux sauvages refusant de se soumettre à la raison. La conscience lithuanienne actualise les sentiments mais ne les contrôle pas. Il n’y a pas là une dictature, d’ailleurs inutile, de l’entendement; les dictatures sont toujours le signe de la misère. Un organisme sain n’en a pas besoin et ne la supporte pas. Parce que le Lithuanien n’éprouve pas d’angoisse il n’a pas besoin de se défendre par le contrôle de la conscience. C’est pourquoi la vie lithuanienne est exubérante, prenant en elle un essor ailé, moins entravée que la vie en Europe occidentale. Elle est transportée par le torrent puissant de la vie qu’aucun barrage ne reserre. Le charme du naturel distingue le Lithuanien, ce qui rencontre rarement a l’ouest. On trouve la plus belle simplicité chez le paysan. 10 [pageend12]Il est capable de partager joie et souffrance. Il prend part au préoccupations spirituelles de son prochain comme s’il les éprouvait lui-même. Il sent immédiatement la vie d’autrui. Son premier principe s’appelle sympathie et confiance. Il croit à la bonté de son prochain jusqu’à ce qu’on lui fournisse la preuve du contraire, mais souvent bien au-delà même. Le Lithuanien n’est pas seulement toujours prêt à apporter une aide bénévole mais il sait aussi donner sans blesser. Sa délicatesse pleine de tact, la chaleur avec laquelle il porte secours, enlèvent à son offrande cet égoisme oppressant qui gâte la plupart, du temps les dons eux-mêmes. Les Lithuaniens ont le cœur joyeux d’un des peoples les plus hospitaliers de la terre. L’hôte est sacré pour eux; ils lui offre ce qu’ils ont de plus précieux. Je désirairais souligner en particulier la merveilleuse féminité de l’âme du Lithuanien: sa façon de donner, sa vénération, son humilité, sa patience et sa foi ainsi que le principe de la plénitude organique ont un caractère féminin. Les cultures qui présentent cet élément féminin sont en particulier les plus riches, les plus fécondes, les plus artistiques, les mieux douées du sens de la beauté. Combien de beauté s’est répandue sur le continent indien, sur la Grèce et la France. La Lithuanie est le pays de possibilités spirituelles illimitées. Mais l’essence de ce people demeure toujours, dans la synthèse spirituelle et dans le domaine éthique, la conciliation et la fraternité. Le mouvement de la vie s’accomplit chez lui du dedans vers le dehors. D’une façon permanente son monde inferieur se déverse dans le monde qui l’environne, de la même façon qu’il s’identifie à lui. Aussi ce qu’il exprime délivre-t-il les forces de son âme. Sous ce jour on comprend alors l’âme dévouée et la merveilleuse contemplation des “Daïnos”. 11 [pageend13]ALFONSAS NYKA-NILIUNAS LITHUANIAN LITERATURE IN EXILE Exile writers and exile literatures are not creations of our age alone. Throughout history, writers had been compelled to defend their freedom, their human rights and, often, even their lives by leaving their homeland. Their list comprises Ovid, Victor Hugo, Adam Mickiewicz and countless other famous names. It is the very concept of exile that has changed today. What formerly had been a heroic existence, with a romantic halo, has been converted today into a commonplace everyday phenomenon. To employ a paradox, exile has become the fate of almost all contemporary men, because every one of us is exiled somewhere, is being persecuted in some manner. In the summer of 1944, when Soviet armies were approaching the borders of Lithuania, about two thirds of all Lithuanian writers-—some acting upon a conscious decision, others to avoid physical destruction—fled westward. This was the first exodus of such scope in the history of Lithuanian literature. Among those who fled were such famous poets as Mykolas Vaitkus, Faustas Kirsa, Stasys Santvaras, Bernardas Brazdzionia, H. Radauskas, as well as noted prose writers: Vincas Krévé, P. Tarulis, Vincas Ramonas, Liudas Dovydenas, Jurgis Jankus, Pulgis Andriusis, Stepas Zobarskas, etc. They were joined by the writers who at the time of Lithuania’s annexation were living abroad: Vydunas, Ignas Seinius, Jurgis Savickis, Juozas Tysliava, Jonas Aistis, Antanas Vaiciulaitis. In the early period of exile, before the emigration overseas, the mood prevailed among the Lithuanian exile writers that their departure was only temporary 12 [pageend14]and that the return to the homeland was just around the corner. This mood was nourished by the hope for an eventual decisive confrontation of the former East- West Allies. Consequently, almost all the refugee writers were continuing the literary traditions created in Lithuania, recreating* the institutions that were liquidated by the new regime in the homeland, using the old titles, and scrupulously maintaining the hierarchical gradation based on earlier merits and exploits. In the beginning, this unconditional faith in a rapid return, and the enthusiasm generated by this faith, inspired considerable output, although more in the realm of sheer numbers than of quality. The numerous publications, the unusually large circulations (in comparison with the limited number of prospective buyers) and the variety of literary periodicals— all these are witnesses of the initial enthusiasm. Yet as the years in the displaced persons’ camps went on and the hope for a swift homecoming began to vanish, the fictional character of the entire situation emerged in all clarity, and emigration was recognized as the only solution. The impact of this disillusionment led to a disintegration of the ideals based on the hopes for a rapid return and on enthusiasm resulting thereof. To this must be added a purely economical factor—the currency reform in Germany which inflicted a mortal blow to the publishing houses and periodicals. From the esthetic point of view, literature in the initial exile period—in spite of the radically new situation—was almost absolutely dominated by the ultra-patriotic school which was making every effort to maintain the status quo ante and which regarded itself not merely a part of Lithuanian letters, but Lithuanian literature as such; it extolled patriotism 13 [pageend15]not only as the highest virtue, but also, to a certain degree, as an esthetic norm of literary creation. This transformation of patriotism into an absolute value (typical for an exile’s psychology and, perhaps, in a certain sense, historically justifiable) was greeted most favorably by the majority of exile writers and readers who had lost the foundations of their existence ; patriotism thus acquired the function of a bastion in the chain of collapsing hopes and shattered expectations. But a severe and active opposition to this trend came from the youngest generation of writers, who developed their art and came into the limelight already under conditions of exile. Having “lost less”—both sentimentally and materially—these young writers were inclined to treat their deprivation on a purely human level. Their opposition gave birth to several typical dialogues (a man conscious of his situation vs. a hyper-patriot; intellectual openness vs. national seclusion), which formed the background for the truly emigrational period of Lithuanian exile literature, from about 1950 onwards. The writers of the independence period, or of the generation preceding it, had reached the zenith of their creativity at the time of their departure from Lithuania. In their exile output, some of them managed to maintain their former level, while others obviously regressed. At any rate, their works belong to the pre-exile literature. The exceptions here are the prose writers Vincas Ramonas and Pulgis An- driusis, whose most mature works were published in exile, and, especially, the poet Henrikas Radauskas, whose last two volumes of verse place him in the very first ranks of Lithuanian poetry. During the last decade many of the younger Lithuanian writers in exile have gathered around the magazine Literaturos Lankai (Literary Sheets), 14 [pageend16]edited iu tlie USA and printed in Buenos Aires. Tlie main collaborators of the magazine were poets: Juozas Kekstas, a revolutionary cosmopolite and a perennial exile ; Kazys Bradunas, the bard of soil and mythology; Henrikas Nagys, a representative of emotional expressionism; Vladas Slaitas, the singer of the condition of exile; Jonas Mekas, a modern primitivist; Jurgis Blekaitis and Leonas Betas, explorers of linguistic alchemy ; as well as the very youngest—Alfonsas Gricius, Eugenijus Gruodis, Algimantas Miackus. Among the prose writers, Antanas Skema and Algirdas Landsbergis have made the largest contribution to the modernization of the newest Lithuanian fiction, both with regard to its content and form; there are also the chroniclers of the traditional Lithuanian village, Marius Katiliskis and Antanas Baranauskas, the prolific story-teller Aloyzas Baronas, and others Exile saw some remarkable achievements in drama which, with the exceptions of Vincas Krévé and Balys Sruoga, had never been a genre of great solidity in Lithuanian literature. The main contribution here was made by the already mentioned Antanas Skema and Algirdas Landsbergis as well as Kostas Ostrauskas. (The author of this sketch, Alfonsas Nyka-Niliunas, is one of the outstanding Lithuanian poets and critics of the younger generation. — Editor.) TWO VOICES ARE STILLED ANTANAS SKEMA (1911—1961) FIRST REQUEST As one star falls, others remain aloft. They soar and await their fall. 15 [pageend17]A man dies, and the others say: “Thank God! It isn’t I.” A frog croaks in a marsh, her head thrown back —the dog lowers his own. (He cannot seize the frog.) When oranges ripen in the south, the Arctic boulders feel naked without moss. And in a glass a woman gazes at herself: “What color 'should I dye my hair, now it is gray?” she asks her wrinkles. Stars, people, frogs, dogs, oranges, moss, perhaps you will explain the sense of things to me. Translated by Mariejo Fonsale. JUOZÁS TYSLIAVA (1902—1961) SPRING The last day of April made her bed, As whole forests of cloud, capsizing, swayed in the West. With a moonbeam knife the night sliced The loaf of the sky, porous with stars. No herd of wild mustangs neighed in the prairies, No Mississippi in flood swept away the towns; A windmill, urged by the southwestern, rose like Christ, A windmill grinding grain on another planet. Filtering bird songs through a filter silence, The thunder of Spring will reverberate before cock-crow : 16 [pageend18]Maj- tlie birds then worship me like a lord, At whose command the earth trembles from morning till night. Translated by George Reavey. STEPAS ZOBARSKAS LA TABOURET DE MONSEIGNEUR Le charpentier Baltrunas avait près de soixante ans, mais il ne les marquait pas. Ses joues resplendissaient de santé et (contrairement à la plupart de ses contemporains) il n’était pas chauve, mais avait de beaux cheveux gris. Et lorsque les jours de fêtes il marchait sous un soleil éclatant en promenant la bannière tout autour de l’église, il sentait des centaines d’yeux fixés sur lui. Non sans un certain plaisir il pensait alors à tous ceux qui lui enviaient sa santé, et plus encore ses mains si habiles qui, d’un simple morceau de bois, pouvaient faire tant de merveilles. Baltrunas s’attachait rapidement à chaque prêtre qui venait à la paroisse, mais à aucun d’eux ne s’était-il tant attaché qu’à Monseigneur Labutis. Car Monseigneur Labutis possédait un cœur d’or, un cœur pur comme l’eau des sources et miséricordieux comme celui du Bon Samaritain. Ses cheveux étaient encore plus pâles que ceux du charpentier et lorsque, son chapeau dans la main, il traversait le cimetière de l’église, sa tête brillait au loin, telle une fleur de trèfle blanc. La terre même semblait s’illuminer en sa présence. Il n’avait qu’un seul défaut, si toutefois l'on puisse dire qu’un Monseigneur ait jamais une faiblesse: il était ridiculement petit. 17 [pageend19]Le charpentier n’était pas près d’oublier la première messe que Monseigneur Labutis célébra le jour de son arrivée à la paroisse. Il devait ouvrir la porte du tabernacle pour en retirer le Saint-Sacrement, mais il ne pouvait pas l’atteindre. Il se mit sur la pointe des pieds, s’étira de toutes ses forces — tant que son cou et ses oreilles devinrent aussi rouges que le feu —• mais il ne pouvait toujours pas atteindre le Saint-Sacrement. Ce jour là le sacristin était malade, et Baltrunas avait offert de le remplacer. Ne sachant comment aider le vieux prêtre, le charpentier fit vite un signe de croix, puis il s’empara de la première planche qu’il trouva sous sa main, et courut à l’autel. Il la glissa sous les pieds de Monseigneur et, comme elle était trop mince, il la supporta de dessous avec ses mains crispées. Monseigneur se balança dessus et, sans la moindre difficulté, saisit le Saint-Sacrement. GREIMAS RÉFLEXIONS SUR HENRIKAS RADAUSKAS ET SUR LA PLACE DE SA FLÈCHE DANS LE CIEL LITHUANIEN *) *) La Flèche dans le Ciel (Strélé danguje) est le titre du avant-dernier recueil de H. RADAUSKAS. Le texte que nous présentons ici est la traduction libre d’une esquisse critique parue en lithuanien dans Literatûros Lankai, No 4, à Buenos Aires. (NDR) La poésie à l’intérieur de laquelle on avait l’habitude de vivre en Lithuanie était la poésie des âmes. L’âme était tendue comme les cordes d’une guitare, et le poète y jouait toutes les gammes de sa sensibilité. L’âme du poète c’était aussi le miroir qui reflétait tous les univers. L’âme de Kossu, aliénée, de 28 [pageend30]bure de serf vêtue, l’âme de Brazdzionis, tendue vers Dieu, cruelle pour le monde et pour lui-même; l’âme de Boruta, criant la vengeance, cherchant désespérément la justice fraternelle des hommes. Et leur poésie n’était que l’entretien de l’âme avec le monde. L’originalité de RADAUSKAS: il nous propose une poésie de l’esprit. Il ne s’agit plus de l’homme sensibilisé par le monde, ni du monde qui aliène l’homme, ni même de leur dialogue. C’est l’homme et le monde recréés par l’esprit qui comme “Une flèche erre au milieu de froides constellations, “Et n’ose plus revenir.” Quand on parle de RADAUSKAS à un amateur de poésie, celui-ci le plus souvent ne le connaît même pas: panca panels. Ou bien on entend ce reproche: il est, vous dira-t-on, trop intelligent pour être un bon poète. Un professeur français, parlant de Verlaine, dirait plutôt le contraire: bien que ne brillant pas par une intelligence exceptionnelle, Verlaine est tout de même un vrai poète. Une différence de perspective, comme on le voit. Que le poète soit nécessairement un homme sensible est un préjugé romantique digne des lecteurs de Dely. Le corps et l’âme, la raison et le sentiment, ces dualismes sont marqués par la dent du temps. L’homme est grand globalement, par sa conception totale du monde. Dans les lettres lithuaniennes RADAUSKAS représente, de façon péremptoire, la poésie conçue comme la voie royale, consciente et autonome, de la connaissance du monde, comme un instrument de la recréation de ce monde. La poésie est pour lui un 29 [pageend31]langage que le poète se fabrique pour son propre usage. La réalité du monde, sa représentation orthoscopique ne sont que des illusions; seul ľ effort patient de se l’imaginer, de la rendre symboliquement, existe de tout temps. L’univers européen vieux de quatre cents ans, fondé tout entier sur la géométrie euclidienne est une vue de l’esprit, du même ordre que sa stylisation byzantine ou polynésienne. Notre monde à nous et la signification que nous lui donnons étant le résultat du choix humain, sa description et son organisation nécessitent a fortiori l’élaboration d’un code cohérent et valable. On peut se demander s’il existe dans les jardins lithuaniens un autre poète — je ne parle pas ici de la jeune génération — aussi sensible à cette inaborda- bilité, à cette opacité du monde. Aussi sensible et aussi conscient en même temps de cette ambiguïté tragique de la poésie qui est l’unique moyen de dire un monde indicible. Le monde et la poésie ne font qu’un pour lui et le nom qu’il donne à cet être ambivalent est légende. Il est impossible d’atteindre à la réalité, elle ne fait que jouer de ses reflets dans nos yeux. Le thème de réflexion — d’un monde réfléchi et d’un univers pensé —‘ est au cœur de sa poésie. Les bris de verre de couleur, seule beauté de ce monde, un espoir entretenu, mais condamné d’avance à l’échec, d’approcher la vérité, et, planant dans les éthers de ce monde absurde, le rire froid de Y ange: telle est la légende que nous conte, dans toutes ses variations, RADAUSKAS, une légende belle, triste, désespérée. * On reproche à RADAUSKAS son peu d’actualité, son refus de répondre aux événements douloureux 30 [pageend32](les autres n’étant pas poétiques) de l’heure présente: comme si l’on pouvait choisir, selon la forme et la couleur, parmi les innombrables objets éparpillés de par le monde et eu intégrer quelques-uns, sans plus, dans une vision poétique préarrangée. Le poète ne joue pas avec les objets, mais avec leurs équivalents symboliques. Et le gros lot qui parfois échoue au poète consiste dans l’établissement des rapports possibles entre ces équivalents, dans la construction d’un système d’expression autonome, dans la création, si- cut dii, d’un univers authentique et valable. La poésie peut être actuelle ou inactuelle. Seule la poésie actuelle vaut quelque chose. Son actualité toutefois est d’ordre global et non fragmentaire. Elle n’est jamais fonction de ce savoir-faire poétique qui consiste à coller aux événements du jour, à photographier les faits “réels”; bien au contraire, elle est le résultat de la correspondance qui s’établit entre la vision poétique et la problématique vécue de l’humanité à une époque donnée. L’essentiel, c’est qu’avant tout, la poésie doit être poétique. La poésie est un langage. Elle sert, comme n’importe quel langage, à communiquer avec autrui: elle doit être comprise d’un seul coup, globalement. Il n’y a là ni mystères, ni ésotérisme. Cependant, la langue poétique doit être apprise, comme on apprend l’anglais ou le français, comme on s’habitue à penser mathématiques ou philosophie. Et la tâche du critique littéraire n’est pas tellement différente de celle du professeur de langues étrangères. Il faudrait d’abord, évidemment, procéder à la description phénoménologique du vocabulaire, de la morphologie et de la syntaxe poétiques, mettre ensuite en évidence les valeurs verbales, en 'structurant la vision du monde radauskienne, en explicitant sa thématique essentielle 31 [pageend33](les thèmes de V ange, de Veau, du vent, etc.). La poésie de RADAUSKAS se prêterait admirablement à de tels exercices de critique structurale. Ou bien on pourrait, sans trop de difficultés, descendre aux enfers de l’inconscient, à la recherche non plus des superstructures de la vision du monde, mais afin d’y explorer les sources de son lyrisme à froid, d’y découvrir les formes élémentaires de son moi angoissé. L’œuvre de H. RADAUSKAS en sortirait agrandie, la critique littéraire lithuanienne aussi. L’actualité de RADAUSKAS: il est le produit pur de la période d’entre-deux-guerres, de cette brève époque de la Lithuanie indépendante, à l’intérieur de laquelle lui-même fait d’ailleurs époque. Les poètes qui écrivent, qui écriront après lui ne pourront faire d’œuvre valable qu’en le dépassant, non en le contournant. PRIESTLEY A VOLUNTARY EXILE There are many reasons why the news about my friend Runnerman, the short-story writer, should be so annoying. I call him my friend, although we have not met these many months and never did meet frequently, because we have no term that would indicate our exact relationship. We were warmer than acquaintances, but had not reached, perhaps never would have reached, the intimacy of real friendship. When we 69 [pageend71]did meet, which was rarely by design, I think we were always both glad to see one another, and always we settled down to a good ding-dong exchange of news and views. We liked one another’s work, too, at least I liked his, particularly those richly concrete stories in which a full glowing imagination brooded over an immense host of facts, stories of the English countryside, of ploughboys and tramps, that will yet give Runnerman (but that is not his name, of course) his niche. And now I hear that he is leaving his tiny cottage in the heart of an English wood (where many a tramp has found a night’s lodging and paid for it with a strange tale or even an odd image), and not even leaving it for London, though that would be bad enough, but leaving' it for the Continent, and for Vienna of all places. Runnerman living in Vienna ! I know no one more English, and hardly anyone whose work is more native to this soil. The farther he moves away from the lanes and meadows and woods of Berkshire and Oxfordshire the weaker his stories always are, and the few he wrote during that short Italian holiday of his were, in my opinion, downright drivel. And now the man must go and live in Vienna ! I shall miss him, of course, and that counts for something in my annoyance. I shall regret the absence of his dark vivid face, wonderfully alight when he came to mention a good short story or poem he had just read or some incident in his woodland life, perhaps a tree crammed with starlings on a foggy morning, at which times he had a trick of rubbing his head in ecstasy. It is true, too, that I am always a little annoyed if ever I hear of anyone going anywhere when it involves a fine long journey, but that is merely because I want to be off too and am envious at the thought of other people catching gigantic expresses or lounging on the boatdeck. But my annoyance remains 70 [pageend72]even when this regret and this touch of envy have been taken into consideration. I mistrust this practice, now so general among literary people, of voluntary exile. If writers are commanded, by the police or the doctor, to leave this country, there is no help for it ; that is their misfortune. Those who exile themselves, however, merely to escape the income tax, to find a cheap villa and a little extra sunshine, or to acquire a cosmopolitan air, are making a colossal mistake, unless they are the kind of writers who are born for cosmopolitanism and produce books that seem to have been written in and for hotel lounges. Some of these exiles, unlike Runnerman, we can very well spare, reserving our sympathy for the Parisian quarter or Italian village horribly destined to receive them. That the others should act in this witless fashion in a little tragedy. They do not seem to realize that the best books are always written at home, that the writer should be the last of all people to sever his roots. It is not the going abroad, for a glance or two at an alien life, but the living abroad that works the mischief. The real exile, with a hunger in his heart, may write more beautifully than ever he did at home, seeing the life he has lost as an old man sometimes sees his youth, something far away and glamorous yet wonderfully clear. Literature can be well served even by nostalgia, for passionate desire and dream are there. It can only be served very dubiously, however, by a mere feeling of pleasure at having dodged the income-tax inspector or at having found a very cheap sunny villa. The voluntary exile, unless he should be one of those very exceptional persons who find their own souls only in a foreign land, is in an absurd position. He is merely a tourist who is lingering on. It is true that some glorious things, to be found in the works of Shelley, Byron, Browning, for example, 71 [pageend73]have been written by exiles, though few of these, strictly speaking, were voluntary exiles, having strong reasons for absenting themselves. Even so, I take leave to wonder if their work would not have been even better if they had remained at home. A Shelley slowly maturing in England might have given us poetry even more subtly beautiful than a Shelley suddenly ripened by Italy. Byron might easily have been a better poet if he had not discovered the cheap and gaudy effects of the Levant. Browning might have stumbled on some English ring and book and produced a masterpiece that we might all have read instead of merely praising at a distance. And these are the poets, whose primary task is to reproduce for us the colour and bloom of their emotions. The novelists—and most of our voluntary exiles now are novelists—have still more cause to remain at home, where there is a kind of life they know ready for them to interpret. But off they go, so many of them, to Paris, to the Riviera, to Italy, to Vienna, to Honolulu, and you can watch their work steadily declining. Runnerman’s, I am certain, will go to pieces. All the English sap and savour will run out of it, leaving a dry husk of affectation. It is possible—though the thought appals—that he himself will gradually assume cosmopolitan airs. Is there any one more boring and futile than your cosmopolitan aesthete? He knows all the opera houses and theatres, has been at hand when all the movements were initiated, has had his little talk with M. IGNAZIO SILONE TOLSTOY IN THE ABRUZZI I was still a highschool student but in the holidays I often spent my time at the premises of Lega dei contadini, the Farmworkers’ League which had been established in our village a few years before. The League had been founded in a year of drought and hunger, within a few hours almost a thousand poor farmworkers and tenant-farmers had joined it, most of them illiterate cafoni. At that time, before the earthquake, our community had about eight thousand inhabitants. But as most of the members were illiterates, the meetings could not be called simply by posters. They were announced by a trumpet fanfare and they usual74 [pageend76]ly started an hour after the signal was given—a practical method, saving time and money. Still, there was one disadvantage: the posters could have given information not only about the place and time of the meeting but also about its purpose ; the trumpet could not perform this service. Whenever therefore the trumpet sounded (it was always in the evening when the cafoni were coming home from the fields) a feeling of vague anxiety spread throughout the village which for obvious reasons, was heightened into fear, often even into terror in the families of the landowners. The office of the League was in the centre of the village. A large, bleak room, the walls unpainted, the floor beaten earth, dusty in summer, muddy in winter. Opposite the door there were three pictures on the wall. The first, in a red tone, depicted the leonine head of Karl Marx. The second was Christ— in a long, shift-like, red robe—delivering the Sermon on the Mount to a crowd of poor people. (Blessed are they that thirst and hunger for justice! said the inscription underneath.) The third was an allegorical picture: it showed a half-naked woman dispersing the darkness with a torch—and a whole flock of bats seemed to flee at the same time. “This is a symbol of education,” one of the men explained to me. The motif of education was repeated in a transparency; slanting" across the opposite wall there was a long streamer of paper with the inscription: “Knowledge is Power” and the signature of Wilhelm Liebknecht. Socialist agitation and the fight against illiteracy went hand-in-hand in those days—they were the twin faces of progress. No one had even the slightest suspicion that the alphabet could ever be used for anything except to serve truth. Though I only frequented the League in the few 75 [pageend77]months of my vacation, it still called an unpleasant attention to me. Most of my acquaintances thought it a scandal ; especially as I was still a student and belonged to a family which was not rich but was thought to be a cut above the poor landworkers. *) There wasn’t very much I could achieve in the League. I wrote letters and petitions for the illiterate cafoni—naturally without being paid. The poverty in which these people lived was undescribable. They toiled from sunrise to sunset and subsisted almost entirely on vegetables and maize bread. Yet to many the memory of these years appeared as happy and serene—probably because of the long peace. *) Cafoni is an untranslatable Italian word which Silone uses in its original sense for the poor landworkers and tenant- farmers, still to be found on many large, entailed estates in Southern Italy. In the parlance of Northern Italy and of the cities ‘cafone’ has become a term of abuse, applied today less to the members of the agrarian proletariat than normally to the poorest of the folk who have forsaken the South to seek work in the industrial North. Silone’s farther was a small landowner, his mother a weaver. He was fourteen when he lost both of them in the earthquake. There came a terrible year in which the familiar order of things collapsed from one hour to another: on the morning of a snowy January day an earthquake destroyed in thirty seconds most of the villages in our district and a few months later Italy became involved in the First World War. The men who had survived the earthquake had to join the army. Only we remained in the ruins: children, old men and women. The stubborn old secretary of the League had established a new centre in a wooden hut. It was mostly 76 [pageend78]empty. Of tlie three pictures which had decorated the meeting-hall only Karl Marx survived. On a nail under the portrait of the lion-maned man the trumpet with which the meetings used to be called hung’ unused. The hut, surrounded by donkey-stables and pig-styes, opened once or twiee a week but only a few old cafoni turned up—and the secretary who also kept the key. The old men had quite unequivocal views about the true cause of the war—and of wars in general. Wars, they said, were the means for governments to reduce the ever-growing population of poor peasants. For this purpose and in order to implement the destructive work of arms, occasionally they also spread a little cholera and other epidemics among the poor. Of course, it was not in the interest of the governments to exterminate completely those who tilled the soil. Otherwise, who would do the work and how would the masters eat? This also provided sufficient explanation why wars always had a limited duration. One evening when I passed the hut with a pile of schoolbooks under my arm and, as usual, called a greeting through the door, one of the old men asked: “Why don’t you try to read us something from all your printed stuff?” This idea excited me. Why hadn’t I thought of it myself? Only when I considered what to read to them, did I realize what a tough task I had shouldered. All my knowledge was mere book-learning, scholastic wisdom. I remembered a strange doctor who practised in the nearby village of San Benedetto. He was held to be an anarchist, led a miserable life and the wealthy families treated him with suspicion and contempt. “I have a few good books I could lend you,” he told me once. Next day I gave the excuse of wanting to 77 [pageend79]visit some of my classmates living in San Benedetto and ran straight to the doctor to ask his help and advice. JELEN SKI : Il me semble qu’il y a deux problèmes que nous devrions affronter aujourd’hui: d’abord celui du style de l’écrivain qui, tout en vivant en exil, c’est à dire en dehors de son terroir linguisti92 [pageend94]que, continue à écrire dans sa langue maternelle. C’est un problème qui pose un certain nombre de questions, dont je vais indiquer quelques unes, mais je suis sûr que les participants vont développer ce sujet. L’écrivain qui est coupé du milieu où sa propre langue continue à être vivante a des problèmes assez particuliers. J’ai moi-même beaucoup d’amis qui, tout en vivant en exil et tout en connaissant parfaitement bien la langue du pays qui leur a donné l’accueil, continuent à écrire dans leur langue maternelle. Certains d’entre eux maintiennent que cela tend à rendre leur style plus classique, plus châtié, plus parfait, dans un certain sens. D’autres disent que le style devient beaucoup plus personnel, qu’il se personnalise en quelque sorte parce que l’écrivain étant coupé de son milieu d’origine, où sa propre langue se développe d’une façon vivante et libre, tache parfois d’enrichir ou de maintenir le contact avec sa langue en ayant recours à des sources telles que des écrivains archaïques, des dictionnaires, etc., c’est à dire, il y a un moment où son vocabulaire devient beaucoup plus personnalisé. Je prends par exemple le cas d’un écrivain comme Alexis Remizov: c’est un écrivain qui a vécu 40 ans en France, qui a toujours continué à écrire le russe et qui était pour ainsi dire “sur-russe” ; c’est en exil qu’il est devenu l’écrivain le plus russe possible puisqu’il enrichissait sa langue qui devenait du “Remizov”, sorte de langue russe poussée à l’extrême. Il ya a, évidemment, le côté social du problème: pourquoi continuer à écrire dans sa propre langue tout en vivant en exil? Il y a certains écrivains qui choisissent délibérément d’écrire dans la langue de leur pays d’accueil parce que cette langue leur donne accès à un public nouveau, à un public beaucoup 93 [pageend95]plus large. D’autre part, il y a des écrivains qui disent: naturellement, je pourrais peut-être écrire dans une langue occidentale, dans la langue de mon pays d’accueil, mais là je deviens un des mille écrivains qui écrivent cette langue, tandis qu’en continuant à écrire dans ma propre langue, j’ai un public beaucoup plus limité, mais un public plus vivant, qui réagit d’une façon beaucoup plus aigue à ce que j’écris. Il y a un autre cas et je crois que certains d’entre nous sont particulièrement qualifiés pour en discuter: c’est celui de l’écrivain bilingue ou même trilingue, ou alors de l’écrivain qui a délibérément choisi de ne s’exprimer que dans une langue qui n’est pas sa langue maternelle, et là il y a plusieurs questions. Il y a le problème de la psychologie d’une langue, pour ainsi dire; je crois que l’écrivain qui écrit en plusieurs langues à la fois s’aperçoit d’une espèce de dédoublement de personnalité, ou même d’une personnalité triple s’il écrit trois langues. L’esprit de la langue lui-même met en relief certaines de ces qualités. Je connais un écrivain qui écrit à la fois le polonais, l’anglais et le français, et il tend à dire toujours que son côté le plus raisonnable et le plus rationnel ressort en français; qu’en anglais c’est son manque d’assurance qui ressort, et qu’il ne se sent libre que quand il écrit le polonais. Il y a ensuite le problème du style dans une langue étrangère, et là aussi il y a je crois plusieurs façon d’approcher ce problème. Nous avons le cas d’un écrivain comme Cioran qui écrit une langue extrêment classique et châtiée, qui s’est fait écrivain français et qui est devenu pour ainsi dire l’écrivain français par excellence, c’est à dire qu’il est entré dans une tradition d’une littérature française extrêmement classique, extrêmement pure. Or, nous avons en même temps à 94 [pageend96]notre table Piotr Rawicz, qui écrit aussi en français et dont la langue est plutôt une langue imaginative, imaginaire, qui porte même en elle-même, pour ainsi dire, l’équivalent transposé en français de son propre terroir originaire. Puis il y a le problème de écrivain trilingue, comme par exemple Nicolas Nabokov, qui dit qu’il n’est pas écrivain et peut-être a-t-il raison en ce sens qu’il est d’abord un compositeur, mais c’est en tout cas un homme qui a écrit de très beaux livres et qui les a écrits et en français et en anglais et en russe. Joseph CZAPSKI: Je voudrais répondre ici à un des problèmes que Jelenski a touchés. C’est justement un lieu commun qu’on trouve écrit dans les journaux, dans les revues de mon pays (je suppose que c’est la même chose en Tchécoslovaquie, en Roumanie, en Pologne et en Hongrie). S’il s’agit de la littérature polonaise, la seule que je connaisse un peu mieux, c’est une idée qui me semble absolument fausse. Toute la langue polonaise du 19ème siècle, par exemple, a été forgée dans l’émigration. Mickiewicz, qui avait le génie de la langue, était d’un terrible “sans gêne” quand il s’agissait d’assimiler des mots étrangers, il se méfiait d’une langue épurée et pseudo-classique. Des Sonnets de Crimée qui sont les perles de notre littérature, un poète pseudo-classique de son temps, Kozmian, disait: “C’est peut-être la langue de Crimée, c’est peut-être du turc, mais ce n’est pas du polonais.” Arrivé en 1832 en France, après quelques mois de séjour à Paris, Mickiewicz écrit des articles où il met des mots français à peine transposés, et en fait des mots polonais. Le plus russe des écrivains russes de notre temps, Remizov, transposait de même des mots français en russe. Quelques mois après son arrivée à Paris, Mickiewicz faisait de ruminer, “rou- 97 [pageend99]minowat”; d’émeute, “emeta” ; d’éclaireurs, “ekle- rery” ; et tout cela devenait des inventions verbales polonaises. Il faut savoir le polonais pour sentir combien cela semble encore aujourd’hui inventif, insolite et sonore. Un autre écrivain qui a créé la langue moderne polonaise poétique, c’est Norwid, mort à Paris en 1883 et qui a quitté la Pologne à peine âgé de 20 ans. Mais c’est un langage que ses contemporains ne comprenaient pas et ce n’était pas étonnant: il introduisait une construction de phrase nouvelle influencée par ses lectures françaises, latines, grecques, italiennes; cela a été de nouveau un genre d’écriture dont parle Jelenski et qui est à un certain point une littérature personnalisée à l’extrême. La patrie, pour lui, est une idée, le lieu de ses rêves, une fin, non un fait. JANIS MEDENIS The Latvian poet, Janis Medenis, writing about the long years he spent at Kolima where he had been deported for penal servitude, says that he had forgotten even his own name while he was there and could remember only the number he had been given: V-139. But the Latvian people will' never forget the name of this poet who, with immense conviction, portrayed great historical events and their repercussions on the life of the individual. Medenis’ volume of poetry7 Tedia (Whetstone, 1933), introduced fresh values into Latvian poems and ballads. He entirely cut across the new direction poetry had taken as verse became increasingly less formal and was to be enjoyed by the eye alone. His own poetry is meant to be read aloud and listened to ; its strength lies in the melody of' rhythmical, powerful, sonorous lines. In poems of an accomplished, strictly formal style, Medenis portrays the Latvian fighter in the Revolution of 1905 and in the First World War, ending in the tragic rout and dispersal of Latvian riflemen in the vast Russian interior. In his later books Vareniba (Majesty, 1936), Tdksmu raksti (Legends, 1942), he continues to write poetry in the epic manner, inter123 [pageend123]spersed with satirical pieces modelled on Horace, but there is an ever increasing number of lyrics mirroring the individual’s experiences in the midst of catastrophes brought on by historical events. This kind of poetry rises to its highest achievement in the sonnets Janis Medenis wrote about the siege of Kurzeme in the spring of 1945—sonnets in which, against the background of individual experiences, he forcefully depicts the raging fires of war and the fate of a nation trampled underfoot by two occupying powers, Russian and German. But even his most sombre forebodings in these poems are outweighed by his faith in the freedom of the spirit and in the ultimate victory of justice. On the Kurzeme front, fighting ended in May 1945, but for the Latvian people it was then that the real fight began: it began for the partisans hiding out in the forests, for the peasantry against arbitrary collectivisation, for the young against Russification, for the deportees against untold terror. Janis Medenis attempted to flee to Sweden in a small tug-boat but was overtaken by Russian speed-boats and forced to turn back. This incident was followed by long years of imprisonment, deportation and penal servitude. Janis Medenis was first taken to a so-called screening camp in which the Soviet occupation forces placed practically the entire Latvian male population in the summer of 1945, from young boys to whitehaired old men. Data about Medenis’ subsequent years in prison are fragmentary. It is known, however, that he was an inmate of Riga Central Prison in 1946. In 1947, he was at Bolderaja, a small harbour town near Riga, where the Russians at that time had set up a large prison or a concentration camp. Seven poems written by Medenis while imprisoned at Bolderaja have been preserved, and have reached the free 124 [pageend124]world; they were composed between July 16th and August 3rd, 1947. The longest of them, The Empty Land, was conceived as a letter to his mother in the Western world; he speaks of the fate that overtook their farmstead under Soviet occupation, the wrecked buildings, the members of the family either fallen in battle or deported, and the Russian strangers who took possession in the enforced absence of the owners. Four of these seven poems are written in the shortened sonnet form introduced into Latvian literature by the poet Rainis (1865-1929), the three stanzas consisting of four, three and two lines each, ending with one single line. It is a sonnet form frequently used by Janis Medenis during his years of imprisonment and deportation. Every one of these short poems is a cry out of silence and icy loneliness. At that time, death sentences were carried out daily at Bolderaja, and the poet is preoccupied with premonitions of death, expecting to be shot any night. From there, Medenis was transferred to the concentration camp in Forest Park, near Riga. Sentenced to penal servitude, he was then deported to Kolima. In all probability, this happened towards the end of the 1940’s when the Soviet occupation authorities deported about 60.000 Latvians, most of them farmers. At Kolima, Medenis was given the number V -139. At first he was set to work in the forests, and the final years of his sentence he spent on construction work, building wooden barracks in the Arctic forests. In 1953 the poet suffered a stroke which, after paralysing the right side of his body, left a permanent after-effect—in his own words: “I walk slightly dragging my right leg.” Referring to Kolima, he later wrote to his mother that he had forgotten his own name in the long years he was there and could only remember his number, V -139, and his mother was not 125 [pageend125]to worry about his memory growing faulty as he became older. About 1955, Medenis wrote a number of poems at Kolima which form two cycles: The Path of Migratory Birds and Dreaming in Exile. One can imagine how he wrote those poems in woodcutters’ huts at Kolima, with a pencil on rough grey paper— after his severe illness when “the doors that opened out to hope are all slammed shut”. His mode of expression is economical, his language direct, and his proud spirit -defying oppression finds its voice in powerful cadences. After his stroke, Janis Medenis developed cardiac trouble which led to sudden fainting fits. His term of penal servitude ended in the autumn of 1955, and on November 3rd he returned to Riga, sick and in rags. He had to be carried out of the train on a stretcher and was taken to the Second City Hospital in Riga, where he remained for two months. Afterwards he spent some time at the “Cirulisi” sanatorium near Cesie, and then at the rest house of the Writers’ Union in Dubulti. In February 1956, two of Medenis’ poems appeared in the magazine “Karogs”. The Soviet occupation authorities, after practically hounding the innocent man to death in prisons and penal servitude, allowed him to publish his work again, to show their goodwill. It goes without saying that there is absolutely nothing in Medenis’ poetry that would further the aims of the Soviet regime. His proud, independent nature did not permit him to exchange a poet’s spiritual freedom for the material comforts of a Party propagandist. His poems shine with unparalleled brightness in the twilight of Socialist Realism. None of the poets labouring in the monotonous greyness of offi126 [pageend126]cially prescribed poetry in occupied Latvia come anywhere near Medenis’ achievement. Although continuously reminded of his ruined health and suffering from frequent heart attacks, Medenis gradually settled down to work again. In 1956 he started a cycle of poems and ballads on the theme of the Revolution of 1905 ; it was then that the Latvian peasantry rose against Czarist despotism and the German landowners. The underlying idea had been national independence, but the Soviet regime represents this uprising as a purely Socialist effort, and had therefore no objections to Medenis writing about the Revolution of 1905. The poems and ballads gradually came to form a whole volume, U g unis nakti (Fires in the Night), which Medenis concluded in February 1957 and handed to the State Publishers in Riga. But various complications arose in connection with its publication, embittering the poet; he began to have more frequent heart attacks, which shortened his life. The Soviet guardians of literature found the book unacceptable. No doubt the occupation authorities were afraid that the poet’s masterly evocation of the peasantry battling with scythes and axes against Czarist despotism might be misinterpreted by the reader as the symbol of an uprising against Soviet despotism. In a letter to his brother in the USA, Medenis said that he was expected to reduce his poetry to the dull, grey level of the poetry of Socialist Realism, “for everybody to sup, but of a flavour nobody likes”. Publication of his book was put back for several years; it was not till the summer of 1961, when the poet was already dead, that U gunis nakti (Fires in the Night) at last appeared. But only a small proportion of his poems on the theme of the Revolution of 1905 has been retained in this volume. The Soviet guardians of literature excluded everything that might, in 127 [pageend127]their eyes, constitute a danger to the regime, and they did so when the poet himself was safely dead and could no longer protest against such a mutilation of his work. Towards the end of 1958, a volume of selected poems by Janis Medenis appeared in Riga, containing previously published poems as well as new ones. Although an exceptionally large number of copies were printed (8000) the book was sold out in a very short time. Medenis had the rare ability to delight in the beauty of life even in the midst of disaster; this explains the rather unusual title of the book — Dienu krasnums (The Days’ Magnificence). This book, too, was not published without grave interference with the original text. A large number of lines as well as individual words, regarded as dangerous to the Soviet regime, are found to have been altered. Any reference to God or to ancient Latvian mythology has been consistently deleted. In the poem entitled Komeis (The Comet), the line “red star, your rays spread fear and dread foreboding” has in the amended version become “you long-tailed star whose rays spread fear and dread foreboding” Although Medenis was only concerned with the literal meaning of the word, i.e. a star in the firmament, the censors were afraid it would be mistaken for the Soviet emblem. In the poem Aizbrauceji (The Wayfarers), Medenis portrays the tragic inner conflict of the Latvian riflemen when Czarist army units did not give them adequate support in their attacks on the German lines in 1916. Embittered, they retreated into Russia and turned against the Czarist regime. In the poem, a rifleman exclaims: 128 [pageend128]. our hands are tied by Russian cowardice ...” In tlie Soviet era, of course, Russians must not be called cowards—even if they were Czarist Russians— and this line in Medenis’ poem has been arbitrarily changed into “. . . our hands are tied by our own cowardice ...” While the occupation authorities continued to raise objections to the publication of his ballads about the Revolution of 1905, Medenis found it very diücult to earn his living. In order to get some money, he had to translate books at great speed. This work sapped his strength and impaired his health still further. A friend of his, writing from Latvia, said the following about him: “Towards the end of 1960, he over-taxed his strength, translating Dostoevsky’s ‘The Idiot’. Having signed a short-term contract, Janis had to work without respite until the manuscript was ready to be handed in. Afterwards, he never was really well all this last year, sometimes he felt better, at other times he was utterly exhausted. He had various clashes and difficulties both with the Writers’ Union and with his publishers, which profoundly upset and depressed him.” On March 22nd, 1961, Janis Medenis again had to go into hospital, where he suffered two severe heart attacks. By the end of April he had sufficiently recovered to leave hospital and to return to the bungalow he was building for himself. But on the 10th of May he was found dead there, with a book lying on his chest. The Soviet occupation authorities have shortened Medenis’ life by many years. He had originally been very robust in health, and this helped him to survive the years of penal servitude at Kolima. But he re129 [pageend129]turned from there an invalid and died shortly before he reached his 58th year. Medenis could only publish a small proportion of the poems he wrote in the last years before his death. The majority of them were protests against the enslavement of the spirit, inhumanity and the impoverishment of life. For obvious reasons, these could not be published in Latvia, but Medenis succeeded in sending them to his brother in the free world. They appeared in the Latvian literary magazine, Cela Zimes, No. 40, in August 1961. The poems are arranged into four cycles, each under an eloquent heading: Cietum- nieks sana zeme, Putnu cels, Sapnojot trimda, Atkal mu majas (Prisoner in his Homeland, The Path of Migratory Birds, Dreaming in Exile, Home Again). Janis Medenis was a man of great strength, brimming over with energy both in his life and his work. The Soviet regime broke him physically within a few years, but his spirit remained unbroken until his last breath. The poems he wrote in prison and in penal servitude at Kolima are a last proud protest against tyranny, voiced by a man under sentence of death. With the verse he wrote in the years when Latvia was still independent, he introduced new forms and enlarged the scope of Latvian poetry. If a congenial translator could sometime be found, Medenis’ ballads and poems would show other nations how poetry can be rescued from the soundlessness forced upon it by being meant to be read with the eye alone. With the poetry he wrote as a prisoner, he joins the great community of poets who have uncompromisingly stood up for an author’s right to follow his own conscience and not to bow down before the dictates of tyranny. La gauche sait qu’elle est parfois impuissante face au crime, mais elle ne veut pas appeler ce crime une bonne action.” De toute évidence, c’était là une revendication inacceptable, étant donné que l’arbitre philologique est la pupille des yeux de tout pouvoir autoritaire. Que resterait-il d’une dictature qui ne pourrait altérer la signification courante des mots? Tl a toujours été plus facile de faire violence aux mots qu’aux choses. Tel ce moine qui jeûnait en baptisant morue les poulets et les biftecks ; tel Mussolini qui résolut le problème de nos œuvriers agricoles en leur accordant le terme latin de ruraux; ainsi, sous le régime soviétique ou de démocratie populaire, les salariés de l’économie d’Etat restent salariés, leurs produits sont ultérieurement amputés de la plus-value au bénéfice de la “nouvelle classe” et de l’appareil d’Etat hypertrophié, tandis qu’ils n’ont eux-même pas davantage à dire sur la gestion de leurs entreprises que ne l’ont, en Italie, les dépendants des chemins de fer de l’Etat, de l’ENI ou du monopole du sel et du tabac ; mais, voici la feuille de figuier : juridiquement, ainsi que tous les autres citoyens de l’Etat, ils sont considérés copropriétaires. C’est un droit formel sans conséquences positives, mais il sert à justifier l’interdiction des grèves. La documentation des artifices nominalistes d’origine stalinienne pourrait, sans difficulté, remplir des volumes entiers, et suggérer l’hypothèse que l’arme la plus efficace contre ceux-ci serait, de la part des hommes de bonne volonté, un vocabulaire honnête. Le mérite de Po Prostu fut d’avoir posé la critique du régime dans le sens démystificateur et son danger pour le régime était aggravé du fait que ses rédacteurs ramenaient une réalité masquée d’oripeaux pseudo-marxistes 137 [pageend137]aux véritables critères du marxisme. Ex ore tuo judico. D’autre part, à quoi le marxisme se réduirait-il s’il renonçait à sa qualité majeure, qui est la critique des fictions juridiques? Le socialisme n’est-il pas né de la critique de là démocratie formelle ? L’orientation de la critique marxiste au cours de ces dernières années devrait favoriser une reprise dans le sens autocritique. Le motif principal de ces études est maintenant l’aliénation, de même que pour les générations précédentes c’était la vérification du matérialisme historique ou de la théorie de la valeur. Mais dans le concept de l’aliénation la pierre de touche pour juger un ordre social est l’intégrité de la conscience. Le critère moral prévaut sur le critère technique. Or, du point de vue de l’aliénation, il n’y a guère de différence dans la condition humaine de ce côte ou de l’autre du “rideau de fer”. L’anonymité, la bureaucratisation, la dépersonnalisation, la dissociation de l’homme en sujet et objet, en un être qui s’efforce de contrôler sa propre conduite et en une chose manipulée par les autres — tout cela se retrouve partout. Le simple transfert des titres de propriété d’une bureaucratie privée à une bureaucratie de l’Etat ne suffit pas pour abolir l’aliénation. L’exploration du cosmos n’y suffira pas non plus. C’est un problème grave, qui dépasse les polémiques partisanes. Ce serait le rôle des socialistes de toutes les tendances, de ceux du moins qui n’ont pas l’obsession du pouvoir et désirent une révolution au service de l’homme, d’étudier sérieusement la thèse de Simone Weil sur le caractère naturellement oppressif de la grande industrie, quel que soit le régime de propriété. Pour faire une brèche dans l’âme d’un socialiste, je peux ajouter que cette vérité fut déjà pressentie par Friedrich Engels, et qu’on en trouve la trace dans sa polémique avec des anarchistes italiens. “En ce qui concerne le heures du travail du moins — écrivait-il — on pourrait écrire sur les portes des usines : ‘Lasciate ogni speranza voi ch’entrate’. Si l’homme a réussi, par ses connaissances et par son génie d’invention, à subjuguer les forces de la nature, celles-ci se vengent en le soumettant, dans la mesure où il les utilise, à un véritable despotisme, indépendant de toute organisation sociale.” “Au moins en ce qui concerne 138 [pageend138]les heures de travail”, écrivait Engels; mais les marxistes ont toujours refusé la notion de l’égalité politique bourgeoise en objectant que l’œuvrier ne peut être, dans la cité, l’égal du dirigeant s’il lui est asservi dans l’usine. Dans l’expérience de Po Prostu, étant donné sa brève durée, les résultats de l’action démystificatrice sont moins intéressants que la vivacité d’esprit. Mais c’est précisément pour cette raison que son importance majeure n’est pas réduite pour nous à un caractère rétrospectif. Il est vrai que l’anthologie de Jelenski nous rapporte aux réalités agitées, pleines d’inconnues et d’espérances, de la fin de 1956 et des mois qui suivirent. Mais en lisant ces textes, ces chroniques, ces poèmes, dans leur langage imprévu, nerveux, audacieux, parfois impertinent, et avant tout vivant, comme peut se sentir vivant l’homme qui sort de prison, une émotion finit par prévaloir sur les autres sentiments : celle d’avoir peut-être trouvé un élément de réponse à la question la plus difficile concernant l’avenir. C’est la question de ľ “après”. Il y a déjà un problème politique actuel de l’“après” dans les pays neufs qui sortent du joug colonial, comme il y en a un, théorique pour l’instant, car il est difficile d’y appliquer des termes chronologiques, pour les pays qui se trouvent séquestrés à l’intérieur du bloc communiste. Il serait vain de se demander, dès maintenant, quel sera l’aspect institutionnel du post-communisme ; mais on peut par contre se demander quelle direction devraient prendre les efforts de l’intelligentsia de gauche face à une situation de crise du monde soviétique et des démocraties populaires. Eh. bien, il n’y a pas de solutions toutes faites, ou alors elles sont réactionnaires ; mais il me semble que Po Prostu représente une flèche dans la bonne direction. Il n’y aura rien d’autre à faire qu’à recommencer de dire “pain” pour le pain et “vin” pour le vin, tout simplement. Ge o r g e FALUDY, the Hungarian poet, novelist, essayist and translator has packed into his fifty-one years enough adventure, suffering and experience to last three ordinary lifetimes. He was one of the outstanding poets of his generation in Hungary and his inspired free versions of Villon’s 141 [pageend141]poems were a runaway bestseller. The Second World War found him in France whence he escaped to North Africa and after fantastic tribulations made his way to the United States. He edited an anti-Nazi Hungarian newspaper and later served in the American army. After the war he returned to Budapest, becoming one of the editors of the Socialist daily “Népszava’ until he was arrested, tortured and spent more than five years in various prisons and the notorious death camp of Recsk. In 1956 he left Hungary and made a tour of South America lecturing about the Hungarian Revolution. In 1957 he settled in London as one of the editors of the free Hungarian fortnightly, “Irodalmi Ujság”, a post he held until January 1962. He is now connected with a publishing house of limited editions in addition to his literary work.
