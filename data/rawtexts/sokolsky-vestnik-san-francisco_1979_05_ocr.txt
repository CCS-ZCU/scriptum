Later, other units sprang up in Oakland, Portland, Scio, Malin and Los Angeles. They were organized in so called "Western District" and later in 1909, the name was changed to "Pacific District." The seat of the District was in San Francisco and at that time the strongest unit. The Pacific District had to face many difficulties right from the beginning. The distance between units - 1,300 miles between north and south of the borders was the biggest obstacle. There was also a shortage of experienced instructors. Support from the Chicago headquarters was minimal as they themselves went through the crisis and arguments of what colors of the shirt should be - red or blue - should be chosen for the Sokol uniform. Only when the first Sokol instructor, Br. Karel Dvorak arrived in 1914, from Chicago and started to build the units morals and drilling system, the situation changed. The northern units improved considerably. Other units in the south were doing much better thanks to Jirsak's brothers who came to California from Sokol Vinohrady and became the pillars of Sokol ideas on the Pacific coast. Before the full strength of Sokol could be reached, the 1st World War came and with it, most of the young gymnasts were drafted and many never returned back to the ranks. During the war, the Sokol units formed special national organizations called, "National Councils" to help T.G. Masaryk and other leaders in their fight against Austrian monarchy. When the war was over, the life of many units suffered by the loss of their best members. Again it was necessary to call for help to the newly organized American Sokol Organization in Chicago but the help never came. In 1921, Br. Ant. Kohoutek arrived to San Francisco with his wife Marie. They were convinced that something could be done to increase the activity of the northern units. They spent several weeks up there, setting up classes and organizing new life within the Sokol units. The years after the 1st World War , slet of Sokol gymnasts and instructors were coming from Czechoslovakia to the United States. They were directed to help in the established units and carried them through the struggle during the afterwar years. Several well-known instructors came to visit the Pacific Distric and amongst the names we remember were: Bros. F. Chmelik, Josef Kos, Frantisek Příhoda, Lada Kadlec, Harry Jozifek, Oldrich Malý, Josef Pokorny and the director of women, sis. Milada Prchal. They all helped to boost the gymnastic attendance and uplift the sagging moral within the units. The Pacific District recorded increased activities and several District Slets were held as a result of increased participation of Sokol members. Especially Sokol San Francisco and Los Angeles represented Sokol ideals on many occassions in front of the American public. When the Olympic Games were held in Los Angeles in 1932, the Sokol exhibition was regarded as very outstanding amongst the«ethnic groups performing at this ocassion. Again in 1938, San Francisco International Exhibition, just during the Munich dark hours, Sokols were presenting the Czechoslovakian good name to America. Shortly after came the 2nd World War and the units joined again the fight of Dr. Ed. Benes and others with financial and moral help. When the war ended, the life of the units was in ruins and the activities were limited. But soon the new blood was coming in the form of new immigration. Thousands of Sokols left Czechoslovakia after the communist coup in 1948 and they were strenthening the membership roster of the units. With this improved situation, the Pacific District slet was held in Los Angeles in 1950. In 1959, the district undertook great challenge when accepted to conduct the Mid-slet of American Sokols in Los Angeles. The same year the District headquarters were transfered to Los Angeles. Sokol San Francisco held a very successful slet in 1962. Unfortunately, the Sokol units up the northern Pacific coast were not able to get a new blood from the 1948 immigration to U.S. and their life became more and more difficult. The years following the 2nd World War marked the end of Sokol Malin, Scio, Oakland and Portland. In the year we are celebrating 70th anniversary of the Pacific District and the following units are activly organized in it: Los Angeles, San Francisco, Fresno and Seattle.
