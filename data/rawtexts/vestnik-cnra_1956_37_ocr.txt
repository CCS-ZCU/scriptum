Masaryk.and the Idea of European and World Federation” and feel that this modest booklet of less than sixty pages should be on the reading list of every American who wishes to be broughtup to date on the problems of tomorrow. Unlike so many other attempts at evaluating T. G. Masaryk in the light of contemporary world history, Dr. Zenkľs treatise lays no claim to being exhaustive or conclusive. It merely suggests in broad, general strokes Masaryk’s ideas on solving world, problems of peace and coexistence by advocating a federation of small European Nations first and later expanding this concept to a world wide full scale federation. Some of the ideas are better than fifty years of age, and yet they are so pertinent and timely today thah one marvels at Masaryk’s vision and projective political wisdom. The author makes extensive use of references and direct quotations from Masaryk’s own works, and this in itself makes the booklet an indispensable piece of work to be used over and over again, whenever and wherever world problems come up for discussion. The authors own fifteen point program based as it is on a mature understanding and firm belief in Masaryk's political credo, is worthy of implementation in our own foreign policy and in our individual attitude towards world problems. In substance the author states that in our present struggle against communism we cannot fight effectively unless we know what we are fighting and how best to approach the complex struggle between Democracy and Communism. The two ideologies are self-excluding and cannot exist side by side in a world aching for PEACE. Dr. Zenkl's booklet is warmly recommended as a concise guide, as a means of living in the spirit of T. G. Masaryk whose unique political profundity is so sorely needed in today’s troubled times. In its pages one finds the answers to questions so often asked by American born children whose parents try to impress them with the importance and former glory of their native Czechoslovakia.
