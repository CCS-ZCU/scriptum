Fania Many Slovak-Americans are interested in leamlhg more about their family histories. Family memorabilia and hometown church rwnhUgjarp.the key sources of infonnatlon, but when tj^e^Ěě exhausted, what's next? Work- in^wnh the archives in Slovakia, either through the Czechoslovak Embassy in Washington. D-C-. or by going to Slovakia, is very expensive. But there is a Slovak genealogical resource In America that is inexpensive and probably not available in Czechoslovakia. It 1$ Ihe 1828 ccnsusof Hungary, which, of course. Slovakia was part of at the time. The census was microfilmed by the Genealogical Society of Sall Lake City. Utah, of the Church of Latter Day Saints. (How to use it is discussed later.) The 1828 census is sometimes neglected as a genealogical resource because of Its special purpose: tax collection and conscription information. But while later censuses collected much more information about family members, only small portions of their records remain, while the 1828 is nearly complete. Thus. Lt Is likely that at least some of the households of your Slovak ancestors are listed In the 1828 census of Hungary. Hungary in 1828 was under Austrian control, and Latin was the language of government. It seems that except for special cases such as mining areas, the same printed census form in Latin was used throughout the entire country. It is not hard to get the general gist of the printed form using a Latin dictionary and genealogical Latin word lists, and Ehe handwriting is generally quite legible. In a way. the census is an amazing document, because Hungary in 1828 was still a feudal stale. The peasants farmed land owned by the local noble, and they paid for its use in produce and labor. Sometimes they were treated very badly. Note that the census uses the term "Possessio” meaning "manor," for small settlements. For each household the census lists the head of household, usually with given name then family name, but sometimes in the reverse order. Widows are identified as "Vidual." or "Rellcta” (or their abbreviations). Sometimes the given names are in Latin form, such as Joannes, but mostly non-Latin. ethnic forms are used. Obviously, the spelling of your ancestors' names may be different than used later. For example, in my case. Fabula and Choma were found as Fabulya and Homa. The census counted the persons In each household between the ages of 18 and 60 classified as: Honoratiores. Cives. Colon!. In- qullini, Subinquilini. Fratres. FULL. Fllae. Servi. AnclUae. Opifices. Mercatores. Quaestores. which might be translated as Honoraries. Citizens, Colonists. Tenants. Subtenants. Brothers. Sons. Daughters. Servants. Maids. Artisans, Merchants. Tax-Collectors. In the villages. every household has a "1” under either Colon!. Inquilini. or Subinquilinl. apparently describing the relationship to the local landowner. 1 do not know the full implications of those three terms, because I have not yet found a history of Hungary that discusses them fully. Perhaps the notables attracted peasants to their lands as Colon! in order to increase production. Perhaps as sons married, they became Inquilini while waiting for new lands to be opened for Ccloni. There are ten kinds of taxes listed on the census form. A few apply only In cities on houses and land: most are agricultural, such as taxes on produce from cropland, meadows, vineyards, and orchards, and on animals. Everything is figured in compound fractions« and the arithmetic is Impressive when taxes- are summed for hundreds of households.* Taxes are calculated in "Rfl" and "xr." where I Rfl - 60 xr. Clearly, these are the florin and the krajear (from the German, kreuzer. for a; cross in Its design). The census Includes space for remarks. (''Observations'') about each household,- although that column Is often left blank- A remark may be flattering or not. depending on your point of view. "Llbertinus ' (freeman, or one released from duties to the landowner) is rare. "Ex-miles" (former soldier) and “Ex- insurgens" are more common. I suspect the latter means one served in the "Insurrectio" of 1809. the last peasant army ever raised by the Hungarian nobles. Of course, you might be Just as pleased with your ancestors being listed under "Deserta” Ln 1828. Just 20 years before from the revolt of 1848-1849 that led to the end of serfdom. Most remarks are about more commonplace matters, such as the occupation of an artisan ("Faber ferrarlus" = ironworker) or why someone was not counted tn the census ("Hospes oh senium exemptus." which means. I guess, that a house guest was exempt because of old age). Each census report has an appendix (or. rarely, a foreword) that apparently responds to questions tn the census Instructions. You might find your ancestor listed Ln It as the village "Judex" (judge) or one of the others who helped with the census. The appendix mainly gives extensive information and calculations about crops and prices Some of Lt is like a property appraisal. sometimes with sections headed '‘Benefícia" and "Maledicia." apparently denoting the good and bad features of the village. To use the census, you need to know the villages, towns, and cities In which to look for your ancestors of 1828. There is a good chance thát they lived in or near the places from which your later ancestors emigrated Lo America. There are genealogical handbooks for Czechoslovakia and Hungary to help you research your ancestors' places of origin, and databases of Slovak family names and villages of origin are being collected- And there are cross-indexes of historical and modem place names with which you can find the place names and the Hungarian counties for 1828. Using the 1828 census of Hungary takes a little work, but we arc really fortunate that the Mormon church microfilmed it in Hungary' as part of its worldwide family history data collection program. And you do not need to go co Utah to use it, because there are 800 or so Family History Centers of the Mormons scattered around the U.S. in which you can use microfilms borrowed from the Genealogical Library. These centers arc open to the public, and friendly volunteer staff will help you get started. To find the center nearest you, look tn your phone bock under Church of Latter Day Saints or write to the Family History Library. 35 North West Temple.
