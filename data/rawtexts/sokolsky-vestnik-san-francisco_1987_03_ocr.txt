[pageend7]Str .8 Věstník Sokol San Francisco April-May 19^^ "I made a commitment to myself that every day of my life, I would make somebody happy.” Zdena Marek was born in Czechoslovakia and spent a happy childhood in the large compound of “Lanner Brewery” in the city of Klatovy, where her father, Jan Liska, was a brewmaster. She loved to describe the large gardens surrounding the Brewery, filled with flowers blooming from early spring, till a white blanket of snow covered the ground. She loved to participate in taking care of the vcgatable gardens and harvesting all kinds of fruit. Exposed to the intricate brewery operation under her father’s watchful eye, Zdena learned as a child to respect people of all levels of skills and responsibility, since, as her father explained to her, each and every position in the over all operation was important. “My father,” she said “never raised his voice.” “Kindness has its power” he used to say. Zdena liked to tell the story how she tested her father’s words. There were several vicious dogs guarding the facility at night. They were kept in a special fenced in area on long chains and let loose only at night. No one dared to enter the courtyard without the special protection of trained watchmen. Zdena started sneaking them leftovers from the table and was feeding them regularly through the chicken wire fence. Some of those dogs were on chains too short to reach the fence so Zdena couldn’t feed them her goodies. She knew them all by name and talked to each one each and every night. So as not to favor only some of them, Zdena started working herself slowly into the cage. Those vicious dogs started to like her, licking her hands and legs. One night the watchman saw Zdena inside the cage surrounded with her loving dogs, and he called her father and demanded that these tamed dogs must be replaced. Zdena’s father ordered him to get her safely out of the cage. This was a mistake. As the watchman entered the cage, two dogs bit the poor watchman protecting Zdena from him. Zdena’s father decided the dogs were vicious enough to hurt anybody but Zdena, and never replaced them. Zdena was a courageous lady. From far away villages, the men would arrive at the Brewery at night to leave early the next morning for their villages in their horse drawn buggies loaded with beer barrels. They had a special area where they received free overnight lodging and only one beer to drink. Zdena often sneaked out from the house to listen to their life stories. “It was very educational for me,” she used to say, “hearing other peoples hardships, suffering, and joy. Often when they discovered 1 was listening, they switched to spooky stories to scare me off, but I was not afraid.” Zdena said, “I trusted my mother, that ghosts and spooks are only in people’s minds, and that only people can hurt people and that most people are good and kind. What I did learn from those men’s stories, was human life is a myraid of small episodes, joyful and sad,and as a child, I made a commitment to myself that every day of my life, I would make somebody happy. Zdena started as a small baby to attend gym glasses. Every Tuesday and Thursday, she went to “Sokol” Gymnastic organization. First with her mother and sister, but in the third grade, she went alone without the escort. It was a two kilometer walk each way, by walking around the large city park, or only one and a half kilometers each way by walking through the park. When the days started to be short and the evenings dark, Zdena never walked through the park. “I was not afraid”, she recalled, “but why chance it? Around the park I felt safe. I could run fast, whistle like a boy and I was armed with gym shoes wrapped on fists.” Jaromir wrote a poem for Zdena in their native tongue for that occasion, and after the priest had concluded the service, Jaromir asked Zdena if she would like to hear it. “Yes, I do, I do.” She responded. Zdena was in tears, and declared it was the most beautiful poem Jaromir had ever written for her. Early the next morning, when Jaromir and her son Peter came to visit her, waking up from her dreams Zdena pointed to the door behind her where the doctors were talking and looking at Jaromir, Zdena said: “Listen carefully. Do you hear it? They are reading my poem from you.” “Do you really like it?” asked Jaromir. “Very, Very much.” Zdena answered, “and I thank you from the bottom of my heart for it.” Now at the conclusion of this remembrance of you, our dearest Zdena, your friend Eliska will read you “your poem” from Jaromir that you liked so much.
