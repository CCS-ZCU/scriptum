The Sokol ideology is based on the belief, that mankind should strive for physical and mental perfection: healthy mind in a healthy body. The founder of Sokol was M. Tyrš. He formulated the basic principles and led the movement for a number of years. From his early youth, and into his mature years as professor of philosophy at the Prague University, Tyrš was interested in the Greek and Roman civilizations, and particularly in the relationship of physical culture to the mental excellence of the ancient Greeks. He formed an opinion that their magnificent archievements in literature, arts, and philosophy were the results of sensible upbringing,which stressed the unity of mind and body, as we id as certain basic values and rules which are necessary for the so^Hid development of both. Like Darwin, Tyrš conceived .the evolution of the species as a continual fight between the healthy and strong against the weak and unfit. This rule applies to nations as well; a nation will perish once it has lost its vitality and stopped going forward. Neither its size, nor traditions or past glories can stop the effects of this law. Apathy, weakness and decadence kill nations as well as individuals; the fight for survival requires strength ---- non only physical, but mental and moral as well. During Tyrs's lifetime, the Czech nation was strugling for its very existence, preserving only with the greatest difficulty its traditions and language. In order to help the Czechs to win this fight, Tyrš started the Sokol movement. The first unit - Sokol Prague — was founded in 18Ó2. A period of rapid growth foil owed, during which Tyrš constantly strove to give Sokol its ideological direction and sound philosophical foundation. Most of his thoughts were embodied in a long article, titled "Our task, directions and goals", which became a kind of Gospel of the movement. It is difficult to condense the whole Sokol concept into a few lines. It stresses love of life, and everything that is good, and beautiful in it. The Sokol ideal is a man who loves goodness, and adheres to truth, progress; He loves his nation and the whole humanity. He willingly subordinates his ego to the needs of others and his group, knowing that from it stemms much of his strength.He is a democratic man, and believes in equality of all people. [pageend5]1 4 In the Sokol movement, a good man and selfless worker is valued a-, bove all others. He is judged by others not on the basis of hisfi- nances or power, but on his human values, on the deeds toward his fellow beings, for the group and for the nation. All Sokols are brothers and sisters to each otner. Hate and contempt are unthinkable. The*movement stands quite apart from politics: people, not programs are important. It tries to unite men of all progressive, political creeds and thus alleviate disagreements and frictions. This aim of complete objectivity and impartiality in regard to politics, has been rigidly and jealously maintained throughout Sokol history. In short, the movement — as conceived by Tyrš —is based on universal and eternally valid laws governing the behavior of sane human beings. It contains a blueprint for a better future for both, individuals and the nation. It particularly tries to influence the youth, to form their characters, develop their self-esteem, and their sense of responsibility toward themselves and toward their national group. It teaches them civic duties, decency, love of freedom, the principles of democracy. Sokol believes,that by producing strong bodies and characters in its members, it does significantly add to the strength and vitality of the whole nation Today, those ideas are as valid as they were a 100 years ago.Since the movement has been outlawed in the land in which it was born.it is all the more imperative for us here to keep it alive, develop, enlarge-and strengthen it. To be a good Sokol worker, to carry on the Sokol tradition, is a duty to our country and to ourselves. J.K.
