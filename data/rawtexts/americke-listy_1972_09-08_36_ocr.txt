Ever since, she represents the United States in the Olympics. We should consider it as a great honor for all American Czechs and Slovaks that one of us was selected to carry the star-spangled banner in Munich. However, we do not, because Mrs. Connolly has subsequently launched an attack on American policy and Government in her ar. tide in the New York- Times, where she voices what cannot be described otherwise than as political propaganda. Moreover, it is a very one-sided political propaganda. It may be her prerogative to speak up. but her super- l.beral stance and opposition to the US. Government and policies are not balanced by any criticism of Communist policies. Mrs. Connolly’s native country is still being occupied by Soviet forces, Czechoslovak athletes who do not openly support the Quisling regime cannot compete in the Olympics. She knows this fit I well. She knows the fate of the last Olympics’ favorite champion, Mrs. Vera Čáslavská Odložilová and of her husband, Josef Odložil, who cannot even get a decent job. because they had protested against the Soviet invasion. She knows that the famous runner Emil Zátopek who used to be an ardent supporter of Dubček, was totally broken by the pressures from the Communist police and had to recant his “heresy’’. How is it then possible that she snipes at President Nixon and American policy and keeps completely silent about the injustice and crimes. committeed against Czechoslovakia? What is the explanation for her lack of criticism of Soviet imperialist tactics, displayed not only toward the Soviet-block nations, but also in the Olympic Games? Mrs. Connolly knows better than anybody that athletes from the Communist countries are no amateurs. She used to be subsidized by the Communist government just as any other promising sportsman o r sportswoman whose sole job was to prepare themselves for international competition, not for several months, but over several years. The regime foots all their bills and keeps them in considerable comfort, as long as they do not voice any antagonistic po- Vtical views. Perhaps Mrs. Connolly has her valid reasons why she keeps silent about the Communists. We do not know what conditions they have imposed on her rohen she desperately wanted to leave Czechoslovakia; let us streess that it was still during the ultra-Stalinist period. Whatever the situation was and is, at least she should show some restraint in her anti-Esta- blshment rhetorics. Speaking of the Olympic spirit and brotherhood, she should have expressed her moral indignation over the fraudulent methods of the Communists. Speaking of peace, she should hare expressed a desire that all nations, particularly her native country, be given an opportunity to decide their own affairs and to obtain justice. Let us make it clear that Mrs. Connolly does not express the thinking and feeling for America of the overwhelming majority of • American Czechs and Slovaks. Painful as it is, it is our duty to speak up against her and reject her actions. V obou případech jde o roz- vratníky a nepřátele socialismu. Ergo: není-li postup československé a jugoslávské justice naprosto shodný, je tak podobný, že ho musíme soudit podle stejných měřítek. Události v Jugoslávii, o nichž se zde solidárně hovoří, mají být alibi pro vlastní akce “československých ko- munistů”. A komentátor záhřebského deníku Vjesnik dal jasně na srozuměnou, že toto alibi nejsou jugoslávští komunisté ochotní poskytnout. Není možné porovnávat — pokračoval Zvonimir Kristi — ani obsah soudního jednání ani metody soudů v Československu a v Jugoslávii; kdo to dělá, považuje Jugoslávii nesprávně — a v rozporu s jugoslávskou skutečností — za součást sovětského bloku. Jugoslávský komentátor dal při té příležitosti československým soudruhům radu: aby svou politiku laskavě dělali “svým vlastním jménem a na vlastní účet”. I Josef Martínek, když přijel z Cech do Ameriky, musel zatnout zuby a pilně studovat jazyk své nové vlasti . . , His hat is off to Geronimo Whose beautiful Arizona put poetry in his soul, Czech poet finds his dream in enchanting Arizona — Citizen Photo by Manuel Miera By JAY HALL Citizen Staff Writer ‘‘It is so wonderful,” mused Joseph Martinek, "that here I am spending my last years in what was the land of my enchantment when I was but a lad in Czechoslovakia.” Arizona became his land of enchantment when, in the Czech language, he read a dime type publication called "Geronimo, Chief of the Apaches.” This year, the 83-year-old Martinek is celebrating his 25th year in Arizona and his 60th as editor of a number of Czech publications in America. But it is as a poet that Martinek is most interesting. Long before he emigrated to Cleveland in 1909, at the age of 20, he had been turning out poetry in his native tongue. Although poetry continued in his soul in America, he found the general style of it was altogether different from that poetry in Czechoslovakia. “In the old land,” he explained, “much stress was laid on rhyme, alliteration and assonance. In America, rhyme was not too strong and not much attention was paid to assonance (sound resemblances of words or syllables.) “So one day I figured the whole thing out. I would adapt in my poems in English, to the free verse and go strong on alliteration.” He had a poem published in an obscure Texas publication which, to his delighted amazement soon afterward, was reprinted in the New York Herald-Tribune. He now is in Who’s Who in America, Who’s Who in the West and International Who’s Who in Poetry. His major work in English is called “Songs of the Desert.” Other poems in the Czech lang- uage are about the Papago, the Hopi and the Navajo. He now carries on from what he calls his "Mexican shack” at 2931 N. Edith Blvd, his work as editor-in- chief of Americké Listy, an antiCommunist weekly newspaper published in Perth Amboy, N. J., for the Czech population of America. By air mail and long-distance telephone, he gets his articles into the paper. More poems, too, are being written and being compiled into books. Meanwhile, here’s an aperitif from “Songs of the Desert:” Desert, my pet, My beautiful tigress, Your Indian war paint Does not scare me, I don’t scare easily, I can see through your mask, You are not so fierce As you appear Wearing your summer war bonnet. Haven’t I surprised you Smiling for yourself But through a sieve of spines Tossing at me One of your April smiles Of cactus blossoms? And haven't I forced a smile Upon your proud and parched lips With my patch of zinnias I planted last May?
