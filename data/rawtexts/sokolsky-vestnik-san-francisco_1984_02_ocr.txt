[pageend4]MARCH-APRIL, 1984 SOKOL SAN FRANCISCO VĚSTNÍK, STRANA-PAGE 5 Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk (1850-1937), founder and first President of Czechoslovakia, was one of the most universally revered liberal democrats of modern times — a man who perhaps came closer than any other to embodying the Platonic ideal of the philosopher-statesman. The life, work, and outlook of this leader may seem to have been smothered by the fall of the small democratic state of Czechoslovakia before the MACHTPOLITIK of Hitler's Third Reich. Seven years of Nazi domination and almost half a century of Communist rule, however, do not appear to have obliterated the seeds of democracy which Masaryk so realistically sowed and nourished. Now, more than four decades after his death, the ideals and life work of this social philosopher and statesman still live - his legacy to all - especially to those of Czechoslovak heritage. We were Masaryk's hope — those future generations that would carry on his beliefs and ideals. Yet, what the future brought instead, was a nightmare for his beloved country, — for our beloved country. What would Masaryk have done had he lived to see the ensuing years of subjugation Czechoslovakia would have to endure? Strength in the face of adversity would surely have been one of his mottos. We must be that strength. Masaryk gave all that he could -- it is our turn now. We must prove ourselves worthy of the legacy he left us, and, bear witness to the fact that truth and freedom are worth fighting for - settling for anything less amounts to the death of our spirit. The fight for the freedom of the Czechoslovaks, just as the fight for the freedom of man everywhere, continues. As Henry W. Nevinson so aptly stated, "The battle of freedom is never done, and the field never quiet." Yet, we should be happy the battle is still being fought - that the human race has not given up, that we are not living in George Orwell's 1984. In light of this, perhaps we have become Masaryk's strength. We chose freedom over tyranny, justice over injustice. We keep celebrating Masaryk's ideals.and beliefs, and we must keep on doing so. We must adhere to that same spirit of democracy and faith in freedom to which this heroic man gave life. They have withstood the test of time -— and that attests to the wisdom and greatness of the first President of Czechoslovakia Masaryk's beliefs are, in part, epitomized by the following quote attributed to George Orwell: "There was truth and there was untruth, and if you clung to the truth, even against the whole world, you were not mad." We are the ones that must, in fact, ensure that "Truth Will Prevai1". (Speech given by Sr. Zuzana Bursik at the program commemorating the birthday of Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk.) BEDRICH SMETANA 1824-1884 was one of the three leading composers of the Czech nation, beside Antonin Dvorak and Leos Janaček. His aim was to produce a Czech national music based on folk songs and folk dances. He expressed that tendency most clearly in the "Bartered Bride" in 1866, one of the greatest of all comic operas. His other operas include "Two Widows", "The Kiss", "The Secret", and "Libuše". Smetana also composed symphonies. His cycle of six symphonic poems called "My Country" (Ma Vlast) ranks foremost among his orchestral music. "My Country" contains one of Smetana's best known works, "The Moldau" (Vltava). Bedrich Smetana was born in Litomyšl, Bohemia, near Pardubice. He conducted the Goteborg, Sweden orchestra from 1856 to 1861 and became conductor of the National Theatre in Prague in 1866. He resigned the position in 1874 when he suddenly became deaf. Bedřich Smetana Centennial In the Centennial Commemoration of this great Czech composer we would like to present a collection of his greatest works in the Starlight Bowl in Burbank on July 14, at 8:30 pm to be performed by the Burbank Symphony Orchestra, conductor Hansel Rayner, in conjunction with the Czechoslovak Dancers, Director Josef Jaki. The work received the second prize — the first prize was not awarded. The composition has an interesting history. In 1919 Suk decided to write a military march, so that his teenage son "could once march to the sound of his dad's march," in case he volunteered for the new Czechoslovak army then engaged in the conflict with Hungary in Slovakia. He composed a draft in one day and put it aside as he lacked a text. A year later a competition to create a march for the upcoming Vllth Sokol Festival caught his attention. Suk revised and finished the march, submitted it in a piano 4-hand form under a pseudonym, and won the first prize, though the jury had some reservation because of its rather symphonic conception. After some further modifications Suk played this Sokol March for the first time for President T. G. Masaryk, during a visit at the Drtina family. He asked the poet Machar to write a text to the march, but Machar declined. A piano version of the march was published in 1921, with the title Toward a New Life. The composer later added the opus number 35c, thus connecting it with his Meditation on the Chorale "St.Venceslas" (op. 35a, 1914) and the Legend of Dead Victors (oo. 35b, 1919) written to honor the Czech legionaries fallen during WWI. However, Suk stilj. continued to search for a text to his march. In 1930 the poet Petr Kricka consented to undertake this difficult task of creating the lyrics to existing music. The final version of the march Toward a New Life was then first performed in Prague on December 3, 1931, with the Czech Philharmonic and the choir "Smetana." Yet the march became most popular in various less exacting arrangements, e.g., for a band, piano 2-hands, or a small orchestra. The translations of the poem into English by Pavel Selver and into French by Gaston Milliot were available by the time the march was played in Los Angeles.
