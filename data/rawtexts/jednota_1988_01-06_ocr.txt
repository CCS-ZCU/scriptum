THE CAUSE OF DEATH A main feature of the JEDNOTA newspaper over the years has been the monthly death list of Society members. In recent times the names of the deceased members are listed along with their branch and town, but seventy years ago the paper also listed the age of the deceased member, date of admission to the organiz tion. and the cause of death. A study of the causes of efrath of Jednota members f vb ty years ago can reveal much about the early life of the Slovak immigrant. In looking at these lists one is Immediately struck by the young ages at death Ťhere were few people over 60 and many times few over 50 years of age. In June. 1917. for example. the average age of death was 43.6 years: November. 1917. 43-5 years and in August. 1918. 45.7 years. This low life expectancy can be partially explained by looking at the age of the Slovak immigrant. In 1917 there were probably few immigrants over 65 , years of age. Most Slovak immigrants arrived in the United States as young men. Most had been bom in the 1860s and 1870s. so by 1917 they were only in their 40s and 50s. Still, there was an alarming number of deaths of members during the prime of their lives. The leading cause of death today in the United States is cardiovascular disease, followed by Cancer, and with diabetes around number 10. ■ Butin 1917 and 1918 diabetes was almost unheard of and heart disease, although it existed. was not what it is today. In the Jednota Society seventy years ago the main cause of death would vary a little but most of the time It was suchotiny or tuberculosis. In January. 1916 deaths from 30% of all deaths: in March. 1916. 15%, The number of deaths from tuberculosis variecLeach month from-10% to over 30%. With the exception of alcoholism, tuberculosis was the main health concern In the Jednota organization. In October 1907 Reverend Jan Por ubsky wrote a series of informative articles on tuberculosis and in January. 1918 there appeared a study on the disease. "O suchotinách." written by Dr. A. Weismayer and translated into Slovak by Reverend Anthony Pavco. These articles attempted to explain the nature of the disease of tuberculosis (or consumption, as it was called then), its course, causes and treatment. A second leading cause of death during the early years of the organization was by accidents. Nearly every month there were Jednota members killed in factories, in mines, by trains or by drowning. In December. 1917 accidents made up a staggering 31% of all deaths. The third leading cause of death was pneumonia. For certain (ypesof pneumonia there was no treatment and death was almost certain. Other causes of death included heart disease, stroke, typhoid fever, appendicitis, dropsy (edema), nephritis, cancer, gall bladder, and others, many of which are treated today as routine. A look at (he death lists of our organization seventy years ago is a sobering reminder of the difficulties and tragedies faced by the early Slovak immigrants.
