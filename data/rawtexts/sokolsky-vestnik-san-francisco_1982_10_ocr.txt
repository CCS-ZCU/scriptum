[pageend3]As per request of our readers, we are presenting another of the “Lunettas” by the beloved, great Czech artist, Mikoláš Aleš - this one for the month of May. Mikoláš Aleš was the greatest Czech artist to paint and draw pictures on historical themes. His creative art came closest to the heart of Czech People. He was an avid reader of old Czech chronicles, and himself a chronicler of his people to whom he gave pictures illustrating great epochs of Czech history, so that they could become familiar with them and draw strength from them for the present and the future. Ales’s interest in Czech history which provided him with a rich source for his art, began with the distant mythical past in the dim twilight of myths and legends. His drawings have beautiful purity, charm and lightness. They are full of wisdom and easily understandable. He was capable of creating serious and splendid works, but at the same time his simple little drawings gave evidence of his warm humor. When the occasion demanded, he could create gay and amusing little pictures and illustrations which spoke directly to the broadest masses of the people. In these illustrations, he brought to life the countryside, its nature, its eternal charm and poesy, as well as the wisdom of the world of fairy tales, the day-to-day work on little farms, the changing seasons, children’s games, the rhythm of nursery rhymes and songs. Ales’s illustrations of folk songs were amongst his finest of his works. It was in these little charming drawings that he was closest to the people. Besides his individual great works, an important place was occupied by his cycles of paintings in which he made full use of his splendid compositional ability. In first place in this sphere is his cycle of 14 lunettas, called “The Homeland,” which adorn the walls of the foyer of the National Theatre in Prague. The entire work of Mikoláš Aleš is permeated by love of life. It could only have been created by an artist who knew how his people lived, who had shared their poverty and hardships. And he remained faithful to his people throughout his life and in his entire creative work, which remains for all time a treasure of Czech national culture. Lecture was given by Marie Provazníkova. .1 One hundred years ago, to be exact on February 16, 1862, the "Prague Gymnastic Unit" came into being at the Prague Normal School, located then in Panska Street, Prague, Bohemia. At first it appeared that this would become a bilingual , Czech-German organization. At least these were the intentions of the gymnasts at the Schmidt Gymnastic Institute, which was then very close to extinction. These enthusiasts had already begun to solicit subscriptions among Czechs and Germans to support this organization. The only reason the planned bi-lingual club did not materialize was the opposition of the hard core chauvinist German bank-director Seutter vonLeutzen. This philantropist refused bluntly to contribute to a Czech- German organization, but promised very substantial support if the new society would be strictly Garman, This led to the final decision. The Germans cancelled their agreement with the Czechs, who withdrew their subscriptions and decided to establish an independent Czech Gymnastic Club. The then thirty years old Dr.Miroslav Tyrs readily joined this movement. He saw in this a clear path towards the realization of a far more important idea, which had been germinating for some time in his mind. All this took place during the wave of youthful patriotic bustle that spread throughout the Czech nation following the demise of Bach’s absolutism. Youthful national life began to pour forth across the lands. Tyrs was at this particular time employed as tutor in the family of industrialist Bartelmus in New Jáchymov. This naturally excluded him from direct participation in the wild turbulent life of the big city. He was thus able to think through in peace and quiet on the needs of his nation, and how he could best contribute to its upward rise. In a letter written about that time he confesses his quest for a role then as yet not provided for and one which he felt he could fill better than anyone else. At this time in histoxy the National Rebirth (# 1) was almost accomplished linguistically, artistically, scientifically, economically, and socially. But this did not satisfy young Tyrs. He was constatntly being disturbed by the question about his nation’s future. In his philosophic studies he was deeply involved in Darwin’s theory of the evolution of species and the survival of the fittest. In his studies in history he was deeply concerned with the question of the destruction of ancient nations. To be continuedI page #1. [pageend6]LO YOU KNOW THIS FELLOW? - - - - According to Slavic Mythology - in old Bohemia, his name is SETEK, a household God. He is believed to resemble a small boy with claws on his hands and feet, and h< generally stayed in the sheep shed, though he also hid in the flour, or in the peas, or on a wild pear, while in winter he sat on the oven and warmed himself. The Setek protects the flocks from disease and brings good harvests and money; and he also - so it is said - was able to go without eating and drinking for nine years, returning, after the lapse of this time, to the place of his birth, where he annoyed everyone. He may be bred out of an egg carried for nine days in the armpit. In the belief of the Styrian Slovenians the Setek of olden times was a good spirit, about the size of a thumb, who generally haunted places where salt was kept, or lived in stables near young cattle. Unless a portion of all that was boiled or roasted was put aside ■for him, he caused the fire in the oven to go out, or made the pans crack, or caused the cows to yield blood instead of milk, etc. Being of small size, he could hide in any place and play tricks on those who teased him. Another Hobgoblin was known as Skritek, and this goblin appear in the shape oi a small boy. He usually lived behind the oven or in the stable, favouring the household and sharing the joys and sorrows of the family; and he liked to o some work in the home, such as weaving on the loom, sweeping the floor, or tending the flocks. In order to court his favour, the household set aside a portion of their meals or his consumption, especially on Thursdays and at Christmas dinner, when three bits from every dish were assigned to him.
