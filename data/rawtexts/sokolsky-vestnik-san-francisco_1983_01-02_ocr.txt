Stir In chopped nuts. Set aside 2 cups of the mixture. Lightly press remainder of crumbs Into a B^by-13 Inch pan. Bake In a 325° oven for 15 minutes. In a bowl beat together the cream cheese and sugar until blended. Stir In milk, lemon peel, lemon Juice, vanilla and eggs. Beat until smooth. Pour over crust. [pageend6]— Continued from December The rapid growth of the new movement aroused the suspicion of the Austrian government who saw in it the center for '"subversive elements." This is why they fought against every effort aimed at concentrating the Sokol Units into a higher organizational unit. They even forbade the Prague Sokol from extending its physical education classes to units outside of Prague. Tyrs saw clearly the need for concentrated, unified leadership as a means of preserving the basic idea. With this in mind he kept flooding the authorities with one suggestion after another, but to his death none of these were realized. However he did not turn away from his basic thought, and continued to overcome the organizational insufficiency with mutual visits among the units. He did so with sending out well qualified gymnastics instructors, also by the publication of a year book and finally by establishing the "Sokol" periodical, To presaxvs the basic concepts during this violent evolution it was necessary to establish a clear program both as to its ideological concepts as well as professionally organized material pertaining to physical education. With this in mind Tyrs covered the ideological concept in his article "Our Task, Direction and Goal," which became virtually a Sokol bible and has not lost its importance to this very day. For the actual physical education tasks he developed a system, terminology and method, which still have not been improved upon by anyone. Tyrs even worked out the organizational rules and regulations and bylaws. Thus he could justly declare that th® Sokol idea was his creation from head to toe. It was not until after Tyrs’ death that it was permitted to combine several units into districts which later became the Czech-Moravian Sokol Community. It was not until 1906 that all the sovaral districts together with the Lower Austria district formed the only Czech Sokol Community.(2) All of Tyrs* efforts weife directed to one culminating goal namely the placing of our nation amidst other free nations as equal among equals.At the beginning he had faith in the possibility of realizing this project within the framework of federated Austria, a belief shared at that time by all leading Czech politicians. This hope or faith is corroborated by the fact that during the Austrian-Prussian war in 1866, Tyrs offered the Austrian government the support of several volunteer Sokol corps, an offer which was not accepted. However the creation of the dual monarchy finally led Tyrs away from this illusory hope. From then on it was evident that the nation cannot wait for the moment of justice and that it will have to win its rights by its own armed might. From thia time too the need for ability to bear arms is emphasized in Tyrs’ declarations. "Arms in every fist. Military organization. Let it be said hundred times, yes,, thousand times." In his very study under the title "The extent to which gymnastics and Sokol Units contribute to national armed preparedness" Tyrs is seriously thinking of armed equipment for the Sokols, but he places physical fitness above this. He stresses this in the followings "Weapons can easily be bought; people can be fairly well trained in a short time, and can even be inspired, but they cannot acquire physical bellicose characteristics, here only many years of preparation can have decisive results." —4— Continued next month [pageend7]Marriott Hotel. More or less this superstition survived even to our times. Whenever this thrilling performance of nature is observed, superstitious man connects its appearance with such natural catastrophies as are earthquakes, floods, epidemics, etc. Science, however, believes that these celestial bodies actually are composed of methodane, ammonia and water. This combination forms icy water in the cold regions and makes up 70 to 80 percent of the comet head. This icy matter contains rocky and metallic material which is formed from interstellar dust. As it nears the sun, the outer layers evaporate and release particles and gasses, which form the tail of the comet. The comets, however, have characteristics which do not depend on the above theory. For instance, they follow an eliptical path around the sun which is called a period. They orbit the sun just as planets do and during such orbits the head of the comet always faces the sun, leaving the tail streaming behind. Her bright light is partly reflected sunlight and partly the luminescent glow of particles caused by the sunlight. It has been calculated that the sparkling central core or nucleus may be some 8,000 miles in diameter. The head'surrounds the nucleus and may extend up to 100.000 miles. The tail is 100,000 miles long. Astronomers discover new comets from time to time, the newest one, the Comet Kohoutek, was discovered by the Czech astronomer, Dr. Lubomír Kohoutek. One of the most famous is Halley’s Comet, named for Prof. Edmund Halley, an English astronomer who foretold its return in 1682. This is the comet we are referring to at the beginning of this article. According to the old scriptures, it was first seen in 240 B.C. It has a 75.1 year period. It was seen last in 1910 and should reappear again in 1986. This is the famous comet that came so close to the earth in 1910 that its tail actually fell across the entire earth, making it visible without a telescope. Its return in 1986 has been eagerly awaited by the astronomers all over the world, as it will enable them to study and learn more about this noted visitor from space.
