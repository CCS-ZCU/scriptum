ISSN Following the recent elections, Dr. Miloslav Rechcigl Jr. has been inducted as the new President of the Czechoslovak Society of Arts and Sciences (SVU), at the General Assembly meeting on August 28. The meeting was held in Brno on the occasion of the Society’s 18th World Congress. Mila Rechcigl, as he is generally known, is a native of Mlada Boleslav, who has lived since 1950 in the U.S. After receiving a scholarship, he went to Cornell University where he studied from 1951-58, receiving his B.S., M.N.S., and Ph.D. degrees there, specilizing in biochemistry, nutrition, physiology, and food science. He then spent two years conducting research at the National Institutes of Health as a postdoctoral research fellow. Subsequently he was appointed to the staff of the Laboratory of Biochemistry at the National Cancer Institute. During 1968-1969 he was selected for one year of training in a special USPHS executive program, in research management, grants administration, and science policy. This led to his appointment as Special Assistant for Nutrition and Health in the Health Services and Mental Health Administration. In 1970 he joined the Agency for International Development as Nutrition Advisor and soon after he was promoted to the position of Chief of Research and Institutional Grants Division. Later on he became a Director with the responsibility for reviewing, administering and managing AID research. He is the author or editor of over thirty books and handbooks in the field of biochemistry, physiology, nutrition, food science and technology, agriculture, and international development, in addition to a large number of his scientific articles and book chapters. [pageend4]The newly elected SVU Executive Board had its first formal meeting on September 28th at the Rechcigls’ house in Rockville, MD. In addition to seven Executive Board members (Rechcigl, Skalný, Ulbrecht, Mucha, Borkovec. Cerny, Hasalova-White) present were also B. Kudej (former Secretary General) and A. Elias (Editor, Zprávy SVU). The main points of discussion were the SVU goals, its priorities, strategy and the planning of future activities in the upcoming term. Cooperation between the members in CR and SR and those abroad remains the top priority, with three Vice Presidents assigned to this task, i.e. Juraj Slavik, Alexander Tkac and Pavel Novak. Work will continue on the development of an effective Information Clearing House. In order to satisfy concerns of the members on the North American continent, where the large bulk of the SVU members reside, President Rechcigl proposed that the next Annual Meeting be held in the U.S. In conjunction with the meeting a special conference will be held on some suitable topic of interest to American and Canadian SVU members. The Executive Board decided in favor of such conference, preferably in an area where Czech/Slovak emigrants settled, such as Texas, Nebraska, or Iowa. In its evaluation of the SVU Congress in Brno, the Executive Board concluded that it exceeded all expectations. The academic program, as a whole, was of high quality, as was the music program prepared by the Brno organizers. The official opening with its academic procession of the University Deans and Rectors in their splendid academic attire, while the musicians trumpeted, was an unforgettable sight. The artistic performance of the musicians who came primarily from Moravia was equally impressive. The gala party at the Spilberk Castle was an incredible feat which topped all social events in the past. The Brno organizers went out of their way to make the SVU Congress a great success and an event which the participants won’t ever forget. The Executive Board again wishes to express its gratitude and thanks to the Brno hosts and particularly to Masaryk University for their initiative, enthusiastic help and friendship, as well as sharing with the SVU visitors their historical heritage and splendid culture. Part of the meeting was devoted to discussing the „modus operandi” of the Executive Board and the responsibilities of the individual Board members. The consensus was that there should be more equity between the members and that attendance of meetings is mandatory. In terms of the long-term plans, the Executive Board wants to revise the SVU Mission Statement and make efforts towards rejuvenation of the Society. In addition to the emphasis on recruitment of young members, the Executive Board will attempt to identify promising individuals from the younger ranks for various SVU Committees and special activities they could take lead in. As far as SVU finances are concerned, the Executive Board renewed the pledge of the past Board to balance its budget and make it a “sine qua non” for all its activities. The European members will again be reminded to pay their dues directly to the Treasurer if they wish to retain their membership status and their right to participate in election. A decision has been made to publsish a new SVU Directory which has been necessitated by the numerous changes, especially in the members’ addresses, as well as the addition of new members since 1992. This time only those members’ biographies will be listed who have paid their membership dues directly to the SVU Treasurer. A substantial part of the meeting was devoted to the problems of logistics of printing and distribution of the SVU periodicals. Zprávy SVU and the Kosmas. Before 1914 he travelled extensively in Austria-Hungary and introduced the Slovaks to the English-speaking world. During the First World War he assisted T. G. Masaryk and the movements for Czech and Slovak independence to great effect. Between the wars, as Masaryk Professor of Central European History at the School of Slavonic and East European Studies in the University of London, he was the chief British expounder of Czech and Slovak History, and a tireless advocate of the interests of the Czechoslovak state. In 1938-39 he was an uncompromising critic of the Munich settlement, and during the Second World Was campaigned for Czechoslovakia’s restoration. Except in the years 1917-18 and 1939-42, Seton-Watson held no official post; he exercised his influence as a private citizen, publicist and scholar. This work is a joint Czech and Slovak publication, trilingual (English, Czech and Slovak), and has an international dimension. Its three editors — Jan Rychlík, Thomas D. Marzik and Miroslav Bielik — are Czech, American and Slovak. Volume I contains the documents (letters, memoranda, published articles) in their original language, mostly English and German. It has a biographical introduction in English (with a Slovak translation) by R. W. Seton-Watson’s younger son, Christopher. Volume II contains six sections, in both the English and Czech/Slovak languages: a Preface; a Register of documents; a list of R. W. Seton-Watson’s itineraries in Central and Eastern Europe 1905-1946; a bibliography of his main publications relating to the Czechs and Slovaks; a Name index; and a Geographical index. The Name index, which features biographical sketches of about six hundred individuals mentioned in the documents of Volume I, is an especially important reference source for the study of Anglo-Czechoslovak relations and of the history of the peoples of Czechoslovakia during the first half of the twentieth century. František Krčma se narodil 16. ledna 1910 v Domažlicích, vystudoval v Praze práva, byl blízkým přítelem Olgy Schneipflugové, vydavatelem jejích spisů, dlouhá léta předsedou Společnosti bratří Čapků a účastníkem veškerého kulturního dění v Praze. Mimoto byl i vynikajícím sportovcem: byl to talentovaný atlet, hokejista, šachista, hráč golfu a rozhlasový sportovní reportér. O tom všem se psalo v českých novinách. Méně však o jeho činnosti v SVU. Mnozí z nás, kteří pokládáme Františka za svého přítele, vzpomínáme s obdivem na jeho poslední — pátou v životě — cestu do Ameriky. Přijel na pozvání SVU na 15. světový kongres, který se konal 11.-14. října 1990 v Torontu v Kanadě. Bylo mu už 80 let. Přednášel na téma Karel Čapek a Olga Schneipflugová. Z Toronta dělal dlouhou cestu do Los Angeles, odtud pak do Washingtonu a New Yorku, kde přednášel na toto téma v místních skupinách. Když jsme se s ním ve Washingtonu loučili a odjížděl od nás do New Yorku k dr. Steinbachovi, netušili jsem, že ani jednoho z nich už neuvidíme. Jeho přítel Karel Steinbach ho však předběhl. František Krčma byl poslední léta vážně nemocen, ale dožil se letošního jara. V Praze pěkně zhodnotil život Františka Krčmy jeho přítel Jiří Ješ: „František Krčma se nespokojil jen s pečlivou prací na souborném vydávání spisů Olgu Schneipflugové a doplňování edice Karla Čapka, ale také onou masarykovsky drobnou prací, kdy v dobách nelehkých s chytrým využíváním právnických kliček udržoval komunisty nemilovanou Společnost bratří Čapků a v ní pomáhal organizovat takové programy, které pro mnoho lidí byly světlem v dobách temna. Spojuji dnes záměrné jméno Alice Masarykové s Františkem Krčmou nejen proto, že oba téměř ve stejnou chvíli zemřeli, ale proto, že oba mohou být příklady toho, jak lidé vybaveni pocitem odpovědnosti vůči svému užšímu i širšímu okolí mohou do vysokého věku velmi účinně napomáhat k tomu, aby tato země byla zafixována do kulturního podvědomí civilizovaného světa." Speech Presented by Dr. Dagmar Hasalova White at the Wilson House, October 28,1996 In any nation’s existence a future without any connection to the past does not exist. So it is with the Czech nation. Looking back to the past we mold our future. From the distant past there runs an unbreakable line that links us through glory, tragedy, and sometimes shame to the present time. On September 14 an exhibit of 1,000 years of Czech culture was inaugurated at the Gallery in Old Salem, Winston- Salem, North Carolina. It contains many riches from the National Museum in Prague, never before seen outside the country. The exhibit will be continued through March 16, 1997. Why in Winston-Salem? Because the town of Salem, now restored as Old Salem, has an historic connection to the exhibit. Salem was established in 1766 by members of the Moravian Church. Known as Moravians here, they have their ancestoral roots in Moravia and Bohemia, which are part of today’s Czech Repubic. The exhibit shows the continuity of a nation through a thousand years of varied history, through sovereignty, tremendous expansion, but also subjugation. It also shows the unquenchable spirit of the nation for freedom, for which it has fought again and again. Through the pages of its history are marching talented and industrious people with their leaders forging and renewing the nation. The line stretches from the dawn of history beginning with Chieftain Czech after whom the nation was named. Cyril and Methodius who brought Christianity to the land, and creating the Slavonic script planted the seed of Czech literature and education. The Premyslid Dynasty with St. Wenceslaus, the patron of the Czech lands, the Holy Roman Emperor and Bohemian King Charles IV, Jan Hus and Comenius, but also the Habsburgs and the Battle of White Mountain. A few centuries later comes the National Revival and the movement toward independence, culminating in the realization of Masaryk’s dream of a free and independent Czechoslovakia. In the parade of leaders who had a great impact on the nation stands next to president Tomas Masaryk American President Woodrow Wilson. He stood at the cradle of the new nation and with his 14 points on self determination helped to secure its independence. President Masaryk spoke of those who fought for the independence during World War I as “men, understanding their times who made decisions for action and sacrifice.” Alas, Masaryk’s humanitarian democracy lasted only twenty years, 11 Ján Simko, Washington, D.C. [pageend11]a mere step in the passage of history. A new world war and struggle under Nazi occupation again sent “men understanding their times” to join the Czechoslovak armed forces abroad or on the home front to fight for freedom. After a short-lived post-war freedom a dark cloud of communist tyranny settled over the nation for forty years. Again the nation rose. With the new liberation of the Velvet Revolution a new generation who, as Masaryk said, understood their times would make the right decisions and bring true democracy, prosperity, and lasting peace to their country. Today in commemorating the foundation of the Czechoslovak Repubic on October 28, 1918 we are reaching through the past to the link of history connecting us to the present times. In another thousand years what will a new exhibit tell about the Czech nation? Hopefully it will say that they looked on the past and learned from it, that they looked squarely in the eye of the future and that they persevered.
