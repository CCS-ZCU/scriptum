Červenec-July 1981 THE TRAGIC ANNIVERSARY. On the 10th of May 1940 at 05.35 a.m. the German army launched its military campaign against the Western Allies. Facing the Belgium and Luxemburg borders were two German Army Groups commanded by generals Von Beck and Von Rundstedt; there was also one tank group commanded by general Von Kleist. The Allied Command didn't even think that the main thrust of the German attack could be directed through the Ardennes Highland and left to its defense only units consisting of reservists, i.e. older and less trained soldiers. On top of this, the Northern Group of French army started that day to move in a north-easterly direction to render help to the Dutch troops, which were attacked that morning. The Germans opened their assault with the support of their bombers and dive-bombers in the direction of the Sedan - Dinant - Namur line. Because they met only negligible resistance, the German army already reached the eastern bank of the river Meuse on May 12th and two days later Sedan fell, th On the 15 of May after the crossing of the Aarden Canal, the Germans occupied Rossy sur Seine, more than 30 miles west of Sedan. On May 17th Bruxelles fell. In the main direction of this thrust the German army penetrated to the vicinity of Amiens and reached Boulogne-sur- Mer. , On May 28L the Belgian army laid down their arms and the British Expeditionary Force began its evacuation at Dunkerque, which was a legend in its own right. The defense of Northern France began to break down and it became painfully evident that the fabeled Maginot Line would be France's grave instead of a shield against German attack. For this reason an order was issued by the French High Command to bring in the Czechoslovak division to the most vulnerable part of the front. This division was concentrated first in the general area of Mon- tigny-sur-Aube. During the days of 11th and 12th of June 1940, our 1st regiment moved under the command of the French 23rd division and the 2nd regiment was moved to the 239th French division. This unfortunate manoeuvre parted both Czechoslovak regiments and, therefore, their destinies until the evacuation were different even though similar in a sense Our 1S^ regiment together with the 2n<^ and 3r<^ batallion of the French 126th regiment took over the bridgehead of the river Grand Morin in the Boissy-le-Chatel - Coulommiers - Mouroux sector. Early in the night of 13-14 of June the Germans penetrated to the right of this division and this development necessitated a move to the south bank of the river. At 23;00 hours the divisional commander issued an order to retreat and the Czechoslovak regiment was given the task of covering the retreat of French units. With minor changes the retreat continued to Montereau, where it changed into panic. The highways were blocked by civilian refugees and represented a formidable obstacle to moving military units. After considerable battle losses at Gien, the commander of the Czechoslovak division announced that Czechoslovak units would retreat [pageend2]Červenec-July 19bl Věstník Sokola San Francisco Strana-Page 3. on their own to the south coast of France. The superior French officers O.K.'d this order, as there was nothing else to do in that existing chaos. The 2nd Czechoslovak regiment got its first battle order on June 6, 1940 to hold the river Marne crossings about eight miles north of Coulommiers. Two days later, just when the regiment took over that segment, our soldiers beat off the enemy's first reconnaissance units and the next day encountered the advancing main enemy body. Our 2nd regiment defended the south bank of the river Marne in spite of the fact that its left neighbor, the French 59^ infantry regiment, began a premature retreat. Not until the following night did the Czechs begin their retreat, and formed a defense line on the south bank of the river Grand Morin. After a two hour long exchange of fire our units began a retreat which was again covered by the 1st battailion and a battery of anti-tank guns (KPUV) to slow down the enemy. Still the same evening, the regiment crossed the river Seine in the vicinity of Comery-Moret and on the 16th of June the regiment reached Gien where it formed a defense line alongside the south bank of the river Loire. The next two days were spent in a feverish build-up of the fundamental system of defense. The regiment got only a very limited supply of ammunition and on the 18^ of June it was involved in a successful encounter with the enemy but only for one reason: to again cover the chaotic retreat of the French troops. It is, unfortunately, a sad truth, that our units got their ammunition from retreating French troops in order to have something to fire at the perfectly equiped Germans. A detachment of Czech engineers and communication troops which were directly subordinated to a French High Command had to fight their own way from the vicinity of Bar-Sur-Ource all the way to Bordeaux, where they merged with a detachment of our airmen. On June 22, 1940 the French government representatives signed in the memorable railway-carriage at Compiegne the armistice with Germany and on June 25th at 01.35 a.m. France ceased fighting. France was on her knees and her army, as even the French historians describe it, wasn't anything else but a panic-stricken, unruly crowd of infantry men, without equipment and very often without the weapons which they cast into ditches while retreating. To top off the chaos the soldiers were joined by retreating civilians, moving all their belongings on any type vehicles in the general direction of retreat. No wonder that under such circumstances the Czechoslovak units' kept contact with each other with utmost difficulty. It happened much too often that the commanders of French units gave retreat orders to their units without notifying our units, which caused a dangerous situation for the suddenly exposed flanks of our units which were resisting and slowing down the enemy's onslought. The disintegrated French army not only left the Czechoslovak units to their fate but on top of that they hindered and were in the way while our units fought the enemy. Evaluation of our merits in the battle of France came from the French General of the Army Faucher, chief of the French Military Mission with the Czechoslovak Army in France. He said, " You accomplished under the most difficult circumstances what was in human power. You absolved the exhausting forced marches in highest discipline and order; you fought brilliantly and kept cool under the heaviest bombardment as ought to be expected from Czechoslovak soldiers and you won a full acknowledgement of all French higher commanders." Nobody knew what would happen next. Fantastic rumors spread and chaos reigned even in the as yet unoccupied France. Czecholovak. exile leaders, after seemingly endless difficulties, received from English leaders a promise to furnish ship^ for transportation of our units to England. Our units-more or less instinctively- found their way to the southern ports of s£te, Porte Vendre, Marseille and Bordeaux. On the 17th of June, 1940, a special order of the day was issued with instructions for evacuation of our units. It stated, that the Czechoslovak units will continue their fight against Germany, (See: Special order of the Headquarters of the 1st Czechoslovak division at Beziers dated June 17, 1940). It contained also that fatefull clause that in those units will remain the volunteers only. It is a fact that this order of the day was revoked in a few hours but meanwhile those men who were mobilized into our army from among Czechoslovak residents in France simply disappeared into thin air. Today we know that that clause was there because nobody knew if we would get enough transports to England. The result of all these phenomenons and their causes was that three-fifths of our soldiers quit the Czechoslovak army. Britania, Appapa, Neuralia, Mohamed Ali el Kebir, Rod El Farey, Forbin and several others were the ships given to our disposal to transport us to Gibraltar and from there in a convoy under the protection of the historic battleship Hood we reached without incident the port of Liverpool. This was how the remnants of the Czechoslovak Army in France reached England. It was the only higher military unit reaching England fully armed even with ammunition, a fact unique in those days. That was how Czechoslovak soldiers recompensated for the betrayal of Czechoslovakia by French politicians in the days of Munich. Our gratitude and honor to those who lost their lives and thanks to those who steadfastly kept fighting for our cause until the end of the war. V.F.B. Proto jsme ztrojnásobili dávky maláriových pilulek. Také jsme se naučili první slovo ve svahilštině - jambo - což znamená - dobrý den. V utery 10. března konečně opouštíme Tanzanii přejetím mostu přes Rusumu Falls, do prosperující zemičky Rwanda. Zde na nás *ekají novinky. Až dosud, počínaje Jihoafrickou Republikou jsme byli v zemích pod britskou správou, což mělo za následek, že mnoho lidí mluvilo anglicky a silniční doprava je po levé straně silnice. Počínaje Rmndou vstupujeme do zemí které jsou nebo byly pod francouzskou nebo belgickou správou a jazyk se tedy mění na francouzštinu a doprava se stěhuje na pravou stranu. Obyvatelé jsou přátelští, ale co se týče táboření, nejsou moc nadšeni. Cestovní ruch tu není podporován. Je to pěkná země, plná zeleně, kopců a údolí. Je zde přelidnění, takže každý kousíček půdy je obděláván. Na kopcích jsou políčka na terasách. Hlavní město Rwandy, Kigali, vyžaduje k prohlídce jen asi půl hodiny kdy taky stačíme nakoupit pohlednice. Mají zde křižovatku tří silnic. Jedna vede do Tanzanie, druhá do Ugandy a třetí do Burundi, což je malá zemička na jih od Rwandy. My se vydáváme směrem do Burundi. Následující den se však cesta znovu dělí - vlevo do Burundi, rovně do Zaire a vpravo alternativní spoj do Ugandy. Volíme přímou cestu ke hranicím Zaire, bývalé Belgické Kongo, kam vstupujeme dne 12. března 1981, hraničním přechodem u jezera Kivu, které leží v nadmořské výšce 1460 m. Na zairské straně je letovisko Goma. Vydáváme se vzhůru lávovým polem ke kráteru sopky v oblasti Mt. Kariambi /4.507 m.n.m./, která měla velkou erupci v roce 1979. 0 dva dny později projíždíme chráněnou oblastí Vi runga National Park, kde se nám podařilo spatřit různé skupiny hrochů, slonů, lvů a množství opic. Šplháme do hor přes malebné vesničky s přátelskými obyvateli a máme hezký výhled na jezero Edward. Pohybujeme se v nadmořské výšce asi 2.000 metrů. Překračujeme rovník a obědváme zde v pravé poledne dne 15. března 1981. Zde, uprostřed Afriky, se ve stanech v noci třeseme zimou. Ocitáme se na severní polokouli a pokračujeme nyní k severu. Táboříme u opuštěné belgické farmy. Je to několika patrový dům, který vypadá spíš jako zámeček a v pondělí, 16. března oslavuji můj den číslo 150 od opuštění San Franciska, banánovým vínem. Z pokraje hor se nám naskýtá nezapomenutelný pohled. Od obzoru k obzoru se rozprostírá nekonečná, neprostupná rovina pařící se tropické džungle, povodí veletoku řeky Congo. Zvolna sjíždíme dolů, pohlcováni hustým stromovím, ztrácejíc se v zeleném příšeří. Porost zde pohlcuje 80% světla. Večer se stále těžce dýchá a leje z nás pot. Sedíme kolem ohniště v kruhu stanů, když se náhle ze tmy ozvala jakási hudba. Byli jsme obklopeni několika desítkami nahých, skutečných trpaslíků, třímajících v rukou oštěpy. Pro dnešek končím a ozvu se opět za měsíc. Srdečně vás zdraví George F. Kíša.
