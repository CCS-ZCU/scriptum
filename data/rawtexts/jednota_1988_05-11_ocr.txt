Love of the nation comes from the love of the family, from the love of the land of one's birth and Trom all the values that the nation represents; these are its spiritual, moral, religious. and cultural values. We are therefore not surprised that even after many years, often decades. Slovaks from all parts of the world yearn to visit the land of their birth or of their parents or grandparents. This yearning is as natural as breathing or the throb of one's heart. We do not hold It against anyone who would do anything to fulfill a dream. We have heard of the humiliating questionnaires that applicants must fill out for entry visa. We are familiar with the blackmailing system which demands that emigrants redeem themselves like slaves. We have knowledge of various financial machinations with foreign exchange. Nevertheless, we do not condemn anyone, we do not even warn those who are willing lo put up with all this as the price to be paid for a visit to Slovakia. But th is applies only to Individuals who. except for themselves, do uel represent anyone. it is necessary to look at this question quite differently when the visit concerns people who can say that they represent Slovaks abroad and their large organizations. The leaders of these organizations should be aware of some very Important considerations before they visit their homeland. Even if we wanted to. we cannot forget that none of the countries with a communist government exerts so much pressure on Its citizens as does the Prague government through the government of Slovakia. How unnatural this situation Is. Is Confirmed by their own admission that only 10-12% of the population belong to the communist party. In other words. 88-90% of the population are non-com- munlsts. Therefore, whatever we do abroad, we must not rely on those 10-12% communists. but rather respect the majority of non-communlst citizens who form 88-90% of the population and seek contacts with them. For these reasons, whoever goes to Slovakia in an official or semi-official capacity must keep in mind that the basic approach of the domestic regime toward culture, the Individual. the Christian and the citizen is not comparable with what Is guaranteed in the Helsinki Final Act regarding human rights. This Act was also ratified by Czecho-Slovakia and consequently it forms a part of the constitutional law of the C-SSR. A delegation of Slovaks living abroad should go io Slovakia with the aim of finding out the real situation and of getting information about the possibilities of change. It would be appropriate to discover what can be done to Improve relations between the homeland and Slovaks living abroad and vice versa. In fact, several communist states have already made some concessions to improve relations between their emigres and the old country. Why couldn't the Slovak government do as much? Upon their return such a delegation should inform the Slovak community in the free world of their experiences. As a matter of principle, we issued a statement at the General Assembly that we will seek to have closer relations In the cultural and non-ideological fields with Slovaks at home. It is up to the Slovak government whether and how it is willing to accept our efforts. We note that Slovaks abroad are opposed to atheistic communism and our struggle against communist violence will continuc-to be a part of the fight for human rights, the rights of the citizen, and the Christian. Until the situation changes, we consider it our duty to call the world's attention to noncompliance with international agreements in the field of human and civil rights.
