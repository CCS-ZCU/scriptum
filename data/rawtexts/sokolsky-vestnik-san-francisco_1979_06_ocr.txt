This article actually started as a joke told to our Sokol members attending the banquet commemorating the 70th anniversary of Sokol Los Angeles. Sister Ivanka Bevc, Director of women of Jugoslav Sokol and also our active member, was the originator of this story. One day she made a great speech about Sokol in front of the Napa Senior Citizens Club to which she belongs. When she finished she received nice applause and many people approached her with questions and comments. One of the listeners shook her hand and surprised her with the comment: ”1 did not know that your SOCCER SLUB had such a patriotic and nice following in Jugoslavia and Czechoslovakia. I wish that the Same would be true in the United States.” Sister Ivanka Bevc’s face changed somewhat and she started wondering whether she had presented the Sokol Ideals correctly. She decided immediately that she would have another presentation as soon as it was her turn. The above little story goes, of course, farther than to a local senior citizens club. I realize that a large majority of the people do not know the answer to, "What is a Sokol?” Especially the young people who have come lately from Czechoslovakia have hardly heard about Sokol ideals and if they have, it has been the Communist version. Then what is a SOKOL? The Sokol ideals stemmed from the spirit of Czechoslovak people. The national heritage, passed down from father to son, from mother to daughter, includes sincere democratic feelings, moral earnestness and a true yearning for liberty. A Sokol is opposed to every ideology that deprives a free people of the right of free choice in a free land. A Sokol strives for physical improvement, moral purity and love of fellow men, equality and sincerity. A Sokol defends the priceless ideals of independence and the democratic way of life. A Sokol shields these ideals against all attacks and has proven so in two great world wars with his noble deeds. Sokol graves on far flung battlefields attest to that. Shortly after, Czechoslovak people emigrating to America, brought < th them, among other things, the understanding of benefits derived from training minds and bodies to make their lives richer. ”A sound mind in healthy body” has been a Sokol slogan since the founding of the organization. In 1865 the first Sokol unit was founded in America in St. Louis. Shortly after, units came into existence in New York, Baltimore, Cedar Rapids, Chicago, Cleveland and Detroit. From the beginning, the gymnastic activity was a major part of the Sokol program. There were classes then, a s now, of children, teenagers, women and men. There were many National Festivals and Gymnastic Competitions held in the past years. Games, singing and dancing were very important parts of Sokol agenda. Sokols today, as in the past, strive to combat the dangers of apathy, indifference and complacency. Sokols today, as in the past, develop character, self-reliance, patriotist and citizenship in their youth, and to promote understanding by both adults and youth of the principles of freedom and liberty. Sokols believe, by building qualified leaders today, our nation will contin continue to be a great nation tomorrow.
