The memo deals with Section 501(c)3 of the Internal Revenue Code which prohibits tax-exempt organizations from participating or tniervenlng in political campaigns to support or oppose any candidate. The USCC holds a group tax exemption covering some 28.000 U.S, parishes and other church entities. Violation of the code by one could Jeopardize the tax exemption for all. Chopko said. "During an election campaign, exempt organizations remain free to address issues of concern to them and to their membership, even when such issues are relevant to the campaign." the memo says. "However, such discourse must focus on issues and not personalities." Activities prohibited Include political endorsements. financial contributions or other campaign support, formation of political action committees and distribution of partisan campaign literature. "Exempt organizations may not make statements (oral or written) supporting or opposing any candidate for public office." the memo states. The prohibition applies, for example. to sermons, parish bulletins or editorials in Catholic newspapers. Even statements which indirectly support or oppose a particular candidate should be avoided, the memo warns. This includes labeling candidates as "pro-abortion” or "anti-peace." for example, or rating candidates' positions or records with plus or minus signs. Exempt organizations also may not contribute money or other resources — such as free volunteers, facilities or mailing lists — to any candidate or political party, the memo says. Likewise, exempt organizations are not permitted to distribute campaign literature that supports or opposes a specific candidate or party. What kinds of public activities are acceptable (or church organizations during an election year? Voter education activity. Exempt organizations may distribute — with some limitations — newsletters, pamphlets or other materials that include candidates' statements, voting records or the results of candidate polls or questionnaires. "Whether such distribution constitutes prohibited political campaign activity depends upon the format and content of the publications and the manner of their distribution.” All voter education publications — including those distributed in parish bulletins or by the Catholic press — should "explicitly state their educational purpose and disclaim any intent to endorse or oppose any candidate.” the memo specifies. It recommends that all such materials be reviewed by the exempt organization's legal counsel before publication or distribution. Voting records of all members of the legislature on "a wide range of subjects” may be distributed during an election campaign "provided the structure and content of the material avoid bias." the memo says. "Materia) would likely be considered biased if it indicates or implies a} that a legislator agrees or disagrees with the organization's position, or b) that the organization agrees or disagrees with a legislator's vote." Similar guidelines apply to the publication of questionnaire results. Polls or questionnaires must not be biased — that is, must "not pose questions designed to make the candidates appear acceptable or unacceptable to the organization or its members." The polls or questionnaires must cover a wide range of Issues "selected on the basis of Importance to the electorate as a whole” and the results must be reported "in an accurate and unbiased manner. No editorial comment is permitted." Non-partisan voter registration drives. Exempt organizations may encourage rpem- bers to vote antľmay sponsor voter registration drives that do not favor one party or candidate. Non-partisan public Jorums, debates and lectures.
