Czechoslovak elections used to be contested by more than 20 parties; of which 15 or 16 managed to get representation in the Parliament. A coalition of at least five parties was required to form a stable Government. Multiplicity of political parties was caused by ethnical reasons (each nationality formed their own party,ag. there were 8 Czechoslovak, 1 purely Slovak, 5 German and 3 Hungarian parties) and Class consciousness (Czech and German Agrarian Parties, Retailers' P a r ty). The many parties thrived much better under the proportional system than they would have done under a piajority system, Political parties prepared a list of candidates for each election and the voter had to vote for this list "en bloc". The voters were not given a choice of personalities but of parties. This system was one of many ý/ays of how to enforce rigid discipline within political parties. The executive power was shared by the President and the Government. But it was the Government who enjoyed the full scope of executive power except certain specific powers conferred on the President by the Constitution or by subsequent Acts of Parliament. The reason for this dualism which was another major departure from Western constitutional practice, was the urgent wish of the Fathers of the Constitution to remove every trace of monarchism. Three courts require special mention in this brief outline of the Czechoslovak Constitution. They are the Constitutional Court, the Electoral Court and the Supreme Administrative Court. The function of the Constitutional Court was to determine ..whether Acts of Parliament were incompatible with the Constitution. This Court acted only on special request from the House of Deputies,the Senate and the Highest Court. _!t is to the credit of Czechoslovak legislation that the Constitutional Court has never been called upon to sit. The Electoral Court was an 'unparalleled, original institution.__ Its function v,Tas to verify the validity of elections and to deal with cases where a member of Parliament should lose his seat. This practice made the Electoral Court a very questionable body, because it was dominated by lay judges elected for that purpose by the House of Deputies.These were practically political appointees through whom their political parties controlled the Court. The Supreme Administrative Court dealt with the validity of Government decrees, adjudicated on conflicts of competence between ministries and provincial selfgovernments and acted as a Court of Appeal against decisions of all administrative authorities.
