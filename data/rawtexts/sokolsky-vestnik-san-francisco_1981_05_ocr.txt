In our world there exist certain things about which we know nothing - or rather, about which we wish to know nothing. When confronted with such instances, our first desire is to forget them. One such case is a dying world of human beings - the unrecognizable ruins of yesterday’s majesty. It is a world of retarded children, of mongoloids, of faceless, limbless children. It is a world of children with destroyed nerve systems and with their physiological functions hopelessly out of control. This world is Dante’s inferno on earth. If you have the strength to enter into this world, you would meet the angels - women in habits, each wearing a cross. Such is the destination assigned to the sisters of religious orders by the Czechoslovak Socialist Society, as they prefered to be called. In this limbo sought by no one on the very brink of humanity are supposed to hide from the world - not only those defenseless, disinherited individuals trapped by their unfortunate fate, but also those who are caring for them. Until recently no mention was made of the religious as long a^s they were not publicly insulted. They are exposed to oppresion and discrimination, but they carry the symbolic cross with a smile because even this Calvary is part of their mission. They expect no rewards for their work, devotion, humiliation and hardships from their fellow-citizens. It is obvious that in this world there is no justice for them, much less rewards of any kind. Should you be able to visit these institutions, you would be left with permanent images such as the eyes of a hydrocephalous child who will never get up, deaf, mute and blind children, strange crawling' creatures emiting horrendous shrieks. One does not dwell on such tragedies, so we turn to the other pole of that world, to Sisters Illuminata, Ambrosia, Sanctia, Lubomíra - to their eyes with which they embrace" those wretched creatures they are lovingly caring for. Of the original 10.000 religious there remain approximately 7.000. Of those, 5.000 are emploed and the rest are retired. You can find them in Slatiňany, Vidnava or Javorník, in dozens of;Charity homes and hospitals for terminal cases. who assessed them this bitter destiny? Why were they sent into half-ruined convents in depopulated frontier territory guarded by morose frontier guards with German shepherds? Who expelled them from hospitals and schools and transferred them from Slovakia to Silesia’s border region to expose them to mental and physical abuse, ridicule, and, not infrequently, to torture in investigation cells and jails? We can say it was the era and atmosphere of the 50*s. Leaders during that era initiated many actions against humanity. During the nights of April 14 - 15 1 and 27^ - 1950 trucks came to stop in front of cloisters, monasteries and convents. The inhabitants of those build- ' ings were forced to leave with only limited personal belongings and started their long journey of abuse and injustices. Under guard they were immediately transported to a few liquidated monasteries where they were kept as prisoners and where they were forced to do work far removed from their qualification and education. After horrendous pressure only two broke their vows of poverty and loyalty to their orders. Until now, many of the sisters vegetate and do manual worm in extremely sad conditions. The more fortunate of them, if we can call them that, work with self-sacrifice 'in health institutions - but always exclusively in those places where that terrible limbo starts. They serve patients who are in resthomes for the incurably ill and they serve the hopelessly retarded, mutilated and crippled children. Again and again the question arises, how was this and how is this still possible? Czechoslovakia had a constitution and certain fundamental rights. Among them we always recognized freedom of conscience and religious faith. Evidently, the letter of the law differed diabolically from its practice, a s a matter of fact, even Czechoslovakia proclaimed her adherence to the Declaration of Human Rights on December lO^^, 1948. It’s sufficient to remind ourselves of three articles in this freedom declaration: article No.3.: No one must be subjected to cruel, inhuman or humiliating treatment or punishment..... Ten thousand nuns and two thousand monks were subjected to cruel and rudely humiliating treatment which was not formerly a punishment but a much worse violation of human rights. Article No.9.: No one must be arbitrarily arrested, kept in detention, or be deported into exile. .... This measure was flagrantly unheeded and earned out against 12.000 monks and nuns in 1950. The actions against them were without legal basis and customary court proceedings. Article No. 19.: Everybody has a right to freedom of religion. This right also includes freedom to express his religion or belief publicly or privately. The prove alone, that just the religious belief and its demonstration together with others, could never be the sole reason for the action against any group...... bince then nobody tried not even publicistically less so legally to construct substantiation for the action against them. Even if it would be true that absurd assertion which was heard during the process with Reverend pí cha-Tůma., that in the fifties there were some nuns in the Ostrava region practicing machine gun handling in order to get ready ho carry out an armed coup, then nobody would have a right to launch an action against all in the spirit of the middle-age liquidations of groups of various denominations. In the spring of 1968 there was a ray of hope for rehabilita- | tion of the persecuted members of different religious orders. In a resolution signed by 7.000 monks and nuns and mailed to the minister of culture they mentioned their bitter fate as disinhereted beings and repeated the well known fact that they all proved themselves in their jobs. It contained even a promise that they would continue to work diligently where- ever they would be proficient for the unity and peaceful life of the society. . i They demanded the abolishment of illegal measures of the fifieS which are held legal to THIS day. At the same time they expressed understanding about serious obstacles ’which they comprehend as a reality. "The world needs proof of an all-encompassing love, humanity and unselfishnesswhich does away with hatred and all injustices." This was the closing sentence of their resolution. Together with the dream about socialism with a human face also disappeared the hope for rehabilitation. Nowadays if you miraculously run into a nun in the street, her sight is invariably fixed to the pavement, borne people don’t perceive' her and look back alter her as after a phantom, as after a messenger of yesterday’s world. I see in my mind those hundreds of humble hard working women who at one time renounced all worldly joys, all wealth and ordinary happi- nes^Ln order to be able to serve others, even the indigents. I recall how'they were slandered and exploited, After lives of devoted work they only received 200 Czech crowns a month which was not enough even to pay for their stay in the charity home. Those Sisters who were able to work and were paid around 500 - 600 crowns a month contributed part of their pay to the upkeep of those unfortunate ones. Through my mind crossed the picture of a paralyzed and mute human being in one of the institution where civilian nurse would not serve for all the money in the world, to whom this Sister - God knows how - was able to explain that sixteen years of suffering without interruption on this earth would bring him redemption in heaven. I saw the eyes of these suffering humans in a moment when a Sister placed her soothing hand on their tormented foreheads - an unforgetably powerful and tragic experience. There should be instituted full rehabilitation and restitution of all members of all religious orders. That is the least they should get right NOW! We plead for those remaining eight thousand Czechoslovak pariahs and we will keep reminding the present God-less regime of this hideous mass crime c-ommi tied in the name of the Communist Party of Czechoslovakia on the most innocent and undeserving.
