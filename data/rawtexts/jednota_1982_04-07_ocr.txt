Monday and Tuesday, were anticipated throughout Slovakia with great pleasure by boys and girls, as well as young single men and women. These involved a give and take exchange of two fun-filled days, not altogether unknown in village life today Easter Monday was assigned by the age-old customs to the boys who visited families to pay respects to youthful females ranging somewhere between 4 and 19 years of age and io older women out of respect, too. There were, three widely different modes of expressing the mood of the season In some parts of the country, chiefly In the north and east, this was in the form of sprinkling the girls with perfumed water, or dousing them with an ample supply of cold water. An entirely different custom prevailed in the western and southern areas Water was commonly used in villages where there were no apothecary establishments. 1e., old-fashioned drug-stores, to purchase a bottle of perfume, rose, or violet-scented totiei-water. The contents were sometimes diluted and poured into a small bottle which was capped with a perforated stopper to control (besprinkling to a few drops so that the precious liquid would last for the day Sometimes. If the sprinkler was too generous with his supply, he would have to rush home for a refiD. Generally, the boys made their rounds In groups of three or four Festivities began at dawn and usually lasted until neon as one group after another made Iheir annual visits Mothers of the girls warmly welcomed the boys who were then greeted with apparent delight by their giggling hostesses The sprinkling ritual completed, the visitors were rewarded with colored eggs, cakes, and even with a coin or two in well-to-do families Pay-off time by the girls came the lotlowing day. Easter Tuesday was their day to repay their boy-friends who had called on them on the previous day Everywhere, It seemed, a group of girls appeared around the comer to greet an unsuspecting schooi-boy or young fad with a shower of cold water. The only consolation lor this surprising compliment was that the recipient of a spring shower was not expected to reward his admirers Somehow, the girls did not mind that the custom was in one respect onesided they received only the satisfaction of embarasslng the poor boy. Elsewhere, post-easter customs for both Monday and Tuesday took an entirely different turn. While tn the northern and eastern regions of Slovakia boys were filling their flasks with Sweet-scented liquids In preparation for Easter Monday forays, their counterparts In the west and south were busily occupied with weaving willow-wands (tn six to eight strands) for their Easter Monday rendezvous. Instead of sprinkling the girls with a mist of aromatic liquid, these stalwarts, wielding their willowy wands at random, were met not by giggling girls but by squealing victims who happed up and down amid a series of well-directed swings at their ankles . . and sometimes in the swirling dance the swish of the beribboned wlBow wand found its mark on the knee or posterior. At any rate, though somewhat primitive, perhaps, than the.perfume-ritual, this was usually controlled by the presence of elders and accepted as innocent fun Lest one should think, for a moment, that these “macho” boys had all the fun. he should remember that the foDbbring day the girls had their chance to get even and that they'dld with a vengeance, especially if some guy got carried away and showed off his masculine strength to Impress his victims and bystanders The boys did not gel away with anything as the now grown-up girls will recall and relish with glee. Ind., the goal of the music of an Easter liturgy is to evoke "a sense of joy and rejoidng and praise” In people — something the themes of Easter readily encourage. Those are themes she explained, of new We, of moving from darkness to light, of victory over death. Composer John Scales now Ilves in a Northern Virginia suburb of Washington. As a parish music leader, he stressed, his "commitment Is to provide the best music 1 can for the liturgical setting." Scales is largely a Self-taught musician. His Jazz-pianist father used to teach him music informally. But now his compositions are known in a number of parish communities. His contemporary music makes use not only of a choir, but of guitars and piano too. In choosing music for an Easter liturgy. Scalea keeps several aims In mind. As a practical matter, he is happily aware that he can pull out all the stops in planning music for an Easter vigil service. When singing the Gloria of the Mass, for example. Easter is such a clearly defined feast, he remarked, that he - generally has little difficulty finding appropriate music. This Is due partly to his affiliation with a small group of 12 composers who have banded together in what they call “Cooperative Ministries" to publish their own music. Both Mrs. Conklin and Scales emphasized that a primary alm of theirs Is to get the congregation actively Involved In the singing. Pripravoval sobotňajšie relácie pre našich poslucháčov gréckokatolíkov a v posledných rokov, najprv v nedeľu a potom vo štvrtok hovoril o dejinách gréckokatolíckej cirkvi na Slovensku. Naši poslucháči ho mali veľmi radi, jeho prácu osobitne oceňovali historici a profesori dejín pre cenné výsledky jeho vyhľadávania v archívoch a knižniciach. Nemožno zabudnúť jeho diela v tone inštitúcii, ktoré reprezentujú a združujú diasporu slovenskej komunity ďaleko od vlasti. Prednášky, výskum, stretnutia, aktivita, naozaj obdivuhodná, ktorá je odzrkadlením veľkej viery a veľkej lásky pre národ a cirkev. V skutočnosti jeho reči, jeho nezabudnuteľná tvár, keď sa stretol bo svojimi, žiarila vnútorným svetlom, čo bolo ovocím jeho kňazského povolania v Spoločnosti .Ježišovej a spomienok na sladké, zelené nížiny Východného Slovenska. Veľký vedec, Otec Michal Lacko bol veľký muž kresťanskej lásky. Bude nám chýbať jeho dobrota, pracovitosť, ochota, jeho dynamizmus bez strachu.
