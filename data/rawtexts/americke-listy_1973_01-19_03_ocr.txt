It is rather ironic that President Nixon is starting his second term in office after a great display of confidence in his policies here in the United States, while some statesmen in Western Europe, in countries traditionally allied with the US and dependent on her, are v:olently attacking him for his Vietnam policy. It is even more ironic — and sad — that some of his fiercest critics are the very same people who owe their own survival to the American participation in World War II. The unprecedented prosperity of West Europe makes her leaders feel smug and safe from Commvn'sm and so they think it fashionable to heep insults on Nixon and to criticize American “imperialism”, while displaying sympathy and even admiration for the alleged undergod — North Vietnam. Not only the politically eccentric Swedish prime minister Palme, but now such statesmen as Pompidou and Brandt arc joining in the vilification of Nixon’s policy. Such attacks are unjust as well as dangerous, more so for West Europe than for the United States, because they have confused the real issues. The origins of the conflict in Indochina are so distant and dim to many people, and therefore, it is extremely easy for the. Commun:sts to shift all guilt on the victim of their aggression, on South Vietnam, and on the United States which defends the country invaded by Com- munist armies.
