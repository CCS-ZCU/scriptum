In reply to “A personal reminiscence of a summer in Paris” by Mr. Nathan Cohen in your issue of March 26,1966, ! would like to draw your attention to a regrettable mistake made by Mr. Cohen in respect to the question whether anti-Jewish pogroms were known in Czechoslovakia. I have more often disagreed than agreed with views of Mr. Cohen in his criticism of the theatre and cinema arts, because it seems that his main aim is to diminish rather than truly criticize and appreciate the worth of a work of art. However, I have never been more upset nor have more violently disagreed with him, than on the above mentioned article. He says that rhe film “The Shop on the Main Street” has one significant flaw in the plot. “The implication is - says he - that, save for a miserable handful, the people in the town disapprove of the Nazi treatment of Jews." Nowhere in this film is this actually implied. On the contrary, save for the main male character Tono, Piti baci/ the town-crier/, Kuchar/ “the white Jew" /, and two secondary characters, woman and man / warning Tono about the coming deportation of the Jews /, who are most definitely active in disapproving of the Nazi treatment of the Jews, the town is for the nationalization of the Jewish property and independence furnished them by joining nazism. In this respect the film shows the stage in Slovakia how it really was at the beginning of the war, though this changed later on. Even if it were true, as he says, that the town’s sin is one of inaction rather than participation, I fail Io see why this should constitute a flaw in the film, if it just portrays the truth? After all, the whole world was inactive, looking on what the Nazis did. Further more. A pogrom is no unknown word to any Jew, however young or old, for their history fells them that they have always been persecuted.
