Most of the participants started arriving in Bratislava on Sunday July 5 and were accommodated in modern hotel rooms at SUZA or DRUŽBA. In the afternoon there was on open house at SUZA, i.e. a continuous Social Get Together with flowing open end sandwiches and other refreshments which were constantly being replenished. This gave an opportunity for meeting old friends or making new acquaintances from both sides of the Atlantic. The general mood was festive and very cordial. Opening ceremony of the 19th SVU World Congress took place at 10 am on Monday, July 6, in the beautiful „Aula“ Hall of the Comenius University, Šafarik Square 6. The event started with the academic procession of Rectors. Deans and other university dignitaries, dressed in their colorful academic gowns and golden chains, signifying their university rank, with trumpets plying. It was an unforgettable sight. The large gathering of SVU members and invited guests which filled every available seat of the magnificent Hall, was officially welcomed by the Rector of Comenius University Prof. Ing. Ferdinand Devinsky, DSc., who hosted the meeting. The text of his remarks, as well as that of other presenters, appears elsewhere in this issue of Zprávy SVU. He was followed by Dr. Ing. Igor Hudoba, CSc., Rector of the Slovak Technical University, who greeted the gathering on behalf of the Slovak Conference of University Rectors, of which he si President. The SVU Congress was officially opened by the President of the Czechoslovak Society of Arts and Sciences, Dr. Miloslav Rechcigl. The next speaker was His Excellency Ralph Johnson, the svu [pageend1]U.S. Ambassador, who presented his views on the future relations of the U.S. and Slovakia. Then came an enjoyable music interlude by the chorus TECHNIK, followed by brief addresses of Dr. Jaroslava Turková, president of the Prague SVU Chapter and Prof. RNDr. Ing. Alexander Tkáč, DrSc., president of the SVU Chapter in Bratislava and the Chairman of Bratislava Organizing Committee. The ceremony was closed with “Gaudeamus igituf’. In the afternoon there was a plenary session held in the Moyzes Hall at the Music Conservatory, comprised of six major addresses, including that of Miloslav Rechcigl, Juraj Stern, Josef Tiňo, Zlatica Plašienková, Ján Morovič and A. Tamir. The festive day was concluded with a reception and a cultural program of folk music and dance at SUZA. Tuesday morning, Wednesday morning and afternoon and Thurday morning were devoted to academic sessions, many of which reflected the central theme of the 19th SVU World Congress: “Sciences and Arts on the Eve of the 21th Century”. In addition, the program included a variety of symposia, and panel discussions on various topics, organized by different disciplines of human endeavor. Tuesday afternoon was reserved for a guided tour through the historical Old Town, followed by a concert at the St. Martin’s Cathedral, with the participation of the opera singer Zuzana Vaseková, Maroš Kittner, organ, Milan Vonderka, violin, Lucia Majerská, violin, Dimitrij Kopčák, Eva Sochmanová, viola. The music repertoire included music of J. S. Bach, E Handel, W. A. Mozart, A. Dvořák, E Schubert and M. Markiézy. The program included words of welcome by Father Anton Srholec and poetry reading by Ladislav Chudik. The Congress participants were than received in the Lord Mayor’s Office at the Primacial Palace and treated to the Music of W. A. Mozart and Antonín Dvořák by the Bratislava Capella Istropolitana Chamber Orchestra, under the direction of Robert Mareček. The extraordinary evening was concluded by a hospitable reception of scrumptious food and wine. On Wednesday evening the Congress participants were taken by bus to the Bratislava castle to view the art exhibits and hear music by Mikuláš Schneider, Ludwig van Beethoven, Johannes Brams, Frederyk Chopin and Antonín Dvořák. The performers included the leading opera singers Zuzana Vaseková. soprano and Peter Mikuláš, baritone and two piano viruosi Daniela Varinska and Marian Varinsky. Then followed a reception with light refreshment to commemorate the 40th anniversary of the Czechoslovak Society of Arts and Sciences. Zprávy SVU (SVU News). The academic program was concluded with three keynote addresses, beginning with the of Petr Zuna, Rector of ČVUT in Prague, who talked about the role of the Czech technical universities at the turn of the new millennium. The other two speakers included Father Anton Srholec from Bratislava who talked on the subject “Role of God in the Contemporary World”, and Igor Kiss “Is Humanity on the Way Out?" The talks by the representatives of the Catholic and Protestant faiths, respectively, were duly followed by an extemporaneous presentation of Prof. Pavel Traubner, M.D., Ph.D., Honorary President of the Union of Jews in Slovakia. The academic program was formally closed by the SVU President who expressed thanks to his American collaborators and the Bratislava coorganizers, headed by prof. Tkáč, for the time and effort they put into organizing the 19th SVU World Congress. On Thursday night there was a farewell reception in SUZA with a cultural program, featuring a virtuoso on a cymbal whose mastery cannot be equaled. Friday was reserved for a bus excursion into the picturesque Slovak countryside, with a guided tour of the Devin Castle and visit of the Smolenice Palace where the participants were served lunch. The group was than taken to Dolná Krupa to view the house of Beethoven and learn about the composer’s private life and productive stay in Slovakia. Then followed an unforgettable visit to Modrá and its famous ceramic artistic factory and laboratories Majolika. The very enjoyable day ended in the small Slovak village of Budmerice where the Congress participants were treated to first rate goulash and other Slovak goodies, accompanied with wholesome Slovak wine and beer. While the eating and drinking was going on, the musicians played in the background the old tunes. Then people begin dancing, while others sang... I almost forgot to mention that we were first welcomed by the honorable Mayor of Budmerice who expressed his joy over our visit in his town and as a token of appreciation presented the SVU President a historical chronicle of Budmerice. As you can imagine, the overall mood was genial, exuberant, enchanted, fantastic... What a way to end the Congress! Irrespective of criteria used, the Bratislava Congress must be judged as a highly sucessful event, not only in terms of its content and quality but above all, how much it contributed to enhancing understanding between one another, between Czechs and Slovaks, as well as other nationalities, between those in our old countries and those abroad, between scientists, artists nad humanitarians, as well as across the gender and the age lines. It has clearly fulfilled the society’s aim as a cultural, nonpartisan and nonprofit organization, dedicated to the principles of free search for truth and knowledge, free contacts among peoples and free dissemination of ideas. Je pre mňa potešením vás čo najsrdečnejšie privítať na akademickej pôde Univerzity Komenského v Bratislave, najstaršej a najväčšej vysokej školy v Slovenskej republike. Celá naša akademická obec si pokladá za česť, že sa predsedníctvo 19. Svetového kongresu Česko-Slovenskej Spoločnosti pre vedy a umenia rozhodlo usporiadať toto významné podujatie pod záštitou a v priestoroch našej Alma mater. Som presvedčený o tom, že práve akademická pôda, ktorej už historickým poslaním je podnecovať voľnosť myšlienkových prúdov, vytvára najoptimálnejšiu atmosféru pre vedecký a umelecký dialóg a je teda aj tým najvhodnejším priestorom pre napĺňanie ústrednej témy kongresu venovanej aktuálnym problémom vedy a umenia na prahu 21. storočia. Teší nás, že máme dnes možnosť medzi sebou privítať toľko významných osobností z duchovnej sféry, ktoré reprezentujú intelektuálny potenciál nášho národa na významných postoch vo všetkých kútoch sveta. Veľa vedcov, vysokoškolských profesorov, umelcov, významných verejno-činných pracovníkov muselo proti svojej vôli v historických prelomových rokoch opustiť našu domovinu a svoj talent a tvorivé schopnosti rozvíjať mimo hraníc našej vlasti. Aj mnohí z vás patríte medzi nich a preto aj vám prináleží náš obdiv a uznanie, že ste na svoju vlasť nezabudli a že ste sa aj v tých najťažších obdobích izolácie snažili udržovať profesionálne kontakty a v rámci možností podporovať rozvoj slovenskej vedy a umenia. Našťastie život v krajinách strednej Európy má už v súčasnosti inú. slobodnú tvár a nič nebráni duchovnému zbližovaniu národov. Dôkazom toho je aj to, že dnešný 19. svetový kongres Spoločnosti pre vedy a umenia pripomínajúci si aj 40. výročie založenia Česko-Slovenskej spoločnosti pre vedy a umenia sa môže uskutočniť, na rozdiel od minulosti, na pôde Slovenska. Napriek tomu, domnievam sa, je nevyhnutné aj v súčasnosti si pripomínať udalosti rokov 1938, 48 a 68 a ich devastujúce dôsledky nielen pre vedy a umenia, ale aj pre život celej našej spoločnosti. Dnes aj slovenská veda a umenie má šancu nielen čerpať zo svetovej duchovnej klenotnice, ale aj do nej výrazne prispievať. Je to aj vďaka záujmu a starostlivosti členov Spoločnosti pre vedy a umenia o duchovné napredovanie slovenského národa, vďaka vašej ochote konkrétne pomáhať rozvoju tvorivého talentu a schopností našich ľudí. Ústredná téma kongresu — Aktuálne problémy vedy a umenkna prahu 21. storočia — obsahuje v sebe obrovské množstvo podnetov a intelektuálnych inšpirácií. Svetový vývoj na prelome tisícročí charakterizujú dramatické zmeny, ktoré vtlačujú ľudstvu celkom novú tvár. Globalizačné a integračné procesy. 6 [pageend6]podporované rozmachom informačných technológií už dnes ovplyvňujú spôsob myslenia, postoje a hodnotové systémy, menia štýl profesionálneho i osobného života. A niet pochýb o tom, že to bude práve kultúra a umenie, ktoré budú zachovávať identitu národov, ich esprit a originalitu v medzinárodnom kontexte. Ako ďalej svetový vývoj naznačuje, tak popri informatizácii bude rozhodujúcu úlohu pri hospodárskom a sociálnom napredovaní zohrávať humanizácia všetkých sfér života spoločnosti pri súčasnom raste vzdelanostnej úrovne obyvateľstva. A už dnes je skutočnosťou, že vyspelé štáty považujú rast vzdelanosti za společenskú prioritu, za klúčový faktor rozvoja spoločnosti tretieho milénia. Charakter a postup týchto rozsiahlých zmien, ktoré do hlbky transformujú spoločenské vedomie, kladie množstvo otáznikov svetovej vedeckej i kultúrnej komunite, provokuje k novým umeleckým víziám podoby človeka a jeho života v budúcich rokoch. Zial, paralelne k stále vyššiemu životnému tempu prebieha takmer na celom svete aj proces erózie demokracie. Vážnym problémom sa stáva intolerancia, rasizmus a xenofóbia, prepuká nedôvera, podráždenosť a neochota riešiť problémy diskusiou. Problémy sa čím ďalej tým viac riešia silou, čo je spôsobené viacerými faktormi, jedným z nich je aj nízka ochota k dialógu. Dialóg si totiž vyžaduje vzdelaných ľudí ochotných počúvať aj druhú stranu a zamýšľať sa nad argumentmi. A je úlohou aj takýchto kongresov založených práve na dialógu, aby rozhodujúcim spôsobom prispeli k zabrzdeniu erozívných procesov a súčasne prispeli aj k väčšiemu rozšíreniu umenia tolerancie. O to viac je neyhnutné a je viac ako žiaduce zbližovanie vied a umení. Nakoniec dobrá a kvalitná veda je skutočne istým spôsobom aj umením. Stačí pripomenúť len samotný život, ktorého chod je založený na biopolyméroch a ktoré sú skutočným umeleckým dielom prírody. Na druhej strane bez trojrozmerného a viacrozmerného poznania sveta, ktoré nám pomáha pochopiť práve umenie, by sme nemohli úspešne riešiť nielen mnohé vedecké, ale ani filozofické otázky kľúčové pre trvalo udržovateľný rozvoj spoločnosti i celého sveta. Je výzvou aj pre nás, pedagógov vysokých škôl, aby sme dokázali mladú generáciu na nový model spôsobu života človeka tretieho tisícročia dôkladne pripraviť. A k tomu nám výrazne pomáhajú stretnutia, ako sú tieto. Mnohé z týchto otázok budú iste predmetom rokovania kongresu a ja verím, že jeho program výrazne obohatí naše vedecké a umelecké poznanie. A že toto stretnutie bude tiež príležitosťou aj k neformálnym priateľským diskusiám, príležitosťou k nadviazaniu nových pracovných a osobných kontaktov. Prajem rokovaniu kongresu bohatú tvorivú atmosféru, iželám vám, aby prinieslo výsledky, ktoré opäť posunú dopredu hranice nášho poznania a budú dôstojnou vizitkou, dôstojným príspevkom k úrovni našich vedomostí na prahu tretieho tisícročia. Ťažisko jej činnosti v uplynulých piatich rokoch bolo hlavne v klubovej činnosti, v organizovaní prednášok, besied a diskusií s vynikajúcimi osobnosťami vo vede a umení ako z domova, tak i zo zahraničia. Veda a umenie vždy patrili k sebe. Keď sa pozrieme späť do histórie, nájdeme mnoho príkladov vzájomnej harmónie vied a umení, symbiózy osobnosti vedca a umelca. Toto si už uvedomili mnohé vyspelé demokratické krajiny Európy a sveta. Slovenské vysoké školstvo nastúpilo roku 1990 na cestu tranformácie v zmenených celospoločenských podmienkach. Už osem rokov sa snažíme spravovať si naše slovenské univerzity a vysoké školy v podmienkach autonómie a samosprávy. Máme zákonom garantované akademické sloboby a práva tak, ako ich definovala Magna Charta európskych univerzít, ktorú v septembri 1988 v Bologni podpísalo viac ako 250 reprezentantov najvýznamnejších európskych univerzít združených v Asociácii európskych univerzít známej pod akronymom CRE. V uplynulých desiatich rokoch sa mnohé významné podujatia na celoeurópskej úrovni vracali k myšlienkam obsiahnutých v texte Magna Charty európskych univerzít. Dovoľte mi zacitovať len niekoľko vieť z tohoto dokumentu. For those who are not familiar with our Society, let me give you a very brief account of its history and its aims. The Society was officially organized in 1958 at the initiative of Czech and Slovak intellectuals living abroad, at a time when the communist regime in II [pageend11]the former Czechoslovakia had repudiated the country’s historical traditions and suppressed free expression. The Society wanted to provide a forum for the free development of Czech and Slovak culture in exile and make the world aware of the Czech and Slovak cultural traditions which date back to the beginnings of Christianity and have consistently upheld the ideas of humanity. Following the successful Velvet Revolution and the end of the communist regime in former Czechoslovakia, the Society’s functions greatly expanded. Now, in additon to its original mission, the SVU, as we call our Society for short, has become a bridge between Czech and Slovak professionals and those of other countries. The Society has been instrumental in assisting the fledgling democracies in reestablishing the normal functions of a free society through material assistance, lectures, seminars and workshops, and most of all through personal expertise and experience of its members. Pavel Machotka, professor emeritus of art and psychology at UCSC, was named the recipient of the degree of Doctor of Education honoris causa, bestowed on him, according to the university announcement, “as an expression of high esteem for (his) lifelong contributions to the psychology of art and empirical aesthetics, and the study of art as a means to socialization and the humanization of the world". Machotka recieved his degree in early April at conference titled “The Role of Universities on the Threshold of the 21st Century”. The conference took place at Charles University, which was founded in Prague in 1348 by Charles IV, King of Bohemia and of the Romans. Machotka came to UCSC in 1970 and retired in 1994. He continues to teach psychology and research the subject of art and personality. Machotka was born in Prague, Czechoslovakia and came to the United States with his family at the age of 12. His landscape and figure paintings and photographs have been on exhibit in the U.S. and in Czechoslovakia. Among publications are Cezanne: Landscape into Art, The Nude, The Articulate Body, and Style and the Man: Siegriest and St. John (in press). Machotka notes that, "The Velvet Revolution in 1989 made it possible for me to return to Prague for the first time since my childhood. Although I arrived first as a visitor, I soon developed contacts with sociologists, art educators, and fellow psychologists, with whom it was easy to make an immediate connection through our essentially shared education.” "My contacts now are both scholarly and practical. There was, for example, a need to build up the library in the psychology department, which many of my colleagues at UC Santa Cruz helped me do by donating some of the best books in the field. Now I try to help knowledge flow the other way, by reviewing books dealing with Czech contributions to psychology, for example." “I am very moved by this honor, which connects me with the period when universities first developed in Europe. Praha: Terezínská iniciativa 1997, 95 pages. Michael Flack is an active member of the SVU. By vocation, he was a professor of political science (now retired) but. by avocation, he is a “poeta natus” (a born poet) and translator of poetry. On Thursday, April 3. 1997, and on Tuesday, March 3. 1998. the public had a rare occasion to savor his poetry presented in his own reading. The book contains 35 lyric poems written shortly before, during, and shortly after his horrible incarceration, first in the Terezin ghetto (1941-44), and then in the infamous death camps of Auschwitz-Birkenau and Meuselwitz- Buchenwald where, in 1945. he was liberated by the American army. Since 1946, he has been residing in the U.S. On Thursday, December 5. 1996, again, the public had a chance to see a rare documentary film about the Terezin camp children whose warden he had been. The Romans coined a tag: “Inter arma silent musae" (Amid arms the muses are silent). A born poet cannot, however, be silenced or remain silent when confronted by monumental evil and unspeakable inhumanity perpetrated by depraved persons against their fellow persons. The cruelty and horrors of WW I had been exposed by the English poet Wilfred Owen (1893-1918) who, in a twist of tragic irony, had, however, been killed one week before the 1918 Armistice. Micheal Flack, on the other hand, had extreme fortune on his side. Not only did he survive the horrors raging in the most infamous concentration camps of death but. moreover, found the incredible leisure time both to write poems under those life-threatening conditions, save them, and have them recently published, over fifty years after those events, as shattering eye-witness testimonials to the "hell on earth" through which he had been forced to go. He, too, was aware, as J. W. Goethe had been two-hundred years earlier, that he was endowed with the rare gift, and obligation, of speaking up for the millions of speechless victims of the war brutality. He could as well have borrowed the memorable words “J'accuse," used one hundred years ago. on January 13. 1898. in a historic letter written by Emil Zola in defense of French captain Alfred Dreyfus. While these lyrical poems do not narrate a consecutive story, we can nevertheless formulate the bare skeleton of one: From Terezín M. Flack is shipped to 19 [pageend19]camps of death, which he survives; he captures the tense but exciting moments expecting the end of the ordeal, welcoming the liberation in 1945 but also warning against the potential dangers of another military conflagration. The poet’s daily “diet” in the extermination camps consisted of witnessing brutal beatings, senseless killings, mass annihilation as his constant “companions”. The earth was littered with the graves of the dead. Another scourge felling the unfortunate inmates was the frequent massive hunger and starvation. The poet is to be commended for this absolutely humanistic posture of a total absence of hatred of his inhuman captors, let alone a call for retribution. He embraces people, especially his fellow-victims, irrespective of race or creed. The reading of individual poems has struck me as shorthand notes or a painter's quick brush strokes sketching the bare outlines. The totality of impact of these poems inevitably brought to mind Picasso’s huge Guernica canvas depicting the horrors, devastation, human agony and suffering during the Spanish Civil War (1936-39) raging only a few years before our poet’s own agony (1941-45) and considered to be a “prelude” to the very much bloodier WW II. Michael Flack ends his collection with an impassioned “Appeal” (of 1945) to mankind not to kill, to preserve peace, and to wage war “nevermore nevermore nevermore”, echoing The Ravan written by E. A. Poe (1809-49) in 1845, i.e., exactly one-hundred years ago! (A complete review will appear in the quarterly Bohemia, Munich, Germany.) Ján Simko, Washington, D.C. IN M E M O R I A M Milič Capek (1909-1997) Belatedly we have heard about the passing of one of our distinguished members, Milič Čapek, Boston University Professor Emeritus of Philosophy, on November 17, 1997, in Little Rock, Arkansas. He was 88. Čapek was born in the village of Třebechovice, Bohemia on January 26, 1909. He received his PhDr. in philosophy at Charles University in 1935. Talking his way out of occupied Czechoslovakia, he studied at the Sorbonne and directed Czech language broadcasts back to his homeland. Fleeing Paris on a bicycle 10 days ahead of the Nazi tanks, he made his way to America, via Dakar. Casablanca, and a Vichy concentration camp in Morocco. During the war, he taught physics in the Army Specialized Training Program at the University of Iowa, the Navy VI2 program at Doan College, and at the University of Nebraska. Returning to Czechoslovakia after the war, he taught briefly at the University of Olomouc before fleeing once again, one month before the communist coup d'etat, to take up permanent residence, and citizenship in the United States. Professor Čapek joined the Carleton College philosophy faculty in 1948. In 1962, after a distinguished career at Carleton both as a teacher and as a produc20 [pageend20]tive scholar, he accepted a position at Boston University, where he served with distinction until his retirement in 1974. Visiting professorships included the Davis Campus of the university of California, Emory University, North Texas University, Yale and again Carleton, as the Donald J. Cowling Distinguished Visiting Professor of Philosophy. In 1983 Carleton honored him with a Doctor of Letters degree. He was the author of A Key to Czechoslovakia: The Territory of Kladsko (1946), Philosophical Impact of Contemporary Physics (1961), Bergson and Modern Physics (1971), The Concepts of Space and Time (1975), The New Aspects of Time: Its Continuity and Novelties (1991) and numerous articles in scholarly periodicals. Milič Čapek made major contributions to the understanding of the philosophical implications of relativity theory and quantum mechanics, and of the philosophy of time. MR Václav E.
