Would the British Government then have taken the view that the Communist regime must he given "de jure" recognition because it was in effective control of the main part of Greece and that no further support should be given to its opponents? Or would they have used British seapower Wo protect the anti-Communist remnant in the islands and prevent their capture by the Communists from the mainland? No certain answer can be given to this question, but it is probably that in reaching a decision British statesmen would have taken into account the moral effect likely to be produced in neighbouring countries by a policy of first helping one's friends and then abandoning them to their fate as soon as the situation .became difficult. If the case of China is compared with that of Greece, the most important difference is one of time. Britain intervened decisively in ^Greece at the end of 1944 and conti, nued to give full support to the Greek Government (with America gradually taking over responsibility from the spring of 1947) until the collapse of the Greek Communists following Yugoslavia's defection from the Soviet bloc in 1948. In China, on the other hand, American armed forces were not directly used to help resistance to Communism, un- ^til the decision to neutralise Formosa in the summer of 1950 nearly 5 years after the end of the Pacific War and at a time when the whole ^mainland of China was already in ^Communist hands. Previous to this, American policy towards China had been one of vacillation and indecision. American marines, landed in North China in the autumn of 1945, had orders only to disarm the Japanese and not to interfere in fighting between Chinese factions. Throughout 1945 the foreign policy of the United States, in the Far East as in Europe, was dominated by the pro- Soviet orientation of Roosevelt " Grand Design". In consequence the American Government refrained from protesting against the help given by the Soviet Union to the Chinese Communists in Manchuria, and so far from hacking Chiang Kai Shek in his jattempt to crush the Communist revolt, it compelled him by economic pressure to conclude a truce with them at a time when his troops were advancing, and to negotiate with them for a coalition government under the mediation of G-eneral Marshall. Bor nearly a year an arms embargo was applied against the Chinese Uat- tionalist Government v^hereby it was not only denied supplies of arms on subsidy but was even prevented from buying any. It is widely but erroneously believed in Great Britain that the Chinese Communists won their civil war without any assistance from Russia.
