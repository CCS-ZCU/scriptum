Eugen Relgis [pageend2]SUR LA CONDUITE DES CHOSES HUMAINES — Tout pouvoir qui, pour s’établir, doit abaisser les hommes, diminue son prestige de ce qu’il retire de noble à l’homme. La taille morale d’un régime suit celle de son peuple : grande elle est, quand les têtes restent droites; petite, quand les échines se courbent. — Quiconque dispose de ses semblables dans leur vie matérielle ou leur conduite morale, ne devrait jamais oublier, pour rester digne de son pouvoir, que dans chaque destinée humaine est impliquée l’humanité entière. Quel que soit ton orgueil, l’homme est plus important que toi, et la puissance que tu t’attribues vient seulement de celle que te confère sa présence en toi. 1 ARENA [pageend3]— Cette surhumanité à laquelle ľ être aspire, les religions la font miroiter dans un au-delà métaphysique; la politique, dans un plus tard terrestre. Aux mains des maîtres qui savent l’utiliser, nul moyen n’est plus sûr que l’espoir pour faire de l’homme un instrument docile. Les hommes ressemblent moins à de futurs surhommes qu’à de vulgaires poissons : c’est par l’appât le plus illusoire qu’ils se font prendre. — Dans quelque régime que ce soit, les responsables de la vie sont nécessaires. Qu’ils s’appellent chefs ou commissaires, délégués ou patrons, leur rôle est de faire travailler pour produire. Reconnaître que les élites sont une nécessité, c’est admettre ľ existence d’un corps de privilégiés. Le nier ne change rien au fait, mais influe sur le vocabulaire. — Le devoir d’un chef est d’agir et non point de discuter, car, sitôt conférée, l’autorité devient une force et cesse d’être un principe. L’autorité n’est pas la force qui se fait craindre, mais celle avec laquelle on ne discute pas l’obéissance. — On dit d’un chef dont on veut excuser les lacunes, qu’il est trop grand pour voir ce qui se passe à ses pieds. Mais, s’il était vraiment très grand, il le verrait quand même, comme rien n’échappe, jusque dans la vallée, à l’aigle sur son aire. L’autorité ne vaut que par l’idée qu’en donne celui qui la détient. 2 [pageend4]Sur ses chefs, la communauté sociale a des droits, moins parce qu’elle les a nommés, que parce qu’ils incarnent, au nom de tous, le principe même du bien public. L’homme qui détient l’honneur de gouverner des hommes n’en reste digne que dans la mesure où il se met à leur service. — Tout pouvoir, parce qu’il s’établit au profit de quelques-uns, ne s’exerce que par la contrainte. L’autorité est donc, en fait, non un principe philosophique, mais une question de rapport de forces. On n’impose pas un chef. Il s’impose, ou il n’est pas un chef. — - Le respect des lois n’est dû au pouvoir légal que s’il est le pouvoir légitime; sinon, ce serait rendre les lois complices de l’oppression, et, comme telles, engager le peuple à les combattre. Quand la conscience des hommes rejette une certaine forme d’autorité, personne n’obéit physiquement sans moralement désobéir. — Dans les périodes de dictature, le respect des lois est assis sur le chantage à la terreur; dans les autres, on change de mots. Aucune autorité morale n’est assez forte pour s’imposer à tous, si elle n’a l’intimidation pour complice, et pour auxiliaire la violence. 3 [pageend5]— Nulle condamnation, n’atteint plus définitivement la justice, que celle qui frappe un accusé, lorsque le pouvoir lui a donné pour juge un adversaire. Quand la rigueur devient inhumaine, elle se confond avec l’injustice. — Comme les défenseurs des droits de l’homme, les bonnes âmes et les faux jetons, les juges regrettent qu’il faille recourir aux services d’une certaine forme de police, mais celle-ci, plus encore, de n’avoir pas affaire qu’à des apôtres de la non-violence ou à des objecteurs de conscience. Pour maîtriser la nature humaine, ce n’est pas une poigne de fer qu’il faut avoir, c’est deux. — Certaines indifférences apparentes de la part des législateurs indiquent moins un manque de doctrine qu’une absence totale de courage. Rien plus que le caractère n’est étroitement lié à la politique : la faiblesse du pouvoir entraîne celle des valeurs morales; quand ľ armature sociale s’affaiblit, la notion de l’autorité disparaît. — En politique, pour se faire agréer, le commun se suffit à lui-même; les hommes supérieurs doivent s’adjoindre une forte dose d’humilité. Quelles que soient ses tendances, tout ouvrage sur l’art de gouverner débute par un chapitre en filigrane sur l’art d’avaler des couleuvres. 4 [pageend6]— L’art du manœuvrier politique consiste à mener ses troupes en terrains variés, et, malgré ses voltes-faces, à les garder toujours en mains. La politique n’a de rapport avec la vérité, la justice, les droits de l’homme, l’idéal, qu’en ce qu’elle est l’art de les utiliser à son profit. — Devant un tribunal politique, un accusé qui ne s’est jamais renié vaut mieux, par cette preuve de droiture, que son juge même qui n’a fait que retourner sa veste. Chaque fois qu’à l’esprit de parti s’oppose l’opportunisme, c’est comme au mal s’oppose un mal plus grand. — Le plus grand crime des hérétiques ou des déviationnistes est moins encore de rompre l’unité, celle du camp et de la foi, que de compromettre le mouvement et de briser le bond en avant. Qu’un régime politique moderne cesse de progresser, il perd sa justification. Tout ce qui concourt à entraver sa marche contribue ainsi à sa perte. S’il ne le supprime. — Quand un régime défend son existence, prétendre ne pas faire de politique, c’est en faire, et la justice qui refuse d’être partisane parce qu’elle doit rester impartiale, prend position contre lui. On ne juge pas ses ennemis, on les abat. Ou ils vous abattent. 5 [pageend7]— En politique, il faut être pour ou contre, comme il faut Vôtre en présence du Bien ou du Mal. Mais il ne peut s’agir, pour l’homme libre, que d’un engagement volontaire et non de service obligatoire. Un régime, tout comme une religion, qui ne laisse place à aucune discussion, ne porte pas de germes féconds pour l’avenir : en stagnant, il immobilise l’homme; en le repliant sur lui-même, il l’étouffe. — Chaque fois qu’il t’est demandé de prendre parti, de te prononcer pour ou contre, de t’engager, sans que te soient révélés le fonds de la question et ses dessous — si tu ne veux pas être dupe, abstiens-toi. Dans tout homme de parti, il y a un roublard ou une dupe; dans tout indifférent, une future victime consentante. — La discipline ne supprime pas le désordre; elle ne le rend qu’inapparent. Vous pouvez dompter la colère et imposer le silence, vous n’étoufferez jamais dans les esprits les fermentations d’une révolte. L’ordre qui ne règne que par la force, c’est en réalité, la force qui règne sur le désordre. — Ne dure que ce qui change. L’homme pourrit au dedans, s’il stagne. Une société qui meurt, c’est qu’elle n’évolue pas. Les hommes qui, au cours de leur vie et faute d’occasions, n’ont pas aidé à transformer le monde, appartiennent à une génération qui n’a pas fait ce qu’elle pouvait faire. 6 [pageend8]— L’influence des idées et leur action dans la vie sociale tiennent moins à leurs raisons ou à leur valeur propre qu’à la puissance de suggestion et aux moyens dont disposent leurs propagandistes. Ce que la nature n’a jamais pu faire, d’un buisson de ronces un bouquet de violettes ou une colombe d’un faucon, la propagande le réussit si bien qu’elle se transforme elle-même, cette ogresse, en agnelle. — Toute religion, doctrine, philosophie, qui se donne pour fondement l’amour et la charité, commet un crime à l’égard des lois naturelles, celui de protéger les faibles et de sauver les impuissants. Mais c’est l’honneur du spirituel de proclamer que ľ entraide pour la vie est une loi naturelle plus grande que la lutte pour la vie, et que les problèmes humains ne se résolvent pas en sacrifiant des hommes. IN EXILE : ALBANIANS Tayar Zavalani INTRODUCTION L’Albanie a eu une courte période d’indépendance entre l’occupation ottomane, qui dura cinq siècles, et la tyrannie communiste qui dure depuis la fin de la deuxième guerre mondiale. On peut donc affirmer sans exagération que la littérature albanaise est essentiellement une littérature d’exil. Ceci s’applique particulièrement à la seconde moitié du XIXe siècle où se situe le grand essor de la littérature albanaise, marqué par des œuvres de grand talent. C’est alors qu’un grand nombre de poèmes, d’essais et d’études historiques firent leur apparition en pays étranger, surtout en Autriche, en Italie et dans certains pays balkaniques. Le congrès de Berlin fut le signal du réveil national albanais. Les puissances européennes avaient concédé à nos voisins du nord, des territoires de l’Empire ottoman habités par des Albanais. Une résistance farouche et triomphante fut opposée à cette décision arbitraire du « Concert de l’Europe ». Le congrès de Berlin dut modifier les clauses du traité de paix entre la Russie et la Turquie. En fin de compte, le Monténégro put annexer les ports albanais de Dulcigno et Antivari sous la protection d’une escadre navale des grandes puissances. Le choc de Berlin fit comprendre aux Albanais que le moment crucial de leur longue histoire était arrivée : 9 [pageend11]ou ils devaient former une nation capable de se gouverner elle-même dans le monde moderne, ou bien ils devaient disparaître comme les débris d’une race ancienne et inadaptée. Or cinq siècles de domination ottomane avaient laissé une situation presque désespérée. Deux tiers des Albanais avaient embrassé, pour des raisons variées, la religion islamique; le reste de la population étant divisé entre catholiques dans le nord et orthodoxes dans le centre et le sud du pays. Dans ces conditions, la religion ne pouvait pas servir de ralliement d’un mouvement d’émancipation nationale, comme cela avait lieu dans les pays voisins. Mais le sentiment d’identité nationale était profond chez les Albanais et la diversité des religions n’avait pas créé entre eux un antagonisme inconciliable. Une tradition millénaire marquée de luttes incessantes contre de multiples envahisseurs dont les derniers venus menaçaient d’usurper ce qui restait de leur patrimoine illyrien, créait un fort sentiment d’unité exprimé dans une langue commune parlée depuis trois mille ans. Une phalange de poètes, de pamphlétaires et d’historiens mirent leurs talents remarquables et variés au service de la lutte pour l’indépendance nationale. Comme il arrive presque toujours avec les peuples qui dépassent la phase du folklore pour entrer dans celle de la littérature proprement dite, les œuvres poétiques prédominent dans l’essor littéraire albanais de notre époque. Nairn Frasheri, le poète national par excellence, fut pour ainsi dire un exilé dans son propre pays, perdu dans l’immensité de l’Empire ottoman. Il venait d’une famille célèbre qui a donné à l’Albanie, outre son premier grand poète moderne, deux générations d’hommes politiques et d’écrivains. Son dernier représentant, Midhat Frasheri, essayiste, érudit et leader de la résistance albanaise pendant la guerre, mourut en exil il y a quelques années. 10 [pageend12]Naim Frasheri marque la transition entre les influences orientale et occidentale dans la littérature albanaise. Il trouve son inspiration tant chez les poètes persans Sadi et Firdusi que chez Virgile. Ses poésies les plus populaires, comme « Les Fleurs du Printemps », « La Terre et les Bêtes », chantent les beautés du pays natal. Mais Nairn Frasheri mit aussi son talent poétique au service de la cause nationale. Il est l’auteur d’une histoire en vers du héros national albanais George Castrioti alias Scanderberg. Même la religion doit servir à réveiller le sentiment national chez les Albanais et à cimenter leur unité. Il appartient lui-même à la secte des Bek- tashis qui est opposée à tout point de vue à la religion musulmane orthodoxe. Son poème intitulé « Qerbela » décrit en termes émouvants la lutte pour le siège suprême 4e l’Islamisme après la mort du Prophète : le poète stigmatise le comportement des Mawiya et des Yazid qui pour les Bektashis, comme pour les Shia de Perse, «ont des usurpateurs. « En tant qu’albanais, — écrit Giuseppe Schiro junior, auteur d’une histoire récente de la littérature albanaise, — Nairn Frasheri contemplé Dieu à travers la terre et son peuple de sorte que l’aspiration religieuse et l’idéal politique se confondent et ne font qu’un. Nairn parle de l’Albanie à tous les âges, dans un esprit mystique, et chante la grandeur et la bonté de Dieu en exaltant les attraits de la terre natale, les vertus sublimes des ancêtres et des héros du passé, la simplicité naturelle du petit peuple ». La carrière poétique de Nairn Frasheri remplit la seconde moitié du XIXe siècle et marque les premiers pas vers la création d’une conscience nationale albanaise, prélude aux combats pour l’indépendance. Avec le moine 11 [pageend13]franciscain George Fishta, nous entrons en pleine période contemporaine. Son génie poétique subit l’empreinte profonde du sort de l’Albanie dans ce demi-siècle. Pour en immortaliser les divers épisodes, il excella dans trois genres : épique, lyrique et satirique. George Fishta, de même que son vénéré prédécesseur Nairn Frasheri, fut, pour commencer, un poète en exil dans son propre pays. Il passa sa jeunesse parmi les montagnards de l’Albanie du Nord, berceau de ses ancêtres. Il put ainsi les connaître à fond, avec leurs qualités et leurs défauts, leur enthousiasme pour tout ce qui est frappant ou héroïque, leur susceptibilité extrême pour toute atteinte à l’honneur et leur tendance invétérée à laver l’affront dans le sang du coupable, leurs croyances et leurs superstitions. II avait écouté avec une attention profonde et passionnée les bardes du pays récitant les chants du folklore traditionnel sur les actes des héros du passé, moitié légende et moitié fait. Inspiré par ce passé rendu si vivant et si actuel par le folklore, George Fishta écrivit son chef-d’œuvre, « La- huta * e Malcis » (Saga des montagnes) qui est considéré à juste titre comme l’épopée nationale albanaise. Le morceau en allemand publié dans cette anthologie donnera au lecteur une idée de ce magnifique poème que les Albanais récitent à toute occasion comme des versets de l’Ecriture Sainte. Le temps me manque pour parler en détail des autres aspects du génie poétique du Père Fishta qui a trouvé son expression dans des recueils de poèmes intitulés « Le Buisson des Fées », « Les Guêpes du Parnasse ». Son poème sur le génie humain dont le lecteur lira plus loin la traduction en langue anglaise (pâle reflet de l’original), exprime bien la puissance de son génie évocateur et sa vision de poète philosophe et chrétien. * « Lahuta ». Instrument à une corde dont les troubadours de l’Albanie du Nord s’accompagnent dans leurs récitals. 12 [pageend14]Le meilleur de l’œuvre poétique de Fislita, en commençant par son poème épique « Lahuta e Malcis », fut publié hors d’Albanie pendant que nous étions encore sous l’occupation ottomane. Après l’invasion du Vendredi Saint 1939, les Fascistes ont voulu exploiter sa popularité en le nommant senator. Il resta jusqu’à son dernier soupir le poète national vénéré de tous sur son piédestal. Un phénomène assez rare, pour ne pas dire unique, est l’essor littéraire parmi les colonies albanaises d’Italie. Il y a en Italie du Sud et en Sicile plus de deux cent mille habitants d’origine et de langue albanaises, descendants des réfugiés albanais qui ont fui l’invasion ottomane au XVe siècle. Quoique assimilés à la culture italienne et servant leur nouvelle patrie avec dévotion et loyauté, ils ont conservé le souvenir vivant, pour ne pas dire la nostalgie ineffaçable, du pays natal et continuent à parler l’albanais le plus pur dans les familles. Lorsque le mouvement pour l’indépendance de l’Albanie fut lancé après le Congrès de Berlin, les écrivains de langue albanaise nés en Calabre et en Sicile, en furent les promoteurs les plus ardents. Parmi tant de poètes et de prosateurs, aussi doués les uns que les autres, je n’en mentionnerai qu’un : Joseph. Schiro, l’aîné. Son neveu, qui porte le même nom et qui enseigne à l’université de Padoue, s’exprime ainsi : « Joseph Schiro est le dernier des poètes italo-albanais de la Sicile et parmi les plus grands poètes albanais qui aient vécu sur les deux rives de l’Adriatique (1). » Schiro exprime l’envoûtement et la gloire du passé dans un long et touchant poème intitulé « En terre étrangère ». Il y décrit en termes d’une émouvante simplicité les circonstances qui ont forcé tant d’Albanais à chercher refuge en Italie plus de quatre siècles aupa- (1) (Giuseppe Schiro — Storia della letteratura albanese — page 211.) 13 [pageend15]ravant. Chacun des neuf chants du poème est construit autour de la vie d’un héros albanais qui s’est distingué dans la lutte contre l’envahisseur ottoman et aussi dans la résistance contre cette domination étrangère qui durait depuis quatre siècles et avait détruit tout vestige de civilisation et de culture occidentales. Le poème se termine par un cri d’espoir dans la résurrection de l’Albanie grâce à l’appui de l’Italie hospitalière, unie et fortifiée par son Risorgimento. Avec Faik Konitza commence la génération des écrivains qui ont refusé de se soumettre à l’occupation italienne et à la tyranie communiste qui l’a suivie. Il venait d’une famille aristocratique musulmane et avait étudié au collège des Jésuites à Scutari. Il continua ses études à l’université de Dijon où il fut reçu bachelier ès lettres. De là, il se rendit à Paris où il suivit les cours de littérature au Collège de France. En 1897, il fonda à Bruxelles une revue littéraire, ou plutôt culturelle comme on dirait aujourd’hui, appelée « Albania », qu’il dut plus tard transférer à Londres et qu’il continua de publier jusqu’en 1909 au prix de grands sacrifices. Il était obligé d’écrire sous un pseudonyme de peur que sa famille en Albanie ne souffre des représailles de la part des autorités turques. En 1909, Faik Konitza se rendit aux Etats-Unis où il devint l’animateur de la colonie albanaise en prenant la direction de Dielli (Le Soleil) publié à Boston. Il trouva moyen de continuer ses études à l’Université de Harvard et devint à la longue un écrivain de langue anglaise ainsi que le lecteur s’en rendra compte en feuilletant cette anthologie. La création d’un Etat Albanais indépendant fut la source d’une profonde désillusion pour ceux qui avaient 14 [pageend16]combattu afin de réaliser cet idéal. Faik Konitza en était un et il n’hésita pas à mener une campagne virulente contre le régime qui s’était instauré à Tirana après 1920. Ses sarcasmes furent le régal de tous les mécontents et le journal « Dielli », qui était l’organe de l’association des Albanais d’Amérique, « Vatra » (Le Foyer), fut interdit pour un certain temps en Albanie. L’émule albanais de Voltaire s’imposa un exil volontaire; et, sous le règne du roi Zog, il occupa, avec honneur, le poste de ministre d’Albanie à Washington. Faik Konitza n’était pas ce qu’on pourrait appeler un écrivain professionnel. Il ne saisissait la plume que sous l’effet de l’indignation, quand il s’agissait de combattre l’injustice, l’ignorance, et surtout la fatuité des pseudointellectuels. C’est pourquoi ses écrits sont épars et attendent d’être recueillis et publiés en œuvres complètes. Son ami et successeur à la direction du journal « Dielli », M. Panariti, a réuni quelques essais en anglais parmi lesquels j’ai choisi celui sur la langue albanaise pour cette anthologie. « Les maladies infantiles » de l’indépendance furent aggravées en Albanie par la propagande subversive et l’intervention à peine voilée des pays voisins. Déjà sous l’Empire ottoman le clergé orthodoxe de l’Albanie du Sud agissait ouvertement comme agent de la propagande grecque. Il s’agissait tout simplement de faire annexer à la Grèce l’Albanie du Sud qu’on désignait sous le nom d’Epire du Nord et qui fait encore aujourd’hui l’objet des revendications du gouvernement d’Athènes. Un évêque albanais, Mgr Fan Noli, y apporta la solution radicale qui s’imposait. Il venait d’un village albanais de Thrace et avait émigré en Amérique. Par des prodiges d’efforts et de persévérance, il fit ses études à l’Univer15 [pageend17]sité de Harvard et obtint le diplôme de Bachelor of Arts cum laude. Il se joignit à Faik Konitza dans ses labeurs ayant pour but de consolider l’organisation Vatra des colonies albanaises à travers le vaste continent américain et de publier régulièrement son journal hebdomadaire. Mais son but principal était de mettre fin à l’immixtion de la Grèce dans les affaires de l’Albanie en se servant du clergé orthodoxe. Pour y parvenir il entra dans la prêtrise, fut consacré évêque et proclama l’Eglise Autocéphale albanaise. Après avoir éliminé le clergé de formation grecque, Mgr Fan Noli s’attaqua à la langue grecque. Il traduisit en albanais les Ecritures Saintes et toute la liturgie orthodoxe. Depuis qu’il s’est à moitié retiré dans sa belle villa de Florida, Fan Noli a transcrit en prose poétique certains chapitres du Nouveau Testament que nous publions dans cette anthologie. A part ses œuvres originales, Fan Noli a apporté une contribution précieuse à la littérature albanaise par ses traductions de chef-d’œuvres de la littérature mondiale; entre autres, les principaux drames de Shakespeare en vers libres, Don Quichotte et les poésies de Longfellow et d’Omer Khayam. Etant lui-même foncièrement un épicurien (comme un de ces abbés du temps jadis), il mit toute son âme dans la version albanaise du poète persan, et l’on peut dire qu’elle soutient avantageusement la comparaison avec la traduction anglaise dont il s’est servi. A un âge déjà avancé, Mgr Fan Noli se mit à étudier la musique et, en juin 1938, il obtint le diplôme de Bachelor of Music in Composition. Pour sa thèse, il écrivit une monographie sur Beethoven et la Révolution française. La publication de cette thèse en librairie en 1947 lui valut des lettres de félicitations de la part de sommités musicales d’alors. Entre autres, G.B. Shaw le complimenta en ces termes : 16 [pageend18]« It is the work of a first rate critic and biographer. I read it through from end to end with the utmost satisfaction, being myself a connoisseur as musician, critic, and artist-philosopher by profession. » J’aurais voulu parler longuement de tous les gens de lettres dont les noms figurent dans cette anthologie, mais je vois dans mon esprit le secrétaire de rédaction qui me jette un regard courroucé parce que cette introduction lui semble avoir dépassé la mesure; je me bornerai donc à dire quelques mots sur Ernest Koliqi dont le talent littéraire et l’inspiration poétique atteignirent leur apogée pendant la période de l’indépendance albanaise, et Arsyi Pipa qui écrivit ses poèmes sur des bouts de papier pendant qu’il gémissait dans les camps de concentration et les prisons communistes. Ernest Koliqi, né à Scutari en 1903, est l’auteur d’une œuvre considérable en prose et en vers. Il excelle aussi bien dans la production littéraire originale que dans la traduction de l’italien en albanais et vice versa. Dans son exil à Rome il dirige la meilleure revue albanaise intitulée « Les Pléiades » et vient de publier une anthologie de poèmes albanais qu’il a lui-même traduits en italien. Par excès de modestie, il n’a retenu que deux courts poèmes de sa main, que je me fais un devoir et un plaisir de reproduire ici. Pour terminer, je parlerai du plus jeune des poètes en exil, dernier rescapé du régime communiste : Arshi Pipa. Comme on peut se l’imaginer, « Le Livre de la Prison », est l’histoire poignante des souffrances et des humiliations subies par l’auteur et ses camarades prisonniers pendant dix ans de « construction du socialisme ». Certains titres de poèmes en disent plus que de longs chapitres : « Le Printemps au sous-sol », « La mort du soldat », « La Prison de Burrel », « Le chant du martyr ». Le jeune poète, si éprouvé physiquement mais à l’esprit inébranlable, ne se contente pas de décrire la vie de prison dans ses aspects matériels. Son âme de poète s’efforce de noter soigneusement et sans aigreur les réactions de son être aux avatars qu’on lui fait subir. Il décrit avec une étonnante sérénité d’esprit les sentiments qu’il éprouve dans des moments où il sait qu’il peut être insulté, torturé, exposé aux affres de la faim et du froid. Ses descriptions des conditions de vie dans les camps et les prisons font également preuve d’une acuité d’observation exceptionnelle; l’ambiance est recréée par des traits sobres et incisifs comme les grands coups de pinceau qui font la beauté des tableaux de Van Gogh. Ce petit chef-d’œuvre qu’est « le livre de la Prison » attend son traducteur dans les principales langues européennes. Faik Konitza ALBANIA : THE ROCK GARDEN OF SOUTH EAST EUROPE Language and Literature I The language spoken by the Albanians is a very old original language of their own, said by some to be the oldest in Europe. There is nothing mysterious about Albanian. But linguistics, as a science, is of very recent date; previous to the establishment of the new science, all talk about languages was more or less in the nature of guess-work. So, second-rate refecence books, written sometimes by clever but uninformed amateurs and not by up-to-date specialist, continue to repeat about Alba44 [pageend46]nian old erroneous opinions, of which the educated reader should beware. The Albanian language has been for years thoroughly studied and definitely classified by competent scholars. Albanian is an Indo-Germanic language. This does not mean, as I once heard a lady declare, that it is a mixture of Indian and German. Indo-Germanic or Indo-European (and in the nineteenth century they also used to say Aryan) is the name of a group of ten kindred languages which include ancient Indian or Sanscrit, Iranian, Armenian, Slavonic, Baltic or Letto-Lithuanian and old Prussian, Albanian, Latin, Greek, Celtic and Germanic. And because it would be too long to have a synthetic word embodying all these ten languages in one expression, scholars have taken one name at both extremities of the chain and adopted a hyphenated appellation, which includes by implication the remaining eight unnamed languages. There are no real dialects in Albanian. As an eminent authority on Indo-Germanic philology, Dr. Holger Pedersen, professor at the University of Copenhagen, remarks, «Albanian is a single language if some small dialectal variations are expected». These variations are mainly five. In certain words, where the south has r the north has n; vura and vuna = I put; — ë in the south becomes ä in the north : zë and za = voice; in some words, h in the south is f in the north : shoh and shof = I see ; — the south has something analogous to what Latinists call a «dissimilation» where the north has an «assimilation» : kendoj and kennoj = I sing (sf. Latin adfero and affero) ; finally, the northern speech shortens the end of certain words and, for instance, has kam pa = I have seen, instead of the fuller southern form kam parë. In part of a province (Laberia) in the south, they have i instead of y (both pronounced as in Latin) : si = eye, instead of sy. A district in the south (Zagoria) has y in certain words where everywhere else there is an i : 45 [pageend47]shpyrt instead of shpirt = soul. In addition, in two districts of the north, viz. Mati and Mirdita, the i often is pronounced ai : thus they say there maik = friend, when everywhere else they say mik. Finally, in the southern district of Kurveleshi, of which the small town of Bëncha is the centre, they have an archaic form kat, meaning he has, instead of ka in the rest of the country. These are about all the dialectal variations. But it must be added that the intonation sometimes differs considerably from province to province. One thing that puzzles the non-Albanian is the variety of forms that Albanian place-names assume in dispatches; and I am repeatedly asked whether Berat or Berati, Tirane or Tirana is the correct form. In common with some other languages like Rumanian and Swedish, Albanian places the article at the end of the word. Thus, plak means old man, and plaku, the old man; mal, mountain, mali, the mountain; dere, door, dera, the door; krye, head, kryet, the head. In Albanian, even proper names assume articles; so we have Leskovik and, with the article, Leskoviku; Berat and Berati; Tirane and Tirana. Both forms are correct, and both are used in turn according to their place in the phrase. Not a little confusion is created by the fact that, on the maps of Albania published in various countries, no consistent system has been followed in deciding whether to give the place-names with or without the article, so that some names are given with the article and others without, in a haphazard way. A brilliant exception is the map of Albania published in 1928 by the German cartographer, Dr. Herbert Louis, to whom we have already referred and who has prepared the only scientific map of the country. In this map all place-names are consistently given without the articles. To every man his own language has a sweet sound. But Albanian seems to be attractive also to other people. 46 [pageend48]Sir Edwin Pears, a grand old Englishman who lived forty years in Constantinople and visited every part of the old Ottoman Empire, writes of the Albanians : «Among the Albanians are beautiful heads which recall those of Classic Greece. All speak the Albanian language, though with certain dialectal peculiarities between Northerners and Southerners. In both forms it is a pleasant language to hear». Some years ago, having delivered a lecture in Albanian at the Y.M.C.A. centre of Jamestown, New York, for the benefit of the numerous Albanians living in that city, I was approached by an American lady who had been present, and she told me that of all the foreign languages she often listened to, she felt Albanian and Swedish were the softest and most agreeable to the ear. One might get a different impression from Albanian as it is written; but that is due to modern cumbersome spelling which, in its effort to represent every phonetic shade, often gives to the words a repellingly complicated form. II There was an Albanian literature already at the beginning of the fourteenth century. We have a first- rate historic document attesting the fact. In the year 1332, a German monk, Brochard, in a book dedicated to Philippe de Valois, King of France, bears witness to the existence of this Albanian literature. He also notes that «the Albanians, although they have a language altogether different from that of the Latins, nevertheless use the Latin alphabet in all their books». And the credibility of Friar Brochard, or Brocardus Teutonicus as the Latin chronicles call him, is highly established. 47 [pageend49]«As a writer», says an erudite Frenchman, «Brochard often is almost barbaric; he is a monk, and an illiterate monk. As a traveler, he has been more fortunate; the circumstances in which he found himself have enabled him to see well; and his spirit of observation, his love of truth, lend him authority... A precious harvest of authentic documents on geography and history is the result». Brochard himself proudly boasts that «he writes of things he has experienced and seen rather than heard». The Benedictines, who in the earlier Middle Ages were the only monastic order in Albania, were succeeded in that country by the Dominicans in the thirteenth century. Friar Brochard, as a member of the latter order, had therefore good connections with Albania which he may have visited more than once. His book raises an interesting problem. If by 1332 there was an Albanian literature in existence, it is obsious that the writing of the vernacular must have started much earlier. It would take many years from the mere introduction of an alphabet to the development of a literature. One wonders at what time Albanian began to be written. In February 1272, Charles of Anjou, the younger brother of Sain Jouis, King of France, was elected King of Albania; and he sent to Durazzo, as his Viceroy, a nobleman named Gazon d’Echinard, to organise the new realm. It is possible that Echinard, if he found himself in a country with a national but unwritten language, may have experienced difficulties in his government work and may have suggested that something should be done to reduce the vernacular to writing. In this case, it would be safe to place the beginnings of written Albanian around the year 1273. But it is strange to find so soon afterwards the existence of a national literature : a longer time would seem necessary for such a development. 48 [pageend50]I am rather inclined to place the beginnings of written Albanian before the accession of Charles of Anjou, under the Hohenstaufens and even a few generations earlier, at the time of the Normans. The Croat scholar Emilianus de Sufflay, the man who best knew Medieval Albania, has expressed the opinion that, at that period of her history, Albania was not behind the other European countries in the way of living and in general progress. The country was much more a cultural and commercial prolongation of Italy than a region under Byzantine influence. In the twelfth century the Italian vernacular, «il volgare», timidly began to assert itself against Latin; and the first known literary monument in Italian, the Song of a Tuscan Jester, dates from that period. As everything happening in Italy was bound sooner or later to have an echo in Albania, it is permissible to believe that the idea of writing their own language may have come to the Albanians from Italy by the end of the twelfth century. Some day a definite answer to the question may be found in the archives of the Vatican or in the unsearched contemporanious records of the Benedictine and Dominican orders. What that early Albanian literature was like, it is impossible to say. But it is permissible to venture a guess or two. There may have been a few poems and tales of chivalry, and more probably some anecdotic collections on the pattern of the Italian Cento Novelle Antiche; but by far the greatest number of works must have been books of religion and piety — and there is some ground for this supposition. Then, as it was much later the case, the Albanian layman needed only a little knowledge of writing, just enough to scribble a short note; he had no use for literature — in spite of Lord Broughton’s assertion at the beginning of the nineteenth century that «were an Albanian to sit for his picture, he would wish to be drawn, like the Admirable 49 [pageend51]Chrichton, with a sword in one hand and a book in the other». The authors of all the Albanian books printed in the sixteenth and seventeenth centures were priests; and all these works, with the exception of a dictionary published in 1635, are religious books. It is a remarkable fact that these authors speak often of old manuscripts in their possession. That manuscripts of early literature had survived as long as Christianity remained numerically strong in the country, and were destroyed only after the seventeenth-century mass-conversion to Islam, is a substantial proof of their Christian character. We have to wait until the eighteenth century for a book of literary merit. It is a Life of the Blessed Virgin in verse by the priest Giulio Variboba, an Albanian of Sicily. Although I cannot share the opinion of an eminent German scholar, the late Professor Gustav Meyer, who calls Variboba’s work a «classic», the poem has freshness and charm, though often it comes dangerously near to doggerel. At the same period as Variboba, a poet of an entirely different character was living in Albania. His name was Zyko Kamberi, and he was born in Kolonja, in the south. He is the author of amusing but generally scurrilous epigrams, and of a poem called «The Bride’s First Night», a work of formal beauty but extremely licentious. Zyko Kamberiês work, because of its offensive character, has never been printed. The following close adaptation of a famous epigram, often quoted in Albania, will convey some idea of his style. I will kick thee to the cesspoll, thou blind gold, That hast made the noisy upstart rich and bold. The nineteenth century saw both a revival of the national language and an intensified persecution on the part of the Turkish authorities against it as the Sultans 50 [pageend52]learned from experience that knowledge is a dangerous weapon. A person found in possession of even a harmless book in Albanian, such as a grammar, was seized, handcuffed, and either put to death or sent to some distant province in Asia or Africa to rot in exile for the rest of his life. Nevertheless, societies were formed abroad and a good many books found their way to Albania from Italy, Greece, Bulgaria, Rumania and Egypt. But they were mostly patriotic drivel in anaemic prose and endless doggerel. There were two exceptions. A studious man from Elbasan, Kostandin Kristoforidhi, made a beautiful translation, in vigorous idiomatic Albanian, of the New Testament and the Psalms (though he, otherwise a man of great integrity, was here guilty of an intellectual dishonesty in giving out the Psalms as a direct version from the Hebrew, when a comparison with the Hebrew and Greek texts shows conclusively that he made a translation from the Septuagint). He was also the author of a monumental Albanian-Greek Dictionary, a work upon which it is said that he spent some forty odd years and which is considered his most important contribution to Albanian letters. In 1879 there appeared in Alexandria, Egypt, «The Albanian Bee», a collection of old traditional folksongs, edited by Efthim Mitko, — a work similar to Percy’s «Reliques of Ancient English Poetry» and Arnim’s «Des Knaben Wunderhorn». In the twentieth century, we have a few distinguished works that may be classified as genuine literature if by literature we understand an artistic choice of words in order to express thoughts and emotions. I would mention first of all Gjergj Fishta, a Franciscan friar born in Zadrima, who is more than a mere poet : he has been an impressive intellectual leader for a quarter of a century. Poems, drama, comedies, Father Fishta has tried his hand at every branch of literature, and always with success. But outside Albania he is known only as the 51 [pageend53]author af «The Lute of the Mountainland», a pastoral and chivalrous poem, of which the German translation published in Leipzig some years ago gives but a feeble idea because the music of Fishta’s rhythm and his verbal colour could not be translated. A southerner, Anton Zako, in a book called «Father Tomori» (Tomori is the highest mountain of Albania), has gathered a collection of his lyric poems. Whether he writes of a shepherd playing the flute after a storm, or broods on the passing of life and impending death, his verse has always a soft rhythm and a captivating charm. Zako, who was a man of wealth, spent the last twenty years of his life at Heliopolis, Egypt, in the solitude of a villa, with no other companions than a sweetheart and his books. He is said to have been addicted to opium; but nothing in his verse betrays this weakness, although if true it would explain his passionate longing for the pastoral freshness of his early days in the mountains of Albania. Among other works, Zako has published a verse translation of Lafontaine’s Fables, remarkable for the happy rendering of French idioms in corresponding Albanian idioms and the complete absence of neologisms, -— an impressive illustration of the flexibility of the Albanian language when used by a skillful writer. At his death in 1930 he left some manuscripts, among them perhaps a play in verse. He had sent me the manuscript of this play a few years before the World War, inviting my opinion and criticism. I had read it carefully and returned it with praises and the suggestion of some changes. It would be a pity if, taking my remarks to heart, he should have destroyed the manuscript. In Egypt, also, there still lives an old North Albanian, Filip Shir oka, who, under the pen name of Geg Postripa, published in the magazine «Albania» (Brussels, 1897- 52 [pageend54]1904) a series of ringing sonnets, worthy of being collected in book form. Of the younger generation, Ernest Koliqi, who now teaches Albanian at the University of Rome, deserves a mention : he has published a novel, «The Shadow of the Mountains», and a book of poems with the stamp of originality both in feeling and rhythm. Father Vincent Prennushi, a Franciscan, is the author of a book of poems of penetrating charm, «Twigs and Flowers». In 1908 Spiro Dine published in Bulgaria his «Waves of the Sea», a collection of Albanian folk songs and folk tales. Born in the middle of the nineteenth century in Vithkuqi, a village of southern Albania, Dina migrated to Egypt during his youth and joined other Albanian patriots in reviving the national language. Unquestionably the foremost modern Albanian writer is His Grace Bishop Fan S. Of my own contributions to the literary movement of Albania, naturally I can say nothing. 53 [pageend55]Metropolitan Fan S. Noli, Ph.D. THE POET OF NAZARETH There is no doubt that Jesus was a poet. Like all the great Rabbis of Israel, Jesus had to formulate his doctrines in a poetic anti epigrammatic form for mnémotechnie reasons, that is to say to make them easy for his disciples to memorize. As a matter of fact, almost all the sayings of Jesus, including the Lord’s Prayer, have their poetic and epigrammatic parallels in Rabbinic literature. It is this poetic and epigrammatic form that saved the sayings of Jesus until they were translated into Greek and incorporated in our Four Greek Gospels. Last but not least, the sayings of Jesus, in spite of their Greek prose garb, have all the unmistareable characteristics of Hebrew poetry. Curiously enough, some of them are even rhymed in their Greek translation as they were in their original Aramaic. Now, in order to have a clear idea of Jesus as a poet, his sayings should be translated rhythmically as poetry. That is what I have tried to do in this version, in which about three fourths of St. Matthew’s Gospel have been rendered in blank verse, with a few rhymes here and there. Under the circumstances it is practically impossible to restore the original beauty of the sayings of Jesus. So I am far from thinking that my version of St. Matthew 54 [pageend56]will solve that problem, but I am quite sure that I am on the right track as a pioneer: I hope that somebody else will do a better job some day and give us a version worthy of the great poet of Nazareth. Er las Shakespeare, Calderon und französiche Theaterwerke. Dann, bie der Ausbreitung der Cholera in Neapel im Oktober 1836, kehrte er nach Hause zurück und brachte ein Büchlein mit, die Milosau-Lieder, die er im August jenes Jahres veröffentlicht hatte. In Neapel nimmt er aktiven Anteil an den damaligen revolutionären Bewegungen. I met my first Englishman on the boat which brought me to Great Britain in June, 1940. My wife and I had left Paris two days before the Germans occupied it. Through sheer luck we were able to find a taxi whose driver agreed to take us to Orleans, provided we supplied the necessary petrol. We managed it by agreeing to take with us two French girls who 72 [pageend74]wanted to join their families in the South of France. We were quite sure that from Orleans onwards it would be easy to travel. The traffic jam on the Route Nationale outside Paris was simply impossible to describe. All day long we advanced only a few miles. By nightfall our chauffeur, who knew the countryside well, managed to get out of the endless stream of cars and took a narrow lane. From then on we travelled at reasonable speed but by devious routes which greatly increased the distance we had to cover. By midnight our driver felt exhausted and had to stop on the road for a nap. I didn’t feel like sleeping at all. In the warm stillness of the summer night my mind kept on wandering. I thought of Paris on the eve of the approaching scourge, of the frantic urge felt by millions of Frenchmen to avoid at all costs witnessing the desecration of their beloved capital, of the almost biblical exodus of a whole nation... And now this perfect, soothing silence, as if nothing unusual were taking place. How could nature be so indifferent to the tragedy of man ? At dawn we continued our journey and before noon we were at Orleans. The town looked like a huge refugee camp. The cinemas, the churches, the cafés and the open squares were swarming with people carrying the most extraordinary pieces of luggage. We were lucky enough to find a room in a hotel. As we were four people to share it we had to put a mattress on the floor. Next morning the proprietress who had been duly informed of our wickedness, made a terrible row. She shouted at us in a rage : « Vous avez osé étaler par terre mon beau matelas tout neuf que j’ai payé mille francs ? » I tried to explain, to apologise, but that made her even angrier. «Don’t worry, Madame, — I said, — the Germans will 73 [pageend75]be here in a few day’s time and they might do something worse.» As I went out I heard the military authorities announce that those who wanted to go South should do so at once as the bridges on the Loire were to be blown up before the end of the day. No trains were running and no taxis could be found. We left on foot, carrying all our luggage. As soon as we crossed the bridge, I realised that we could not go on like that. We stopped outside a bistrot, along the road, made a bundle of some essential clothes and left our heavy suitcase with an old lady. She promised to keep it safe for us and gave us her address. I never tried to trace her since. We walked more easily for a few miles under the blazing sun. The ripening crops amid the orchards, the meadows and the trees made the countryside an endless tapestry of gold and green. We crossed a village and in the open courtyard of a house we found an abandoned pram. We put in our bundles and I pushed it merrily along the road. But when we thought we had solved our transportation problem something terrible happened. A squadron of German planes was returning from a bombing expedition on the power station of La Ferte. Flying over the crowded road the pilots trailed their machine-guns on the helpless civilians. There was a frightful panic. We all rushed into the ditch. A small boy was wounded in the leg. My ladies lost their stamina and refused to walk any farther. The two French girls found room in one of the cars which moved very slowly along the road and stopped at short intervals. My wife and I were given a lift by a lorry driver in military uniform. She was seated in front and I stood inside with two French soldiers who looked as if they were just back from hell. In fact they came from the front and told hair-raising stories about the diving Stukas with their awe-inspiring whistle. 74 [pageend76]Suddenly the lorry stopped. Our man had reached his destination, a village not far from the main road. My wife and I felt despondent, not knowing what to do next. He saw our despair and drove us back to the main road. Thanks to his military uniform he was able to persuade the driver of an open lorry filled with refugees from Paris to give us a lift. We jumped with difficulty into the loory, grasping firmly the passengers’ outstre- ched hands. They had to squeeze against each other in order to make room for us. They looked like the people who keep stalls in the open market at the Porte de Clignancourt. We felt almost happy to be moving again, but not for long. Just a few miles from Vierzon the lorry broke down. The driver and his mate tried hard to get it going again. We waited patiently and hopefully but were finally told that an important piece of the engine needed replacing. It was dark by then and there was nothing left but to spend the night by the roadside, wrapped in our heavy coats. At dawn we were soaked by the dew and got up to warm ourselves. The driver set our for Vierzon in search of the needed spare part but he was not sure if he could find one, so he said we had better make our own arrengements. The traffic jam had considerably eased by then. Motorcars were following each other at very short intervals but there were no bottlenecks. We made desperate signs for a lift. After a time which seemed interminably long, one of the cars stopped. It was a kind of midget service van with two seats in front and space in the flat back for luggage. La seconde guerre mondiale touchait alors à sa fin. On espérait 93 [pageend95]ardemment la paix, dans un monde de souffrances et de désastres — mais personne ne savait quand viendrait le jour de la délivrance : de la paix, tout simplement. Personne ne pouvait savoir qui serait encore en vie en ce jour suprême, lorsque l’orage de fer et de feu qui déferlait sur la terre, sur l’eau, dans l’air, s’évanouirait comme un cauchemar en laissant partout les signes de son atroce réalité : les ruines, les cimetières, les estropiés... Dans la capitale de la Roumanie, on connaissait déjà la paix — cette « paix », armée elle aussi jusqu’aux dents. L’armée ennemie, portant la svastica nazie, a été chassée pendant l’été de l’année passée — et une autre armée « alliée, libératrice », a occupé le pays : c’était la « révolution populaire », de la faucille et du marteau. Cela suffit pour expliquer pourquoi ce carnet est resté intact. A quoi peut bien servir un « journal », si l’on ne peut pas y inscrire la vérité, toute la vérité ? Il vaut mieux la garder en soi-même, au fond du cœur, aux tréfonds de la conscience. Le jour viendra — si l’on vit encore — quand la vérité, c’est-à-dire la réalité d’une pauvre existence entre des millions d’existences, remontera vers la lumière, pour chercher ensuite la communion avec « nos frères, nos semblables ». Et voici qu’un soir, en fouillant parmi de vieux papiers, ce carnet en blanc m’est apparu, dans le coin d’un tiroir, comme un témoin muet des années tragiques, des noires années vécues là-bas, au delà de l’océan, à l’autre bout de l’Europe dévastée par la guerre, par les révolutions, par les dictatures. Je feuilletais les pages blanches — et je lisais ce que j’ai voulu écrire alors, durant ce temps de meurtre, de mépris et de silence — j’y voyais, comme sur des écrans rétrospectifs, tout ce que j’ai vécu alors, tout 94 [pageend96]ce que j’ai vu autour de moi, les désespoirs et les malheurs des individus et des peuples, dans la mêlée apocalyptique d’un vieux monde qui disparaît et d’un monde nouveau en douloureuse gestation. Et maintenant, en exil, dans un autre continent, après cinq ans de vie américaine, dans cette capitale qui s’étend le long des rives du Rio de la Plata, ce carnet — le même carnet oublié et retrouvé comme un autre moi-même — me sollicite ce soir, avec ses souvenirs, avec les réalités d’un passé déjà lointain et que je ne peux pas séparer des réalités présentes. Les racines du passé persistent sous le tronc de mon existence qui renouvelle, dans sa couronne, les branches cassées, les feuilles jaunies des saisons, les fruits qui ont nourri mon corps, mon cœur, ma conscience... Et j’écris les premières lignes, sans le vouloir — pour ainsi dire — poussé par je ne sais quelle puissance, enfin délivrée. Un « Journal » ? Maintenant, quand j’ai dépassé la soixantaine tant bien que mal, après avoir parcouru presque toute la route de ma destinée, de mes rêves, de mes idéaux, de mes efforts, de mes œuvres... Route semée aussi d’erreurs, de désillusions, de souffrances — mais ayant toujours devant moi, au loin, les illusions renouvelées, les espoirs idéalisés, les images de la confiance entêtée en plus de liberté, de vérité et de beauté, en plus de justice, de fraternité et, enfin, de paix consolante et créatrice dans ce monde- Non ! Ce n’est pas un « Journal » que je vais commencer, mais non plus un livre de « Mémoires ». Quand on vous donne le conseil d’écrire vos mémoires — quelle que soit votre vie : grande ou petite, célèbre ou obscure, quelles que soient vos œuvres : sociales ou éthiques, scientifiques ou artis95 [pageend97]tiques, politiques ou économiques — on vous invite alors à faire le bilan de votre existence, on vous fait savoir, d’une manière plus ou moins franche ou brutale, que vous êtes au bout de votre rouleau, que vous n’êtes plus bon à autre chose. Tout au plus, vous pouvez amuser les curieux avec vos Mémoires (qui ne sont qu’un « journal » rétrospectif et synthétique) ou avec votre Journal (qui est un « mémorial » analytique du devenir, d’un jour à l’autre). Peut-être pourrait-on tirer des enseignements utiles de votre expérience, de vos succès et, plus encore, de vos erreurs et de vos souffrances. Peut-être pourrez-vous donner aux historiens, aux investigateurs des réalités « pratiques » ou des réalités intérieures de l’esprit, quelques éléments, quelques témoignages, quelques fragments qui leur serviront à reconstruire l’image d’une époque, d’une société, ou du moins d’un individu perdu dans la foule ou la surpassant à certains moments et à certains points de vue. Peut-être... Mais je n’écris ni un Journal, ni des Mémoires. Il est trop tard pour un Journal, et trop tôt pour des Mémoires, malgré le demi-siècle de travail et de luttes. Ma vie, elle est dans mon action sociale, intellectuelle et spirituelle, — et dans les œuvres par lesquelles j’ai essayé de m’exprimer. Le temps fera son choix, ce temps terriblement dissolvant, ce temps impitoyable, qui transforme tout, broie tout, même les pensées et les sentiments, et fait couler la poussière humaine, mêlée de sang, d’humeurs et de pourritures, dans le moule des formes nouvelles, des apparences toujours renouvelées, des créations qui cherchent des expressions meilleures, plus véridiques, plus conformes aux lois de la vie, plus équilibrées entre les changements incessants de la nature. Des créations plus belles et 96 [pageend98]plus justes, peut-être, dans le tourbillon des laideurs et des vanités, des cruautés et des monstruosités engendrées par les millions d’humains, que certains prétendent représenter et diriger. Ce sont les faux pasteurs qui les mènent vers les usines de l’esclavage et vers les abattoirs de la guerre et de la fausse révolution. Seuls, ceux qui sont restés dans l’océan de la vie humaine, en s’imprégnant de sa puissance, de ses souffrances et de ses espoirs, peuvent — s’ils savent se dépasser eux-mêmes — représenter l’humanité : celle d’un groupe d’individus, d’un peuple, d’une race et, parfois, de l’espèce tout entière. Ils sont des compagnons de travail et de combats, mais aussi des guides librement acceptés. Ils enseignent par leur propre exemple, par leur vie et leur œuvre. Leur témoignage est en même temps celui du moment et de l’éternité. Ils sont les auteurs des « livres de sagesse » dont le texte est trop souvent perdu, ou nous est parvenu fragmentaire, mais palpitant de l’essence de la foi et de la vérité. C’est ce qui compte : transmettre la vie de l’esprit, comme le germe d’une génération à l’autre. C’est dans ce sens que l’on peut considérer aussi les textes chaotiques des Bibles, des Evangiles, des Vedas, des Epopées, comme les « mémoires » des siècles, comme le « journal » des peuples et des races à travers leurs prophètes, leurs sages, leurs apôtres et leurs poètes. Chaque génération a écrit sa page dans ces livres immortels. Et même parmi nos contemporains, il y a de Grands Solitaires, dont la fermeté d’âme et la sincérité des confessions nous ont aidé à supporter les tourments et les désespoirs dans la mêlée la plus gigantesque et la plus tragique de l’histoire de l’humanité. Sachons écouter leur voix dans le hurlement des forces déchaînées. Car ils sont vraiment nos compagnons — et nos guides. Ils 97 [pageend99]sont de l’Orient, ils sont de l’Occident. Ils ne connaissent pas les frontières temporaires ni les haines des clans et des troupeaux. Ils sont les frères des égarés, les consolateurs des martyrisés, les sauveurs d’aujourd’hui et de demain- Voilà ! Mon carnet commence avec le témoignage de gratitude envers les précurseurs. Je suis leur débiteur. Et si je peux me réaliser moi-même, si peu que ce soit, je n’oublie point que c’est par leur héritage que je suis arrivé à ce que je suis — et que je préserve vers les mêmes idéaux, engendrés par leur sang, vivifiés par leur esprit... C’est le souvenir de leur existence héroïque, la présence de leur réalité impérissable qui compte avant tout. Le reste n’est que « littérature » ou vain verbiage. Mr. Ivey’s article was squeezed out of our last issue; yet we feel it fits very well indeed into the series about the triumphs and defeats of exiles we have been publishing since Arena No. 1. A woodcarver who emigrated from a city in central Europe to a small North Carolina town in the USA spoke his grievance. 98 [pageend100]«Everywhere I go people point me out to other people,» he said. «They think that I don’t notice, but I do. They point me out and they say ’Psst!» The immigrant woodcarver said he knew he didn’t look like other North Carolinians, and that he must look strange to them, the people of the town. But he was growing frustrated by the pointing and the sur- reptitous signal, «psst.» He confided his complaint to a friendly visitor passing through the town—the vice president of the University of North Carolina, in Chapel Hill. «1’11 tell you what to do,» said the vice president. «Come and live in Chapel Hill. There we have so many strange- looking people that nobody pays any undue attention to others. Nobody says «psst» at anyone else.» Whether one is a woodcarver or a doctor of philosophy, there is a haven in Chapel Hill. That is probably true of many other university communities in the United States. Scholars have been coming since even before World War I, but the flow has been strong since World War II, and continues unabated. Foreign scholars are welcomed, and encouraged. If they are pointed at, it isn’t in a gossipy, suspicious manner. It is pointing with pride. For the University of North Carolina’s reputation as the leading southern university, and one of the leading state universities in America, has been brought about in no small part by the contributions of men and women who were bom on other continents. They come from Europe, from Britain, from China, Japan, Australia, Africa, South America, other parts of the Orient, and from India and the Middle East. Their presence and work have lent stature to the University of North Carolina’s teaching and research in mathematics, statistics, physics, chemistry, medicine, classics, modem languages, English, public health, political science, and other disciplines. 99 [pageend101]Noteworthy are the participation of natives of India in the Institute of Statistics. In the Department of Psychiatry are doctors from England and Germany. «Just yesterday our trustees appointed an immigrant from Germany to fill one of the most distinguished endowed chairs in the University, the James G. Hanes Professor of Philosophy,» said Chancellor William B. Aycock. The man named to the Hanes professorship is Werner David Falk who is a «triple immigrant»—from Germany to England to Australia to the United States. He was bom in Berlin and was one of the first professors forced to leave after Hitler’s rise. He was teaching at the German School of Politics, an institute dedicated to teaching Germans how to think objectively about politics. Goebbels took over the school and it became a Nazi propaganda center. Dr. Falk went to England, learned the language, and learned how to argue «in a quiet, informative objective manner.» «The English are by nature more objective,» said Dr. Falk, «Germans become very heated when arguing.» In England Prof. Falk altered his way of philosophical thinking, and learned to express himself in concrete terms rather than depend altogether upon abstract thinking. He later immigrated to Australia and taught at the University of Melbourne, and then came to the United States where he has taught at six American institutions, Chapel Hill most recently, where he now intends to remain. «I hope America will be my last territorial claim.» he said. * * * «If I were to give advice to a scholar about to emigrate to the United States,» said Werner P. Friederich, a Kenan Professor of Comparative Literature who came to America in 1927, «I would say to him: stop glorifying the homeland. Accept this country and all of its novelty. Go back to your homeland after a few years. You will find that it is still 100 [pageend102]beautiful. But it doesn’t have that halo. I go frequently back to Switzerland, but I do not regret having left.» Professor Friederich is the American president of the International Comparative Literature Association, and is one of the several foreign scholars at Chapel Hill who have won coveted Kenan Professorships. Another bit of counsel Swiss native Friederich would give to a prospective American citizen is «marry a girl who speaks English.» This will force him to speak English constantly. Similar counsel was given by one of the professors of the University’s School of Public Health. He remembered a European who had learned his English at Oxford, and spoke with an inflection that could hardly be understood by students in his classes. American students are notoriously patient and polite, and seldom will suggest to a faculty member that his accent is difficult to understand. They often carry such courtesy too far, and a new professor who speaks unclearly will find that he will have a slump in enrollment, unless students are obliged to attend. If they have choice, they pick some other instructor; and the foreign-born man with the fuzzy pronunciation won’t find it out until too late. In the case just mentioned, however, the word got to the man’s collègues and friends. They told him he ought to brush up on his English. «I speak perfect English,» he protested. He said his way of speaking was beyond reproach: much better, in fact, than that of his students and fellow faculty members. He even consented to make a recording of his voice, on tape, and when it was played back to him, he was astounded. «I didn’t know I sounded like that,» he said. «I don’t blame the students.» He enrolled for private lessons with Prof. Lucy Morgan who teaches speech in the University’s Department of English. The Oxford accent became more Americanized—and made to sound more like a «Tar Heel» of central North Carolina. 101 [pageend103]Another immigrant who changed his manner of speaking so that his pronunciation was much like the native American is Professor of German Frederic Coenen, who came from Westphalia in the 1920’s. He married a North Carolina girl, Sudie Creech, and they have lived in Chapel Hill since 1931. Ten years ago at a linguistic meeting in New York, a specialist in language and accents gave a demonstration showing how he could identify what part of the world a man came from by listening to the inflections in his voice as he spoke the words. When he heard Dr. Coenen speak, he said, «You come from a region about 100 miles or so south of Charlottesville, Virginia.» That is the approximate home country of Sudie Creech Coenen, and not far from Chapel Hill. * * * Raj Chandra Bose, professor of statistics and native of India, declares that he speaks and writes English better than his native language. The same was said by several other immigrant scholars, and they said it with a certain pride. Dr. Bose was teaching at the University of Calcutta, and he was frequently in touch with other scholars in statistics, including Prof. Harold Hotelling of Columbia University. When Dr. Hotelling accepted a porfessorship at the University of North Carolina and assisted in building up the statistics center in Chapel Hill, he called on some of the great names in statistics in the world, and among these was Dr. Bose. Following Dr. Bose was a contingent of other Indians, so that there are now in Capel Hill a number of graduate students as well faculty members who are natives of India. Though pulling up stakes in India was not easy, Dr. Bose is pleased with his new life. His two daughters have become Americanized, and the youngest, Sipra Bose, was married to an American in 1963. Another Indian professor of statistics, Samarenda Nath Roy, has a son who is one of the most popular boys ever to attend 102 [pageend104]the Chapel Hill High School. Subir Roy was a star on the Chapel Hill High School football team, was a leader in students’ extracurricular activities, and won a major portion of the honors in the American custom of selecting «superlatives» when students are graduated. Subir Roy is now a sophomore in the University of North Carolina. Indians in the southern part of the United States are likely to be subjected to embarassment when they travel, for they are sometimes mistaken for Negroes. Dr. Bose, for instance, has been denied lodging and questioned at restaurants regarding his nationality. Such discrimination is gradually coming to an end in North Carolina and other parts of the South—and there isn’t the slightest difficulty for Indians in Chapel Hill— but strained moments have occurred for Prof. Bose. As a matter of practice, when he has to travel, Mrs. Bose accompanies him, and wears a sari. No questions are asked. * * * Indians, even though they may come from an upper class in their native land, can understand the nature of prejudice, in the United States or elsewhere. They don’t like it but they understand it, for they are familiar with the caste system in India. «We don’t have to invoke any special rules or provide any crutches to help support the statisticians from India, or our other foreign faculty members,» said Prof. George Nicholson, chairman of the Department of Statistics in Chapel Hill. «They are mature people, and don’t have to be spoon fed. They are cosmopolitan in their thinking and actions.» Isai Hashimoto, a native of Japan who is now a research associate in the chemistry department, said he has experienced a «feeling» of prejudice, or of holding back, only on certain very isolated occasions. It may be prejudice because of race, he said. But it doesn’t annoy him. «I have prejudices, too, and biases, and I understand such things,» he said. 103 [pageend105]Edward Majchrowicz, a biochemist and native of Poland, says he has noticed a «squint in the eyes» of some Americans even though they were not obviously unwilling to accept him. But he added that «I have had no difficulties because I am a foreigner. You can’t find a society where they always pretend acceptance. I have prejudices, too.» Majchrowicz likes Chapel Hill because of its cleanliness and its quietness. He added, «When I stand in just this spot and look across the campus from the student union towards Franklin Street and downtown, it looks like a bit of Paris.» * * * Hans Strupp, a psychologist who came to the United States as a boy in 1939, leaving Germany because he was a Jew, was educated in night schools of the northern cities in the United States, a form of education not practiced in Europe. He worked at many jobs in the day and attended night classes, and now is a psychologist in the Department of Psychiastry in the School of Medicine. The United States is not perfect, said Strupp. The racial problems of the southern states are serious. He added: «Yet there are many positive aspects of American life that are unfortunately overshadowed by the race problem as pictured in the press and other media. The good things are taken for granted and the false blown up—I think that human failure is true so far as the image of the American in the world is concerned.» Mr. Stoupp’s vigorous defense of his adopted country is appreciated by many Americans, and probably pooh-poohed by others. Another Chapel Hill faculty member who emigrated from Germany in 1947 and remembers the lack of freedom in 104 [pageend106]Hitler’s Germany is Prof. Eugen Merzbacher, who teaches in the Physics Department, and is one of the circuit of lecturers all over the United States for the American Institute of Physics. «We may be distressed when we run into examples of oppression and discrimination in the United States,» he said. «But as one who has seen real oppression and prejudice, I can tell you that the freedom in this country is considerable.» Dr. Merzbacher first headed for England, but it seemed apparent to him that opportunity was better in the United States. He was made to feel more welcome here, and the way was made easy. So he entered Harvard University, and has become a leading physicist in the United States. «I don’t believe there is any country in the world where you can become so completely amalgamated,» he said. «It is a cliche to say that this is the great melting pot, but that is the way it is.» Would he go back to Europe, to teach, if opportunity occurred? Prof. Merzbacher remarked that although it may seem an extraordinary thing to mention such impressions, one of the first and the lasting impacts of America, so far as he is concerned, is «the greenness of the countryside—how one can travel miles and miles and see nothing but greenness and trees and the beauty of the landscape. Brauer is a native of Germany, a captain of the German Army of World War I, who was permitted because of his war record to remain teaching in Berlin longer than many who finally became refugees. He came to Chapel Hill in 1941, transferring from Princeton University. He came to America at age of 45 and knew no English at all. But in Chapel Hill, he quickly became one of the most popular professors in mathematics. His popularity is not based on an easy method of teaching, or making it easy for the student. Students like him because he has a burning desire to get his lessons across. He wants his students to make A’s on the course. He demands much of them. When students fall behind in class, he asks them to remain afterward, in the afternoons, and goes over the problems with them, shows them their errors and tries to picture to them the glories of mathematics as Brauer sees it. «Some of the faculty criticize me because I give so many A’s,» he said. «I believe students should make and earn the A’s. If he were requested to give counsel to a professor in Europe who was thinking of emigrating to America, Dr. Brauer would emphasize the difference in the elite system of Europe and the democratic system of America. In Germany, he found that a teacher had to be good, if he was to hold students. In fact, the poor or average teacher, didn’t last long or never got to teach in the first place. «Here in the United States, students depend on you for teaching and for grades,» he said. «In Germany the teacher depends on the students for his own success. He may not even receive a salary, and have to serve for years as a privatdozent before finally becoming a professor. Indirectly teachers may be paid—by the students. But the students in Germany have to be pleased, and instructed well. They may come and size a professor up before deciding wheter to take the course, and pay for it. If he makes a mistare one day, or if he doesn’t speak distinctly, the next day his class may fall off ten students, and it will cost that much in his income. The students hold the whip hand.» «If I were to go to a class in Germany and a student was reading the daily newspaper as students in Chapel Hill may be reading the Daily Tar Heel,» said Dr. Brauer, «I would not dare ask them to put down their paper. A. Oras est né en 1900 à Tallinn, Estonie. Actuellement professeur de littérature anglaise à l’Université de Floride, U.S.A., il est un des guides intellectuels les plus écoutés de la nouvelle éclosion littéraire en exil. Doué d’un regard qui perçoit les indices cachés ou épars dans le temps, qui en devine la direction et en révèle la signification, Ants Oras a joué dans son pays le rôle d’un initiateur littéraire de premier ordre. Il y a été conduit d’abord par ses interventions de plus en plus fréquentes dans la critique littéraire courante. Ayant imposé sa compétence au début des années trente par des essais pénétrants sur quelques 120 [pageend122]écrivains estoniens d’une réputation déjà établie (1), il fut chargé en 1933 de la rédaction des comptes- rendus annuels de la production littéraire du pays et tout particulièrement de la poésie. Ces aperçus, paraissant dans les deux revues les plus importantes du pays, discutés dans les assemblées des organisations littéraires, et dans la presse générale, jouissaient d’un prestige considérable. Leur auteur devint tout naturellement l’arbitre du goût et du mérite littéraire. Depuis l’offensive des « orbiteurs » en 1930, la critique littéraire s’était orientée de plus en plus vers la seule critique de l’utilité sociale des œuvres. Le critère de la « commande sociale » y figurait au premier rang du barème des valeurs (2). L’œuvre devait être accessible au plus grand nombre possible, refléter la réalité et les préoccupations des couches essentielles de la population, elle devait leur offrir sinon toujours des modèles à suivre, du moins des raisons de vivre et de juger le monde. Dans l’application de ces principes, les critiques finirent cependant par se mettre à la remorque d’une opinion publique formée principalement par les idéologies des groupements politiques dominants. A. 134 [pageend136]ARGUMENT Vladimir C. Nahirny and Joshua A. Fishman WITHIN THE ETHNIC FOLD Leaders and Languages of Immigrant Groups We regretted greatly that exigencies of space and time made it impossible to publish the extremely interesting article by Vladimir C. Nahimy and Joshua A. Fishman in our January 1964 issue. We feel, however, that this serves as an admirable contribution to our «Argument» series and hope to publish further articles on the same theme, so vital to exiles, in future issues, It has frequently been asserted that the mass immigration to the United States (1880-1920) consisted primarily of individuals recruited from the lowest strata of society. To follow the popular cliche, these immigrants were young and energetic, hard-working and ambitious; at the same time, the very costumes, facial features and calloused hands of these people unden scored the fact that they came from that segment of humanity which had been traditionally steeped in poverty and ignorance. By and large, this widely accepted stereotype is true; yet, like any other stereotype, it is most certainly one-sided. It glosses over the fact that the same immigrants were accompanied, as 135 [pageend137]a rule, by individuals of urban background and higher education. The latter not only included educated religious leaders but also a sprinkling of secular intellectuals—be they writers and journalists, teachers and lawyers, or doctors and scholars. Some of them joined the mass exodus from their homelands out of sheer populist commitment to the insulted, injured and exploited; others, in turn, searched on their own for adventures and for careers in this country of expanding opportunity; still others hailed from the growing stratum of declassed intellectuals—teachers without students, journalists without newspapers or readers, and lawyers without clients. Last but not least, there have always been present among educated immigrants a small but significant group of political refugees and exiles whose arrival on these shores has continued since earliest colonial days. All in all, their number and proportion have varied from one ethnic group to another and from one wave of immigration to the next; nevertheless, they have been present in every group as well as in every wave. Those educated immigrants who have contributed in one way or another to the cultural growth of the host society are duly mentioned in its historical annals. Those who have exclusively or primarily devoted their lives and works to the development of their respective ethnic cultures have rarely if ever come under the purview of general American society. It may well be that most of them are scarcely remembered by the very groups on whose behalf they labored and whose cultural values they cultivated. For the collective memory of American ethnic groups (especially of their native born generations) tends to be contingent upon that of the host society; they are more prone to remember and to be proud of those individuals whose accomplishments have been incorporated into the cultural heritage of American « core » society. This kind of dependance upon the host society may well be reflective of the more general fact that, in the long run, American ethnic groups lose the sense of their own history and separate identity. 136 [pageend138]Yet, it is worth emphasizing that at one time or another, most ethnic groups in this country possessed their own cultural leadership, including not a few highly creative writers and intellectuals. For this reason alone ethnic cultures in the United States must not be equated with venerable but unchanging traditions consisting of anonymous folk elements such as costumes, songs and dances. It can be reliably estimated that Germans and Norvegians alone published no less than sixty-five different literary magazines in the United States since the late 186O’s. A comprehensive study of these as well as other ethnic publications would disclose quite a few works (short stories, novels, poetry) whose literary merit is scarcely inferior to the bulk of American literary works of the same period. One may justifiably speak of German-, Norvegian-, Jewish-, Polish, or Ukrainian-American literatures, art, and even music. The very existence of these elements of «representative» culture clearly reflects the creative efforts ef ethnic cultural leaders and the respective cultural interests of sizeable audiences for whom their creations were most directly intended. Such leaders and such audiences exist to this very day, even though they are surrounded by ample evidence that ethnic minorities in the United States cannot long remain culturally creative and dynamic. This essay is basically concerned with the cultural and organizational elite, the ethnic leadership, of four American ethnic groups : German, Jewish, Polish, and Ukrainian. More specifically, it attemps to assess the maintenance of ethnic cultures among ethnic leaders and their children in the continued use of the appropriate mother tongues, membership in ethnic organizations, and views concerning the future of language and culture maintenance. The close linking of culture and language stems partly from the particular focus of our Language Resources Project. But it also has its historical justification. Language has not ontly carried but has also symbolized the national ideals and values of many modem nationalities. Very often the existence of such a collective 137 [pageend139]entity as nationality has been determined in terms of language and language-related criteria. * * * The universe of this study has been defined as consisting of those leaders (of four specific ethnic backrounds) considered to be most actively engaged in the maintenance of ethnically related cultural values and symbols. On the basis of a compiled list of names, 826 data gathering contacts were attempted and 222 (27.0 %) were successfully completed during 1961-62. On the whole, the rates of response from these four ethnic groups tend to be inversely related to the proportion of foreign born leaders among their respondents. Two of the groups — Jews and Ukrainians — contain higher percentages of foreign born leaders (82 % and 92 % respectively) and their relative rates of response are lower. On the other hand, the percentage of foreign born leaders among Germans and Poles is lower by far (68 % and 64 % respectively) and their rates of response are appreciably higher. The four ethnic groupes differ considerably with respect to generational composition, age, education and occupational status. The primarily immigrant Jewish group, for example, is clearly oldest: 27 percent of its members being past retirement age, and its median age (59.8) is highest. On the other hand, the Ukrainians represent the youngest group, their median age (50.5) being lower than that of the three other groups. Another differentiating characteristic of Ukrainian leaders is their considerably lower occupational level. Some 20 percent of the responding Ukrainian leaders are either unskilled or skilled laborers as contrasted with 2.4 percent for Polish and 2.8 percent for German leaders. Jewish leaders occupy the highest rung in the occupational hierarchy; indeed, they are exclusively concentrated in the category of professionals. At the same time, the formal educational attainments of Jewish respondents are relatively modest with some 13 percent 138 [pageend140]of them possessing no more than high school education. The reverse holds true for Ukrainians. While their occupational standing is relatively lowest, their overall educational achievements are higher than those of the three other groups. This discrepancy between educational level and occupational success can be readily traced to the fact that Ukrainian leaders are primarily recent refugee-immigrants. In terms of age, education and recency of arrival in the United States, the Ukrainian leaders constitute a highly dynamic group of ethnic activists; however, there can be no doubt that a high proportion of them is active in ethnic cultural life on a part-time basis only. On week-ends, within the context of ethnic «communal» life, they carry the burden of cultural leadership; on weekdays, in the occupational structure of the dominant society, they are freqeuntly unskilled and semi-skilled laborers — dishwashers, elevator operators, etc. Like many other recently arrived educated immigrants, they may best be viewed as declassed intellectuals. On the other hand, Jewish cultural leaders, although equally of foreign birth, can hardly be considered as declassed with respect to their present occupational standing. Either their occupational position in general American society is congruent with their ethnic leadership status or their ethnic leadership is expressed via full-time cultural organizational involvement on behalf of their ethnic group. Finally, the two generationally «mixed» groups — Germans and Poles — present still a third picture. They are professionals in the occupational structure of the dominant society in that about half of them presently teach in American colleges and other educational institutions. They are ethnic intellectuals and leaders in that the area of their specialty happens to be their ethnic language, literature, or history. Such professionalized interest in the ancestral culture (whether among foreign bom or among native born leaders) need not entail participation in ethnic cultural organizations or personal involvement in ethnic cultural life. To speak figuratively, the ethnic culture of one’s forbears may cease to be «alive» and 139 [pageend141]may ultimately approach the status of an object of cognitive orientation, not unlike Greek, Roman or other national culture of general intellectual import. * * * In general, mother tongue use among ethnic leaders is less common than ethnic friendships. While very few ethnic leaders have a majority of non-ethnic or other-ethnic friends, quite a few no longer speak their ethnic mother tongue to their friends. The extent ot which English has replaced the ethnic mother tongue, even within the context of highly informal and personal relations, is noticeable indeed, particularly among American born leaders. Of fifty-two native bom leaders, 44,2 percent use English exclusively in conversations with their three closest personal friends. On the whole, native born leaders tend to maintain friendship ties primarily with individuals of their own ethnic origin. Foreign born leaders, albeit ethnically somewhat more homophilous in their friendship patterns than their native born counterparts, also display some propensity to associate with foreign born individuals of other than their own ethnic group. The Jewish group is most homophilous in this respect. Its leaders — foreign and native born alike — confine their friendship almost exclusively to other Jews. Seventy-eight percent of them have exclusively Jewish friends (three closest friends) as compared to 60 percent among Ukrainians and 44 and 38 percent among German and Polish leaders respectively. The noticeable dearth of enduring personal relations between Jews and gentiles only confirms what has been known for some time to students of American minority groups. However, it is quite difficult to interpret this unique insulation of the spheres of privacy and intimacy in the absence of greater spatial, economic and cultural separation. There remains but one other and less tangible factor — the religious factor — or, as one author has phrased it, the «traditional religious values requiring a separate communal existence». Whatever 140 [pageend142]the merit of this explanation may be, there can be little doubt that Jewish communal existence in the Torah centered shtetl must be carefully distinguished from Jewish communal existence in American urban centers. For the unique feature of American ethnic life lies precisely in the fact that it does not constitue a separate communal but a separate associational existence. The very proliferation of ethnic voluntary organizations (Jewish and non-Jewish) lends ample testimony to the associational character of ethnic «communal» life in this country. To the extent that this is true, patterns of friendship and interpersonal relations in general cannot but heavily depend upon participation in ethnic organizations. It may well be that Jews have constructed a more self-contained web of parallel organizations than have other ethnic groups in the United States. * * * Ethnic organizational affiliation tends to be more frequent among native born than among foreign born Polish leaders. This finding is quite surprising. Not only are native bom Polish leaders more extensively affiliated with ethnic organizations than foreign born Polish leaders, but they are also quite a bit more frequently affiliated with ethnic organizations than Ukrainian leaders — the most recent immigrant group in our sample—or than Jewish leaders with their long organizational experience. Likewise, native born German leaders also display a similar tendency to join more ethnic organizations than do foreign bom German leaders. Native born ethnic leaders are both more active and more extensively involved in ethnic organizations. Are we to conclude from the above that they are also more intensely attached to their respective ethnic cultures? While any «yes» or «no» answer is unwarranted and would simply disregard the complex problem at hand, it is significant that native bom 141 [pageend143]ethnie leaders are least frequently members of literary and cultural organizations, that is, of those very organizations that are most directly relevant to the maintenance of ethnic cultures, including mother tongues. Indeed, only 14.5 percent of native born ethnic leaders currently belong to ethnic cultural and literary organizations. On the other hand, the highest percentage of native bom leaders (58.2%) report membership in fraternal societies, that is, in those ethnic organizations which now have only the most indirect bearing upon the maintenance of ethnic cultures. Two other types of ethnic organizations with which native born leaders report frequent association are scholarly (40.0%) and religious (30.0%), i.e. organizations that are also oriented toward the more marginal forms of ethnicity. It is this shift from personal involvement in ethnic cultures to involvement in ethnically-related organizations that is of crucial significance here. It prompts us to conclude that native-born leaders, far from being cultural leaders, should more properly be considered organizational leaders. This is not to deny, of course, that by actively participating in ethnic organizational life native born leaders may try to reassert their attachment to ethnicity. But organizational participation in itself does not necessarily lead to personal and creative involvement in the ancestral culture. In point of fact, native born leaders are largely de-ethnicized (both linguistically and otherwise) while, at the same time, they remain extensively involved in ethnic organizational life. What one encounters here may well be a kind of ethnic loyalty that finds major expression in organizational association. * * * It is against the background of the preceding analyses that we shall briefly discuss some of the beliefs and opinions of ethnic leaders with respect to the maintenance of ethnic cultural and communal life in the United States. The ethnic mother 142 [pageend144]tongue will be the center of our attention, particularly the importance that ethnic leaders attribute to its preservation as well as the fears and hopes that they express about its future in this country. Our various prior studies have prepared us to expect that not all ethnic leaders are concerned with the preservation of ethnic mother tongues. Neither are they all equally interested in the maintenance of ethnic organizations or ethnically related traditions and customs. Indeed, eight respondents in our sample are opposed to the preservation of the ethnic mother tongue by their own children and grand children; thirteen oppose the retention of ethnically related customs and traditions; and thirteen disapprove of the very existence of separate ethnic institutions and organizations. While the number of such «anti-ethnie» leaders is quite small, their presence is not without significance. Far greater is the proportion of leaders who claim to favor ethnic continuity but who also claim that the continuity of ethnic cultural and communal life in the United States may be secured without the preservation of their ethnic mother tongues. Almost half of native-born and slightly less than a third of foreign bom leaders share the view that the retention of the ethnic mother tongue is hardly necessary for ethnic group continuity in the United States. Not a few —19.6 percent of the native born and 30.7 percent of the foreign born — are also of the opinion that the retention of ethnic customs and traditions is unnecessary for the continuity of ethnic life in this country. What most ethnic leaders-— native born (84.3%) and foreign born (88.2%) like — do believe to be extremely important for the continuation of ethnic group identity are knowledge and appreciation of past and present national achievements in art, literature, religion and other areas of cultural endeavor. Taken at their face value, the above findings may seem to be of little interest. However, they should not be dismissed too lightly, if only because they touch upon the very survival of the ethnic groups. What ethnic leaders believe, amounts 143 [pageend145]to a conviction that knowledge of and attachment to such unique and particularized values as language and tradition are less important and less necessary for ethnic continuity and group identity than are knowledge of and attachment to such highly generalized ethnic representations as Goethe’s «Faust», Sienkiewicz’s «Quo Vadis» or Beethoven’s «Ninth Symphony». The least tangible attributes of ethnicity — the ones that are of least significance to the daily life of the ethnic family and community — are thus rated by the leaders to be more important for ethnic continuity than are the very attributes (language and tradition) which most clearly differentiate one ethnic collectivity from the other. This depreciation of the «folk» components of ethnicity and the simultaneous preference for its more dignified «representative» components may but reflect the intellectual perspectives of our respondents. It may also represent an experienced response to the hard facts of life since native bom leaders and their children have largely become de-ethnicized. There is certainly no denying the fact that leaders view the retention of their ethnic mother tongues to be relatively least important for ethnic group continuity in the United States. They are joined in this view by many lower level functionaries in ethnic organizations, schools and parishes. However, this tendency on the part of ethnic leaders should not be construed to mean that they oppose language maintenance efforts. Indeed, most leaders favor the retention of the ethnic mother tongue and approve its continued use, albeit not always for the same reasons. Foreign bom leaders justify language maintenance om somewhat different grounds than do native born leaders. The former are concerned with language maintenance because the mother tongue is basic to their own activity and leadership; they regard it as a value per se that should be cultivated and appreciated for its own sake. Accordingly, leaders of Jewish and Ukrainian origin favor the retention of their mother tongues on the ground that they express and symbolize the cultural heritages of their 144 [pageend146]1 respective groups. On the other hand, ethnic leaders who are more generationally «mixed» in their background — German and Polish — most frequently justify the retention of their ethnic mother tongues on the basis of more practical considerations. They simply argue that, in general, knowledge of languages yields many advantages in scholarly, commercial, diplomatic or other spheres of life, and, consequently, ethnic mother tongue ought to be retained in order to make instrumental bilangualism possible. Native born leaders are likely to be instrumentally oriented towards the retention of their mother tongues and pari passu towards their ethnicity itself. To the extent that this is true, ethnic mother tongues cease to be of unique or intrinsic value. The very reasons advanced in favor of language maintenance, being basically utilitarian, are applicable to languages in general, and not limited to mother tongues in particular. Indeed, native born leaders seem to be interested in language maintenance on a non-ethnic basis. Their interest and that of many American leaders concerned with the language resources of the American population would seem to be similarly practical and similarly de-ethnicized. * * * W'th the passing of the immigrant generation the reservoir of cultural leadership becomes greatly depleted. From this point onward, it would seem, ethnic cultures tend to become increasingly «organized», and, ultimately cease to be dynamic. The generational turnover in ethnic leadership most adversely affects the ethnic mother tongue, especially its use in interpersonal relations, publications and organizations. All the evidence that can be brought to bear on this fact points unequivocally to the progressive disappearance of the ethnic mother tongue from both family and communal life. We know, for example, that the number of books in the ethnic mother tongue or on ethnic topics published by German and 145 [pageend147]Polish ethnic leaders during the past decade is much smaller than the number published by Jewish and Ukrainian leaders during the same period, even though the former publish more frequently than the latter. Similarly, we know that the proportion of ethnic leaders who have contributed literary works (novels, short stories, poetry, articles) to their respective ethnic publications during this same period varies directly with the proportion of foreign born respondents among them. LUIS CERNUDA One of the great Spanish poets of this century, Luis Cernuda, has died in exile in Mexico. So far, the only echoes of his death in his own country have been a few brief press-notices— one of them stating, among other absurdities, that «his Marxist opinions were the cause of his exile» — and one or two conven151 [pageend153]tional obituaries (1). It goes without saying that no poet was ever less of a Marxist than Cernuda. But some people confuse rebelliousness with Marxism, as if the two things were the same. Cernuda was certainly a rebel. But he was never a political rebel, but a spiritual one, like Rimbaud. His rebelliousness was aimed at the middle-classes, and it sprang from his love of the natural and the genuine, and from his contempt for social conventions and for everything vulgar and inflated. There is, in fact, in Cernuda — one can see it in his writing — a constant dissatisfaction and a sense of protest against society — against its moral ugliness, its unjust and monstruous laws — which is perhaps one sign, among others, of his Romantic affinities. In his «Historial de un libro» (The story of a book), an autobiographical document of immense value, Cernuda admits to «a vein of protest and rebellion which I think should be remembered in reading my poems». And in a letter he wrote to me from Mexico in December, 1953, referring to some pieces on his poetry which I had published in American magazines, he pointed out to me «the aspect of shadow, of protest and rebellion» which was always present in his work. «I believe», he wrote, «that this is the driving-force behind everything I have written.» This is an exaggeration, and no doubt a reaction against the fact that I had stressed the elegaic strain, the note of Andalusian nostalgia which runs through his poetry, and had overlooked its more rebellious side. Cernuda seems to refer to this in the opening lines of «Diptico espaňol» (Spanish diptych), which appears in his last book, Desolación de la quimera (Desolation of the chimaera) : When people over there say that my poems spring from separation and nostalgia for what was once my country, do they only hear the most distant of my voices? Different voices speak in the poet : let us listen to the harmony of the full choir, in which the voice we thought the loudest is only one voice among the others. (1) Luckily, Cernuda could not read the kind of things which the newspapers said when he died. If he had been able to, he would have died again with disgust. One of his final satisfactions, on the other hand, was the splendid homage number dedicated to him last year by the excellent poetry magazine, «La Cana Gris», published in Valencia. 152 [pageend154]1 But it often happens that a poet, even a great poet, misjudges his own work. Perhaps that elegaic, nostalgic strain is not the most important quality in Cemuda’s poetry, but I still believe that some of the poems written in exile, in which he is remembering Spain and Andalusia, are among the most beautiful and poignant he ever wrote. I am thinking particularly of the two «Elegias espaňolas» (.Spanish elegies') from his book Las Nubes (The Clouds), and of «Tierra nativa» (Native land), «Primavera vieja» (Ancient spring), «Hacia la tierra» (Towards the land) and «Elegia anticipada» (Elegy in anticipation), all from Como quien espera el alba (As one who awaits the dawn), the collection he himself most preferred. The fact that Cernuda later ceased to feel this nostalgia and reacted with bitter scorn against his own country, does not take away from the depth and truthfulness of his earlier attitude, nor can it lessen the importance which the theme of Spain, of the country’s essence and drama, assumes in his work after the Civil War. These were terrible years for Cernuda, as he confesses in «Historial de un libro», now included in one of his books of criticism, Poesía y literatura (Poetry and literature). The awareness of these tragic events, he tells us, clouded his everyday existence, and the tragic death of Lorca was continually in his mind. And, living in England, far from that «country of nadness», he had a recurring nightmare which lasted for months : again and again, he dreamed that he was being hunted and tracked down. During his first few months in England, when he was teaching at Cranleigh School, Surrey, Cernuda tells us that he felt a sharp longing for his country, for its atmosphere and for his Spanish friends. At that time he wrote a number of poems inspired by his conscience as a Spaniard and by a preoccupation with the fate of his country which were later collected in his book, Las Nubes. If we are to believe him, this patriotic concern left him, never to return, although his purely nostalgic feelings for his native Andalusia continued to haunt him in the poems I have already mentioned from Como quien espera el alba. In the end, it seems that both concern and nostalgia disappeared from his mind. Cernuda’s relation to Spain is like the suffering of a man who has been tragically disillusioned by the woman he loves. The original passion changes to bitter scorn, to deep contempt, and finally to indifference. We can follow this evolution in his books, from Las Nubes to the final one, Desolación de la quimera, Read, for example, the «Elegia a un poeta muerto» (Elegy on a dead poet — García Lorca) and «A Larra, con violetas» (To Larra, with violets), both from Las Nubes. The 153 [pageend155]image of a harsh, unforgiving country comes through the accurate observation of such poems with a flavour of bitterness. The Spanish theme continues to haunt the next two books, Como quien espera el alba and Vivir sin estar viviendo («Alive though not alive»), written in England between 1941 and 1949; but by now any feeling of longing has given way to scorn and indifference. Instead, there is the image of a Spain which treats its children harshly and cruelly, like that which appears in the afrancesado (i.e. Francophile) poets of the 18th century. Melendez Valdés or Moratin, writing in exile in France. Between 1950 and 1956 Cernuda was in the United States and Mexico, where he wrote the poems which form Con las horas contadas (Againts the clock) — part X of La Realidad y el Deseo (.Reality and Desire) — in which the theme of love which runs through the whole of his work once more becomes the dominant note. The old wound, he says, is finally healed : Your country is lost to you, and you even forget your wound, now it is healed. (Pastime.) In May, 1956, the year in which he completed Con las horas contadas, Cernuda wrote to me about a piece I had published on the nostalgia for Andalusia in his poetry. In this letter he said: «Do you think I really feel this longing for Andalusia you ascribe to me? I don’t feel it in the least.» But in my study, naturally, I had referred to the poems of the two previous books, Las Nubes and Como quien espera el alba, in which the nostalgia was clear enough, though there was no trace of it in the following collections. Nevertheless, the Spanish theme does not disappear altogether from his last poems. In Desolación de la quimera, the last book published in his lifetime, Spain appears in a double image, both desolate and tender, in the two poems of «Diptico espafiol», the one terrifying and the other gentle and visionary. What bitterness and solitude are compressed into this splendid pair of poems! Surely they contain the same kind of paradox and inconsistency we find in Unamuno? Sometimes I think that CernUda’s soul was made up, like Unamuno’s, of warring passions and contradictions. There is in him a mixture of heat and ice, of tenderness and hate, as in his own description of the Andalusian temperament: 154 [pageend156]Shadow formed from light, repelling as it cools, the Andalusian is fire and ice. Enigma against the light, alone in a crowd, the Andalusian is love and hate. The fiercely unjust picture of Spain which Cemuda presents in «Làstima que fuera mi tierra» (A pitÿ it was my country), the first half of «Diptico espafiol», reminds us, indeed, of certain savage poems in Unamuno’s Romancero del destierro (.Ballads of exile), written in exile in France. Like Unamuno, Cernuda starts from a truth about Spain, only to become passionately unjust (2). Cernuda was often extreme in his judgements of writers and peoples, particularly when he disliked them. Few will recognize Spain in an image like the following : That is the way of your country, the country of the dead, where all things now are born dead, live dead and die dead. To escape this image of a lost and distant Spain, Cernuda clutches desperately at a Spain remote in time, the richly suggestive Spain of the novels and Episodios nacionales of Galdós, the favourite reading of his early years. And in the second poem of his «Diptico espaňol», he evokes the «magic of Spain» which is still alive in the pages of the novelist : Today, when you no longer need your country, it is still dear and necessary to you in these books, more real and rich in dreams than the other. Let us end on this final image of the Spain he loved, now that we remember him after his death. And let us forget that other vision, so monstruously unjust, and yet, in its furthest depths, so true. Let us not allow «ignorance, indifference and oblivion» to fall, like a tombstone, on the work of one of our finest poets, who was always distressed-—hence, perhaps, his bitterness and wounded pride — that his poetry did not achieve the recognition in his own country which its high quality (2) He is also unjust to a number of great Spanish writers, including Ortega y Gasset. 155 [pageend157]deserved. Not even his death has brought this recognition, as he sadly foresaw in one of his last poems : And my voice not heard, or scarcely heard, will still sound on when I am dead, alone, like the wind in reeds beside the water. With a conference of this type — partly professional, partly social — such a great deal depends on the right atmosphere. In previous years there was always a small group of people, willingly or not, keeping apart, the red ribbon of their ideology tying individuals into one unnatural, and anonymus body. YVES DE CONSTANTIN est né à Paris le 30 août 1897. Après avoir fondé en 1925 et dirigé la Revue des Pays du Nord, il débuta dans les lettres en 1930 avec une triologie « Les Dieux sans âme », dont le premier roman « Don Juan-les-Pins », publié dans le Mercure de France et édité chez Plon, le fit connaître. Ce roman, plusieurs fois réimprimé, a été repris en 1943 dans une version nouvelle sous le titre « La Gloire de l’Homme ». Vinrent ensuite « Le Paradis empoisonné », traduit comme le précédent, en dano-norvégien et en tchèque, et « Liquidation du monde »; puis « La Douleur et la Joie », et « Rivages humains ». Yves de Constantin se consacre, depuis dix ans, à un ouvrage en 4 volumes et 20 chapitres, « Points de vue, enquête sur l’homme », auquel il met la dernière main. Les passages que nous en publions sont extraits du chapitre VI. CLARK MILLS, author of The Migrants, A Suite for France, and The Circus, is known for his translations from the French Symbolists. More recently he has worked in the literatures of Central Europe. He teaches English at Fairleigh Dickinson University, U.S.A., is director of Voyages Press, and has been associate editor of the series of Scholastic World Affairs MultiTexts. He has just completed (in collaboration with Algirdas Landsbergis) The Flowering Lime — a selection of Lithuanian folksongs scheduled to appear shortly. ALEXANDER JANTA —• poet, novelist, essayist and playwright — is the author of some thirty books, mostly in Polish, three in English (I Lied to Live, Bound with Two Chains, Psalms of Captivity), and is an occasional contributor to American magazines. The New Mexico Quarterly recently published his long poem, The American Story. He is also a translator from American poets. His book of short stories, The Flute and the Apocalyse, will be published shortly, in Polish. Scheduled for publication this year is also a collection of Polish translations from Japanese poets. — Alexander Janta spent a number of years in the Far East, prior to World War II; he was foreign correspondent for the Polish press in Tokyo.
