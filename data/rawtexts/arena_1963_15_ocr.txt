[pageend2]THE MODERN PLAYWRIGHT AS AN EXILE « The convict experiences the human condition in our time more intensely and more directly than any of us, » Martin Esslin has stated in exploring today’s avant-garde drama. The convict — yes, but perhaps even more so the exile. The anguish of banishment echoes through all of world drama. Medea’s wail and Prometheus’ lament are colored with the threnodial hue of exile; in the French medieval epic drama, the door of paradise closes irrevocably behind the first man and woman; Shakespeare’s kings and queens find bitter wisdom in the loss of crown and native soil. Yet in all these plays the author’s eye is not an exile’s : he is the man of the « polis », of the medieval world order, of the Elizabethan realm — still committed to communal life and values, still believing in their relevance. In the very darkness of their despair, Adam and Eve of the « Mystére ď Adam » are aware of the history to unfold and of redemption in time. But when the curtain opens on a contemporary avant-garde play, the spectator often sees a world 1 ARENA [pageend3]refracted by the prism of extreme exile : the « polis » as an alien conglomeration, the historical continuum torn, the revelation lost. Today the spirit of exileness pervades drama to an unparalleled degree, and some of the best known modern playwrights create in adopted languages. The exile of Beckett or Ionesco, Arrabal or Schehade may have been more or less voluntary, but they cannot help carrying in their hearts landscapes and words other than French. Impossibility of communication is one of the basic themes of today’s avant-garde drama, and some of its most eloquent moments occur, paradoxically, when language fails completely, as in the « speeches » of the Orator in « Les Chaises » or of Lucky in «Waiting for Godot». This failure of language is an essential part of an exile’s experience. To be exiled means to see one’s native language, a blend of meaning and power, reduced to insignificance and its place usurped by a foreign tongue, an instrument of alien power, rootless and incapable of engendering true communication. The distance which today’s avant-garde playwright maintains toward the world he depicts, has a parallel exile attitude. Both do not accept the « self-evident» or the « matter of course » of their present communities which they frequently regard as capricious collages, often absurd and always to be questioned. An exile playwright carries within himself two irreconcilable realities, and realism can rarely satisfy him, for he has seen people reduced to obsolete ciphers and nations vanish from the map overnight. Not for him is the stage set with the « real » parlor walls framing solid « flesh- and-blood » people — behind each wall and each skull nothingness lurks. How can he observe the 2 [pageend4]purity of the genres, when the tragic and the comic are inextricably meshed in his life path ? With the continuity of his experience broken, the plot of his life severed from the once meaningful ritual, is it surprising that the exile playwright’s plots embrace incoherence and violent twists? Reason is held in distrust both by the exile and the avant- garde playwright — for both it has proven ephemeral. Their plays turn away from rationalization to depict instead, in Francis Fergusson’s words, « direct suffering of the perception of change ». This issue of « Arena » has intentionally bypassed the internationally famous names to allow the reader a glimpse at a body of drama unknown to him, although existing at his very elbow. Not all the fragments published here fit under the umbrella of the avant-garde theatre; some belong to another era or to a different mentality. But the reader will probably notice the unifying thread of satire and the prevalent questioning attitude. He may feel a special sense of discovery in coming upon the excerpt from the comedy by Gombrowicz, a germinal work harboring almost the entire spectrum of themes and attitudes of today’s « theatre of the absurd ». Historically, anxiety and fear of non-being have been a special burden of exile. But our time has caught up with the exile’s condition : the marginal is becoming the norm, and many an indigenous playwright is discovering a spiritual kinship with the exile. Today’s avant-garde drama holds a faithful mirror to our era of passage, of violent change, and of what Camus had called the « irremediable exile ». In describing some similarities between the condition of an avant-garde playwright and the extreme 3 [pageend5]exile situation, we have dwelt mainly on the destructive features of exileness, tracing the marks of acid, as it were. But that is only one part of the story; acid not only destroys but purifies as well. Let us recall that in Sophocles’ « Oedipus in Colo- nus» it is the «.impious, blasphemous, shameless» stranger, who pacifies « ... those whom it’s futile to fight, Those whom we tremble to name. » The furies of the modern world are more numerous and awesome than those that, haunted the ancient Greeks. It may be that the exile playwright with his special burden has a special contribution to make in naming and placating these furies : in creating dramatic works that not only look into Medusa’s eyes but also beyond, that maintain the tension between anguish and hope, chaos and form. The hundreds of exile theatrical groups — between Hong Kong and Toronto, Sydney and Chicago — are making that contribution (however dismal their working conditions) in being witnesses not only of the universality of the play instinct of « homo ludens », but also of his stubborn insistence on being allowed to make his own choice of word, gesture and theatrical faith — of his unextinguishable desire to play in freedom. GEORGE TABORI FLIGHT INTO EGYPT This play was first presented by Irene Mayer Selznick at The Music Box, New York City, on March 18, 1952. It was staged by Elia Kazan and the set was designed by Jo Mielziner. The distinguished cast included Zero Mostel (Glubb), David Opatoshu (Tewfik Bey), Gusti Huber (Lili Engel), Paul Lukas (Franz Engel), Voytek Dolinski (Bubi Engel), Jo Van Fleet (Miss Foster) and Don Keefer (Bronson). The play is set « one frightful day in 1949 in the interior of Glubb’s Hotel, in Cairo, Egypt ». Its theme is universal : the struggle of the individual against fate, personified by bureaucracy, greed and lust; a struggle that can never end in final defeat whatever the indi37 [pageend39]victual’s destiny. To many an exile, waiting interminably for a visa or facing a weary and indifferent consular official, the plight of the Engels will be only too familiar. STREM RITUAL AND POETRY IN EUGENE IONESCO’S THEATRE As a playwright Ionesco has a feeling of uneasiness, to say the least, about the contemporary theatre, especially about the contemporary French theatre. He accuses the latter of being too doctrinary. Too many writers, he says, are using the stage as a pulpit to expose and impose their philosophies. His own convictions in matters of theatrical art are summarized in his play L’lmpromptu de l’Alma. Ionesco stages himself under his own name in that play, demonstrating the way in which he works, or rather, how his works arise by spontaneous generation. The accent is on impromptu: in the beginning there is the creative urge. The author, vehicle of creation, patiently waits until the first impulse is followed by another, a first word evokes a sequence, a question its reply, until finally the dialogue falls into a pattern according to its own law. However, while his play is still in gestation, philo105 [pageend107]sophers of the market-place urge him to elaborate a plot which should conform to their own, individual and contradictory doctrines. One of them wants to convert him to the epic theatre á la Brecht, the other to essentialism (existentialism), the third one to another fashionable philosophy. For a while he endures the swell of pompous cliches, but manages to state his own profession of faith regarding the art of playwriting : « Le théatre est, pour moi, la projection sur scene du monde du dedans: c’est dans mes reves, dans mes angoisses, dans mes désirs obscurs, dans mes contradictions intérieures que, pour ma part, je me réserve le droit de prendre cette matiére théätrale.» (1) These lines reveal the nature of the quarrel between Ionesco and the conventional theatre. Ionesco does not seek theatrical substance in dialectics brought in from the outside; rather, he finds the conflict within himself and uses the stage to exte- norize it. In the strict sense of the drama his theatre is therefore undramatic (2): nonetheless, it makes excellent theatre. Since he admits that his plays aim at self-expression, he must turn away from the conventional techniques of playwriting to achieve this aim. He repects the categorical imperative of a plot — conceived as a pre-arranged concatenation of events — for he is not interested in telling a dramatized story. In Victimes du Devoir he takes issue with this conventional requirement of plotmaking. To think out a plot, he says, is not different from writing a suspense story; in fact, he contends, all playwriting has been since antiquity the writing of suspense stories. Where there is a plot, there is a solution found in advance, held in reserve to be produced at the right moment. It is the deus ex machina to save author and spectator from embarrassment; it is reassuring, its existence is known in advance, it therefore perverts the basic law of life which is unpredictability. His theatre, as he puts it, is not written : it writes itself. His medium, the words of the human language, fascinates him. This Rumanian born author is a great master and juggler of the French language in which he writes. In this respect he is the direct heir and continuator of Rabelais, as well as a disciple of Rimbaud, Mallarmé and Baudelaire. Words, he realizes, participate in the universal duality as much as anything else. They are valid as keys to conventional understanding but also in their own right as musical entities. As such they possess a certain evocative quality. Deprived of their rational connotation, words in La Canta- trice Chauve produce a sound-pattern characteristic of the social contacts of Western man, epitomized in the British middle-class. The chatter of nonsensical or silly phrases symbolizes the inability of man to communicate with his fellowmen. By giving up all pretense of meaning, the author shows that our conversations have no regard for true communication. He brings his point across by reproducing the ritual of daily life in its innumerable manifestations. The comical effect achieved by the nonsense of the words, accompanied by that of the gestures, has deep tragic implications. The play conforms to Ionesco’s own definition of the theater, for it is the expression of his anguish about the nonsensical character of our daily lives and, on a higher plane, also about the meaning of our existence. The same will hold for all of Ionesco’s productions : his theatre presents the manifold aspects of the Human Comedy, the force and satire of which surpass Balzac’s interpretation. Once more, as in La Cantatrice, the characters are interchangeable. This is indicated by the ingenious artifice of calling all the relatives of the male protagonist Jacques, all those of his female counterpart Roberte. The various members of the same clan are differentiated by numbers only. Both tribes gang up on Jacques No 1, whose tragedy and crime is that he is an individual. The pathos of the play, underlining the futile struggle of the individual against the all-submerging forces of our mass civilization, is enhanced by non-conventional techniques based once more upon the performance of certain ritualistic acts and the magic of sound effects. At first, Jacques No 1 boldly faces the hostile armies of his enemies. He proclaims that he dares to dislike the universally adopted dish of potatoes with bacon. Then all the pressure, clamor and hatred of public opinion weigh down upon him. He must conform; with the dish he must accept all the paraphernalia of the mass man, must choose a bride not according to his own taste, but according to the needs of the community. Once defeated on the score of the insipid nourishment, our sad hero turns his former defiance into a negative one by going all the way on the road towards his debasement. Not only will he espouse an ugly bride but he will choose the most repulsive of the Robertes, the one with three noses and nine fingers of each of her hands. The ritual that consecrates this immolation of the individual on the altar of public opinion is most expressive and depressive. In his use of symbolistic, impressionistic and surrealistic techniques he was influenced by the early one-acters of Maurice Maeterlinck, as well as by the stage-tricks of Jean Cocteau with whom he shares the belief that all truly artistic creation is poetry. He has learned from Pirandello, some of the reversal techniques, and from Beckett the simplifying and reducing methods. Like Beckett, Ionesco strips man of his veneer of civilization to point out his savagery, his greed and selfishness, his inability to love, as well as his helplessness in an uncomprehending and incomprehensible universe. Nevertheless, in his critical portrayal of man Ionesco never descends to the limit of utter despair which characterizes Beckett. One can say that Ionesco has not lost sympathy and solidarity with his heroes. This is natural, for he portrays his own solitude, he writes subjective theatre. In addition to other forerunners, it is almost sure that Kafka has left his mark upon Ionesco, though this cannot be proven by any direct reference in Ionesco’s writings. Such plays as Jacques ou La Soumission and Victimes du Devoir strongly 117 [pageend119]remind one of the hallucinating short stories of Kafka, Der Hungerkuenstler and Die Verwandlung. Also Ionesco’s repeated presentation of the woman who degrades man is reminiscent of some of the feminine figures around the hero of Das Schloss. Ionesco’s revolt against the rational theatre is understandable. In France, the theatre is too often mistaken for a lecture-hall. Racine, the omnipotence of logic, the well-co-ordinated play have not lost their prestige there. Yet France and the rest of the world no longer live in a prowerful monarchy, in a universe where all stars revolve around a central Sun. Our present world is one of kaleidoscopic images which blend and change too fast to be fully intelligible. On the other hand, modern man and especially the modern artist tries to break out of the prison of man’s limited intelligence and aspires to grasp things that are outside of the domain of his reason. It is the theatre’s duty to mirror this bewildering world of ours. Ionesco’s revolt repeats, at a distance of about 130 years, that of the French Romantics which began with the battle of Hemani. However, instead of a rebellious new poetry and unruly scenes he uses satire. He recognizes Moliere as one of his masters and tries to follow in Moliere’s footsteps by presenting human situations and human frailness that are valid at all times, while striving at the same time for some guiding light that allows the individual to assert himself against the levelling and stultifying forces of our society. In view of Ionesco’s horror of mass civilization, he naturally heaps scorn and sarcasm on all totalitarian ideologies which insult man’s reason, resort to any means to inveigle or browbeat him into thoughtless obedience. Indeed, it would be erroneous to believe that, by revolting against the monopoly of reason, Ionesco wishes to abdicate reason. On the contrary, he believes that reasoning is a precious weapon that helps man to maintain his moral integrity. All in all, Ionesco is an innovator in the best tradition of modern art, fighting the encroachment of reason on man’s emotional world, combating conformity that threatens the individual and art itself. His essential contribution is the liberation of the theatre from many of its shackles, the restoration and enlargement of its freedom. 118 [pageend120]w K. G. LISIEWICZ THE POLISH PHOENIX Poles have a special feeling of love and respect for the theatre which is an indespensable part of their lives. The majority of the pre-war Polish theatres was either state or municipally owned. This meant that the program was chosen for its artistic value and not for commercial considerations. It also meant that actors were on annual contracts, fully employed and that a new generation of graduates from the state-run dramatic schools had every chance of getting immediate employment. The Polish Equity (Z.A.S.P. : Zwiazek Artystow Seen Polskich — Union of the Artists of the Polish Stage) even provided old age pensions based on the amount of the actor’s final salary before retirement. Being subsidised, the Polish pre-war theatre could afford to produce plays of a very high standard and this educated the public taste in works of national and international value. 127 [pageend129]Perhaps this was also the reason, why in the last years before the war G.B. Shaw chose Warsaw theatres for his first nights. Schools had a close contact with the theatres which provided special performances as part of the curriculum. This made the theatre for children from the age of seven part of their education and also explains the episode in 1837 in Lwow at the time of partition of Poland when the stage alone could utter words of hope and patriotic inspiration. Count Skarbek gave Lwow, a riverless city the choice of providing at his own expense either a canal joining the rivers Vistula and Bug (creating a link between the Black Sea and the Baltic) or — a fully equipped theatre. Without hesitation, Lwow chose the theatre. Love and need of the theatre followed Poles into exile in 1939. Military and civilian refugees had hardly reached Paris when the professional actors with the help of the Polish Ministry of Education in Exile founded a theatre. This Polish theatre in exile went though three phases which influenced its repertory: (1) The early days of the war when it expected a victorious return to a ruined Poland to which the reborn theatres would bring plays, actors and new technical experience. (2) When the Allied victory proved to be disastrous for Poland, still believing that historical justice must ultimately prevail, the exiled theatre prepared itself for the new task of preserving the spirit of independence and of the struggle for the freedom of Poland. (3) In the years that followed, the Polish emigration started to put down roots and a new generation was born. With it the process of « denationalisation » also began. At the same time during the Stalinist period Poland became silent and withdrawn. The theatre in exile had to adopt itself to new problems. It tried to speak for the silent homeland, both keeping in touch with changed conditions and becoming the custodian of the Polish language in the effort to save the new generation from being submerged by foreign languages and cultures. In the first period, full of enthusiasm and hope, a long and arduous path lay before the Polish theatre in exile in Paris. During the French collapse part of the company was dispersed all over the globe and only some of them reached England or settled there. Quite soon rehearsals began in Drury Lane. But 128 [pageend130]the growing intensity of the air-raids again defeated the tremendous efforts and once more dispersed the company. Those who served in the Army were sent to Scotland where, in spite of difficulties, a new theatre was formed with the First Brigade. At the same time several groups, among them famous Lwowska Fala from Radio Lwow, travelled from camp to camp mostly with a light repertoire. These activities were brought to an end by growing demands of war - readiness. But the Polish theatre is immortal. The Lwowska Fala and the company of the Polish Air Force continued their work. In 1942 the Polish forces from the Middle East arrived from Russia and formed the 2nd Army Corps. This contained the biggest number of actors who had been among Poles deported to Russia and some important producers like, Killanowski Radulski and Domanska. Helped by substantial subsidies, the Middle East Forces formed a company about a hundred strong, including technical personnel of high standards. When Germany collapsed, a multitude of actors and actresses emerged from the P.O.W. and concentration camps and started to work in Germany under the brilliant direction of Schiller, the well known producer. A puppet theatre was also formed. Later most of the actors returned to devastated Poland to help build up a new theatre from the ashes. In England a co-operative theatre was organized in which the actors took the financial risk of every production, the earnings being shared equally after expenses had been covered. They still received limited financial help from the government in exile so they were able to stage even big productions in the Stoll and Dury Lane Theatres. An enthusiastic public of exiles crowded the performances and London was still the centre of the Polish life in exile. When the official recognition was withdrawn from the exile government, the help for cultural enterprises ceased, too. The emigre theatre was a fighting theatre. When the homeland stages were under dictatorship, the free theatre in London spoke for the silent Polish people. It was a time of a rich repertory of classical plays, mostly from the eighteenth century. Now they sounded contemporary : the same circumstances, the same suppression from the same source, the same bayonets belonging to the same soldiers. It was immaterial whether the Russian Ambassador was called Sievers or Comrade So-and- 129 [pageend131]so-nov. Both had the same task to fulfill: to deprive the country of its national freedom, to subordinate it to an alien power and to destroy free speech. In spite of a very limited number of pre-war playwrights a certain amount of new plays was also staged in London : a new generation of dramastists appeared. This golden era of the theatre in exile was short-lived. In search of a livelihood, Poles started to emigrate from England : to Canada, the U.S.A., France and South Africa. Many settled for good in Scotland and in the provinces. The actors, too, had to find some sort of security and many left England. The Polish theatre in London shrank. After the White Eagle Club was burnt down, it found itself a very small stage in the Polish Hearth with a limited company and a dwindling audience. Today the difficulties facing the Polish theatre in exile are manifold and serious. It has to find plays with Polish problems and a national character, but the great classical plays are almost impossible to produce on such a small stage and with such a limited choice of actors. The Poles in London settled mostly in the suburbs where they lead a life of their own, with local clubs and entertainment. The theatre operating in the centre is for many — too distant from their homes. The theatre faces also another major problem : how long can it count on a Polish-speaking audience ? A second generation is growing up, the children of parents already educated in British schools, especially those who have one British parent. In order to secure the survival of the theatre, one of the actresses, Miss Barbara Renská, with the help of other actors organised a dramatic school called the Workshop of the Young. It had to start by rooting out all trace of an English accent and explaining all the more complicated Polish expressions. There is no doubt that the Workshop is extremely successful and that it will produce a new generation of actors. Yet the theatre needs not only actors, but also a public. Five years ago, with the help of the Polish Welfare Association in London a Polish Youth and Children’s Theatre was formed which sought to put on plays with educational themes of historical, literary or legendary interest in the form of entertaining fairy tales or fantasies. This was only possible if the plays were specially written for it. 130 [pageend132]Of course the theatre had a powerful rival in the English pantomimes, lavishly produced. The Polish child would be apt to make comparisons, so it was necessary to make it clear that this theatre, his theatre, was not just a poor relation of the English stage. In spite of very limited resources, it managed to put on big productions at the Lyric, Hammersmith and the Rudolf Steiner Theatres. It presented plays written by the author of this article, such as Enchanted Nights, scenes from the childhood of Chopin ; The Man in the Moon, a play based on two Polish legends and several others by different authors, including some still living in Poland. The most recent production was a very special one : a Resurrection play written by a monk, Nicolas of Wilkowieck in the sixteenth century, brilliantly staged by St. Szpiganowicz. The Polish Children’s Theatre owes its existence to a group of dedicated workers under the gifted leadership of its artistic manager, Regina Kowalewska, one of the few in the world specialised producers of youth and children plays. With very few exceptions, the Polish exiled playwrights, have not succeeded in breaking through to the British stage. Even these exceptions (like Marian Hemar and Jablonski) have not reached the West End. Hemar’s Miracle of a Poor Man was produced in Ireland and in Birmingham but it dealt with religious problems that are not popular in London. Jablonski is nearer to the British theatre; his plays show distinct traces of British influence and, if I am not mistaken, are written in English. Even Cwojdzinski, a brilliant writer of psychological comedies with scientific problems as Freud’s Theory of Dreams or The Einstein Theory, Hypnosis, Theodosyia Lisiewicz with her excellent comedies and many other shows with a deep knowledge of stagecraft and sparkling dialogue, have not been seen on the London stage. Only one post-War play (Silence by Brandstaetter) was produced on the BBC by Vai Gielgud. What is the cause ? Probably the commercialism of the English West End stage, avoiding the slightest possibilty of a financial risk. The exiled playwright is in a difficult position of being both limited in subject by his public wanting to see their own problems portrayed and by the impossibility of breaking through the barrier that separates the British theatre from Continental contacts. 131 [pageend133]Owing to this barrier, a brilliant stage designer Thadeus Orlowicz whose work in the Lyric and Stoll theatres in Polish productions was highly praised by the British press, has only reached the Workshop and the « Q » Theatres. I have tried to avoid mentioning too many names, but I must stress that the Polish exile theatre owns its more than twenty years of difficult existence to the dedicated work of the producer and artistic manager Leopold Kielanowski, the Middle East Theatre to Jadwiga Domanska and Waclaw Radulski and a long list of actors and actresses.
