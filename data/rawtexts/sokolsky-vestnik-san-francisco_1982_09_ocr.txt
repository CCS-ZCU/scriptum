'when I So here it is — Sokol jacket indicating my explained, they asked that the What Events or Sokol Personalities Influenced Me Most in My Sokol Life What event? What personality? They do go together - for without the personalities, we wouldn’t have the events, would we? There are many memories of long ago that influenced my Sokol life, but I think the personalities that are the most outstanding are my Great Uncle Frank Lastufka and my Uncle Wencil Storek. These wonderful men were two of the Founders of our Sokol Unit, and as you know, our Unit celebrated 70 years in 1974, so that goes a long way back - but then, so do I!!! My Uncles were quite different. Their personalities often clashed, but their differences never interfered with their being great Sokols. Uncle Frank taught - no, let us not use the word “taught” as his manner was such that we didn’t realize we were being “taught”. Let’s say we learned through his subtle, patient, and wise ways, all about Sokol - the Ideals, Philosophy, and the whys and wherefores of our great organization. He was a true diplomat, and for a time served as Vice Consul to Czechoslovakia in San Francisco. I remember him as a small, wizened, very bald man. He always wore a small black cap to keep his head warm. He could always calm the stormy Sokol waters by a wise statement presented in his usual calm, distinguished way. I could picture him at many events, all impressive, especially to the young; at meetings, at opening ceremonies, at the Treasure Island World’s Fair, and he was always serene, self-controlled, and dignified. And always with his small black cap upon his head. Just so you don’t get the idea Uncle Frank was perfect - he was a horrible driver! As youngsters, we often drove from the Peninsula to San Francisco with him. It was with great relief that we arrived safely home. As we walked in the door, we always gave thanks to God that we had “made it” once again! Uncle Wencil came to California at a very young age, and was “under the wing" of Uncle Frank, but his personality did not reflect Uncle Frank’s. He was much different. He was blustery, often offensive in his out-spokeness, and argumentive in many respects. BUT! What a wonderful, generous, and loving man he was and what a wonderful Sokol! Here - he DID reflect the lore of Uncle Frank. Many are the people who came to the United States to San Francisco under his sponsorship. They worked in 1975 his leather business until they could get themselves established in their new nation. Many were the material donations to Sokol, and many were the hours donated to Sokol work and activities. Though I often had arguments with him as to what was right and what was wrong - and with Uncle Wencil there never seemed to be an inbetween - I loved and admired him. He served as President of Sokol for many years. He was a self-educated man, and his speeches - thought to be long and boring to me at the time - were well thought out and well presented. The problem of allowing our youth to “take hold” and present their ideas has always been evident in our Sokol Unit. As a “mládež’’, this was our biggest bone to pick, and many arguments ensued with these honorable men. However, I grew and survived, and today I am striving to show our youngsters the true Sokol meaning in the way it wa> so well presented to me. As mentioned previously - I think events and personalities go together. I feel that I must add a few words about some of the many events that are outstanding in my memories. There are the memories of Pacific District Slets, National Slets, Treasure Island World’s Fair with its Czech Day and Exhibits. The memory of our young gymnasts striving to do their best - win or lose, and they sometimes won! The memory of a Sokol Course, and the friends made there! The memory of Barn Dances and New Year’s Eve Dances. The memory of the year I was chosen Queen of Sokol San Francisco for outstanding achievement for our Unit, and the year I placed fourth in District competition when I was pregnant with our first boy, Wesley. These last memories are individual and very personal events of which I am very proud! What keeps my interest and influences my Sokol life today? Our move to San Mateo has once again made attending Sokol an interesting challenge. Since our move I have witnessed the children’s gym classes grow from 15 to 50, plus. I have seen our Unit break records in our participants at a National Slet. I have seen us host a Pacific District Gymnastic Exhibition - the first in over 10 years. I have seen our youngsters improve in our gym from gangling gymnastic “triers" to Trophy winning gymnasts. I still have much to learn, and there is still much to teach. Our youngsters must learn the Sokol Ideals. I think we are slowly, surely, and with determination, doing that. In sincerity, I hope that I am able to influence our Sokol children today, as many years ago, I was so influenced by my Uncles - Frank Lastufka and Wencil Storek. [pageend8]MINUTES OF LAST MEETING - AUGUST 9, 1982 - RESUME Roll call of officers indicated seven were absent probably due to the summer months. There were 11 members present and we had the pleasure of greeting our guest from Praha, Czechoslovakia - Frances Vrbenska, sister of Sokol Sister Libuše Sikl. Correspondence included a letter from Sister Ivanka Bevc regarding travel reimbursement for Slet participants. We also received a letter from George Neuman with a donation.of $10 for the Vestnik. Sokol members who still owe dues for 1981 and 1982 will receive letters informing them that their dues must be paid by September 13, 1982 or face expulsion, in accordance with Sokol By-laws. Work in the breezeway of the building has been successfully completed, and the next project is the painting of the outside of our Sokol. A motion was made and carried that we get estimates in writing for this project. Nacelnik, Paul Kryska, was absent. Sister Duffey reported that gym classes will be commencing when school semester begins. Members were reminded of our picnic at Coyote Point on August 15. Discussion was held regarding travel funds to be distributed to Sokol members who participated in the Slet. Final amounts will be allocated at the next General Meeting. Next dance for the opening of the Autumn Season will be on September 25. Meeting was adjourned at 8:30 pm. Sister Elizabeth Bele recorded the minutes of this meeting in the absence of our Recording Secretary. FROM THE DESK OF THE FINANCIAL SECRETARY: - Our records show that there are many people who have not paid their membership dues in Sokol San Francisco for 1982. We do not send reminders to individuals for the current year dues. Delinquent notices for those people who have not paid for 1981 are in the mail now. If those dues are not paid by September 13, 1982, you will automatically be expelled from Sokol - which is in accordance with Sokol By-laws. Please don’t let this happen to you. We want you as a Sokol member. In order to have all dues payable on the first day of the year, we suggest that you pay for that portion of the year you owe for the current year, and then pay for the 1983 year and all subsequent years on January 1st. For instance, if your dues are $15 a year and you joined in August - pay at the rate of $1.25 per month for September, October, November, and December. A total of $5. (If your dues are $18 you would figure the amount due at $1.50 per month) This will then pay your dues up to the end of the year, Now your dues will always be payable at the beginning, of each year. This makes it easier for you to remember when your. Sokol dues are payable, and it also simplifies the bookkeeping system. If you need any assistance, contact Sister Elizabeth Bele - Sokol Hall, 849 No. San Mateo Drive, San Mateo, CA. 94401. [pageend9]DREAMS DO COME TRUE A dream of a lifetime, came true for me this summer. Today, thousands of miles away, its impact on my life remains concrete. This miracle was to participate in the Sokol Slet in Vienna with my darling daughter, Serena, my beloved Aunt Blanche Duffey, and several of my special Sokol friends. My heart swells with pride to realize the heritage endowed me being the granddaughter of such stalwart Czechs as Mr. & Mrs. Frank Dibelka, my "Moo-moo and Grandpa". At the field of activities of the 1982 International Sokol Slet, the Flags of the Nations caught by gusty winds, wet by Vienna rains called our spirits to a unity of purpose, our heritage as Sokols and Czechs. The torrential downpour that left us drenched after our morning practice was quickly forgotten as the Viennese sun arrived to shine on the afternoon festivities. With my heart and soul dancing together in excited anticipation, I^waited for my trip inside Czechoslovakia to visit some members of my family I had never previously seen. Our entrance into Czechoslovakia was by train. The experience left me numb as I realized at that moment crossing the border I was to be living without some of my American freedoms. I reached for the assurance of my Passport as I felt the chilling shudder. My family in Czechoslovakia is wonderful. They live near each other and maintain close family ties. There is much love and great beauty of determination one feels when near them. Outside the cities, the country side is very lovely equal to the fanciful dream of a growing, imaginative child. My daughter, Serena, went off into this idyllic countryside with her cousins. Serena, not speaking any Czech, and her cousins, not speaking any English, remained in this natural Paradise all day long returning at nightfall. They were enjoying communicating with each other and nature through the international language of children. Inside the cities, the golden sunset on peachy stonewalls allows us for only a twinkling to understand what this country must have been like in its moment of independence. Yet always there are post-war reminders — our dreams and our hopes. Cecily Sowers ARE YOU INTERESTED IN SHARING????
