CHARTER 77 The New Year commenced inauspiclously in Prague but a storm within the Communist Party was already in the making between the two factions, the Stalinist hardliners and Husak’s advocates of “normalization.” The cause of confusion within their ranks was Charter 77, a manifesto written by Czech dissidents, signed by 238 petitioners and delivered personally to the Government and Parliament. It was simply a request to the Government to observe the human rights guaranteed by the Czechoslovak Constitution. In a Communist country, such a simple declaration of human rights is dynamite and calls for police intervention and arrests. Ordinarily, authors with such ideas are sent to prison for “rehabilitation” and their declaration is thrown into the waste-paper basket. Anticipating such treatment, the authors contrived to smuggle the manifesto to the free West and to hand it over to a number of leading Western journalists. Consequently, the Charter was promptly published, in part or in full, in a number of Western publications: Frankfurter AUgemeine of West Germany, Times of London, Le Monde of Paris, the New Leader, the New York Times, the Christian Science Monitor, the Washington Post, and in several other newspapers in the United States. To this day, a month later, the Czechoslovak press has not published a word of the text, but only threats. The Voice of America, however, transmitted the entire text for Czechoslovak listeners. “The purpose of the manifesto is very simple,” explained former Minister Jiří Hájek, one of the original 238 signers, when interviewed by telephone by an American journalist from Vienna (The Washington Post, January 8). “This is a citizens’ initiative to put international conventions on human rights into practice and is fully within the framework of the constitution. We don’t want to make it a political action or create a basis for opposition activity.” As explained by Eric Bourne, special correspondent of the Christian Science Monitor, “today’s dissent has a new style; it is more subtle than in the 1950s. The protest of dissenters is confined to one issue - civic liberties - for which international law and Czechoslovakia’s own legislation and Constitution can be invoked.” THE HELSINKI SPARK The entire text of Charter 77 was reprinted in the Congressional Record on January 26, pp. 563-565. It was presented on the floor of the House of Representatives by Dante B. Fascell, chairman of the Committee on Security and Cooperation in Europe, who when introducing the subject reviewed the Helsinki accords and the present situation in Eastern Europe, namely in the Soviet Union, Czechoslovakia, East Germany and Poland. In his report, Rep. Fascell raisedlhe question: “If this aspect (on human rights) is to be so flagrantly ignored, the other signatories, and especially the United States, must ask themselves how valid are any of the commitments on international security and cooperation . . . Fulfillment of that promise can promote a safer world and the progress of détente. Dishonoring that pledge only worsens international tensions.” Authors of Charter 77 base their claim to human rights and tc the right to be heard on Law No. 120 of the Czechoslovak Collection of Laws, published October 13, 1976, which includes the text of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, and on the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights, both signed in behalf of Czechoslovakia in 1968 and confirmed at the 1975 Helsinki Conference. These pacts went into effect in Czechoslovakia on March 23, 1976. These two documents are to guarantee freedoms to individuals. “Their publication, however, is at the same time an urgent reminder that many fundamental human rights exist only on paper in our Republic,” state the authors of Charter 77 and proceed to enumerate the freedoms denied them. Freedom of Expression and Freedom from Fear The right of free expression guaranteed by Article 19 of the first pact, for example, is quite illusory. Tens of thousands of citizens have been prevented from working in their professions for the sole reason that their views differ from the official ones. They have been the frequent targets of various forms of discrimination and chicanery on the part of the authorities or social organization; they have been denied any opportunity to defend themselves and are practically the victims of apartheid. Hundreds of thousands of other citizens have been denied the “freedom from fear” cited in the Preamble to the first pact; they live in constant peril of losing their jobs or other benefits if they express their opinions.
