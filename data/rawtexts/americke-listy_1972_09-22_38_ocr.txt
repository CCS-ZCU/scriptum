Y. • No. 38. — Price 20¢ NEWS AND VIEWS THE FORCE OF PUBLIC OPINION There are several proofs now that public opinion of the free world can wield an enormous influence over the developments in closed societies, particularly in the Soviet bloc, when it concerns matters of ci- vl liberties and human rights. We have the recently published Nobel Prize lecture by „Alexander Solzhenitsyn, witnessing to the fact that without the expression of concern by the free world, he would have disappeared without a trace in some Soviet prison camp or psychiatric institution. There are other examples. Under the pressure from the West, the Soviets paroled the Russian poet Yosif Brodsky from prison and sent him to exile. At the same time, several other Russian artists, engaged in the protest movement, were permitted to emigrate from the USSR. Just a few days ago, the neo-Sta inist governfent of Sovet-occupied Czechoslovakia has buckled down under the barrage of criticism expressed by the free world folloing the series of political trials in Prague and Brno. They have aggravated the precarious situation of European Communist movements so much that the Czechoslovak government was forced to revise its methods of dealing with the dissenters. Because of its typical lack of originality and imagination, it simply adopted the Soviet pattern and began sending Czech reformists into exile. The first one to go was Jan Sling, a man in his thirties, with his young wife Sling is the son of an English-börn woman and thus, eligible for British citizenship. His father. Otto Sling, a high Communist official, was hanged in 1952 together with Rudolf Slánský and rehabilitated some years later. His son became a supporter of Dubček and opponent of the Soviet invasion. which led him to the Pankrác prison, the same one where his father had spent his last days. Another prospective exile is the Czechoslovak chess champion and prominent m a t h e m a ti ci a n Lu ■ dek Pachman. Once a Communist activist, he became one of the ideologists of the Prague Spring and a vociferous opponent of the Soviet occupation. He, too, has spent agonizing months in prison, remaining unshakable in hrs belief. The harsh treatment and psychological pressure imperiled his health and his days seemed to be counted, but then some individuals and groups in the took interest in him and probably saved his life. Of course, exile is not a panacea; in extreme situa- Uons. however, it can mean the only possible salvation for the victims of political persecution. Těch osm barev znamenalo přesně osm kouzel, které v té kuličce byly zakleté, a každé to kouzlo samo o sobě stačilo na to, aby ten kdo s tou kuličkou hrál musel vždycky vyhrál a obehrát všecky ostatní. Holčička s mašlí jí dostala darem od jednoho dobrého kouzelníka. Zrovna si rozbila koleno a plakala, čaroděj šel kolem a dal jí kuličku. Byl to totiž čaroděj z klubu skautů čarodějů a chtěl mít ten den vykonaný dobrý skutek. Jenže, a to ta holčička nevěděla, všecka ta kouzla v kuličce působila jen tenkrát, když byl majitel dobrý člověk. Tahle holčička teda docela byla. Dovedla ve škole napovídat, nezlobila maminku — nežalovala, vždycky byla milá k starším lidem a nevy- tahovala se na menší děti kuličku půjčila prohlédnout. Ale holčička že ne, že to je .skleněnka, a že se nepůjčuje a když tak jen za dvacet kuliček. A protože dvacet kuliček za půjčení bylo i pro vítěze turnaje trochu moc, tak začali rovnou hrát. A první měl cvrnkat zrovna kluk. Naklonil se nad skleněnku, která vypadala dost odřená, ale jak prvně cvrnknul zazářila první barva, a tak to šlo pořád dál, a když cvrnknul po osmé, rozzářila se kulička celá, oběhla důlek, smetla tam všecky kuličky co byly ve hře a spadla rovnou za nimi. Což už bylo i na gentlemana trochu moc, a tak si tu skleněnku nechal.
