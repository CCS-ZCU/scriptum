OUR LADY OF HOSTÝN FEATURE The Czech Chapel oi' the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception here is to be dedicated June 26 to Our Lady of Hostýn in honor of St. John Nepomucene Neumann, a Czech-born bishop. Our Lady of Hostýn is the “Queen of the Marian Garden of the Czech, Moravian, Sileasian and Slovak Madonnas,” to borrow the subtitle of a 171-page book written by Monsignor Ludvik Nemec, priest, historian and professor at Chetnut Hill College. She is also the Roman Catholic protectress of Moravia, a province of modern day Czechoslovakia. Tradition records the miraculous intervention of the Mother of God on behalf of the Moravians against a hord of Mongols eight hundred years ago (in 1241), and by 1625 pilgrimages to the Marian chapel at Hostýn were begun, Msgr. Nemec writes in his book titled Our Lady of Hostýn. A new church was erected at Hostýn in 1658 containing a new image of the Blessed Virgin Mary with the Infant in her arms. A constantly growing number of pilgrims required the building to have two additional chapels built, and when these proved inadequate, the foundation of a new church was laid in 1721, with completion delayed by wars until 1718. A crowd of 30,000 faithful attended the consecration of the huge edifice on July 28, 1748, erected in circular style. The church was furnished with six altars, the main one double, dedicated to Our Lady of Victory and Our Lady of the Assumption. The popularity of Hostýn grew and it attracted hundreds of thousands of pilgrims each year. But then the Enlightenment Period brought about an oppression of religion and the church and parish at Hostýn were closed in 1787 by imperial order. Many faithful pilgrims, however, continued to come to the abandoned church, but it wasn’t until the end of the destructive Napoleonic wars (1805-1815) that Hostýn slowly began to be revived as a center of religious devotion. In 1828, collections for {he church were'begun, and to commemorate the 600th anniversary of the Moravian victory over the Mongols, during 1841-1849, an official collection was taken up in the Archdiocese of Olomouc. A restoration of the church was begun in 1841 and completed in 1845. Above the main altar stood a new statue of Our Lady of Hostýn. A parish house was built in 1885, and 1887 the spiritual charge was given to the Jesuit Fathers, who maintained supervision until the Nazi occupation. In 1897, Emperor Franz Josef I of the Austro-Hungarian Empire (of which what is now Czechoslovakia was a part) visited the church at Hostýn. Pope St. Pius X in Rome blessed the crown of Our Lady of Hostýn in 1912. The statue at Mount Hostýn was the most ornate of any shrine in Czechoslovakia by that time, through the efforts of Father Antonin Stojan. After World War I when Cechoslovakia was established as an independent nation, the shrine at Hostýn continued to be popular. The facilities at Hostýn were enlarged during the 1920s, and a new altar of the Assumption of the Blessed Virgin was built in 1933. “The Shrine of Our Lady of Hostýn with its retreat facilities,” writes Msgr. Nemec, “became a favored site for any public expression, manifestation or renewal for most of the national Catholic organizations of men, women or youth in their annual conventions.” During the Nazi occupation of Czechoslovakia (1939- 45), the Hostýn shrine suffered much, with official pilgrimages forbidden and pilgrim lodges closed. “Amidst hatred and evil, Our Lady of Hostýn proved a mighty protector as she had through the course of history—the Mountain of Hostýn, even during World War II, perdured as a place much sought by the oppressed, the persecuted and the suffering,” Msgr. Nemec writes. A brief period of new life was experienced from the end of World War II until 1950 when the communist government of Czechoslovakia dispersed the Jesuits from Hostýn and turned administration of the shrine over to secular priests, a circumstance continuing to the present time. In spite of restrictions by communist rulers, Our Lady of Hostýn still attracts crowds of pilgrims. “Our Lady of Hostýn,” write Msgr. JOHN NEPOMUCENE NEUMANN John Neponrucene Neumann was born March 23, 1811 in Prachatice, a small town in Bohemia about 90 miles southwest of Prague. He was the third of six children born to Philip Neumann, a Bavarian who moved in 1802 to Bohemia to get away from Napo- lean’s armies then ravaging much of Europe. Philip Neumann was 28 years old when he settled in Prachatice and where he married a Czech girl, who died unfortunately in November 1801 when their first child was still born. In July 1805, Philip Neumann married Agnes Lebis, daughter of a harness maker, also of Prachatice. John, the elder of two sons born to the couple, was named after his sainted countryman from a neighboring town, John Nepcm- ucene. His brother was named Wenceslaus, and they had four sisters. In his home both German and Czech were spoken, but his schooling was in German. He went to Ceske Budějovice in 1823 for “gymnasium” studies, and in 1831 he was accepted into the seminary there. In 1833 he transferred to the seminary in Prague, completing his studies in philosophy and theology there in 1835. It was there be began thinking seriously of being a missionary to America. In February 1836, he left his home in Prachatice and began a 40-day journey by sea to America, landing in New York the end of May 1836. He immediately contacted Bishop Dubois of New York, and in June 1836 was ordained a priest in New York. (He had not been ordained in Ceske Budějovice because there were no vacancies in that diocese.) His brother Wenceslaus later followed him to the U. S. and become a Redemp- torist lay brother. He worked diligently as a diocesan priest in the Buffalo and Niagara Falls areas of upper New York state. In 1840, for spiritual reasons, he joined the Re- demptories. He was transferred to Pittsburgh and later to Baltimore. Still later he became head of the Redemptorists in the United States, though not without experiencing many difficulties.
