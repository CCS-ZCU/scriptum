He arranged to meet them in Bulgaria, but all three were arrested on the Bulgarian/Turkish border and handed over to the Czechoslovak authorities. Czechoslovakia. THE WHITE HOUSE WASHINGTON April 16, 1985 Mrs. Anna Faltus Washington Representative Czechoslovak National Council Suite 610 1101 New Hampshire Avenue, N.W. Washington, D.C. 20037 Dear Mrs. Faltus: For many years, you have heard me warn of the threat to our country from Soviet-supported violence in the Americas. Soviet activities in Cuba, Grenada, and other Latin nations show that even oceans cannot protect our citizens from the dangers of communism. We are reaching a decisive moment in the history of this hemisphere when we must choose the course of our future. Will the United States stand behind those struggling for democracy and peace in Nicaragua and helping to protect the security of our neighbors and our own citizens; or wall we abandon our friends and permit a pro-Soviet regime to spread violence, chaos, and the threat of millions of refugees streaming across our open border? On one side are the Sandinistas, who came to power in Nicaragua in 1979 as part of a coalition. They promised freedom, democracy, and peace, but they lied to the world. As self-professed communists, the ruling elite quickly followed the pattérn we've seen in country after country, using force and terror to seize control. They set up a secret police force, began an enormous military buildup, persecuted the church, censored the press, confiscated property, and forcibly displaced and murdered innocent civilians. The Sandinistas have welcomed to their soil every Soviet-bloc nation, as well as the leaders of international terrorism — Libya, Iran, and the P.L.O. They have also supported guerrillas in El Salvador and terrorists in Costa Rica and Honduras. A victory for Soviet tanks, planes, and subversion on the mainland of the Americas would seriously undermine our credibility among allies and, for the first time in history, place this Nation in real danger. Opposing the communists are the freedom fighters, whose volunteers have swelled to over 15,000. Many of them are deserters from the Sandinista armed forces. They are struggling for the right of all Nicaraguans to determine their future. On March 1st, they joined unarmed leaders of the democratic opposition and offered the communists a cease-fire in return for an agreement to begin a national dialogue mediated by the Catholic Church. They seek to honor those original promises for democracy, personal freedom, and peace. [pageend4]I have made a proposal for peace that calls for a cease-fire and church-mediated dialogue. I have asked the members of the democratic opposition to extend their offer of a cease-fire until June 1st, which they agreed to do even though it places them at risk. And I've asked Congress to release $14 million for the freedom fighters and their families — aid that will remain nonmilitary as long as the communists agree to a cease-fire and negotiations. The votes on these requests will occur very soon. Our proposal has won the support of Nicaragua's neighbors, including President Duarte of El Salvador who called it "the right step at the right time." He urged Congress to support it. The communists pin their hopes on Congress refusing to support the democratic opposition movement of Nicaragua. If they are right, if Congress abandons our friends, then virtually all restraints on the communists will have been removed. I want you to know that your personal support on this issue is absolutely crucial. The fate of freedom in Nicaragua and the security of the United States and her neighbors are at stake.
