Werner EIN VORWORT ZUM VORWORT Kaum hatte ich mich von der Zusammenstellung dieser Humor-Nummer erholt, als ein Brief auf meinen mit Manuskripten vollbeladenen Schreibtisch flatterte. Mir wurde schonend mitgeteilt, dass ich noch ein Vorwort schreiben müßte. Ein Vorwort zu der Relativitätstheorie von Einstein ist angebracht, aber ein Vorwort zum Humor ? Daß ich nicht lache ! Ich habe mich dennoch entschlossen, ein « Vorwort zum Vorwort » zu schreiben, denn das Vorwort muß vieles erklären. Ein Vorwort ist wie ein klassischer Roman, der überall zu finden ist, aber kaum gelesen wird. Und doch ist eine Büchersammlung ohne klassischen Roman, keine Bibliothek und ein Buch ist meistens kein Buch ohne Vorwort. Ich habe einmal unter dem Titel : « Das Rezeptbuch des Erfolges » auf meine Mitmenschen ein Attentat verübt. 1 [pageend3]Laut Mitteilungen meines Verlegers sind aber alle Leser — trotz gegenteiliger Behauptungen ■■— wohlauf. Ob meine vielen Rezepte irgendwem auch Erfolg gebracht haben, entzieht sich meiner Kenntnis. Das einzige, was ich hörte, war, dass Unternehmer, Werbefachleute, Verkäufer etc. den in meinem Vorwort vorkommenden Satz : « Zur Nachahmung empfohlen... » 100 %-ig wahrgenommen haben. Dann ist es um das Buch still geworden. Das Buch und ich wurden vorläufig nur verbannt und nicht wie so viele, verbrannt. Und so vergingen 20 Jahre... Unlängst erhielt ich von einem bekannten ungarischen Schaufensterdekorateur einen Brief, in dem er mir mitteilte, dass man in Werbefachkreisen mein Buch « wieder » hervorgeholt habe, da es wieder aktuell geworden sei. Ist die Tatsache, dass das Buch dem Nazismus und Kommunismus trotzen konnte, als Erfolg zu bezeichnen ? Ich glaube nicht. Mir scheint, dass das Buch nach 2 Jahrzehnten deshalb hervorgeholt wurde, weil seine Ratschläge und Empfehlungen humorvoll serviert wurden, und Humor bleibt zeitlos. Damit sind wir bei dem ewig aktuellen Humor angelangt. Was ist Humor ? « Humor ist, wenn man trotzdem lacht ! » Diese ausgezeichnete Definition von Otto Julius Bierbaum hat einen tiefen Sinn : dass der Humor eine ganz ernstzunehmende Angelegenheit ist. Lessing scheint in « Minna von Barnhelm » dieselbe Meinung zu vertreten, wenn er fragt : « Kann man denn auch nicht lächelnd ernsthaft sein ? » Doch, man kann’s ! Der Humor kann genauso wie die Feder eines Karikaturisten scharf, spöttisch, ironisch, sogar grausam sein. Und wir wissen, dass die Lächerlichkeit sicherer als jede Waffe tötet. Am meisten faszinieren im Humor die politischen, religiösen, sozialen oder philosophischen Zerrspiegeleien, 2 [pageend4]wenn sie vom Geist durchsponnen sind. Fehlt der Geist, ist es eben kein Humor. Dann lacht man nicht. Humor und Lachen sind siamesische Zwillinge. Und was ist der Humor ohne Lachen : ein Witz ohne Pointe, Paris ohne den Eifelturm, oder Wien ohne den « Stefferl ». Der geistreiche Wiener Spötter Saphir hat die schlechten Interpreten des Humors « von Mördernatur beseelte Pointentöter » genannt « Lachen und Lächeln sind Tor und Pforte, durch die viel Gutes in den Menschen hineinhuschen kann » — behauptet Christian Morgenstern. Shakespeare läßt seine Gedanken über das Lächeln im « Hamlet » zu Worte kommen : « Lächelnder, verdammter Schurke ! Schreibtafeln her ! Ich muß mir’s niederschreiben, daß einer lächeln kann und immer lächeln und doch ein Schurke sein. » Ein guter Anekdotenerzähler muß noch lange kein Schurke sein und ebenso wenig kein Humorist. Er könnte vielmehr ein ausgezeichneter Verkäufer bzw. erfolgreicher Vertreter einer Kakaofabrik sein. Und hier ist der Punkt, in dem der Humorist und der Kakaovertreter etwas Gemeinsames haben : sie verstehen nämlich beide auf ihre Weise, die Menschen « durch den Kakao zu ziehen ». Dabei sollte man nicht vergessen, dass die Arbeit eines Vertreters keineswegs leichter ist als die des Humoristen. G.B. Shaw sagt darüber in einem Lustspiel : « In 8 Tagen kannst du Revuegirl sein, in 48 Stunden Schauspieler, in 5 Stunden französischer Minister, — aber mindestens 10 Jahre brauchst du, um ein guter Vertreter zu werden! * Beim Humor kommt es genau wie bei einer Ware auf die gefällige, reizvolle Verpackung an. Bittere Pillen pflegt man auch mit zuckersüßer Masse zu umhüllen. Um dem Humor Glaubwürdigkeit zu schenken, legen die Humoristen ihre geistreichen Sätze gerne in den Mund eines Königs, eines Philosophen, eines Staatsmannes, Feldherrn, Erfinders, Komponisten, Künstlers, und ähnlicher Stars des Alltagslebens, und dies seit der Antike bis auf unsere Tage. 3 [pageend5]Der Name Ford wurde durch die angeblich von ihm selbst bestellten und verbreiteten humorvollen Geschichten volkstümlich. Ihre fantasiereichen Lebensgeschichten wurden von ihren Publicity- Managern auf die Psychologie des Volkes abgestimmt. Je abenteuerlicher diese Geschichten waren, umso mehr Erfolg hatten sie. This German, whose name was Karl Marx, went to Paris because it was a place where a man 5 [pageend7]could think freely, if not clearly. Subsequently this Leftist left the Left Bank, transporting his thinking to England. At that time England was exporting such quantities of woolen goods, walking shoes, and poems by Tennyson that it had to import social philosophers to maintain the balance of trade (1). How Communism got to Russia, we shall see later. It was not by surface travel but by underground. BIRTH AND BOYHOOD Karl Marx was born in the Rhineland city of Trier early on the morning of May 5, 1818 (2). It was an ungodly hour, which may explain Marx’s later attitude toward religion. He awoke everyone with his cries, and few realized that even then he was complaining about the injustice of it all. While his father washed down a Schnitzel with a stein of lager, poor Karl was allowed nothing but milk, and even this he had to work for. Karl’s father was a lawyer, and he hoped his son would follow in his footsteps, which led from the house to the office to the Bierstube. Like a good son, Karl promised to study law, but there was a gleam in his eye which handicapped him when it came to reading fine print (3). He was a pale, studious lad with a philosophical bent, long hours at a desk having ruined his posture. Even in adolescence he was so busy reading that his morals were beyond reproach. UNIVERSITY CAREER At the University of Bonn, which Karl attended in order to revolutionize the curriculum, an event of no little importance occurred. He engaged in a duel and, momentarily preoccupied with a philosophical problem, lowered his guard. He received a thrust over the left (sic) eye, which marked him for life. About this time Karl began to write poetry and invented free verse, a form which he found suited to writing about free love. It is interesting to speculate about the effect on world politics and the sonnet had he made poetry his career. Since he did not, we have at least been spared recitations from The Selected Poems of Karl Marx by millions of unhappy schoolchildren. After a year at Bonn, young Marx went to Berlin University, ostensibly to pursue the law, which was running scared. But he seems to have taken a wrong turn at one of the corridors, and his notebooks were full of Aristotle, Spinoza, Leibnitz, and Bacon, the latter in long strips used as bookmarks. INFLUENCE OF HEGEL It was at this time that he became a Hegelian, firmly asserting that nothing is static, even when his hair stood on end and you could plainly see sparks. Everything, he insisted, was flowing. To prove this he would go around turning on faucets. It was from Hegel that he got the idea of «dialectic», which has been lucidly defined as «the triadic movement of thought from thesis 7 [pageend9]to antithesis to synthesis» (4). However, the idea of «dialectical materialism» is Marx’s very own. Those who have borrowed it have always been glad to give it back. Because of a disagreement with one of his professors, who refused to sit in the back row while Marx lectured to the class, Karl withdrew rather hastily from Berlin University. A week later he took his doctorate at the University of Jena (5). It must have been a busy week, during which he met all the requirements (6), such as drinking a certain number of liters of beer and waltzing with the wife of the Rektor. At any rate he was now entitled to be called Dr. Marx, though not encouraged to perform appendectomies. JOURNALISM AND JENNY After winning his degree, Marx went home long enough to have a violent quarrel with his mother over finances. She made the mistake of saying that he was a big boy now and should have his allowance reduced. Not as yet having coined the phrase, «to each according to his need», Marx stormed off to Bonn. In this very religious city, he had the brilliant idea of founding a Journal of Atheism, which, because of lack of competition, was certain of success. However, he was unable to convince others of his golden opportunity and gave up in disgust. (4) W. T. Jones, A History of Western Philosophy, p. 1016. Soon he was widely known for his articles defending the freedom of the press, writing them with his right hand while blue-penciling his reporters’ articles with his left. Finally the paper folded (it had been only slightly crumpled before), and Marx left for Paris. At the station to see him off were a dozen or so of his creditors, shaking their fists at him in what later came to be recognized as the Communist salute. Before leaving Cologne, Marx married Jenny von Westphalen, resolutely overlooking the fact that she came of a wealthy family and he was out of work. They had been engaged for seven years, which demonstrates how Marx sometimes quailed before a Great Decision. But now he was hungry. They had an idyllic honeymoon, during which Marx filled five large notebooks with extracts from his reading. Their relationship grew in intimacy as Jenny turned his pages and sharpened his pencils. In Paris, Marx wrote for a revolutionary German- language paper, Vorwärts (7), which was largely ignored by the good citizens of Paris, most of whom did not own a German-French dictionary. Every evening he plotted and schemed in smokefilled rooms, where he developed a dim view of the future. According to one biographer, «Marx’s journalistic pen was dipped in gall» (8). When one of Marx’s articles advocated murdering the King of Prussia, many thought this was going too far. Why not murder the King of France, who was close by? The king, Louis Philippe, grew uneasy at this sort of talk, closed down the paper, (7) Forward, a journal that was always in the van, at least while being delivered. (8) Someone used the wrong bottle when filling his inkwell. 9 [pageend11]and ordered Marx to leave Paris within twenty-four hours. Marx intended to settle somewhere outside the city limits, but left with such a burst of speed that his momentum carried him all the way to Belgium. PERSONAL APPEARENCE Marx soon became a familiar figure in Brussels. His large head, short legs, and square trunk made a lasting impression, especially on his mattress. Failing to gain average height (9), he had grown a bushy, untrimmed beard to hide his disappointment. During years of poverty, it kept his throat and chest warm and saved him the price of a scarf. Only his wife, who died without revealing his secret, knew whether he ever wore a tie or, indeed, had a neck (10). About this time Marx stopped going to a barber, being unable to sit there and listen while someone else talked. So his hair grew down over his ears and neck and mingled with his beard, setting a style for spies, revolutionaries, poets, and makers of cough drops. After all, Marx was a philosopher, and it was hard for him to think if he got tangled up with reality. It is true that he often got his hands grimy and black, but that was from changing his typewriter ribbon. REVOLUTIONARY IDEAS Increasingly Marx came to feel that the world must be changed and that he was the man for the job. Humanity must be freed from three besetting evils: (1) marriage, (2) religion, and (3) private property. Since Marx had no religion and no private property, he himself was in pretty good shape. As for marriage, it may be significant that most of those who subscribed to the Marx-Engels conviction that marriage must be done away with were married men (12). They came from all over Europe, equipped with expense accounts, noisemakers, and pocket flasks, each determined to be the life of the Party. Many wore lapel buttons with such slogans as «I Like Marx» and «Down With Everybody». Needing something to discuss, a nostrum for the rostrum, they asked Marx to prepare them a platform, suitably heavy and wooden. So it was that Marx wrote The Communist Manifesto. Its most memorable part is the opening sentence: «A specter is haunting Europe — the specter of Communism». Obviously Marx hoped by this transparent device to attract readers of ghost stories and make inroads on the Edgar Allan Poe market. The use of the term «Communist» in the Manifesto helped make the word popular, especially among Communists. Indeed the League of the Just, to which Marx belonged, changed its name to the Communist League, possibly because of jibes about «Just what»?From the closing sentences of the Manifesto the Communists devised their stirring motto: «Workers of the world, unite! You have nothing to lose but your chains!» Though in some instances their chains were heirlooms, handed down for generations, they were willing to make the sacrifice. In 1848, Marx was contributing to several newspapers as well as to the general unrest. Because of this he was given 24 hours to leave Brussels. The order to leave caught him napping. By the time he awoke, there was no chance to gather up such personal effects as his toothbrush, his washcloth, or his wife. He particularly regretted leaving behind his straight razor, whoch had been in the family for years and was a well-tempered blade. 13 [pageend15]After a brief sojourn in Paris he went on to Cologne, where he edited another radical paper and hatched plots. This latter was difficult for Marx, a nervous man who seldom sat long in one spot. However, he persevered for the good of the Cause, and was notably effective in making the bourgeoisie and the proletariat see the antagonism which had existed for years between them, unnoticed. But now Marx was given 23 hours to leave Germany. By this time he was adept at living out of a suitcase, and needed only to snap the latches. Back to Paris he went. After only one month he was asked to leave in 22 hours. They were tightening op on him. His picture appeared in post offices and government buildings, marked Unwanted. A philosopher by trade (16), he took this philosophically. Besides, it gave him a new Communist slogan: Join the Party and See the World. Marx had only two places left to go — England or prison. Through an anguished night he was tom by Indecision and tormented by Doubt. At the last minute (17) he decided on England, fearful that the dampness of the Paris dungeon might aggravate his rheumatism. Apparently no one had told him about English weather. Instead of reassuring his family with «Wait till my ship comes in», he would say, with confidence, «Come the revolution...» In London Marx spent the second half of his life attending meetings of secret societies. Some of these were so secret that he was never told their names or where they met, and therefore often arrived late. Most of the members put on false beards to disguise themselves. Since Marx’s beard was the real thing, disguise posed a problem. He solved it by parting his beard in the middle and slicking down his hair. At this time he was also the brains behind a new organization, the International Working Men’s Association, although certain rules about being a worker had to be waived before Marx himself could become a member. Marx went from place to place lecturing on economics. He had to keep moving, because he was being closely watched by the authorities, especially the authorities on economics. During his university career he had majored in law and philosophy, but he had once overheard a conversation about the law of supply and demand and felt qualified to lecture on it. It must have been a relief to Marx that his audiences were mostly made up of working men who knew nothing whatever about the subject, being too busy earning a living to read up on how they were doing it. Marx, having no regular work, had ample time to read the great economists like John Stuart Mill and Adam Smith, most of whom were similarly unemployed (19). There was a close feeling in the Marx family, for Karl and Jenny, along with their six children and a nurse, lived in two small rooms. The place had a cozy, lived-in look. Marx dearly loved to tell his children bedtime stories. While Jenny was getting their beds ready by removing the paper and inkwells, Marx, with three children on each knee, entertained them with such stories as «The Bourgeois Cat and the Proletarian Mouse», «Snow White and the Seven Trade Unionists», and their favorite, «Little Red Riding Hood». How they loved to hear him describe Little Red Riding Hood, a poor working girl, and her grandmother, a downtrodden peasant woman. How they screamed with fright when he got down on his hands and knees and acted out the part of the capitalist wolf, trying to exploit Little Red and Granny. But the story always ended happily. The Revolution came, in the nick of time, and the wolf went down in a hail of hammers and sickles. It was great fun, though the children were sometimes so excited that they could not go to sleep for hours, and Marx himself had to walk the London streets to get a grip on himself. FOREIGN CORRESPONDENT In the 1850s Marx contributed to the New York Tribune, as Republicans would like to forget. The editor was the famous Horace Greeley. His advice, «Go west, young man», was completely lost on Marx, who never ventured beyond London’s West End. Marx specialized in writing vividly about wars and revolutions, though he was unable to see anything of 16 [pageend18]them unless they took place in the street in front of the British Museum. It was there that he wrote his masterful eyewitness accounts of the Turko-Russian War. His dispatches (20) invariably got to New York before those written by impractical reporters in the Near East, who had a penchant for realism. Marx was paid five dollars an article by the Tribune. He considered himself underpaid, and this increased his annoyance with capitalism. Since he would have starved to death without this income, the Tribune might have saved the world from Communism by either paying Marv nothing or paying him more. POVERTY AND DISAPPOINTMENT In his later years Marx was very poor. But for the money from the Tribune and gifts from Engels, who had reluctantly inherited a cotton mill, he would have been unable to buy paper and pencil (21). The Marx- Engels correspondence is full of long, involved letters by Marx concerning economic philosophy, usually centering on such basic questions as «Where is my money?» Marx’s exploitation of Engels is an interesting reversal of Marxist theory. In this instance a proletarian exploited a bourgeois. Always Marx was waiting for the capitalistic world to collapse. Each morning he would throw off the covers and leap from bed cryin, «Workers of the world, arise!» Then he would run to get the newspaper, goose- pimplv with anticipation, hoping for a headline such as «Western World Collapses» or «Bubonic Plague Carries Off Millions of Capitalists». (20) Which, as is sometimes forgotten, were bitterly anti- Russian. (21) He had long since given up food and drink. 17 [pageend19]Unfortunately nothing horrible happened, no catastrophe occurred. Indeed the only really gloomy articles he found that cheered him up were those he had written himself. Marx’s fondest dream was of a great war with long casualty lists made up of bourgeoisie who had stupidly enlisted to protect their property. The proletariat, having no such motive, would of course not enlist, and the intellectuals, like Marx, would have bad eyesight from excessive reading and be exempt from the draft. But war stubbornly refused to break out, and Marx grew terribly despondent. aGott im Himmel», he would exclaim, unembarrassed at having invoked a nonexistent Deity, «when will this furchtbar peace come to an end?» Thus it was that he took great joy in the outbreak of the Franco-Prussian War in 1870. As its bloody battles raged, taking a heavy toll of capitalists and anyone else who got in the way of a bullet, Marx became the happiest man alive. But the war came to an early end. alas, with the defeat of Napoleon III at the Battle of Sedan, and Marx was again plunged into gloom. DAS KAPITAL All this time Marx was writing his magnum opus. This was Das Kapital, the first volume of which appeared in 1867, when Marx was almost fifty and afflicted with neuralgia, pleurisy, and middle age. It is not, as many suppose, a treatise on punctuation. Nor is it a guidebook to the historic sights of Berlin. Engels called it «the Bible of the working classes», perhaps because it was the Genesis of Communism and (he hoped) the Exodus of Capitalism. In this famous work Marx propounded his doctrine of class struggle and his «theory of change». This latter 18 [pageend20]was his belief that, over the centuries, the bourgeoisie had slyly made change in such a way as to impoverish the gullible proletariat. Even more revolutionary was his «theory of surplus value», whereby he declared that all capitalists are surplus and of value only to themselves. In Das Kapital Marx discusses money at great length. Never having had much of it, this gave him a certain vicarious pleasure (22). Marx did not live to complete Das Kapital, nor have many people lived to finish reading it. However, it was continued by his friend Engels, who made use of Marx’s papers, both daily and Sunday, and faithfully carried out his master’s wishes (23). After the work got up to three thick volumes. Engels died, having overtaxed his heart by lifting all three at once (24). The world awaits the eight-page condensation of Das Kapital, to be called, Daskap, which The Reader’s Digest has long had under way. He had no fangs, claws, talons, tusks, or antlers. His only weapon was a pair of spurs which he used for courting purposes only. He was a well-rounded little chap. His young came forth in eggs, like those of Birds. His habits were aquatic, like Fish. And he was a smallish, warm-blooded Mammal like you and me. For the most part he was silent. But every so often, in the voting season, his voice was heard, proving that when given a chance, he was capable of saying Yes or No. All Platypus wanted on this earth was a little home, a little wife, a couple of eggs, a possible dream and an impossible one, and a Sunday picnic in the park with maybe a portable radio at his side. But all too often, some big animal, the king that can see in the dark and always knows which way the wind is blow67 [pageend69]ing, would come and poke his big face into Platypus’s burrow, urging him to love all Kukaburra Birds and hate all Calico Bass. A little later the same beast would turn up again, telling him that while the Calico Bass, as a subspecies, were much misunderstood, the only good Kakaburra Bird was a dead one ; and the same went for Wombats, Muskrats, Mud Puppies, and Polecats. For a long while afterward, the peaceful nights on the river would be disturbed by the sound of screeching, whining, caterwauling, ululating, and death-rattling. When it was all over and Platypus came to count his eggs, he usually found that a few had been stepped on. Platypus hated all this squabbling. He didn’t see any good reason why Birds, Fish, and Mammals shouldn’t hit it off together. As an egg-laying Mammal with a fishy habitat, he was a mishmash of all of them, and he felt fit in body and soul. He knew in his bones that all any creature on earth needed to be happy was a little home, a little wife, a couple of kids, a possible dream and an impossible one, and a Sunday picnic in the park with maybe a portable radio at his side. Yet the banks of his pleasant river were constantly awash with trouble. It did not seem to be the fault of the Platypuses or of their furred, feathered, or flippered friends. The reason must lie in the world beyond, and Platypus resolved that he would go in search of it. He tenderly kissed his wife and eggs good-by, and set off. What strange lands he traveled through, what wonders met his sight, what ditches he fell into ! In some countries everybody barked; in others they mooed and cooed. In the land of the Finches the inches were shorter, in the land of the Pinchers they used longer inches. In one country you paid with shells, in another with pebbles, and in the next you had to change the pebbles into pumpkin seeds. Whenever he crossed a border, a pack of Long-Nosed Monkeys closed in on him and asked him if he was planning 68 [pageend70]to overthrow the government. Then they would turn him upside down and shake him back and forth, explaining that they were only searching his wrinkles and crinkles for contraband periwinkles. Platypus had never imagined that the world was in such a snafu. With a big hole in his pocket, and a martyr to dizzy spells, Platypus finally arrived at the edge of the Political Jungle, a black, blind tangle where light seldom shines, and giant trees wring their twisted branches; where narrow footpaths wind round and end in the middle of themselves like question marks, or worse still, never end, and are beset on every side by quicksands, quagmires, and awful messes where, if you linger long, you may easily catch the collywobbles. As Platypus came to a full stop at the edge of this forest, he could hear from the depths of it a low clatter of bickering and snattering, pierced now and then by a thin scream as of some small animal getting a raw deal. And I, sir, am Max Fox. » « —and I was wondering if you could show me a safe, simple way to the heart of this jungle », Platypus said. The Fox lifted one eyebrow only. The other was incapable of surprise. At a glance he sized Platypus up as one of those innocents from abroad who didn’t know the capital of Ghana, who couldn’t tell a missile from an antimissile missile, and who was continually getting lost in long corridors which ended up in doors marked No Admittance. The awful truth was, he was a cynic, and like all cynics he was lonely. For years he had been sitting next to that tree stump playing Patience, and waiting for an honest man to come along and stand still while he bent his ear. No cynic is cynical enough to shut up. Now Heaven had sent Platypus, and the jubilant Fox picked him up and kissed him on both cheeks, French style, so that Platypus felt as if he were being decorated. All the same, it was too bad that Platypus, having come so far from home with a serious purpose in mind, had to fall into the clutches of this gabby Fox ! So jagte nun ein Einfall den anderen auf zwölf ausgiebigen Manuskriptseiten, jedoch ohne Erfolg. Das « Illustrierte Wunderhorn » erlaubte sich, mir die noch nie dagewesenen Mordfälle zurückzusenden. Andere an meiner Stelle hätten nun die Sache längst aufgegeben und beim Fernsehen, wo man Zeit hat und keine Einfälle braucht, einer Anstellung als Redakteur nachgejagt, ich tat ähnliches nicht. Ich versuchte es statt dessen mit den Naturwissenschaften. Mein Originalgespräch mit einem Marsmenschen am Wannsee wurde im Kreise meiner Bekannten ein ausgesprochener Erfolg. Als der Mann, eingebettet in tausend technische Einzelheiten, davon berichtete, dass auf dem Mars und anderen benachbarten Planeten ein Schwimmeister, Masseur oder gar Zeitungskioskinhaber auch ohne Parteibuch Karriere machen oder in einen sozialen Wohnungsbau eingewiesen werden kann, wälzten sich meine Freunde auf dem Teppich und prophezeiten die unweigerliche Annahme meiner lebensnahen und schulbeispielhaften Reportage. 145 [pageend147]Weit gefehlt. Auch jene Arbeit kam zurück und ich musste sogar Strafporto zahlen, weil Ihre Damen zu wenig Briefmarken zur Hand hatten. So ging es nun wochenlang, monatelang, jahrelang, Herr Dr. Pufferbohne, ich hatte nun einmal bei Ihnen mit nichts Erfolg. Die Redaktion des « Illustrierten Wunderhorns » war wie ein Automat. Oben warf ich meine Manuskripte hinein, unten kamen sie wieder heraus. Seit sieben Jahren, ohne Panne, ohne Störung und mit unvorstellbarer Zuverlässigkeit. Bis ich mir dann die Sache überlegte und hier haben Sie das Resultat : Ich habe Ihnen, sehr geehrter Herr, in sieben Jahren, abgerechnet die Wochen, in denen ich mit Grippe ausser Dienst war, insgesamt 350 Manuskripte eingesandt. Jedes Manuskript bestand durchschnittlich aus 10 Seiten. Ich habe demnach im Laufe der sieben Jahre, für meine unerbetene Mitarbeit für Ihr « Illustriertes Wunderhorn », DM 238.— ausgegeben. Da ich durch erwähnte und unerbetene Betätigung für das « Illustrierte Wunderhorn » dem Sekretariat nur überflüssige Lesetätigkeit und ebenfalls überflüssige Portospesen verursacht habe, von den Energien, die das Absagebriefschreiben erfordert, ganz zu schweigen, biete ich Ihnen, sehr geehrter Herr Dr. Pufferbohne, einen Vergleich an. Da ich eingesehen habe, dass ich niemals die hohen Ansprüche, das Niveau Ihres Geschätzten erreichen kann, resigniere ich hiermit und bitte noch nachträglich um Vergebung.
