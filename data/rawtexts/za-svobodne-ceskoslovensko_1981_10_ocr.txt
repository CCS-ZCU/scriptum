Jan Němec One of the most controversial director: to emerge from the Czech New Wave, Jan Nemec is the director of three feature films, and one episode of the compilation film, Pearls of the Deep. Born in 1936, graduated from FAMU in 1960, he directed several short films, including A Memory of Our Day, Life after Ninety Minutes and Mother and Son. His graduation film, A Loaf of Bread, established Nemec as a prober of the psychology of oppression and persecution, and the talented heir to a visual style with strong camera dynamics, and the successful intercutting of past, present and future realities and dreams. Like A Loaf of Bread, Nemec's first feature, Diamonds in the Night is based on a story by Arnošt Lustig, and is the harrowing account of the flight, of the fears and nightmares of two young refugees from a concentration camp transport train. A Report on the Party and the Guests was directed in 1965; Martyrs of Love, three surreal, comic dream stories about the unfulfilled hopes of deposed heroes, in 1966. Because of the political difficulties surrounding his films, particularly A Report on the Party and the Guests, Nemec has not worked much since 1966; only recently he was allowed to film in Germany where he made a yet uncompleted short. Banned on its release, briefly re- released in 1968, banned again after the Soviet invasion, and recently banned from public screening in Czechoslovakia “forever”, A Report On The Parly And The Guests remains one of the most potent allegories of totalitarianism, and a powerful statement on the individual’s acceptance of dictatorship, compromise, and the acceptance of violence. The situation of the film is idyllically simple—a group of people on a picnic are accosted by a group o! strangers and led to a nonsensical, but very formal and elegant banquet outdoors. From this point, Nemec almost documents the process of self- deception and rationalization which leads to an acceptance of constraint; free will and freedom in the film are seen as a thread easily discarded and difficult to maintain. The doubleentendre of the film is carried an additional step further by the ending: one of the guests (played by the film director Evald Schorm) escapes; his compatriots agree he must be recaptured, and the film concludes with the nightmarish barking of search dogs on the soundtrack.
