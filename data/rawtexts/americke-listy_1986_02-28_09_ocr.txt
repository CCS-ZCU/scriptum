ÚNORA 1986 In declaring Pvbruary, 1986 as American Heart Month, both the Congress and the President have recognized the need to continue the battle against the leading cause of death in this nation: heart disease. According to statistics from the American Heart Association and the National Institutes of Health, more than 63 million Americans have one or more forms of cardiovascular disease. Almost as many people will die from cardiovascular disease in 1936 as from cancer, accidents and all other causes of death combined. In addition, the total economic cost of cardiovascular disease in this year will reach $80 billion in direct medical expenses and indirect costs such as insurnace, disability claims, lost salaries and rehiring and training. As a member of the health appropriations subcommittee in the House for the past five years, I have had an opportunity to examine closely the research efforts of the National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute and hear expert testimony from leading members of the research community on the advances that have been made in this important area. The scope and magnitude of cardiovascular disease demands tnat further progress in research, prevention and treatment be made. Unfortunately, the Gramm-Rudman budged amendment, which I did not support, will reduce total funding for the National Institutes of Health by $230 million, and the impact of Gramm-Rudman in 1987 could be even more drastic. The potential for further impact on the decline in death rates due to heart disease and stroke is evident as new and exciting research pathways emerge. Some examples include: the application of advances in cellular and molecular biology io the study of atherosclerosis and hypertension: research in the relationship of nutrition to cardiovascular disease. The above-mentioned research areas are only a few of the many being pursued by the National Institutes of Health and the private sector, and it is vital that this momentum be sustained so that the incidence of all forms of cardiovascular disease will continue to decline in the coming years.
