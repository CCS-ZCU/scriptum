We clearly recognize and utterly repudiate the Godless philosophy of mate rialistic communism as the most ruthless conspiracy against free humanity, as the political system which threatens all mankind with utter enslavement. Therefore, we do not subscribe to the policy that doing business with and giving material and moral aid to the Soviet Union and the Moscow-installed regimes of its satellites is in the best interests of the United States and the rest of the free world. We strongly denounce the intervention of the Soviet Union in the internal affaires of other countries and, therefore, we respectfully request our Gov ernment to insist on the withdrawal of all Soviet forces from non-Soviet territories. We commend our Government for every effort expended to resist and resolutely counteract the aggressive policies of Soviet imperialism with all the spiritual and material forces at their command, and we heartily endorse every action of our Government to expose, demobilize, and outlaw the evil forces of brutal communism in our Country, the forces which are dedicated tóóui^destruction. 4 We fully endorse the principle that our Government shall refuse recognition to every political formation and form of government which is imposed on any nation by the force of an- alien power and, therefore, we respectfully request our Government to act in accordance with this principle even in the case of the Slovak nation from which we are descended and which today is tyrannized by the Czecho- Communist regime of Prague in flagrant violation of the political will of the Slovak people and of the fundamental principles of the Charter of the United Nations and in utter contempt for the basic principle of self-determination of our American foreign policy. The simple truth — recognized by Slovaks and Czechs throughout the centuries but still not recognized in any practical way by the Government of the United States — is that Slovaks are not Czechs, and the Czechs are not Slovaks. The historical fact is that the Slovaks and the Czechs never had a common history, culture and tradition, nor a common homeland since their advent to Central Europe. There never was a Czecho-Slovakia before 1918—and there never would have been one without the intervention of America. The Slovaks did go along with the Czechs on the projwsition of forming a joint political stare during World War I, but not unconditionally. The proposition was conditioned by the Pittsburgh A- greement of May 30, 1918, which was drafted and signed by Thomas G. Masaryk, the first president of the newly-born Czecho-Slovakia. This agreement guaranteed full autonomy or state's rights to Slovakia. Without this guarantee, Masaryk never would have gained the Slovaks for his Czecho-Slovak cause. ■ Masaryk, however, soon broke his pledge to the Slovaks, repudiating the Pittsburgh Agreement and 'referring to it as a “scrap of paper.” He openly denied the existence of the Slovak nation as an ethnic entity, declaring that it was but a “branch” of the Czech nation, thus planting the seeds of dissention and doom for the vaunted “isle of democracy” even before it became a going concern.
