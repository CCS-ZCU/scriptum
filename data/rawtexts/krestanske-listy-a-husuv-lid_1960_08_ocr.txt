Józa Novotný The Original Languages Of The Bible When I was a student in the University of Nottingham in England, I made a trip to Cardiff where I met a coal merchant. Mr. Deacon. The important thing about this man was not the fact that he was selling coal but that he was the best layman in any church I ever saw. He resolved rightly that when people out of sheer interest become experts in botany, mineralogy. ornilology etc., he might just as well be an expert in his interested field—the Bible. He was especially impressed by prophet Amos. In order to understand him well he mastered the original language of the Old Testament. Hebrew, and finally wrote a learned book on Amos. The following lines want to persuade our laymen, especially the younger ones, to give their free time not only to sport and entertainment but to the original languages of the Bible. Many a layman must surely doubt sometimes the wisdom of the theological schools when they include into their cirriculum the oiginal languages of the Old and New Testament. But even not all the ministers later in practical life arc quite sure if the time spent in mastering these languages was quite well used. For most of them, after a decade or so in pastorates, forget the Hebrew alphabet and only few can read Greek fluently. The question is why, outside of those very few who chose Hebrew or Greek as their life's occupation, the rest practically turn the back on those two languages to say nothing about related Semitic languages. It's no use to close the eyes before the fact that very many teachers of these languages fail to arouse the interest of their pupils. Most of the members of the Hebrew and Greek classes take with them into life memories of extremely dry and most uninteresting time. And even in the pulpits whenever Hebrew or Greek goes astray into the sermons of the ministers. most of the listeners arc rightly under the impression that it was mentioned by a highbrow who had the intention in the bottom of [pageend2]KRESŤANSKÉ LISTY A HUSOV L1D | |b his soul to impress the congregation what a learned minister they have. — The cynics remark that the learned translators of the Bible who arc severely criticized by the preacher are not present anyhow. The purpose of the Hebrew or Greek quotation is scarcely understood by any of the listeners. Yet there are real treasures in the original languages both of the Old and New Testament which may be a source of real blessing even for the most humble and uncultured member of the congregation. From the Old Testament may stand here one word which is very often used both in the Old Testament as well as in today's sermons and discussions of the hearers. It is the English word “prophet’'. No word has been more misinterpreted and therefore misunderstood. The average preacher and his listener when they use this word have formed in their minds the idea of an unpractical dreamer whose interest is turned away both from the foggy past and the unfavorable present into the hopeful future. And that this is the proper interpretation the English dictionary will only confirm. For to prophecy means for the average English speaking person to predict the future. But our age somehow lost interest in the past and all the more in the future. If it is difficult to say something absolutely sure about what really happened, it is all the more difficult to say something reasonably sure about the future. People are rightly interested in the present. And w'ho would doubt that even from the spiritual standpoint the emphasis on the present has a real value? The Germans emphasize “Dies- seitsreligion” in contrast to “Jenseitsreligion”. Now w'hen we turn to the original language of the Old Testament for light, to our great surprise w'e shall find out that the Hebrew equivalent for our English word “prophet” docs not indicate at all predicting future, o: at least philologically the term does not emphasize it. It emphasizes an action which is of great missionary value and which will surely be appreciated by our modern mind. The Hebrew substantive nabi is derived and inncrly related with the verb naba, which means to overflow, to allow to boil, to allow to outspring. Even the Latin equivalent assures ■‘lacrimatus est.” In the related Assyrian “nabu ’ means to call, to cry. Wilhelm Gesenius quotes Hoffmann who thus describes the prophet “one w'ho forces out to the foreground his message loudly and excitingly under deep breathings.” Now after such an excursion into the cthy- mology of the word what a new contents will this worn out expression receive! You can safely take this philology w'ith you into the pulpit before the most simple minded congregation. Who was the prophet? A man who was so full of his message that he overflows with it, a man whose heart “runneth over” with his mission, a person whose soul is boiling with enthusiasm for his philosophy of life. Now' even the most modern minded hearer of ours will apreciate such a grammatical hint. This kind of prophetic spirit is surely needed today in our churches. Ministers of prophetic type with this new interpretation are needed. And as we believe in every member priesthood, a prophetic membership is needed. A whole sermon easily could grow out of purely grammatical study of the text. Our trouble today is that we are too indifferent, too cold. We are not sure of God and His kingdom. We lack the prophetic overboiling. Our cup is scarcely filled. to say nothing about “running over.” The same is true w'hen we turn to the Greek New Testament. We are all in favor of the classical translations of the Bible, such as the King James’ Version in English or Luther’s translation in German represent. We gladly forgive here and there a little incorrectness especially if we keep in mind the insufficient Greek text which they had to use. The hymnical form, the festive style and the holiday like sentences captivate us fully that we hesitate to exchange it for a more correct modern translation. The charm of the tw'enty-third Psalm, the beauti- t tides, of the Lord's prayer force us to keep them in the pulpit. We hesitate to replace them for a more modern, not seldom street language. But there is one correction of the King lames Version which we ought to undertake. It has to do with the Lord’s prayer. “As we forgive” is the proper translation of the Greek “hemeis afiemen. But our present Greek text which prepared for us Nestle as an outcome of the work of Tis- chendorf, Bernard Weiss and Westcott and Hort, uses a reading which has much older MSS as their authority and which the [pageend3]I 16 KRESTANSKE L1STY A HUSVV LID King James’ Version translators did not know as they were not discovered as yet by that time. “As we have forgiven” is the proper translation of “hemcis afekamcn". It is more correct and besides that of more significance. Here it is not only recommended to sacrifice poetical beauty for correctness of the original text, but the ethical application of such a translation is of very great importance. How weak in consequences is the present indicative active “as we forgive!” It may mean a cheap promise of the praying person, which, however, may not be realized at all! And how on the other hand ethically binding is the past tense “as we have forgiven!” It takes for granted that the praying one has forgiven before falling on his knees. What a strong message for the pastoral work! Nobody has the right to clasp hands for prayer before he has forgiven. Not only an effective sermon might be preached on this new text, but how many differences between quarreling members might be removed when using this reading. It is significant that this text is surely in harmony with the teachings of Jesus, especially when we keep in mind Matt. 5: 23-25, Few examples from Paul may be interesting. The King James' Version gives this translation for Eph. 2,10: “Wc are God’s workmanship.” When we, however, turn to the original text we find “auto gar esmen poema.” Wc could translate it in this way: “We are God's poem.” God is the poet. The poet in Greek is the maker. In old English they used the word “maker” for poet, it is a better translation, to say nothing about the beauty of it. God is the Divine Maker. He made us, therefore we are his poem. And what a poem! Of course this leads us to the first pages of the Bible. What does modern science teach about man? We are ninety-six elements plus x. Now what does the first pages of the Bible say about us? We are made by the divine Maker out of dust (96 elements) and He breathed into us part of himself (x). Therefore we arc His poem. In Romans 5,2 Paul says: “Through whom wc have access “pro- sagoge” by faith into his grace.” The word access in Greek has fourfold meaning in various places of Greek literature. And ail four could be applied to Christ and even form four parts of a sermon. These four meanings are as follows 1 (entrance to a harbour in one instance it is used for a ship in stormy sea seeking the entrance into a harbour; 2. (landing stage) the people on the ship want not only to be in the harbour but on the land itself; 3. (great resources: in addition to the fact that the ship as well as the people are in harbour and on land, they want to explore the land for treasures; 4. (introduction) a spiritual visit card to be allowed into the presence of God through Christ. There is one word in Greek for which no modern language has equivalent. It is “synkakopatheson.” It is a combination of three words—with, trouble, endurance. It is used in 2. Timothy 2,3. King James’ Version says: “Thou therefore endure hardness as a good soldier of Jesus Christ.” Perhaps it would be better translated as follows: “Take your share of trouble with endurance.” Paul speaks about the sufferings of Christ and says that He did most of it. Wc therefore ought to take our share. Paul likes the word “syn” (with). He suggests here our American emphasis of sharing. 1 have been crucified with Christ, conformed with His death, buried with Christ, risen with Christ. God made us alive with Christ. “Joint heirs with Christ, if so be that we suffer with Him. that wc also may be glorified together.” Even in evangelistic meetings it is of importance that the New Testament conception of repentance is not an easy “being sorry” but “to change your mind” as the original ‘metanene' indicates. But there are Greek words which might even supply material for a sermon with many parts. To preach the Gospel has a broad meaning in the English language. The Greek New Testament however distinguishes many subtle nuances in this sphere which the translators were not always able to interpret fully. Our modern languages arc poor in this respect. Here could the preacher safely have each Greek term at the head of each division. It is derived from the substantive “ho keryx”, herald. To be a herald with public authority. In New Testament to be God's ambassador and herald or proclaimer of the divine word. To officiate as herald, to proclaim after the manner of a herald. John the Baptist is the leader of this chosen group. This mission was very important for he had to prepare way for the one who came in history of mankind only once. And therefore there were not many heralds. Not everybody can be a herald. But even the next group Is reserved for a few only. To be able to preach the Gospel in the way as the Greek verb “parakaleo” indicates is not given to everybody. It is used about the storm like preaching of Peter, or about the allegoric method of preaching of the orator Apollos. In modern time it might be used of Philip Brooks or Spurgeon. But there is a term in the New Testament Greek which may include everybody and thus help to realize the ideal of general priesthood of a New Testament church. To tell to our neighbors about our religious experiences as the Greek verb “laleo” indicates, surely that everybody can do. It is used about the Samaritan woman who spoke about her experience with Jesus. And finally even the Bible class method of a discussion, asking and answering questions, has its place in the Greek New Testament. The New Testament has for this action the Greek verb ‘dialego'. Even such people who are not able to make a speech, can join in such an action. To know the original languages of the Bible brings inward satisfaction to the person who will acquire them. There is an indiscribable charm and happiness to be able to read the Bible in the original for anybody. But outside of this spiritual enriching of our believers’ soul, there are real and helpful items for everyday use. Bylo to uvnitř skvěle restaurované Betlémské kaple v Praze, kde Mistr Jan míval svá proslulá kázání od r. 1402 až do svého vypuzení z Prahy. Zajisté, že není nic případnějšího, než pozastaviti se nad hmotnými zbytky zdí původní svatyně, jež fanatická protireformace žárlivě zazdila do hladových zdí činžáků soukromého vlastnictví fcudalizující společnosti a tak chtěla odstranit) a vymítiti drahou památku všech věrných dědiců Husových. Masarykova republika dvacet let měla na programu vysvo- boditi tuto archeologickou relikvii z kamenného zajetí, ale byla příliš zaujata odra- koušťováním a rovnáním páteří živých lidí, zakřivených staletým poddanstvím. Za to však nebylo nic snadnějšího pro dnešní komunistický režim než vyvlastniti soukromý majetek, který věznil hmotný, archeologický poklad českého národa — Husovu Betlémskou kapli. Pro lidovou demokracii, která se až dosud zabývala výhradně jen pokrokem a budoucností, je to jistě nečekaný reakční krok — hrabání v minulosti. V každém případě obnovení Husovy kaple bylo politickým aktem, který měl patrně vyrovnávat morální otřes způsobený nedalekou, provokativně a nepoměrně titánskou mohylou kamenných bloků Stalinova pomníku nad letenskou strání. I katoličtí Hapsburkové dovolili postavení Husova pomníku na Staroměstském náměstí vedle ponižujícího sloupu bělohorské Panny Marie Vítězné. Předmětem naší úvahy při 545. výročí Husovy mučednické smrti je Husova pravda. Dějiny nám opětovně dokázaly, že světcova pravda není jen přechodným příběhem nýbrž zjevem, který září do všech věků. Známe proslulá Husova slova o pravdě, než abychom je tu znova opakovali. V jiném směru rozd'lu však není a mnohdy jsme na tom v naší přeorganizované a ztech- nologizované civilizaci i hůře a to jak kulturně, tak i křesťansky neboť nemůžeme nálezti omluvy pro naše zvrhlé koncentrační tábory ať t. zv. pravých nebo levých poli- tiských autokratů a tyranů. Za našich let tam zahynulo statisíce lidských obětí v agónii, jež byla mnohem zdlouhavější než byla Husova v plamenech hranice. V jeho světě proti svému očekávání, Hus, jinak věrný syn církve, ocitl se v nesmiřitelném boji s úpadkovými, nemorálními církevními preláty, spojenými s feudálními prisluhovači kořistnické společenské soustavy Husovy doby. To vedlo k individualitě v životě pravdy a to nejen před světem ale především před vlastním svědomím, před Bohem. kde nelze ukrýti žádných osobních tajemství. To ovšem usměrnilo Mistra Jana nejen k horlivému studiu Bible, ale vykrystalizovalo jeho individualismus, jaký v dnešním totalitním státu, zvláště dialekticky materialistického zaměření, by jej nejkratší cestou přivedl do neblahého kádrového záznamu a konečné likvidace obviněním z proradky. Husův duchovní individualismus, byť třeba byl nesobeckým všelidstvím, nenašel by místa v novofeudádním totalitním státu odlidštčného kolektiva stejně jako byl z něho vyloučen ve své době pokrytecky přecírkcvněné společnosti. Husova pravda, jeho učení a ctnostný křesťanský život, sám sebou se jasněji vykreslí jakmile jej vystavíme do světla dnešního oficielního výkladu husitství a husitského hnutí v Čechách. Tento výklad byl vydán pražským Orbisem v roce 1958 autorem Josefem Mackem. Hus jako pokorný a smířlivý křesťan, ne- cdkázal žádná válečná hesla, nedal pokyn k fanatickým rozbrojům, jeho dopisy Čechům z porýnského zajetí do rodné vlasti jsou o- brazem lásky a odpuštění.
