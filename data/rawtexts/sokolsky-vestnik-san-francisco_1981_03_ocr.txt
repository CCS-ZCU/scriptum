His name is Thomas Garrigue Masaryk, the first president of Czechoslovakia on whom the government bestowed the proud title: "President Liberator". Much was said about him by leading politicians and public figures and we were left with what passing time didn’t dim. I would like to share with you what I recently read. He is the synonym of its acknowledgement when he "crowns" nimself with the renovation and foundation of his modern independent state. For me, Masaryk left the following: masaryk - the head of the state, achiever of national political evolution by liberation. Masaryk - the democrat, philosopher of democracy and gigantic formulator of democratic relations in political life, international as well as national and also in reciprocity of people among themselves. Masaryk - the teacher of cultural and political morality, ethical personality who ensured the primacy of ethics over politics. Masaryk — builder of the national character. Major Harold Gibson, of the British Intelligence Service known as MI-6, resident in Prague, Czechoslovakia, closely collaborated with the Czechoslovak Army Intelligence, and through this channel he received and passed on to London shortly before the outbreak of the World War II the following: the Polish Intelligence was interested and worked for some time on recent German discovery of a cipher machine called ENIGMA - The department B-S-4, the cryptographic section of the Polish General Staff had legally acquired the commercial version of Enigma and cryptanalysts led by two Polish mathematicians, M. Rejewski and H. Zygalski had managed to resolve some of the mathematical problems involved in deciphering its transmissions. The British crypanalytical service knew the proficiency of their Polish counterparts whose ability to read Russian ciphers had resulted in a victory for the Poles in the Russian-Polish war in 1920, a war, that checked the first overt communist westward thrust after the World War I. In June 193tí Col. Btewart Menzies - the deputy chief of MI-6 and chief of the German section of Military Intelligence at the War Office - had received a message that would prove to be the most important in the intelligence history of the World War II. It came again from Gibson in Prague who reported after his return from Warsaw that he encountered there a Polish Jew who had offered to sell MI—6 his knowledge of nnigma for a price of^ 10.000, a British passport and residential permit in France for himself and his wife. Věstník Sokola San Francisco Březen - March 1981 MI-b sent the then the most outstanding brains in cryptanalysis to check out this Bole, known under an assumed name of Richard Lewinski. One was Alfred Knox, the leading cryptanalyst. His companion was Alan Turing, an outstanding mathematical logician. After they checked Lewinski’s knowledge and capabilities they recommended to Menzies that this bargain be accepted. Lewinski and his wife were taken through Gdynia and Stockholm to Faris, where he started working on Enigma in an apartment on the Left Bank and the machine he created was a joy of imitative engineering. It was about 24" square and 18" high, enclosed in a wooden box. It was connected to two electric typewriters and to transform a plain-language signal into a cipher text all the operator had to do was consult the book of keys, select the key for the time of the day, the day of the month and the month of the quarter, plug in accordingly and type the signal /message/ out on the left-hand typewriter. Electrical impulses entered the complex wiring of each of the rotors of the machine, the message was enciphered and then transmitted to the right-hand typewriter. When the enciphered text reached its destination, an operator set the keys of a similar apparatus according to an advisory contained in the message, typed the enciphered signal out on the left-hand typewriter and the right-hand one duly delivered the plain text. By merely changing the key procedure, Enigma could produce an almost infinite number of different cipher alphabets. Hitler evidently trusted Enigma completely as it was used in Wehrmacht’s all channels of command down to the smallest ship, because even the capture of Enigma was not sufficient to enable the enemy to read the encoded traffic. Only knowledge of the keying system and procedures would permit that. And even if these fell into the enemy’s hands, it would be a simple matter to change them. Therefore, the only way to penetrate the secrets of Enigma was to make another machine that could imitate or interpret the performance of each of the thousands of Enigmas that would come to exist in the Wehrmacht. This machine would also have to extrapolate the constant changes of keying procedures that every major German command ordered every day and every night, year in, year out and it would have to be capable of making an almost infinite number of mathematical calculations at speeds far beyond human ability. [pageend4]Březen - March iGol Věstník Bokola San Francisco Strana - Page 5. Such a machine existed only in theory in the mind of mathematician Turing. But the British Tabulating Machine Co. with its chief engineer Harold Keen and a team of twelve men under the guidance of Turing finally produced in complete secrecy the machine nicknamed “The Bomb". It was a cabinet some eight feet tall and close to eight feet wide at the base, shaped like an oldfashioned keyhole. And inside was a piece of engineering which defied description. What it did was to match the electrical circuits of Enigma, Its secret was in the internal wiring of Enigma’s rotors, which "The Bomb" sought to imitate. After several improvements and modifications "The Bomb" which was installed in an ordinary Bissen hut /out of corrugated steel/ in Bletchley Park began to penetrate Enigma at about the time the Germans prepared to attack Poland. "The Bomb", now known as "Ultra" was operational and it was a machine that provided the most valuable intelligence material of the World War II, without which the outcome of the war would have been certainly different and the Allied losses catastrophical. The brains and dedication of those few mentioned in this article saved perhaps millions of lives, assured victories which could otherwise be won by Germans. Most of all, information gathered this way gave to the Allied command an exact picture of the enemy’s intentions and plans and this enabled them to make countermoves and saving lives. Those of you old enough surely remember that deep-sounding voice heard now and then during the World War II over the BBC London. It belonged to Winston S. Churchill and it literally filled us with certainty that the war will be won by Allies. He knew what nobody else - but those directly involved in "Ultra" - knew which gave him the ground for confidence he radiated. But none of those brilliant men mentioned in this article got a recognition they fully deserved. We, who took part in the W.W.II. and who, through reading and research gained this information have deepest respect for these unrecognized heroes, without whom, we, quite possibly wouldn’t be here. This is my little forget-me-not I humbly lay to the monument the mankind chose not to erect for them. V.F.B. [pageend5]Btrana - Page 6. Věstník bokola San Francisco Březen - March 1981. Origin of the blue jeans. As a child I grew up in C. Budějovice, also called "The Metropolis of Southern Bohemia". When we reached about sixteen years of age we "gained" a right to come and meet every evening on the main square. It was a perfect square of 32b ft, bordered by ancient patrician homes with each one of them having an arcade in front, which formed a continuous spacious shelter for pedestrians when the weather turned bad. Outside the arcades there was a wide sidewalk, used in nice weather. There were always enough of us to fill one sidewalk of the square which belonged to students from times "immemorial'] There we were stroll- ing boys and girls in separate groups and you could practically feel the fabled cupid’s arrows flying back and forth. It was a nice and clean fun I have fond memories of still today.. And there in the corner of the square stood a 230 ft high "Black Tower" like a sentinel keeping an eagle’s eye on us. Little attention did we pay to a house two doors removed from the City Hall - situated diagonally across the square from the tower - which housed some sort of business miles away from our interest : ready-to- wear clothes, He also didn’t know the story of two imaginative brothers who owned that store few decades ago, whose sharp brains would influence whole world as far as fashion is concerned. They were brothers Aron and Levi Strauss. Their business wasn’t exactly booming and after months of frustration Levi decided to try his luck somewhere else and that led him to the United States before the turn of the century. Levi established the same kind of business somewhere in the Mid- West. The community in which he opened his business had no train connection and once during an unusually long rainy period the highways turned into mud traps and were rendered impassable. This caught Levi short of material for sale. Many local men wanted new pants which he did not have and nobody could deliver them to him over the muddy highways. In this crucial moment he had an idea: he had on stock a hard and thick tent-cloth out of which in his old country they used to make tents for the Austrian army. His brother shipped it to him long afeo, as This material didn’t move any more at české Budějovice. Levi contracted a local tailor to make a few pants. He himself reinforced the seams with copper tacks and put them on the counter. [pageend6]Březen - March lybl__ Věstník Sokola_San_Francisco Strana_—_Page_7j,_ i'he cheap pants very soon proved to be durable and gradually became more and more desirable. Levi right away asked his brother to ship any quantity of similar cloth still available in the old country. Only this time the color was medium blue which led Levi to open the first mass-production factory of pants which later acquired the name of blue jeans. Until today the blue jeans of this trade mark are considered to be the best. Jeans also somehow "democratized" the whole world so to speak. High dignitaries are wearing them as well as wharf-workers. The film super stars order their evening gowns out of this material and the boys and girls in all walks of life wear them too. Now this material appears everywhere from handbags to ties. That’s the story of a man who came to these shores and thanks to his idea and fortitude he established himself rather well. V.F.B.
