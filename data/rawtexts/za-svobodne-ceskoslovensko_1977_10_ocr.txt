[pageend1]United States oj America Congressional Record PROCEEDINGS AND DEBATES OF THE 95^ CONGRESS, FIRST SESSION Vol. 123 WASHINGTON, MONDAY, AUGUST 1, 1977 No. 131 SOVIET OCCUPATION OP CZECHOSLOVAKIA Mr. President, we shall soon mark the anniversary of the fateful events of August 21. 1968, when Soviet-led-armies of the Warsaw Pact occupied Czechoslovakia and brought to an end the Czechoslovak experiment in liberalization. It was a sad day for the peoples of Czechoslovakia and for the cause of freedom. Now, 9 years later, the peoples of Czechoslovakia are still deprived of the blessing of liberty. This situation is especially distressing to those of us who in our youth came to revere the names of Masaryk and Benes, and for those of us who are aware of the constructive citizenship of the Czechs and Slovaks in this country. It is important that Members of Congress take note of this anniversary and speak out, so that the plight of Czechoslovakia will not be forgotten. Liberty For All A Non-Profit Organization Founded in 1918 Devoted to Promote Co-operation of All Peoples for the Preservation of Democratic Freedom August 19, 1977 Washington Chapter AN OPEN LETTER TO LEONID I. BREZHNEV Mr. President: Nine years ago a Soviet-led invasion and occupation ended a short-lived aspiration of the Czechoslovak people fpr freedom and self-determination. This aggressive act of your country violated several Articles of the United Nations Charter, including Resolution 2131 of December 21, 1965, which had been adopted at your government’s motion and which prohibits intervention into domestic affairs of any soverign state. In 1968 Czechs and Slovaks were striving to improve the social order in their country, based on true progress and humanity. The invasion has created general opposition and hate which endangers world peace, and is, in effect, damaging to your own country. However, the people of Czechoslovakia have not and shall not resign themselves to the role of slaves in your neo-colonial empire. In 1969, a heroic student, Jan Palach, immolated himself as a protest against your occupation of his beloved country. ’ On January 1 of this year, hundreds of brave men and women, including many workers, and representing millions of Czechs and Slovaks, signed the now famous “Charter 77”. Contrary to your contentions, they are not dissenters nor traitors; they only ask that you direct your puppet regime in Prague to live up to the provisions of the Helsinki Agreement which is equally binding on your country, as on the other signatories of the final Act. On this ninth anniversary of this infamous, unprovoked aggression, we-urge you to: (1) remove the more than 60,000 Soviet troops occupying Czechoslovakia, as well as the countless thousands of so called “advisors,” holding the country’ in economic and political slavery and denying its citizens basic, internationally recognized and U.N.-sanctioned human rights; (2) in the spirit of detente, respect the human rights of the Czechoslovak people; (3) allow Czechoslovakia to chose its own destiny in freedom! Remember, Mr. President, that history is replete with careers of men aspiring to conquer the world. They all ended in defeat and disgrace, and have left their country weak, demoralized and open to an enemy’s exploitation. Do you want to follow their destiny, or are you ready to correct your greatest political mistake and release Czechoslovakia from Soviet bondage now?
