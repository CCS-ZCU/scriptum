October 13, 1982 Dear Friends: Today is the 64th anniversary of the founding of Czechoslovakia. Following several centuries of foreign domination and mutual separation between the Czechs and Slovaks, October 28, 1918 saw the two nations reunited in an independent and democratic state under the leadership of the country’s leading statesman and philosopher, Thomas Garrigue Masaryk. Before the Second World War, Czechoslovakia was the most prosperous and most demokratic country in East Central Europe. Its government was based on a Western-style constitution. The most important consequence of this state, however, has been the fact that October 28, 1918. made it possible to create an entire generation of Czechoslovak citizens committed to democracy and freedom who today, after years of Nazi occupation and even more years of communist domination, can cany the torch of liberty both in Czechoslovakia and in the free world. I am proud to note that it was our President Wilson who contributed so much to the founding of independent Czechoslovakia back in 1918 when the Czechoslovak Declaration of Independence was issued in the United States. I am also proud to note that it was our great city of New York which gave Czechoslovakia her initial First Lady in the person of Charlotte Garrigue Masaryk. And I am proud to be assocsiated with so many fine Americans of Czech and Slovak descent who live in our great city. We all are aware of the contribution of the sons and daughters of this small country in East Central Europe in the development of the world’s moral, religious, political, educational and cultural ideas and creativity. Let me mention a few illustrations: the arrival of Christianity more than a millenium ago; the teaching of religious reformer Jan Hus; the contribution of Jan Amos Comenius, the teacher of nations: King George of Poděbrady, who proposed to his fellow kings a League of Nations centuries before that organization became a fact. How often do we listen to Antonin Dvorak’s New World Symphony, composed in our country? We all remember hundreds of thousands of Czech and Slovak immigrants who rseached our shores to start their new lives and who, often under very difficult conditions, built their own organizations, churches, and schools in order to maintain their old heritage in the new country. And we are not forgetting free-spirited Czechs and Slovaks who today live under foreign occupation and an oppressive government. We salute the efforts of those who are willing to risk everything to call the attention of the outside world to their plight as they did in Charter 77, one of the noblest declarations of human rights aspirations. It is for all these reasons that I congratulate my fellow Americans of Czech and Slovak origin on the occasion of the anniversary of the founding of their old country.
