Most seats are designated for the olderly or handicaped, and the young people realy stood heed the posted signs and give up their seats for their elders./ And did I feel strange when a young man stood up and offered me hos seat!/ When we left the hotel in Praha to take the streetcar,we were advised to buy our tickets there at the desk as tickets arenjt sold on streetcars. What they didn't tell us was what to do with them. We thought wejd just get on and do what everyone else did. And ... they did nothing. They just got ®n, stood or sat and then got of at their stop ./???/ Finally we found out, after riding a whole day clutching our tickets, that most people buy passes good for the year, and those little boxes attached to poles in the car were for punching our tickets. That's what the red arrow on the ticket was for... the tickets was inserted arrow-point down into the box, the box was pressed and a numbered code punched into the ticket. Occasionally there is a spot check of passengers for passes and correctly punched tickets. I guess we were lucky that one such check wasn't done that day. I mentioned automobile drivers before and wi11 now go into more detail. I hadrd that the drivers in Czechoslovakia were terrible and the roads were auful. Although this is true, one must remember that virtually no-one had cars 'till after WW.II. Then suddenly when cars were available, everyone had to b^-e completaly educated. There was no one in the family who had driven before, oo -one to teach safety points as we do when a child rides with us. All at once the cars and inexperienced drivers were on the roads.... that were and will be inadequate because of narrow streets lined with homes and building too precious to tear down. There are some super-highways being built to connect major ci ties but most are the two lanes roads that have connected the cities for centuries. That's just the way it is. Driving regulations, on the other hand, are quite strict. The driver cannot drink anything alcoholic during the period he operated the car / and not less than twoo hours before/. If he is stpped and evidence points to infraction of this law, his license and automobile title will be taken away; along with some time spent in jail and a heavy fine. It is said that the police often stay in in the earea of a hospoda and watch for that... looking to see who drinks beer and who drives the car. It was strange to walk into any town square, down any street and see the majority of young people wearing blue jeans and T-shirts with U.S. legos. It 's not strange to see them as such, but to be thinking "Europe" and have home a "millions miles away" and then come face to face with a jeans-clad teenagers wearing a T-shirt adorned with "Washington, D.C.", Indianapolis Speed-way" or "Ohio State University " and know that they'are Czech kids, was quite a surprise. Anything that says " jeans, belt, cap or pants is of great value® It was also strange to see so many people who dosely resembled .friends and relations* at home. The Slavic facial structure is seen everywhere, not as it is here with the mixture of ethnic background we have. It was startling to glance at someone and for a second think ’’that we were looking at someone from home. We had one such encounter at Sv.Tomas in Praha, when a young man came in who could have been our sin's twin's twin brother ! Everywhere we went we met people who would go out of their way to be helpful. The taxi driver who get his passenger to let us ride with him when we couldn'n get another cab... the bus driver who run into the station to see if our train was still there ... the man who took us to make sure we got off at the right place... the old lady at the sta tion in Plzen who tried so hard to get us a taxi/ they were all catering to weddings at noon on Sunday/ ... al! the kind and helpful people who gave us directions to places ve Czechoslovakia [pageend3]Page ip věstník Sokola San Ft * a nc is co December 1^78 wanted to get to.
