CALIFORNIA 311 San Gabriel Court Sierra Madre, CA 91024 April 2o, 1977 The Honorable Senator S.I.Hayakawa United States Senate Washington, D.C.20510 Dear Senator: Re.: Divided Czechoslovak Families Thank you for your inquiry regarding Czechoslovak children at.the Department of State. I wrote in my letter that in Czechoslovakia there are over 400 children of Czech and Slovak parents seeking to join, their families in the Free World. In the letter of Mr. Kempton B. Jenkins, Acting Assistant Secretary for Congressional Relations, a discrepancy is indicated between our number of 400 and his that only 70 individuals are seeking to join immediate relatives in the US, according to the US Embassy in Prague record. The number 400 includes Czechoslovak children who have been denied permission to emigrate to join their parents not only in the USA, but also in Western Europe and other democratic states. Some parents are afraid to ask for their children by official way thru the governments of their new homeland, because the west - democratic government effort in these cases was very often ineffective and the parents are afraid that the revengeful communist regime can use the additional repression against their children. The actual total number of these childepn is 750. By the end of 1976 Czechoslovak authorities permitted about thirty children to join their parents residing in West Germany and promised to allow several children to emigrate whose parents are now in the United States. But, hundreds of families are still waiting for a more humane attitude on the part of the Czechoslovak government. There are not just violations of the US ,— Prague regime relationship, but also violations of international obligations under the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the Final Act of Helsinki. Members of the Commission on Security and Cooperation in Europe heard testimony of Mrs. Anna Faltus, representing the Czechoslovak National Council of America and Czech writer Jan Benes on March 15, 1977. Mr. Jan Benes spent 44 months in communist prisons and he left Czechoslovakia with his wife without their children after the Soviet invasion. Since then, they have made many futile attempts to reunite the family. Mrs. Faltus1 testimony also dealt with the problem of divided families, a field in which she has been active for several years on behalf of Czechoslovak refugees. Thank you for your help and understanding Very truly yours, George Brewer Chairman [pageend5]Návrh rezoluce, vypracovaný výborem odbočky, který byl téměř beze změny schválen a postoupen celoamerickému sjezdu: Czechoslovakia signed the Helsinki Declaration pledging to uphold provisions of its "Third Basket" on human rights. Continuing violation of these rights by Czechoslovak authorities caused the publication of a manifesto signed by 620 writers, priests, journalists, scientists, workers and persons active during the "Prague Spring 1968." Feeling unsafe and under pressure from Moscow, the Czechoslovak Communist regime has embarked on a new wave of repression, intimidation, and arrests. The signatories of Charter 77, which insist on nothing more than the adherence by the Czechoslovak government to its own constitutional language and its international obligations under the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the Final Act of Helsinki, are being persecuted by the Czechoslovak regime in ways reminiscent of the harshest methods of political repression. More than 500 signatories were interrogated by the police, generally over a period of several days, and a number of signatories were dismissed from their jobs. The spokesman for Charter 77, Dr.Jan Patocka, former philosophy professor at Charles University in Prague, died of a cerebral hemorrhage on March 15 after having been interrogated by the police for eleven hours. The other spokesman for Charter 77, playwright Vaclav Havel, journalist Jiri Lederer, former theater director Ota Ornest, physicist Vladimir Lastuvka, and agronomist Ales Machacek are in jail. Therefore, We, the participants of the Convention of the Republican Heritage Group Council, condemn the Prague regime for its violations of human rights. We ask the President of the United States and the State Department to put the suppression of human rights in Czechoslovakia on the United Nation agenda. We make an appeal to the U.S. Congress to raise their voices for freedom on behalf of all those arrested signatories of Charter 77. Hollywood, May 15, 1977.
