TENEBRAE SERVICE: This name of this celebration, which began in the seventh century, means "darkness". Although it is not an official part of the Holy Week liturgies, it is used in many parishes as a prayer service preparing for the three most important days of the Church year. It is a celebration which uses the progressive extinguishing ol candles to symbolize the giving up of Christ’s lile for us. One parish tradition has been to use this celebration on Wednesday of Holy Week as a solemn entry into the most important part of HolyWdbr. toe Triduum. Theceforbation is rich in imagery, since every word of toe celebration comes trom the Bible. TRIDUUM: This word, which means "three days", denotes the climax of the church year. It is not considered part of Lent, but is a separate "season" all its own. The Triduum begins with the Mass of the Lord’s Supper on Holy Thursday and continues through Easier Sunday. All three celebrations of these days are conneetgd in their meaning and should be seen as an ensemble. They are a "retreat of sorts in which we come together at one celebration each day. recounting the saving events ol Jesus’ life and looking to the future with hope. HOLY THURSDAY: This is’the celebration of the Lord's last supper. His 'passover with His disciples In this celebration, we concentrate on the Eucharist, our priesthood in baptism, and what it means to be followers of Christ (seen especially in the symbolic washing ol fee:.” an expression of humility in service of God). GOOD FRIDAY: This day recalls the death of Jesus on the cross. Traditionally celebrated at 3:00 in toe afternoon, it may also be celebrated at a fater hour for pastoral reasons. There has been the tradition of beginning an "Easter fast" on Good Friday because Good Friday reminds us of our hunger for Easter Sunday. Thus, the Mass is not celebrated on this day, though communion may be received. MARCH 2, 1988 Page 7 OHIO INSURANCE DEPARTMENT VINDICATES SUPREME OFFICERS AND THE FIRST CATHOLIC SLOVAK UNION IN CHARGES FILED BY THE REVEREND STEPHEN FURDEK DISTRICT OF CLEVELAND PRESIDENT KRISTOFIK’S FORMAL REPORT 7 February 15, 1988 The first Catholic Slovak Union was founded in 1890 by Rev. Stephen Furdek, and over the past ninety-eight years has experienced all types of trials and tribulations. Our Society has been plagued by financial crises, by epidemics such as the Spanish Influenza disaster in 1918, and by harassment by various members over the past ninety-eight years which seriously threatened the stability and very existence of our organization. In the past few months Dr. Anthony Sutherland, presently compiling the data and historical background for the publication of the 100th Anniversary of the founding of the First Catholic Slovak Union, has published in our JEDNOTA newspaper a series of articles which cover the various calamities which have beset our Society over the past ninety-eight years, and which the membership has found to be extremely interesting and informative. It is strange that some of the events of the past . ninety-eight years appear to be happening again, which brings truth to the adages that “Truth is Stranger than Fiction,” that “History Repeats Itself,” and that “Those Who Do Not Heed the Lessons of History are Due to Repeat its Errors." This background information is offered because the prestige of our Society, its Supreme Officers, and the Office of the Presidency have been seriously maligned and damaged by events which have occurred over the past nine months. During this period of time any variety of rumor was circulated throughout the Society that our organization was in serious trouble. These rumors alleged that Supreme Officers were resigning their positions because of serious internal problems within our Society. There were charges made that the President of the First Catholic Slovak Union “FAILED TO COMPLY WITH THE BYLAWS OF THE FIRST CATHOLIC SLOVAK UNION, WAS EXCEEDING HIS POWERS, FAILED TO COMPLY WITH THE PROVISIONS OF OHIO REVISED CODE 3921, DISSIPATED SOCIETY ASSETS, WAS OPERATING OUR SOCIETY FRAUDULENTLY, AND WAS OPERATING ^OUR SOCIETY FOR THE GREED OF A FEW.” It was not sufficient that these allegations were brought before selected Districts and members of our Society, but these charges were brought before the INSURANCE DEPARTMENT OF THE STATE OF OHIO. Similar charges were directed also to the PRESIDENT OF THE SUPREME COURT of the First Catholic Slovak Union, requesting a trial before the Supreme Court, and seeking impeachment of the Supreme President of the First Catholic Slovak Union. Since there has been little or nothing published [due to pending circumstances], in our JEDNOTA newspaper concerning these matters for the past nine months, it is only appropriate, now that this matter has been brought to a conclusion through the reply received from the Ohio Department of Insurance, that / this complete situation be brought to the attention of all the Districts, Branches, and members of our Society, so that we all collectively learn what happens when arrogant, misguided, and mis-directed District officers are permitted to act irresponsibly and mislead their District membership. This is the case at hand, and as President of the First Catholic Slovak Union, I would now like to ser the record straight for our entire membership. On May 8, 1987, the Rev. Stephen Furdek District of Cleve- land, Ohio, under the leadership of Brother Stephen J. Kandra, President, and Brother John S. TO: George Fabe, Superintendent of Insurance and Stafford McGuire, Chief Examiner Report of Investigation On May 12, 1987, ihe Ohio Department of Insurance (the Department) received a complaint concerning a corporate reorganization of the First Catholic Slovak Union of the United States of America and Canada (hereinafter the fraternal). Accompanying the letter was an article describing the reorganization that appeared in the fraternal's newspaper distributed to its members. As described in the newspaper article, the fraternal was forming four corporations. The fraternal would be the shareholder of one corporalion (the "holding company") and the holding company itself would be the shareholder of the three other new corporations (the subsidiary corpora- V lions). After the formation of these corporalions, the fraternal would transfer ownership of certain properly (a printing operation and real estate) to the subsidiary corporations. In the month following the original complaint, the Department received a number of telephone calls concerning the matter and on June 9. 1987 a telegram was received by the Department requesting "immediate action pursuant to Section 3921.26A-1. A-5 of the Ohio Revised Code." That section provides: “(A) When the Superintendent of insurance upon investigation finds that a domestic society & (1) has exceeded its powers; (2) has failed lo comply with any provision of Chapter 3921 of the Revised Code; (3) is not fulfilling its contracts in good faith; (4) has a membership of less than four hundred after an existence of one year or more (5) is conducting its business fraudulently or in a manner hazardous io its members, creditors, the public, or ihe business" it shall hold a show-cause hearing. Through several conversations with an attorney named Andrew Pulka, who is also a member of the fraternal and one of the persons questioning the propriety of the reorganization, it became apparent that the complaints concerned three distinct issues: $ I. Whether the reorganization could be accomplished without membership or convention approval 2. Whether there were conflicts of interest involving the board members of the holding company and subsidiary corporations. 3. Whether there was fraudulent conduct by the fraternal in represent ing that the Department approved the reorganization. The Department's investigation The Department's investigation was conducted through interviews with the complainants, correspondence with the fraternal (officers or directors), interviews with the fraternal's attorneys and also interviews with Department staff or former staff. In the course of the interviews two additional issues developed, specifically: (a) whether certain fringe benefits were properly provided the fraternal. (b) whether there was compliance with a registration requirement under Ihe insurance holding company act. During the course of the investigation, the fraternal voluntarily corrected any deficiencies suggested by these additional issues. Discussion of issues 1. Representations that Department approved the reorganization Allegations were made that ihe fraternal fraudulently represented to its membership that the Department approved the reorganization. Support for the allegation of fraud consisted of a copy of an original letter on Department stationery expressing approval of the reorganization. The alleged copy of that letter has a typewritten dale of September 23, 1986 but also has a date apparently marked by a date stamp of October 1, 1985, Because of the October I, 1985 stamped dale, the suggestion was made by one of the complainants that the September 23, 1986 Idler (even unsigned) is a forgery. The fraternal's attorney provided a copy of the same letter with signature. That copy doesn't contain ihe October 1, 1985 dale stamp. The signature has been verified by the letter's writer and he had independent recollection of the letter. 1 have concluded that the date stamped alleged fraudulent copy is inexplicable but does not effect Ihe authenticity of the copy of Ihe document submilled by the fraternal's attorney. 2. Conflicts of Interest. Certain conflicts of interest proscribed by the fraternal's bylaws were alleged The complainants asserted that ehe occupations of certain board members and officers (of the fraternal and the subsidiaries) permitted them to earn fees, commissions or other compensation related io ihe reorganization. While the complainants were correct in their description of the occupations, the investigation uncovered none of the proscribed compensation arrangements. 3. Membership or convention approval of reorganization. The propeny involved in the reorganization is not specifically described in the constitution or bylaws of the fraternal. Nonetheless, several provisions of these basic organizational documents (notably those creating the office of editor) arc related co the activities affected by the reorganization. Additionally, the reorganization transfers title to properties of the fraternal and alters long-standing organizational arrangements of the fraternal. Conclusion Each of the issues raised by the complainants involved interpretations of the fraternal’s constitution and bylaws. Those documents also outline a procedure that members may use to submit such interpretative matters for resolution by the fraternal. In (his case, the complainants didn't follow Ihe procedures mandated by these basic organizational documents. NEITHER THE EVIDENCE PRESENTED BY THE COMPLAINANTS, NOR THE EVIDENCE DEVELOPED IN THE DEPARTMENT'S INVESTIGATION WARRANTS PROCEEDING WITH A SHOW CAUSE HEARING PURSUANT TO OHIO REVISED CODE SECTION 3921.26. Additionally, I have no uncertainty that the fraternal presented the reorganization to the Department for consideration during its planning stages in 1986. At that time, sufficient disclosures were made for the Depan- ment to consider the matter. At lhal time, the Department concluded dial the matter was an issue controlled by the investment law provision of Ohio Revised Code Section 3907.14 and approved the matters as proper investments for the fraternal.
