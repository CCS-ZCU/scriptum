In December 1968, immediately after the "events'' (as the second occupation of Czechoslovakia was commonly referred to in the USSR), I with a group of other tourists from Moscow, arrived in Prague. To be in Prague and not visit the ”U Kalicha" pub, so lovingly described by Jaroslav Hašek in "The Good Soldier Schweik", was simply inconceivable. So though we were forbidden to leave the group, five of us quietly slipped out of the hotel and walked through night-time Prague down to the Moldau River, where the famous pub was located in one of those narrow street dating back to the Middle Ages. As we walked through the streets of the city, we Soviets saw, for the first time in our lives, anti-Soviet slogans and inscriptions. Huge letters in white paint proclaimed: "Father - a liberator, son - an invader." Efforts had been made to remove die inscription, but as though hexed it continued to stare boldly at the world. On the building next door, a meter-wide five-pointed red star enclosed a black fascist swastika within it. Next to it, on the building's pediment, were two inscriptions set side by side. "Ivan, look into your mother's eyes and "Bastards, go back to your damn country." In silence we walked through the city filled with hatred for us, looking guardedly around. These inscriptions gave us an almost physical sense of being slapped in the face. Wishing us well, our guide Maria, a Bulgarian, advised us not to speak Russian out in the streets. She said it could be dangerous: we could get hit in the face, or even’worse. But five of us could not walk without opening our mouths at all, though by now we were indeed quite subdued. "We've been walking for quite some time now," said one of us, "yet the street we are looking for is no where in sight." From around the comer, an elderly Czech carying a big bulging briefcase was coming toward us. Apparently he cought the last part of the sentence for he stopped, looked at us with surprise and asked: "Are you Russian"? "Yes," we answered smiling apologetically and shyly. "Get out of here and go to..." Dumfoundcd, the the five of us looked at the departing Czech. He walked slowly, swinging his briefcase in time with his steps, obviously conscious of walking on his native Czech soil. We would long remember that evening. We began fidgeting and talking in whispers. The red-and- black star-swastika looked down upon us from the wall and the Czech’s abusive Russian profanity still rang in our ears. We were undecided: some wanted to return to the hotel as quickly as possible; others, despite what had happened, to continue on our way. The street was deserted, not a soul in sight; gradually we calmed down and proceeded with our search. Finally, at the very end of a street to our right, we saw a pub "U Kalicha". Its small dark halls, with their dark-colored walls and roughly hewn tables and benches, were filled almost to capacity. The beer served there was dark and came in heavy mugs seemingly fashioned with an ax. Everything was just as it was in Schweik's time. A portrait of Emperor Franz-Joseph, covered with flies, was hardly visible on a wall to the right. As became evident later on more than one occasion, the Czechs recognized us whether we spoke Russian or remained silent. It was our clothes that betrayed us. As though by mutual consent, we were all dressed alike, like soldiers in a row. The men all in clothing made from the same material, the women in identical seal-skin coats - all, so-called, "individually-styled", and mass- produced. We stood in the entrance looking for a place to sit down. A monotonous, lôw-level hum of several conversations being carried on at the same time hung over the tables. Suddenly, we knew not how, a chain reaction of hatred was triggered - the Czechs recognized us. The people in both halls, until then busy with their beer, their friends and their converstion, one by one stopped drinking, then talking; turning away from their friends, they turned their heads toward us. A few seconds went by, or so it seemed, and both rooms fell silent, with a total, profound and sinister silence. We found ourselves standing face to face with the Czechs, looking into their hate- filled and threatening eyes. Hatred in their eyes and scorn on their faces. Merciful Lord, not a single face expressing sympathy, not a single kindly glance, not even a single indifferent face - which we would have indeed been glad to see. We were gripped by a strange immobility and breathless with fear. Impossible to go forward, but even more frightening to turn our backs and retreat. Their hatred had a hypnotizing effect, virtually freezing us on the spot - we just could't move. This sort of thing happens to a person only once or twice in a lifetime. I neither knew or remember how ling this went on: seconds, minutes or longer. As though sentenced, we awaited the outcome. Amidst the overall silence an immobility, a middle-aged Czech seated immediately behind the Emperor's portrait in a far comer of the room to the right, began rising slowly, a mug of beer in his outstretched hand pointed toward us. The mug was aimed straight at our heads, in a gesture similar to a gun being aimed just before firing. Then it was as though someone had given an invisible and inaudible command. By now all the Czechs in both halls began rising slowly, delibarately and calmly behind their tables, pointing their gun-like beer mugs at our heads. The last to take aim were the waiters stationed in various parts of the halls. I no longer saw beer-filled mugs, I only saw Czechs armed with guns. God forbid standing there before them when their hearts were full of revenge. Feeling our way down the steps with unwilling legs, stumbling on each and every step, eyes fixed on those mugs of beer, we backed out of the pub. It seemd that no power on earth could force us to turn our backs and run. This would have been even more terrifying. Only when we were out in the street did we hear the sound of singing in the pub. Was it just a song or was it a hymn? I don't know. But the singing was full of feeling and solemnity. And as before, the dark beer glistened in the mugs held high in outstretched hands.
